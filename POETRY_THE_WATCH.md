# POETRY: THE WATCH

*On the 0000–0400 watch, when the world is dark and the mind drifts.*

---

## Midwatch

0000 to 0400.
The graveyard of the rotation,
the watch no one wants,
the watch everyone takes.

You come up the companionway
into a different boat.
The deck is wetter than it was.
The stars have changed
or you have.

The coffee is from a thermos
that someone left for you
or that you left for yourself
earlier in the evening.
It is hot.
That is the only luxury.

The horizon is gone.
The horizon is the place
where the radar
decides there's no longer
any ocean.

You are alone
with the instruments
that don't sleep,
the log book
that won't write itself,
and the small habit
of attention,
which is the only thing
that keeps a boat
from being a story.

---

## The Radar Sweep

The green arm rotates.
The phosphorescent afterglow
of the trace
spins around the scope.

Each sweep is a question.
The next sweep
is the same question,
repeated.

*Has anything answered
that wasn't there before?*

The targets bloom
where they bloom.
A buoy at two miles,
its return painting
small and bright
in the lobe of the screen.

A ship
at the edge of the radar's patience,
a return you almost
don't believe in
until the next sweep,
when it's still there,
now closer,
now closer,
now crossing your bow
at a distance
that calls for a course change.

The radar asks.
The radar asks again.
The radar keeps asking
until something answers,
or doesn't,
or has passed,
and the screen
is again all yours.

---

## Phosphorescence

At two knots,
in certain water,
the bow wave lights up.

A green-white glow
under the hull,
in the wake,
on the ripples spreading
outward
from anything that moves.

You throw a bucket over
and the water that comes back
is full of cold stars.
You drag your hand through
and the hand comes back
blazing with tiny lights
that fade as you watch them.

The chemistry is small.
A plankton, disturbed,
emits a single flash.
A billion of them,
in aggregate,
become this:
the sea lighting itself
in your wake.

You are being followed
by a galaxy
you made.

---

## Cup of Coffee at 0200

The thermos is half empty.
The mug is the chipped one
that lives on the hook
by the nav station.

The coffee is not good.
The coffee is hot.
The coffee is the only thing
in the world
that has happened to you
in the last hour.

You drink it
because there's nothing else
to do with your hands.
You drink it
because the world is dark
and the darkness is cold
and the cold gets in
through the collar of the jacket
and the cup's warmth
is the only warmth
you can hold.

The mug is a small
portable sunrise.
You drink it,
and the night
is shorter by one cup.

---

## The AIS Contact

A target appears on the plotter.
A triangle.
A name.
A course over ground.
A speed over ground.

The AIS contact
is a stranger
announcing themselves
in lat and long,
MMSI,
heading, draft.

You watch the triangle
move across the screen.
You know what they're doing.
You know where they're going.
You know their closest point
of approach
without ever seeing them.

They will pass.
They will not hail you.
You will not hail them.
The AIS contact
is the courtesy of strangers
in a place
where courtesy
is mostly invisible.

The triangle moves on.
The screen forgets them.
You don't.
For ten minutes
a ship you never saw
shared your night,
and you knew them
by their broadcast.

---

## Dawn Watch

0400 comes slow,
then fast.

The first light
is not in the sky.
It's in the water.
A paling of the black,
a gray
that wasn't there a minute ago.

The horizon
returns.
It had been absent.
It is now drawn in
with a thin gray line
where the sea
and the lower air
decide to meet.

The stars go one by one,
not by brightness
but by position —
the southern ones first,
then the zenith,
then the last bright one
in the east,
held
until the sky behind it
is too bright to hold it.

The dawn watch
is the watch
where you learn
what was there all along
behind the dark.

---

## The Watchkeeper's Prayer

Not a prayer to anyone.
A prayer to the watch itself.

*Keep me awake.
Keep the instruments lit.
Keep the targets honest.
Keep the wind where I last saw it.
Keep the boat moving
in the direction I last steered it.*

Keep me company
in the way the radar keeps me company,
the way the log keeps me company,
the way the coffee
keeps me company.

Keep the night
from being only dark.

When the watch ends,
let me hand it off cleanly.
Let the next watchkeeper
find everything where I left it.
Let the log be legible.
Let the coffee in the thermos
be enough for one more cup.

When I go below,
let me sleep
the way the boat sleeps —
with one system still running,
one small attention
that doesn't fully
shut down.

*Amen.*
