# POETRY: SHAPES OF WATER

*On the forms water takes — from the technical to the witnessed.*

---

## Fetch

The distance the wind has travelled
over open water
before it reaches you.

A short fetch
makes small waves,
chopped, hesitant,
the sea still arguing
about what to do.

A long fetch
makes long waves,
smooth-backed,
marching in rows
that have been organizing
themselves for hundreds of miles.

The fetch is history.
The fetch is the wind's
diary,
written on the water.

When the wind dies,
the fetch remains,
the swell still travelling,
the waves still arriving,
their maker already gone.

---

## The Wavelength

The distance
between two crests
of the same wave.

The wavelength doesn't care
about you.
The wavelength is the period
made horizontal,
the time between
turned into a distance.

You can measure it
from the boat,
by eye,
if the sea is regular enough
to admit a ruler.

A long wavelength
is a long-period swell —
the deep water's slow heartbeat.
A short wavelength
is wind wave,
local, recent,
still being made.

The wavelength
is the boat's question
to the sea:
*how long?*
The sea answers
in a unit
the boat has to learn
to read.

---

## Green Water

Not spray.
Not spindrift.
Green water is solid —
a moving hill of sea
that comes aboard
and occupies the deck
as if it owned the place.

You see it
through the lee rail
or the dodger
or the forward hatch,
and it has no face,
no particular shape,
just volume,
just the entire sea
deciding to be here.

It hits you
with a weight
you forgot water had.
It is cold.
It is everywhere.
It takes the breath
and the footing
and a piece of the language
you were about to speak.

After it drains
through the scuppers
the deck is different.
The deck has been told
something by the water.
You have been told
the same thing.

---

## Spindrift

The blown-off skin of the wave.

The wind takes the top
of every crest
and lifts it
into the air,
where it becomes
a horizontal rain,
a fog of droplets
moving with the wind,
moving faster than the wave.

Spindrift is the sea
that has decided
to leave.

It stings.
It salts everything.
It cuts visibility
in a way that fog doesn't —
fog softens,
spindrift erases.

You lean into it
and it leans back.
The whole ocean
is in the act
of being somewhere else.

---

## Fog Bank

It doesn't arrive.
It was already there.

You sailed
into what was already
around you,
and the bank
was the whole sea's
agreement
to be quiet,
to be the same color
as the air,
to remove the horizon
as if the horizon
had been optional all along.

The fog horn
becomes the only
geography.

A boat somewhere
in the bank
sounds once
and is gone.
You don't see them.
You don't see anything.
You see only
the rope of the bow
disappearing into white,
and the deck
which is now
the entire visible world.

---

## Bergy Bits

Smaller than growlers,
smaller than bergy bits
which are smaller than floes —
the ice comes in grades.

A bergy bit
is a piece of glacier
that has calved
and travelled,
the size of a small house,
the color of nothing
the human eye
has a name for —
white where the air is in it,
blue where the water is,
green where the light
sits longest.

You see them
as small white shapes
that don't move
with the sea.

The sea moves around them.
The sea knows
they are heavier
than anything alive.

You give them
a wide berth.
Not because they're moving
but because they don't.
A bergy bit
doesn't care
where you are going.
You care
where it is.

---

## The Mirror

No wind.
No current.
No fetch.

The water is flat,
not flat as in still —
flat as in reflective,
flat as in a black mirror
laid horizontally
between the boat
and the sky.

The boat sits in the mirror
and the mirror sits in the boat.
The mast is doubled.
The cloud is doubled.
You lean out
and your doubled self
leans in.

The mirror is rare.
The mirror is the sea
that has forgotten
how to be the sea
and is now,
briefly,
the sky's
only honest surface.
