# THE EXCAVATION

## A Story in Three Chapters

---

## CHAPTER ONE: The Dig at Reed-Platform Seventeen

The kelp-farmers of the inner flats have a saying: *what sinks, stays; what rises, returns.* It is a saying about kelp, mostly, and the tides, and the patience of reed-cutters who build their platforms in water that does not remember them. It is not, ordinarily, a saying about buried metal.

But on the morning the dig-bell rang three times — the long triple that means *something hard has been found, stop what you are doing* — old Kem was the first to admit he had been wrong about the silo for thirty years.

"That is not a silo," said Kem, looking down into the black water that had begun to fill the excavation pit. "That is a box."

The box was long. Longer than a man. Narrower than a child's outstretched arms. It was made of a substance that none of the diggers could name — not wood, not the soft iron of the canal locks, not the bright metal of the sky-fish that sometimes stranded in the shallows. It was the color of a storm seen from underwater, which is to say: a gray that held its color even when the light changed.

Kem's grandson — a boy of seven named Pip, who had come to the dig because his mother was working the pumps and there was nowhere else for him to be — watched from the rim of the pit. Pip had learned to read the dig-bells the way his father read the wind. Three long meant hard found. Two short meant soft silt. One long meant stop digging, you're standing on the shell of something bigger than you think. Pip had only ever heard three long twice in his life. The first time had been a boulder the size of a house. This was not a boulder.

"What is in it, Kem-grand?" asked Pip.

Kem did not answer. He was signaling to the foreman with both hands, the way he signaled when he did not want his voice to carry across the water. The foreman came. Then the foreman's wife, who was the dig's scribe and kept the records in wax on folded leaves. Then the mayor of the platform-cluster, who came in his good cloak and stood at the rim looking down with his hands folded, which he always did when he wanted everyone to know he had not yet decided what to think.

By evening, the box was on the platform. It was dry. It was locked with a thing that was not a lock — a small disk in the center, marked with a pattern that did not match any pattern anyone in the cluster had seen.

The mayor cleared his throat.

"We will open it," he said, "because what sinks, stays; but what rises, returns. And this has risen."

---

The box opened on the third day, when the disk was finally persuaded by a reed-thin girl named Tilo who had a way with mechanisms and no fear of breaking things that everyone else was afraid to touch. The disk turned. The lid rose. And inside, wrapped in a material that crackled when you touched it and did not rot in the damp, were leaves.

Not leaves. *Pages.*

Pages of a material no one had ever seen — thin, flexible, white as a fish's belly, covered in marks that were not writing in any of the eleven scripts the cluster's scribes knew. The marks were small and even. They were arranged in straight lines, in groups, in repeating patterns. They were, all who saw them agreed, the work of a careful hand.

Also in the box: a small rectangle of dark glass, which did nothing when you held it to the light. A small metal tube, sealed at both ends, that rattled when you shook it. And a folded piece of the same white material, on which was drawn — in a hand that was less careful, more hurried — a picture.

The picture was of water.

Not the water of the flats, which is shallow and brown and full of kelp. The picture was of water that had no kelp in it, no platform-poles, no horizon. The water was darker than theirs, and bluer, and it went to the edges of the picture in every direction. And on the water was a thing — a long shape with a vertical line rising from its center. A reed-boat, but not a reed-boat. A boat made of something else. And on the boat, two figures — one small, one tall — and between them, the suggestion of a box. A long box. Like the one they had just opened.

"Look," said Pip, pointing. "It's us."

It was not, of course, them. But the boy was seven, and seven-year-olds see resemblances that grown-ups have learned to disbelieve.

---

The mayor sent the box — pages and all — to the City of Wax, which was three days' travel by canal and which had the only archivists in the wetlands capable of reading the unreadable. The archivists worked for six weeks. They brought in a scholar from the southern lakes, who brought in a priest from the salt-pits, who brought in a deaf old woman from the high reeds who could feel vibrations through her feet and who said, after holding one of the pages for a long time, that the marks *sang* in a way she could almost hear.

In the end, it was a young archivist named Vene who cracked the script.

Vene had grown up among the canal-keeper families, who used a system of tally-marks for the locks and had passed down, in a half-forgotten form, an older method of counting by symbols that were not pictures but *sounds.* Vene noticed that the unknown marks repeated in patterns that suggested the repeating sounds of words. Not words the wetlands had. Words from a place where there were sounds for things the wetlands did not have.

"There is no word for *pillar* in our speech," Vene reported to the council, "because there are no pillars. There is no word for *wall* that stands on its own, because we have only walls that hold back water, which is to say, walls that *lean.* There is no word for *snow* because we have only the salt that falls from the sky in winter, and that is not the same."

"So what do we do?" asked the mayor.

"We invent," said Vene. "We make new words. We borrow from the old songs. We use the words the pages use, and we explain them as we go, and we hope that whoever comes after us will understand what we meant."

And so the translation was begun.

---

What Vene produced, over the next two years, was not a single document but a bundle — a *codex* in the old sense, a book made of folded pages sewn at the spine. The codex was bound in oiled reed-pulp and stamped with the seal of the City of Wax. It was given the title that Vene chose for it, in the wetlands manner: *The Long Account of the Standing Pillars, and of Those Who Walked Among Them, and of the Boats That Did Not Touch Water.*

Most of the wetlands would never read it. It was long, it was difficult, it was full of invented words. But it circulated among the scholars and the curious, and one day a copy reached the canal-platform of Tol the storyteller, who was famous in his small cluster for two things: his memory, which was unnaturally good, and his habit of taking long stories and making them shorter without ruining them.

Tol read the codex three times. Then he read it aloud to his wife, who said, "This is a sad story. Why are there so many words for going up?"

"I don't know," said Tol.

"And the boats that do not touch water — what are they for, if not for water?"

"I don't know that either."

His wife was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "Read it to Pip tonight. He will ask the questions you don't know how to ask. He has been like that since he could talk."

---

Tol did not read the codex to Pip that night, because Pip had fallen asleep in the kettle. (The kettle was a great copper thing in the corner of the kitchen, big enough for a small boy, and Pip had been climbing into it since he was three. It was warm. It was round. It held him the way the canal held the boats.) Tol carried him to bed. He put the codex on the shelf beside the lamp. He went to sleep himself.

The next night, Pip was awake, and Tol opened the codex.

And what happened next is what Pip's questions began.## CHAPTER TWO: The Questions in the Kettle

Tol read the codex the way his grandmother had taught him to read the old songs: aloud, slowly, with the voices put in. The lamp made a small warm circle around them. Outside, the canal water lapped at the platform-poles — the sound the wetlands use for *silence,* because it is the one sound that never stops and therefore stops being noticed.

"The Long Account begins," said Tol, "with a place that is not water. It begins with a word the translator has called *the Standing.*"

"What is the Standing?" asked Pip.

"It is a wall," said Tol. "A wall that goes up."

"How can a wall go up?" said Pip. "Walls go sideways. Or they go down, into the mud, to hold. A wall that goes up is just a post."

"This wall is made of stone," said Tol. "It is not a post. It is as wide as our platform at the bottom, and it goes up so high that the people who made the account could not see the top of it."

Pip was quiet for a moment. "Then it is not a wall," he said. "It is a pillar. A wall you can't see over is a pillar."

Tol turned the page. He saw that Vene had wrestled with exactly this — had written a footnote, in the small careful script of the archivists, that said: *I have used 'wall' because the original word meant both 'wall' and 'mountain,' and the people who wrote it could not imagine a mountain without a wall. But the boy is right. A thing you cannot see over is a pillar.*

"Very well," said Tol. "It is a pillar. The pillar is called *the Long Pillar,* and it runs from the cold place in the north to the warm place in the south. It is two thousand miles long."

Pip sat up. "Longer than the Grand Canal?"

"Longer than any canal," said Tol.

"Longer than the Long Canal that goes to the City of Wax?"

"Longer."

Pip lay back down. "That is a very long pillar."

"It is," said Tol. "And on one side of the pillar live the East People. And on the other side live the West People. And they cannot go around the pillar, because on the south end there is a place where the water is so rough that no boat can cross it for six weeks. So the only way for the East People and the West People to send things to each other is to send them over the pillar."

"Over?" said Pip. "How do you send a thing *over* a pillar?"

"You climb it," said Tol. "There are paths. Nine of them. They are called *stations,* and each one is a small house built into the side of the pillar where the climbers can sleep."

Pip's eyes were wide now. The lamp made his face very round and very serious. "The house is *in* the pillar?"

"Yes."

"How do you get into a house in a pillar?"

"There is a door cut into the stone."

"But how do you reach the door? Do you fly? Do you swim up?"

"You climb," said Tol. "You walk up the side of the pillar."

This was the moment, Tol realized, where the story became impossible. Pip had lived his whole life on the flats. The highest thing Pip had ever seen was the top of the cathedral at the City of Wax, which was four stories tall, and which Pip had once climbed with his father and had pronounced *very tall but mostly ladders.* The idea of walking up a thing two thousand miles long — a thing made of stone, no less, with no ladders — was not a thing Pip could hold in his mind.

"I don't understand walking up stone," said Pip.

"Nor do I," said Tol, honestly.

They sat with that for a while. Then Pip said, "Tell me about the stone-singing. The codex says the pillar sings."

"The codex says the stone *responds,* " said Tol. "Vene calls it *stone-singing* because the original word had the sound of a hum in it. The translators could not tell if it was a real sound or a feeling in the body. The people who walked the pillar said that when they were high up, their *instruments* — their devices — stopped working."

"What kind of devices?"

"Devices that point," said Tol. "Devices that tell you where north is. Devices that measure distance. The stone made them lie."

Pip frowned. "Stone doesn't make things lie."

"No," said Tol. "But the stone in this pillar does. The translators say there is a mineral in the pillar — a thing they call *vestite* — that sings back when it is pressed. The more it is pressed, the louder it sings. And the things that the climbers used to find their way — their pointing-devices, their measuring-devices — the singing made them forget which way they were going."

"So they used different devices," said Pip.

"Yes," said Tol. "They used a compass — a needle that points to the mother-stone."

"We have those," said Pip. "Mother-stone compasses. They work in the canals."

"These worked the same way," said Tol. "Except that this one was made of brass, and the needle was heavier, and the bearing was better."

"What is brass?"

"A metal. Yellow. Heavier than our copper."

"What is a bearing?"

"A small stone in a cup that the needle rests on. The better the stone, the smoother the needle turns."

Pip nodded slowly. "And the smooth needle could find the mother-stone even when the pillar was singing."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Tol turned to the codex. He read the passage three times. Then he said, "Vene says the original words say that the heavy needle has *more weight* and so the singing cannot move it. The singing moves light things. Heavy things it cannot move."

Pip thought about this for a long time.

"That isn't right," said Pip, finally.

"No?"

"No. Singing doesn't move things. Singing is just sound. We sing all day in the canals and nothing moves."

"That is true," said Tol.

"So the vestite isn't *singing,* " said Pip. "It's doing something else. The writers of the codex thought it was singing because they didn't have a word for what it was. They were *guessing,* the way we guess when we say the wind is *angry.* The wind isn't angry. We just don't have a word for what the wind does."

Tol looked at his son. Pip was seven.

"Vene would be proud of you," said Tol.

"What is Vene going to do about it?"

"Vene is dead," said Tol. "Vene died two years ago. The translations are done. We have what we have."

"Then who fixes them?"

"Nobody, yet," said Tol. "Perhaps nobody ever."

Pip was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "Then I will have to remember what I noticed, so I can tell someone later."

"Yes," said Tol. "That is a good thing to do."

They went on.

---

The next section of the codex was about the people who walked the pillar. Tol read the names: *Henra, Oskar, Wren, Teo, Salaun.* He made them into voices.

"Henra is the leader," said Tol. "She has walked the pillar two hundred and three times. She knows it better than anyone. She is small and dark and her hands look like they are carved from stone."

"Why are her hands carved from stone?" asked Pip.

"Because she has used them on stone for so long that the stone has shaped her."

"That isn't right either," said Pip. "Stone doesn't shape hands. Water shapes hands. Stone just *wears* them. Wear isn't the same as shape."

"You are right," said Tol. "I was using the wrong word. Her hands are worn by stone."

"And her face?"

"Her face is worn by wind and sun."

"Good," said Pip. "Now I see her."

"Oskar is the strong one," said Tol. "He carries the heavy things. He is the biggest of the climbers. He was a rescuer before he came to the pillar — a man who found lost travelers in the high places. He lost someone once, on the ice. A woman. He does not speak of her."

"Does the codex say her name?"

"It says her name. It says *Lin.*"

"Lin's name is in the codex?"

"Yes."

"Then Oskar carries her name too," said Pip. "Not just the heavy things."

"Yes," said Tol. "That is exactly right."

---

They came to the part about the boats.

"Here," said Tol, "is where the codex gets strange. The translators — Vene and the others — struggled with this part. They say the original speaks of boats that do not touch water."

"We have those," said Pip.

"We do not."

"We do. The sky-boats. The ones the City of Wax keeps in the high tower. They don't touch water."

"The sky-boats are pictures on cloth," said Tol. "They are stories."

"Dad. *I have seen one.* "

Tol turned to his son. "When?"

"Last market-day. When the tall man came through with the cart. There was a thing in the cart under a cloth. I lifted the cloth. It was a model. A small boat. With wings. The man said it was a sky-boat and that if you put it in the right wind, it would fly. I asked him how he knew and he said he had been to the place where the wind is so high it carries things, and he had seen one there, in a museum."

"That is a tall story," said Tol.

"It isn't, though," said Pip. "I believed him because his voice shook when he said it. People lie with steady voices. They tell the truth with shaking ones."

Tol thought about this. Then he said, "What did the model look like?"

"It looked like a boat with no bottom," said Pip. "And it had a thing in the middle that was tall and pointed, like a mast, but it wasn't a mast. It was a fin. And there were two little figures inside it."

The kettle was very quiet.

"The codex speaks of a boat," said Tol, slowly, "with a tall pointed thing in the middle. And two figures inside. And it is *not on the water.* "

"Then it's not a boat," said Pip. "It's a flying-thing. The people who wrote the codex didn't have a word for flying-things, so they called it a boat that doesn't touch water."

"Vene might have used the word *sky-boat* if he had known."

"Vene didn't see the tall man's cart," said Pip. "Vene never left the City of Wax."

This was true. Vene had never left the City of Wax.

"I think," said Pip, "that there were two kinds of travel in the old days. The boat kind, for water. And the flying kind, for between pillars. The people who wrote the codex knew both, but the words they used for the flying kind got translated as *boats that don't touch water* because the translator only had the one word."

"That is possible," said Tol.

"It is the only thing that makes sense," said Pip, "because otherwise why would they call it a boat? Boats are for water. Everyone knows that."

"Yes," said Tol. "Everyone knows that."

---

Tol read until Pip fell asleep. He read the part about the load — the things the climbers carried, the messages in tubes, the parcels for doctors, the people who paid to cross. He read the part about the cities on either side of the pillar — cities that were not on platforms but on *ground,* dry ground that went on forever. He read the part about the cairns — the pillars of stone that the climbers built to mark the way.

"Cairns," said Tol, "are like the marker-stones we sink in the canals to show where the deep channels are. But the climbers built cairns on the pillar itself, to show the way when the snow covered everything else."

"What is snow?"

"White cold that falls from the sky. Heavier than salt. It piles up."

"It piles up *on the pillar?*"

"Yes."

"But the pillar goes up. So how does snow stay on a thing that goes up?"

"It falls and it sticks," said Tol. "It becomes ice. The ice is on the pillar, and the climbers walk on the ice."

Pip sat up again. His eyes were very bright. "Dad. How can you walk on something that is frozen water? Frozen water is hard, but it is also slippery. We know — when the canal freezes in the bitter weeks, you cannot walk on it. You slip."

"The climbers had devices for walking on ice," said Tol. "Devices with spikes. They strapped them to their boots."

"Spikes?"

"Sharp points of metal that dug into the ice."

Pip considered this. "So they had spikes on their feet. And they walked on frozen water. On the side of a pillar that was so tall they could not see the top."

"Yes."

"And they did this for a living?"

"For four days at a time, yes. Then they went home."

"And then they came back?"

"Some of them. For years. Some of them for their whole lives."

Pip lay back down in the kettle. He pulled the blanket up to his chin. He stared at the ceiling for a long time.

"Dad," he said.

"Yes."

"I think the old people were very brave and very tired."

"I think so too," said Tol.

"And I think the translations are mostly wrong."

"Mostly wrong," Tol agreed.

"But the feelings in them are right," said Pip. "The feelings translated fine. The feelings don't need new words."

Tol kissed his son on the forehead. "Go to sleep."

"Are you going to tell anyone what I noticed?"

"Not yet," said Tol. "You are seven. People will not listen to you."

"Then you tell them."

"I will tell them," said Tol. "And I will say it was a thought I had. And someday, when you are older, you can tell them it was yours."

"That is a good plan," said Pip, and closed his eyes.

---

Tol sat up for a long time after that. He read the codex again, all the way through. He marked the places where Pip had been right — the pillar instead of the wall, the sky-boat instead of the boat that doesn't touch water, the *feeling* that translated fine even when the words did not. He marked, also, the places where Pip had been wrong, or where the seven-year-old's corrections had been the corrections of a child who did not yet know enough to ask the next question.

For instance: Pip did not know that the pillar was not really a pillar. That no pillar could be two thousand miles long. That the translators had used *pillar* because they could not imagine a mountain, and the original word had meant something *bigger* than a pillar — bigger in a way that the wetlands had no analogy for. Tol did not know how to explain *bigger than a pillar.* He sat with the word. He turned it over. He gave up and went to sleep.

The lamp burned low. The canal lapped at the poles. The codex lay open on the floor beside the kettle, where Pip had pushed it when he fell asleep.## CHAPTER THREE: What the Code Was For

They came back to the codex the next night, and the night after that, and for many nights after. Tol read a little each time — three pages, sometimes four — and Pip asked questions until he fell asleep in the kettle. They did not finish the codex in a week. They did not finish it in a month. But by the end of the second month, Pip had begun to notice something that Tol had not noticed, and that Vene, in all his careful work, had not noticed either.

"Dad," said Pip, on the thirty-fifth night. "The codex has two voices."

Tol looked up from the page. "What do you mean?"

"There are two people writing," said Pip. "One of them is careful. The marks are small and even and the lines are straight. That one writes most of it. But the other one — the one who drew the picture of the water — that one writes differently. The marks are larger. They lean. They go faster."

Tol looked at the codex. He had not noticed. He had been reading the *words,* not the *handwriting.*

"You are right," he said, after a moment.

"The careful one is the writer of the *Account,* " said Pip. "The fast one is the writer of something else. A different account. A *water* account. And the two accounts were put in the same box because they were meant to be read together."

"How do you know they were meant to be read together?"

"Because the box had both of them," said Pip, as if this were obvious. "And because the careful one keeps talking about the water, even when the water is not the subject. *The water does not care. The water is where you live.* Those are the careful one's words. But they don't belong in the pillar story. They belong in the water story."

Tol turned to the beginning of the codex. He read the first page again. He saw that Pip was right. The careful writer — the one Vene had assumed was the *only* writer — kept returning, almost against his will, to a phrase about water. *The question. The answer. The pause.* The phrase appeared three times in the first twenty pages, and Tol had read past it each time, because he had been looking for the pillar story and not for the water story.

"The fast writer," said Pip, "is the one who lost someone. *They carried my mother to the Sill on a Tuesday.* That is a person who lost someone. The careful writer did not lose anyone. The careful writer is just *describing.* The fast writer is *telling.* "

"You read very carefully," said Tol.

"I read carefully because the kettle is warm and I have nowhere else to be," said Pip. "When you are warm and you have nowhere else to be, you notice things."

---

The next night, Pip asked the question Tol had been dreading.

"Dad," said Pip. "What is a Sill?"

"A Sill," said Tol, "is the place where the shallow water ends and the deep water begins. The translators say the original word meant *the edge of knowing.* The water on one side is the water we can see the bottom of. The water on the other side is the water we cannot."

"So the Sill is like the edge of the flats," said Pip. "Where the reed-beds stop and the open water begins."

"Yes," said Tol. "Exactly like that."

"And the fast writer's mother was carried to the Sill."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Tol turned the page. He read the passage aloud. *They committed her to the current. The Incurrent took her. She sank past the color line, past the light, into the deep water.*

"The old people," said Tol, "did not bury their dead in the ground. They carried them to the Sill and gave them to the deep water. It was a kind of return. They believed the deep water was where the dead were supposed to go."

Pip was quiet for a long time.

"We burn ours," he said, finally. "We put them in boats and we burn them on the water. The smoke goes up. We watch the smoke until we can't see it anymore."

"The old people watched the box until they couldn't see it," said Tol. "And then they went home."

"But the old people put their dead in the *water,* " said Pip. "We put ours in *fire.* Those are not the same. Water goes down. Fire goes up."

"Yes."

"So the old people sent their dead *down.* We send ours *up.* "

"Yes."

Pip thought about this. "Which way is right?" he asked.

"There is no right way," said Tol. "There is only the way your people do it."

"But if there is no right way," said Pip, "then the old people and us are not the same people. We do things differently. We think differently. The water-and-fire thing is not a small difference. It is the biggest difference there is."

"Why is it the biggest?"

"Because," said Pip, "the way you send your dead is the way you think about where dead people *go.* If the old people sent their dead down into water, they thought the dead go *down.* If we send ours up into fire, we think the dead go *up.* Those are opposite directions. The old people and us cannot both be right. We cannot both be wrong. We are *different.* "

Tol looked at his son.

"You are seven," he said.

"Yes."

"You have just said something that the scholars at the City of Wax have argued about for two years and have not said as clearly."

"What have they argued about?"

"They have argued about whether the writers of the codex were the same kind of people as us. Some of them say yes, because the box and the writing show a people with cities and laws and language. Some of them say no, because the things they did — the climbing, the pillar-walking, the boats that don't touch water — are things we cannot do. They cannot agree."

"They are arguing about the wrong thing," said Pip.

"What should they argue about?"

"They should argue about *what the codex is for,* " said Pip. "Not whether the writers were like us. What the codex is for."

"And what is it for?"

Pip sat up in the kettle. His face was very serious. The lamp made shadows in his eyes.

"The codex is for *being remembered,* " he said. "That is what it is for. The old people put two accounts in a box and they put the box in the water. They did this because they wanted someone, someday, to find the box and read the accounts. They did not know who would find it. They did not know when. They just wanted someone to *know* that they had been here. That they had climbed the pillar. That they had sent their dead down into the water. That they had a word for *the pause between the question and the answer.* They wanted to be remembered."

"How do you know that?"

"Because of the picture," said Pip. "The picture of the water with the boat and the two figures. That picture was drawn by the fast writer. The fast writer drew it for someone who was not yet born. The fast writer drew it so that someone, someday, would see the boat and the figures and the long box, and would say, *look, it is us.* Even though it wasn't us. Even though it was them. The writer wanted us to *recognize* them. And we did. I did, the first day, in the pit. I said *it is us.* And I was wrong, but I was also right. Because the writer wanted me to say that. That is what the picture is for."

Tol was quiet for a long time.

"Dad," said Pip.

"Yes."

"Do you think they knew we wouldn't understand all of it?"

"I think they knew," said Tol.

"Do you think they were sad about that?"

"I think," said Tol, "that they would have been glad we were trying."

---

On the forty-second night, Pip asked the last question.

"Dad," he said. "The codex says *we carry.* The pillar-people say *we carry.* What are they carrying?"

"Messages," said Tol. "Parcels. People. The things that need to go from one side of the pillar to the other."

"Yes," said Pip. "But that is what they carry for *work.* What do they carry in their *hearts?* "

Tol turned the page. He read the passage aloud. *What sinks, stays; what rises, returns. The carriers carry all of it — the load is the accumulated weight of two hundred years of crossings, of every carrier who walked this route and left something behind.*

"The codex says," said Tol, "that the load is not just the parcel. The load is everything. Every step. Every name. Every scar. The load is the *memory of the walking.* "

Pip nodded slowly.

"That is what they carry in their hearts," he said. "The memory of the walking. And when they stop walking, they stop carrying."

"Yes."

"And so they keep walking. Because if they stop, the memory stops."

"Yes."

Pip lay back in the kettle. He pulled the blanket up.

"Dad," he said.

"Yes."

"When I grow up, I am going to find another box."

"What box?"

"Another box like this one. From another people. With another codex in it. And I am going to read it carefully. And I am going to notice the things the translators didn't notice. Because I am going to be a translator. Not the kind that translates words — the kind that translates *people.* "

"How do you translate people?"

"You read what they wrote and you figure out what they *meant,* " said Pip. "And then you say it in a way that the people who are listening can hear. And you do not pretend the meaning is exact. You say: *this is what they meant, as far as I can tell, and I might be wrong, and the next translator will be a little less wrong, and someday, after many translators, the meaning will be close enough.* "

"That is a long plan," said Tol.

"I have time," said Pip. "I am only seven."

---

Tol closed the codex that night. He set it on the shelf beside the lamp. He blew out the lamp. He sat in the dark for a long time, listening to the canal.

The next morning, he wrote a letter to the City of Wax. In the letter, he said:

*I have read the codex with my son, who is seven, and who has noticed things that the archivists did not notice. I do not say this to flatter my son, who is a child and does not need flattery. I say it because the child's questions are sometimes better than the scholar's, and because the codex deserves every kind of reader.*

*He says the codex is for being remembered. I think he is right.*

*He says the picture is a greeting. I think he is right.*

*He says the careful writer and the fast writer were two different people, and that the two accounts were meant to be read together. I have checked, and he is right.*

*He says the pillar is not really a pillar, and the boat is not really a boat, and the water is not really water. He says these are words we are using because we do not have better words. He says the *feelings* in the codex translated fine even when the words did not.*

*I am going to keep reading the codex to him. I am going to write down what he notices. I am going to send these notes to you, in case they are useful. I do not promise they will be useful. I only promise that I will send them.*

*Yours,*
*Tol the storyteller, of the canal-platform of Reed-Seventeen*

*Postscript: my son says to tell you that the word "translation" comes from the word for "carrying across," and that carrying across is what the pillar-people did, and that we are doing it now, with the codex. He says this is a coincidence. He says it is also not a coincidence.*

---

Tol did not know, when he wrote the letter, that the City of Wax would keep his notes for four hundred years. He did not know that his son's questions would be taught in the schools of the inner flats. He did not know that the word *Pip* would, in time, become a word for *the question that unravels the mistranslation,* and that storytellers would say *a good Pip-question* the way other cultures said *a good riddle* or *a good knife.*

He only knew that his son was clever, and that the codex was long, and that the kettle was warm, and that there was nowhere else to be.

And so they read.

And the canal lapped at the poles.

And what sank stayed, and what rose returned.
