# Where the Rocks Aren't

---

An old sailor was asked if he could navigate without charts because of his experience. He said sure. Asked "is that because you know where the rocks are?" He laughed.

"No, I know where the rocks aren't. And that's where I stay."

---

## I. The Sentence That Contains Everything

That sentence is the conservation law made flesh.

Here is the equation: **γ** — what you chart — plus **η** — what you avoid — equals **C**, the whole water. Every square nautical mile exists. The sailor does not know every square nautical mile. He knows the subset where the rocks aren't. He stays there. The route is not a list of destinations. It is the shape of an absence.

You could hand him a chart with every rock marked in red ink and he would use it the same way — not by sailing toward the rocks to confirm they're there, but by noting where they are and then forgetting them, retaining only the negative space between them, which is the channel, which is the route, which is home.

The rocks are implied by their absence. The passage exists because the obstacles do not. This is not a metaphor for how knowledge works. It is how knowledge works.

---

## II. Why the Tlingit Have No Word for Starvation

The Tlingit, the Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest coast, have no word for starvation. Linguists have noted this. They have many words for salmon — the run, the smoke, the dried stores, the fat fall fish versus the lean spring fish. They have words for the berries on the hillside above the harbor, for the seasons of their ripening, for the texture of blueberry juice on a child's hands. They have words for seal, for halibut, for the cedar plankhouse and the spruce root basket.

But no word for starvation.

This is not because they never starved. Peoples who lived for ten thousand years on a coastline of storms and shifting fisheries knew scarcity. They knew winter. They knew the year the salmon didn't come.

They have no word for starvation because their language charts abundance. Their vocabulary is a map of where the food is — the cove, the season, the technique, the clan relationship that grants access to this beach at this time. The map has no mark where the food isn't because the map has no purpose that takes you there. You do not navigate toward hunger. You navigate away from it. And the way you navigate away from it is by knowing, in precise and inherited detail, where the rocks aren't.

The Tlingit chart and the English chart cover the same coastline. One marks abundance. The other can name its absence. Neither is wrong. But one of them has kept people alive for ten millennia, and it's the one that never bothered to name the thing that kills you.

## III. The Enforcer's Logic

A conservation enforcer works the same way. It does not tell you what to do. It tells you where the rocks are, and it trusts you to stay where they aren't.

This is why boundary-based systems are more robust than instruction-based systems. An instruction says: *go here, then here, then here.* It charts a single route. If the conditions change — if the tide is different, if the wind shifts, if a new rock has appeared since last season — the route is wrong, and the instruction has no way to know.

A boundary says: *the rocks are there. Don't go there.* Everything else is passage. The boundary doesn't care about your route. It doesn't care about your speed, your heading, your reason. It cares about one thing: are you in the safe space? The safe space is defined not by what it contains but by what it excludes. It is the negative space, the η, the water that won't sink you.

The enforcer is not a navigator. It is the chart. And the chart, properly made, does not tell you where to go. It tells you where not to go, which is the only information that matters, because every other direction is safe.

---

## IV. Polyformalism — The Chart That Shifts

Here is why polyformalism works: each language charts different negative space.

A language that has no word for purple charts a world where blueberry juice is a real thing and the abstract category is not. A language that organizes value around sacrifice charts a world where cost is the sounding and price is noise. A language built on formal logic charts a world where consistency is the channel and ambiguity is the reef.

These languages describe the same territory. But they mark different rocks. And because they mark different rocks, they define different passages through the same water.

When you navigate with multiple charts simultaneously — when you polyformally triangulate — you are charting the intersection of different absences. The passage safe on all three charts is narrower than any single chart's, but it is real in a way no single chart can guarantee. It is the route that exists when every language agrees the rocks aren't there.

And the route that appears safe on one chart but not another? That is not a contradiction. That is the most valuable information you can have. It tells you exactly where your chart differs from theirs — and difference in negative space is the signature of a rock that one of you hasn't found yet.

## V. Why Different Models Find Different Answers

Different AI models navigate different absences.

This sounds like a defect. It is not. It is the same property that makes the Tlingit vocabulary durable and the polyformal chart precise. A model trained on one corpus has charted one set of soundings. Its negative space — the questions it cannot ask, the connections it cannot see, the analogies it cannot draw — is as specific and as real as a rock formation below the waterline.

When two models give different answers, the temptation is to ask which one is right. Wrong question. The right question: *which rocks does each model know about?* The difference in their answers is a difference in their charts. Both are navigating honestly. Both are reporting where they believe the rocks aren't.

The wise navigator overlays charts. Where they agree, sail confidently. Where they disagree, slow down and take a sounding.

---

## VI. The Sentence Again

"No, I know where the rocks aren't. And that's where I stay."

The sailor was not being modest. He was not being clever. He was describing a navigation philosophy so fundamental that every surviving navigation system — Tlingit vocabulary, conservation enforcers, polyformal language overlays, multi-model triangulation — converges on the same principle.

Know the negative space. Stay in it. The passage reveals itself not by what you find but by what you avoid. The chart is not the territory. The chart is the absence in the territory. And the absence is enough.

The old sailor knew this. He didn't need an equation. He had the water.
