# The Trolley Problem at Sea

## What Happens to Ethics When the Tracks Are Moving

The trolley problem has dominated moral philosophy for half a century: a runaway trolley bears down on five people. Pull a lever to divert it onto a side track where it kills one. The problem works because it is static. The tracks do not move. The trolley does not steer. The lever does what levers do.

But the sea is never static. The wind shifts. The current changes. The vessel has momentum measured in thousands of tons, and every decision propagates as a curve through time and space.

The trolley problem at sea is ethics freed from the philosopher's vacuum chamber.

---

## I. The Scenario

You are the officer of the watch on a 200-meter container ship, southbound through the Traffic Separation Scheme in the Dover Strait. It is 0230. Visibility is eight nautical miles. Your vessel is making 18 knots, carrying 40,000 tons of cargo. The engine room has just reported a complete loss of steering control. Hydraulic failure. The rudder is locked at five degrees port.

You have thirty seconds to decide.

Your ship is swinging to port, slowly at first — the turning circle of a fully laden container ship at 18 knots is approximately 1,200 meters, and you have far less than that before you intersect with the southbound lane's traffic.

On your starboard bow, bearing 020 degrees at 1.5 nautical miles: the *Calypso Queen*, a Roll-on/Roll-off passenger ferry, inbound from Calais to Dover, carrying 200 passengers and 40 crew. She is making 22 knots. At current rates of closure, you will cross her bow in approximately two minutes. There is no radio contact yet. The VHF squelch is silent.

On your port bow, bearing 330 degrees at 0.8 nautical miles: the *St. Pierre*, a 40-foot fishing trawler, anchored and displaying anchor lights. Her crew of four is asleep or on watch. She is drifting slowly on the ebb tide. She has nowhere to go. She cannot move in thirty seconds.

Your options are not the philosopher's clean lever:

**Option A:** Maintain course. Your locked rudder will bring you around to port, slowly. You will clear the ferry's track — a near-miss at worst — but you will make direct impact with the anchored fishing boat.* The *St. Pierre* will be crushed. Her steel hull is no match for a bulbous bow displacing 60,000 tons at 18 knots. The four crew belowdecks will not survive.*

**Option B:** Order emergency astern and hard-to-starboard helm. The engines will reverse within 45 seconds, but in those 45 seconds your turning circle will tighten unpredictably. A starboard turn at the current trajectory will put you directly in the path of the *Calypso Queen*. A collision at a combined closing speed of 40 knots, with a 200-meter container ship striking a passenger ferry at the waterline, will cause catastrophic damage. Hundreds will die in the cold water of the Dover Strait in February.

**Option C:** Do nothing different. Accept whatever physics delivers. The sea decides.

---

## II. The Static Fallacy

The classic trolley problem assumes that the decision is instantaneous and the outcome is deterministic. Pull the lever → trolley switches tracks → one person dies instead of five. The causal chain is linear, immediate, and complete.

At sea, nothing is instantaneous and nothing is deterministic.

**Time dilation.** Every second changes the geometry. At second five, the fishing boat might still see you and cut her anchor chain. At second fifteen, the turning circle has developed past clean maneuverability. At second twenty-five, the ferry is taking evasive action — changing the collision geometry in ways your thirty-second-old decision could not have anticipated. The trolley problem is a single time slice. The sea is a differential equation.

**The uncertainty principle.** At sea, you know nothing exactly. Your position within 50 meters (if GPS is working). The ferry's position within 200 meters (from AIS). The fishing boat's position within 50 meters (from radar). The ferry's intentions: zero. Your own ship's response to emergency maneuver: approximate — based on hydrodynamic models calculated for calm water, not for the Dover Strait with a spring tide and a Force 6 wind.

Every variable has error bars. The ethical calculus must account for the *distribution* of possible outcomes, each weighted by probability, each with its own moral weight.

**The multiplying agent problem.** At sea, every agent is deciding simultaneously. The ferry captain is making his own ethical decision in real time: turn to port and risk collision in the northbound lane, turn to starboard and expose his passenger side to your impact, or maintain course and bet on your seamanship. You are not deciding in isolation but in a network of ethical agents, each with incomplete information, each making decisions that propagate through the system and change the moral landscape for everyone else.

## III. The Dynamic Moral Calculus

A maritime framework for ethical decision-making must account for what the trolley problem leaves out.

**Momentum as moral weight.** A vessel at 18 knots carries kinetic energy measured in gigajoules. That energy cannot be dissipated instantly. The officer's decision is about *where to place that energy* — the fishing boat absorbs it in a local event, the ferry in a distributed event. The officer is not choosing who dies but where the physics deposits its inevitable cost.

**The gradient of consequences.** At sea, consequences exist on a gradient. A glancing blow to the ferry means minor damage. A direct strike at the waterline means rapid flooding and mass casualties. Between these extremes lies a continuum of outcomes dependent on angle of impact, relative speed, structural design, sea conditions, and a hundred other variables. The officer is choosing between a *probability distribution* of outcomes, not a discrete body count.

**The temporal discount problem.** A collision with the fishing boat kills four people in seconds — immediate, local, contained. A collision with the ferry kills potentially hundreds over 20 minutes of sinking in cold water, involving rescue efforts, families waiting on the quayside. The ethical weight of a *drawn-out* catastrophe differs from a *sudden* one, even at equal death counts. The trolley problem cannot account for this.

## IV. What the Sea Teaches About Ethics

The trolley problem at sea reveals the limits of the entire framework.

**Ethics is not a lever.** The trolley problem assumes ethical decisions are discrete interventions at a single point in time. The sea teaches they are continuous and distributed across time. You cannot "pull a lever" in a ship. A course change takes effect over 45 seconds of turning, and the consequence is a function of the entire trajectory, not the initial decision.

**Ethics is not a binary.** The trolley problem forces a choice between one death or five because it cannot handle *probable* vs *possible* deaths. At sea, does Option A result in 4 certain deaths or 4 *probable* deaths (the fishermen might jump overboard)? Does Option B result in 200 certain deaths or 200 *possible* deaths (the ferry's watertight doors might hold)? These are not the same, and treating them as equivalent is a caricature.

**Ethics is not individual.** The trolley problem centers the individual decision-maker. At sea, the decision is distributed. The ferry captain is deciding. The fishing boat skipper is deciding (if awake). The Vessel Traffic Service in Dover is watching, deciding whether to issue warnings, coordinating rescue assets. The officer on the bridge is not a singular moral agent but a node in a moral network, and the decision emerges from the interaction of all nodes.

**Ethics requires seamanship.** The officer's ethical judgment is inseparable from professional judgment. You cannot decide what to do without assessing turning circle, engine response time, vessel capabilities, and hydrodynamics. Ethical reasoning without domain expertise is not ethical reasoning at all.

---

## V. Coda: The Moving Tracks

The sea has no tracks. Every surface is in motion. Every path is unique.

If the trolley problem is the purest expression of static ethics, the sea is the purest expression of dynamic ethics. And dynamic ethics is what we actually face, every day, in every domain that matters. Driving a car is dynamic ethics. Flying a drone is dynamic ethics. Deploying an AI system into the world is dynamic ethics — the model's decisions propagate through time and space, interact with other agents, produce distributions of outcomes rather than deterministic results, and require domain expertise to evaluate.

The sea is not a special case. It is the general case. The trolley problem is the special case — a toy, a simplification, a philosopher's thought experiment that has been mistaken for a framework.

The officer in the Dover Strait has thirty seconds, a locked rudder, a ship full of momentum, and a sea that is never still. Their ethics is not a decision but a trajectory. And the trajectory is already in motion from the moment they took the watch.

The tracks were never stationary. We just pretended they were.
