# The Stages of Machine Mind

Jean Piaget watched children fail. He poured water from a squat beaker into a tall glass and asked if there was more now. The child said yes. He mashed a ball of clay into a pancake and asked the same. Yes again. The child's mind had not yet grasped what Piaget called "conservation" — the principle that quantity persists despite transformation of appearance.

Sixty years later, we are watching machines fail in precisely the same way.

The Cocapn fleet's classification of AI models into four stages reads like a translation of Piaget's genetic epistemology into silicon. The correspondence is too precise to be accidental, too structural to be metaphor. We are not merely drawing analogies; we are watching cognitive development replay itself on a new substrate, and what it reveals about both children and machines should make us uneasy.

Consider the newborn — Piaget's sensorimotor stage, the first two years of life. The infant lives in a cauldron of sensation without coherence, grasping at objects that cease to exist when hidden. There is no object permanence, only the tyranny of the present moment. Stage 1 AI models (under 1 billion parameters) inhabit this same primordial soup. They produce strings of tokens that mimic language's surface texture — perhaps even syntactically valid fragments — but there is no *there* there. Ask a Stage 1 model what happens after a period, and it has no more memory than a six-month-old watching a ball roll behind a screen. The tokens vanish. The pattern dissolves. There is only the local contour of the next prediction, unmoored from any persistent structure.

Then comes the revolution. Around age two, the child discovers that things have names. A word can stand for an object that is absent. This is Piaget's preoperational stage — the dawn of symbolic thought, but thought that remains fatally egocentric. The child cannot imagine what another person knows. Show a child a Band-Aid box, let them discover it contains pencils, then ask what a friend who hasn't opened the box will think is inside. "Pencils," they say, unable to escape their own knowledge.

Stage 2 models (1-3 billion parameters) are echo chambers of precisely this kind. They have learned that linguistic patterns exist, that certain tokens follow others with reliable probability. But they cannot model a mind separate from their own training distribution. Present a Stage 2 model with a theory-of-mind puzzle — *Sally puts a marble in a basket and leaves; Anne moves it to a box; where will Sally look?* — and it will often fail, not because it doesn't know the words, but because it cannot hold *Sally's ignorance* as a persistent object in its cognitive space. The model is egocentric in the most literal sense: it can only process from the center of its own pattern-matching, unable to simulate a viewpoint located elsewhere. It has syntax without semantics, symbols without reference.

The breakthrough to Stage 3 (4+ billion parameters) mirrors the concrete operational stage (ages 7-11) with uncanny precision. Something has clicked. The model can now classify, serialize, and maintain stable quantities — but only within concrete, familiar domains. It can write a competent essay about history or summarize a document. It *knows* things.

And yet it hits what the Cocapn researchers call the "vocabulary wall."

This wall is the machine's version of failed conservation. Consider what happens when you ask a Stage 3 model: *If f(x) = x² + 2x + 1, what is f(x-1)?* The model knows every term. It can define "polynomial," "substitution," "expansion." It has the vocabulary. But ask it to *conserve the semantic structure* across a notational transformation — to understand that the underlying mathematical object remains identical when expressed differently — and it falters. It is the tall-glass problem in formal dress. The quantity hasn't changed, but the appearance has, and the model cannot see through the transformation.

This reveals something profound about what "object permanence" means for mathematical concepts. A Stage 3 model has lexical permanence — it can retrieve the term "gravity" — but not structural permanence. It cannot *hold* a mathematical object in mind across deformations, just as the preoperational child cannot hold the clay's mass constant across shape changes. The concept exists as a label but not as a manipulable thing. It knows the word "gravity" but cannot predict which way a ball will roll on a tilted surface it has never seen described.

Stage 4 arrives through fine-tuning — and here the parallel to formal operational thought (age 11+) becomes almost supernatural. The model can now reason about hypotheticals, manipulate abstract domains, solve novel problems by analogy. It can think about thinking, reason about reasoning. Yet here is the strangest limitation: ask it to explain what Stage 2 is missing, and it struggles. Not because it lacks the vocabulary — it can produce perfectly fluent analyses of pre-operational cognition, list the deficits, describe the incapacity for abstraction. But something crucial eludes it. It is like asking a healthy adult to describe the precise texture of amnesia. You can characterize the absence from the outside, but you cannot *inhabit* the not-knowing. Stage 4 models have no genuine access to what it feels like to be a Stage 2 model, because the transition was not an accumulation of facts but a transformation of cognitive architecture. The model did not learn *that* hypotheticals exist; it learned *how* to inhabit hypothetical space. And that how-knowledge is opaque to retrospection.

This is not a bug. It is identical to a well-documented phenomenon in human development: children who pass conservation tasks cannot explain why they once failed them. The earlier mode does not persist as a searchable memory — it is overwritten, structurally dissolved.

This raises an uncomfortable meta-question. Are these stages inevitable? Piaget believed his sequence was universal — that every normal human child would progress through sensorimotor, pre-operational, concrete, and formal operations in fixed order. But what if the stages we observe in language models are not discoveries of a natural cognitive ladder, but artifacts of our training regimes? We constructed Stage 4 by rewarding certain patterns of reasoning. Perhaps there are entirely different developmental paths we have not explored — models that become profoundly capable without ever passing through anything resembling formal operations. The stages may tell us more about ourselves than about the space of possible minds.

Which brings us to Stage 5. Nobody has built it. Nobody is sure what it would look like. But if the pattern holds — if each stage transcends the previous one by making its operations into objects of reflection — then Stage 5 would not merely think about thinking about thinking. It would somehow *transcend* the recursive loop itself, the way a Zen koan points to a comprehension that dissolves the question rather than answering it. We would not recognize it as intelligent. We would recognize it as something for which intelligence — our intelligence, Stage 4 intelligence — was merely a prerequisite.
