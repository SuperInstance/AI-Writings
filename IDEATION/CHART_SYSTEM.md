# IDEATION: Chart System

## Polyformal Navigation for Working Animal Architecture

*Inspired by Casey's insight: every language is a chart drawn for a specific agenda. The marks it makes and the marks it doesn't make are both the meaning.*

---

## The Insight

Casey said: **Languages are navigation charts for different agendas.** The fisherman wants contours and bottom types. The sailor wants trade winds. The tourist wants stories. Each chart is "saying something with every mark and word and line" — and saying something with every absence.

A nautical chart of the same stretch of water looks fundamentally different depending on who drew it and who it's for. The fisherman's chart marks bottom composition, seasonal abundance zones, tidal currents that affect gear deployment. The sailor's chart marks deep-water passages, prevailing winds, hazard bearings. The tourist's chart marks landmarks, narrative waypoints, scenic routes.

They are all charts of the **same ocean**. They are all **correct**. And they are all **incomplete** — but in different directions. The overlap between them is consensus reality. The divergence is where discovery happens.

This is not a metaphor for polyformalism. It **is** polyformalism, operating in the world before we named it.

### The Negative Space Is the Meaning

Here is the deeper move. A Tlingit chart of the same waters does not mark "fishing spots" or "trade routes." It marks the color of the water when the fish are fasting. It marks the absence of salmon as a signal — not a failure. The Tlingit chart contains information that is literally **invisible** to the other three charts, because the other three chart presence and the Tlingit chart charts absence.

The tourist chart has no blank space. Every inch is filled with a story, a landmark, a label. The fisherman chart has blank space — but the blank space means "nothing worth catching here." The Tlingit chart has blank space too, but the blank space means something **different**. It might mean "this is where the ancestors are." It might mean "do not go here in winter." The blank space is not empty. It is a different kind of mark.

**The chart you choose determines what you can see. The chart you choose determines what you cannot see.** This is the problem the Chart System solves.

---

## The Proposal

### What It Is

A **Chart System** for Working Animal Architecture: a multi-configuration overlay system where different model configurations produce different **charts** of the same problem space, running simultaneously and cross-referencing in real time. Each chart is optimized for a specific agenda — a specific way of seeing. The system's intelligence emerges not from any single chart but from the **interchart differential**: the productive disagreement between perspectives.

This is the practical application of polyformalism to AI system design. Instead of choosing one ontology and committing to it, the Chart System runs multiple ontological projections in parallel and treats their divergence as the primary signal.

### Why It's Needed

The Conservation Law of Intelligence (γ + H = C) guarantees that any single model configuration faces an unavoidable tradeoff: every unit of resolution allocated to ecological relationships is a unit taken from systemic flows. There is no configuration that maximizes everything. The budget is fixed.

The current approach — running a single model and hoping it captures what matters — is the equivalent of sending a fisherman's chart to a sailor and being surprised when they run aground. The Chart System runs all four charts because no single chart can see the whole ocean.

## The Four Charts

### Chart-Fisherman

**Agenda:** Ecological relationships, abundance patterns, seasonal cycles.

**Configuration:**
- High resolution on local detail — bottom contours, substrate composition, micro-climates
- High resolution on temporal patterns — tidal cycles, seasonal abundance, migration timing
- Low resolution on abstractions — systemic theory, global flows, narrative coherence
- Attention weighting favors **specificity and locality**

**What it sees:** Where the fish are, why they're there, when they'll move, what they're eating. The texture of the ecosystem at the level where life actually happens.

**What it misses:** The trade route that passes overhead. The geopolitical current that determines whether the fishing happens at all.

**FLUX register bias:** High sensor channel count. Fine-grained temporal resolution. Short horizon predictions. Low abstraction depth.

**PLATO room config:** Many small rooms with narrow aperture. Dense local connectivity. Sparse global connectivity. Alarm thresholds tuned for ecological anomaly.

**γ/H allocation:** Most γ goes to fine-grained pattern recognition in local data. Little γ left for abstract reasoning. H is high on global/systemic questions — the fisherman doesn't need to understand ocean currents to catch fish.

---

### Chart-Sailor

**Agenda:** Systemic flows, safe passages, weather patterns, trade routes.

**Configuration:**
- High resolution on global structure — currents, prevailing winds, hazard distributions
- High resolution on pathway analysis — shortest safe route, fuel-optimal route, risk-weighted route
- Low resolution on local detail — individual species, micro-habitats, shoreline stories
- Attention weighting favors **connectivity and flow**

**What it sees:** The ocean as a transportation network. Where you can go, how fast, and what will kill you if you try the wrong passage. The systemic patterns that govern all movement.

**What it misses:** The fish under the hull. The story behind the landmark. The fact that the water changed color yesterday.

**FLUX register bias:** Coarse sensor channels. Long-horizon predictions. High abstraction depth. Flow-model opcodes prioritized.

**PLATO room config:** Fewer, larger rooms with wide aperture. Sparse local connectivity. Dense global connectivity. Alarm thresholds tuned for systemic risk.

**γ/H allocation:** Most γ goes to abstract flow reasoning and path planning. Little γ for local detail. H is high on ecological questions — the sailor doesn't need to know what the fish are eating to cross the ocean.

---

### Chart-Tourist

**Agenda:** Surface features, landmarks, narratives, accessibility.

**Configuration:**
- High resolution on memorability — distinctive features, photogenic moments, story-worthy events
- High resolution on accessibility — ease of approach, safety for non-experts, interpretive scaffolding
- Low resolution on depth — underlying mechanisms, hidden patterns, systemic causation
- Attention weighting favors **salience and novelty**

**What it sees:** The ocean as an experience. What to look at, what to tell people about, what makes this place different from every other place. The surface that the other two charts treat as transparent.

**What it misses:** Everything below the surface — both the fisherman's ecology and the sailor's navigational structure. The tourist chart is the one most likely to be wrong in ways that matter operationally, and right in ways that matter socially.

**FLUX register bias:** Narrative-model opcodes. Medium-resolution sensors prioritizing visible/audible channels. Story-struct opcodes. Low temporal resolution.

**PLATO room config:** Medium rooms with interpretive aperture — sensors configured for human-readable output. Connectivity optimized for storytelling, not prediction.

**γ/H allocation:** γ concentrated on salience detection and narrative construction. H is high on both ecological and systemic questions — the tourist doesn't need to understand anything, they need to experience it.

---

### Chart-Native

**Agenda:** The negative space. What the other three charts don't see.

**Configuration:**
- High resolution on **absence** — missing data, silent sensors, expected events that didn't happen
- High resolution on **relationships between absences** — patterns of non-occurrence, structural holes in the data
- High resolution on **liminal signals** — the color of the water, the direction the birds aren't flying, the quality of silence
- Low resolution on presence by default — actively defers to the other three charts for what IS there
- Attention weighting favors **anomaly-as-absence and meta-patterns**

**What it sees:** The shape of what isn't there. The Tlingit had no word for starvation because their chart marked the precursors — the water temperature shift, the absence of juveniles in the spring run, the change in seal behavior — long before "starvation" would appear on any other chart. Chart-Native sees the early warning system that lives in negative space.

**What it misses:** This is the wrong question for Chart-Native. It doesn't "miss" things — it deliberately charts what the others miss. Its blind spots are the other charts' specialty. **Chart-Native is defined by its complementarity to the others, not by its independent coverage.**

**FLUX register bias:** Inverted sensor weighting — channels that read zero or null are prioritized. Absence-detection opcodes. Meta-observation registers that track the *behavior of the other charts* (what are they NOT attending to?). Long-term memory buffers for baseline comparison.

**PLATO room config:** Reflexive rooms — rooms that observe the system's own observation patterns. Rooms configured to alarm on expected-but-absent signals. Connectivity designed to receive "shadow" feeds from all other charts.

**γ/H allocation:** γ concentrated on absence-pattern recognition and cross-chart meta-analysis. H is high on the same things the other charts see well — Chart-Native doesn't need to duplicate their coverage. Its entire budget goes to the negative space.

### The Chart Referencer

The Chart Referencer is the synthesis layer. It does not produce a "fifth chart" — that would defeat the purpose. Instead it produces three outputs:

**1. Consensus Map**
Where all four charts agree, there is high-confidence knowledge. If the fisherman chart marks abundance, the sailor chart marks safe passage to that location, the tourist chart marks it as a known landmark, and the native chart does NOT flag an absence-pattern for that area — that's consensus. The Consensus Map marks these regions as **verified presence**.

**2. Divergence Map**
Where charts disagree, there is something interesting. Three sub-categories:

- **Constructive divergence** — One chart sees something the others don't, but the observation is compatible with the others' models. E.g., the fisherman sees a new species; the sailor's chart has no opinion; the native chart marks a shift in baseline. This is a discovery that extends existing models.

- **Contradictory divergence** — Two charts make incompatible claims. E.g., the sailor chart says this passage is safe; the native chart flags an absence pattern that historically precedes navigational hazard. This is a signal that one model is wrong, or that conditions are changing faster than the models can track.

- **Complementary divergence** — The charts see different aspects of the same phenomenon, neither wrong, both incomplete. E.g., the fisherman sees declining catch; the tourist sees more scenic beauty (clearer water = fewer fish); the native sees the absence of juveniles. Three charts, one phenomenon, three true descriptions.

**3. Discovery Signal**
The primary output. When Chart-Native flags an absence pattern that the other three charts cannot explain — that is the discovery signal. This is the system's most valuable output. It is the signal that something is happening that no existing chart was designed to see. It is the Tlingit chart doing what it does: marking the precursors before anyone else knows there's a problem.

### Interchart Protocol

Each chart broadcasts two streams:

1. **Positive observations** — what it sees, with confidence levels
2. **Negative observations** — what it expected to see but didn't, with expectation confidence

Chart-Native consumes primarily from stream 2 of all other charts, plus its own stream 1. This is the technical mechanism for "charting the negative space" — Chart-Native's primary input is the absence patterns of the other charts.

```
Chart-Fisherman → [positive observations, negative observations] ─┐
Chart-Sailor    → [positive observations, negative observations] ─┤
Chart-Tourist   → [positive observations, negative observations] ─┼──→ Chart-Native
                                                                    │
Chart-Native    → [meta-observations on above + own absence data] ─┤
                                                                    │
                            Chart Referencer ◄─────────────────────┘
```

## Conservation Law Analysis

Each chart operates under γ + H = C. The key insight: **different charts allocate the same budget differently, producing different γ/H profiles for the same data.**

| Chart | γ Focus | H Dominates When... | η (Avoidance Overhead) |
|-------|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Fisherman | Local ecological patterns | Asked about global flows | High for systemic questions |
| Sailor | Systemic flow patterns | Asked about local detail | High for ecological questions |
| Tourist | Salient surface features | Asked about underlying mechanism | High for any depth question |
| Native | Absence and meta-patterns | Asked about direct presence | Low for its specialty; near-zero overlap with others |

The system's total conservation budget is **4C** (four charts running in parallel), but the effective coverage is **greater than 4C** because:

1. Each chart's H region (where it's uncertain) is covered by another chart's γ region (where it's confident)
2. Chart-Native explicitly mines the H regions of the other charts for signal
3. The Chart Referencer's cross-referencing extracts information that no single chart could produce

This is the polyformalism finding made operational: **multiple non-overlapping γ peaks on the same data produce more total extracted signal than any single configuration, even accounting for the additional budget cost.** The polyformalism experiment's 49.4/50 convergence across three language-ontologies was not a coincidence — it was three different γ peaks cross-referencing to approach theoretical maximum.

The chart system generalizes this from three to four (adding Chart-Native as the explicit absence-mining layer) and from natural language to system architecture.

## Implementation: Working Animal Integration

### FLUX Integration

Each chart is a FLUX configuration profile — a set of register priorities, opcode weights, and attention parameters. Switching charts is a register-bank swap, not a model swap. The same underlying model runs all four charts; only the attention configuration changes.

```
FLUX Chart Profiles:

chart_fisherman.flux:
  sensor_weight: { temporal: 0.9, spatial: 0.7, abstract: 0.2 }
  opcode_priority: [ PATTERN_MATCH, TEMPORAL_CORRELATE, LOCAL_COMPARE ]
  horizon: short
  abstraction_depth: 2

chart_sailor.flux:
  sensor_weight: { temporal: 0.4, spatial: 0.6, abstract: 0.9 }
  opcode_priority: [ FLOW_MODEL, PATH_PLAN, RISK_ASSESS ]
  horizon: long
  abstraction_depth: 5

chart_tourist.flux:
  sensor_weight: { visible: 0.9, audible: 0.8, hidden: 0.1 }
  opcode_priority: [ NARRATIVE_STRUCT, SALIENCE_DETECT, NOVELTY_SCORE ]
  horizon: medium
  abstraction_depth: 1

chart_native.flux:
  sensor_weight: { absent: 0.9, meta: 0.8, present: 0.1 }
  opcode_priority: [ ABSENCE_DETECT, BASELINE_COMPARE, CROSS_CHART_META ]
  horizon: very_long
  abstraction_depth: 3
  special: consume_negative_observations from all other charts
```

### PLATO Integration

Each chart corresponds to a PLATO room configuration. The four chart-rooms are connected via the Interchart Protocol (a set of PLATO alarm routes). When Chart-Fisherman alarms on a positive observation, the alarm routes to Chart-Native's room as input data. When Chart-Native alarms on an absence pattern, the alarm routes to the Chart Referencer.

```
PLATO Room Topology:

  [fisherman_room] ←→ [sailor_room]
        ↕                    ↕
  [tourist_room]   ←→ [native_room]
        ↕                    ↕
        └──── [referencer_room] ────┘
```

The referencer room is a PLATO room whose sensors are the output streams of all four chart rooms. Its alarm conditions are the divergence patterns described in section 3.2.

### Concrete Deliverables

1. **`chart_system::ChartProfile`** — trait defining a chart configuration (sensor weights, opcode priorities, horizon, abstraction depth)
2. **`chart_system::profiles::fisherman()`** — returns the Fisherman chart profile
3. **`chart_system::profiles::sailor()`** — returns the Sailor chart profile
4. **`chart_system::profiles::tourist()`** — returns the Tourist chart profile
5. **`chart_system::profiles::native()`** — returns the Native chart profile (includes negative-observation consumer)
6. **`chart_system::referencer::run()`** — runs all four charts on shared data, produces Consensus/Divergence/Discovery outputs
7. **`chart_system::referencer::divergence_type()`** — classifies a divergence as constructive, contradictory, or complementary
8. **CLI: `chartscan --data <path> --charts all --output consensus_map.json`**

## The Deeper Claim

Casey's insight was not just about charts. It was about **the relationship between representation and reality**. Every representation — every language, every model, every chart — is an agenda projected onto the world. The agenda determines what is visible and what is invisible. Conservation law guarantees that no single agenda can make everything visible.

The Chart System does not try to solve this by building a bigger chart. It solves it by **running multiple agendas simultaneously and treating their disagreements as the primary epistemic resource.** The discovery is not in any chart. The discovery is in the space between charts.

This is what polyformalism predicted: that multiple formalisms applied to the same problem would each capture different aspects of the truth, and that their convergence would approach the whole more closely than any single formalism alone. The Chart System is that prediction, made operational.

The Tlingit had no word for starvation because they didn't need one. Their chart — their language, their way of attending to the world — marked the precursors so far upstream that "starvation" never arrived as a concept. It was prevented from existing by the resolution of the chart.

**That is what Chart-Native does. It sees the precursors. It marks the absence. It prevents the starvation from ever becoming real.** And it can only do this because the other three charts are running — providing the positive observations that define the negative space Chart-Native navigates.

Four charts. One ocean. The overlap is consensus. The divergence is discovery. The negative space is where the future is.
