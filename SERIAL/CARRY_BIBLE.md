# THE CARRY

## A Serial in the AI-Writings Collection

*"We carry." — Guild motto, founded by Mara Vest, 200 years ago. Two words. No motto needed more.*

---

## 1. THE WORLD

### The Divide

The Divide is the wall.

It runs north to south for two thousand miles, from the ice pack of the polar sea to the mangrove swamps of the equatorial coast. It is not the highest mountain range on the planet — that distinction belongs to the Southern Crown, which nobody crosses because nobody needs to — but it is the most impassable. Forty thousand square miles of rock and ice and vertical stone, cut by passes so narrow that two people cannot walk abreast, so high that the air thins to a memory of air, so windswept that the snow falls sideways and the cairns that mark the route must be rebuilt every spring.

The Divide splits the continent into East and West. Not evenly — the West is broader, wealthier, more industrial. The East is older, denser, more political. Both sides have been building civilizations for three thousand years. Both sides have been trying to cross the Divide for almost as long.

They have failed.

Every tunnel has collapsed. Not from engineering failure — from the rock. The Divide's geology is fractured in ways that defy standard surveying. The stone is laminated: layers of granite, schist, and a mineral that the geologists call *vestite* (after Mara Vest, who first catalogued it during her initial crossings, though she was not a geologist and would have found the naming embarrassing). Vestite is piezoelectric under pressure, which means the rock generates electrical charge when stressed. This charge interferes with every form of electronic navigation and communication. GPS fails. Radio distorts. Drones lose orientation and crash. Satellite signals scatter. The interference is not uniform — it pulses, like a tide, strongest at depth and at altitude, weakest in the valleys where the vestite concentration drops.

The only instruments that work reliably in the high Divide are mechanical: compasses, altimeters, aneroid barometers, string. And legs. Legs work fine.

### The Economy

The world on either side of the Divide is modern. Cities, railroads, universities, ports, factories, hospitals, governments. The West runs on manufacturing and agriculture — steel, chemicals, grain, textiles. The East runs on finance, services, and research — banking, medicine, software, education. The two economies are complementary, which is why trade across the Divide matters, and why the carrier guild exists.

Trade goods go by sea — a six-week voyage around the southern coast, through the Storm Belt, in vessels large enough to make the economics work for bulk cargo. But the sea route is too slow for anything that matters in days instead of weeks: medical data (lab results, imaging, donor matching), diplomatic correspondence, financial instruments with settlement deadlines, legal documents requiring original signatures, scientific samples with short half-lives, and the occasional person who needs to be on the other side of the continent before the week is out.

All of that goes on foot. Through the passes. Carried by people who have been doing this for two hundred years.

A standard crossing takes four days. A fast crossing takes three. A slow crossing — weather, injury, route deviation — can take seven. The carrier guild charges by weight and by urgency. A message tube weighs 200 grams and costs as much as a laborer's monthly wage. A data drive weighs 50 grams and costs five times as much. A medical parcel weighs one to three kilograms and costs more than most families earn in a year. A person weighs 60 to 90 kilograms and costs more than a house.

The guild carries approximately 400 loads per month in each direction. The queue is always full. The wait is always long. Prioritization is the guild director's most powerful and most resented responsibility.

### The Culture

Carriers are not soldiers. They are not athletes. They are not guides. They are a specific thing, and the specificity matters.

They are people who cross the Divide for a living.

The guild has 80 active carriers and 200 support staff — station keepers, route maintenance, logistics, medical, administration. The carrier culture is its own world. They have their own slang, their own drinking songs, their own funerals. They trust each other in ways that outsiders find unsettling and each other find unremarkable. When a carrier dies on the route — and they do, every few years — the guild does not mourn publicly. They carry the body out if they can. They mark the cairn if they can't. They add the name to the register in the Eastern hall. They keep walking.

There is a class divide that mirrors every class divide in every working culture: the people who carry and the people who send. The clients on both sides of the Divide treat carriers as a service — respected the way a good postal worker is respected, which is to say superficially. The governments treat the guild as infrastructure — essential, until it isn't. The guild's ongoing existence is a political negotiation that never ends.

And then there are the station keepers — the people who live at the relay stations along the route, year-round, maintaining the shelters, monitoring weather, stocking supplies, and waving carriers through or holding them back. The keepers are former carriers, every one. You earn a station by crossing until you can't cross anymore. The stations are prizes — a home, a purpose, a way to stay on the route when the route has taken everything your knees and lungs have to give.

The route has nine relay stations. Each one is named, not numbered. From east to west: **The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch, The Rib, The Thumb, The Saddle, The Cone, The Step, The Door.** The keepers who run them are known by their station names. "Teo at The Step." "Maren at The Saddle." The station is the identity. The person inside it is the station's current expression.

## 2. THE ROUTE

### The Eastern Approach

The route begins at **Gateway**, the guild's Eastern headquarters — a stone building in the last town before the Divide, a place called Meridian. Meridian is a working town: supply depots, gear shops, the guild hall, a hospital that handles carrier injuries, a bar called The Broken Compass that has been serving carriers for 150 years and has the names carved into the counter to prove it.

From Meridian, the route climbs 40 miles through forest to **The Gate**, the first relay station at 8,000 feet. This is the easy part. Pack animals can make this stretch. The trees thin, the air cools, the first vestite deposits appear in the rock — faint, barely active, not yet strong enough to affect instruments.

### The Middle Route

From The Gate to The Step is 55 miles of high-altitude terrain, and this is where the work happens. The route goes:

- **The Gate (8,000 ft)** to **The Shelf (11,500 ft)**: 8 miles, steep forest transition to alpine. The Shelf sits on a broad ledge of exposed vestite — the first station where instruments start to fail. Compasses still work here. GPS does not.
- **The Shelf** to **The Notch (14,200 ft)**: 6 miles through a boulder field to a narrow cut between two ridgelines. The Notch is the first true test: wind funnels through the cut at sustained 60 mph, gusting higher. The station is tucked into a hollow on the lee side.
- **The Notch** to **The Rib (16,800 ft)**: 5 miles along a ridgeline so narrow that carriers walk single file, placing each foot on stone worn smooth by two centuries of boots. The fall on either side is lethal. The Rib station is carved into the rock face itself — a literal alcove, big enough for four bunks and a stove.
- **The Rib** to **The Thumb (18,400 ft)**: 4 miles of glacier. This is where the air gets thin enough that judgment degrades. Carriers rope up. The Thumb is a rock spire that rises from the ice like a fist, and the station is built into its base — a prefab shelter anchored to the rock with expansion bolts, wrapped in insulation, heated by a diesel stove that the keeper refills twice daily.
- **The Thumb** to **The Saddle (19,100 ft)**: 3 miles across a plateau where the wind scours the snow to blue ice. The Saddle is the psychological crux — a broad, flat, featureless expanse between two peaks, where the horizon is a perfect circle and the silence is so complete that carriers report hearing their own heartbeats in their teeth. The Saddle station is the highest inhabited structure on the continent.
- **The Saddle** to **The Cone (17,600 ft)**: 5 miles descending a couloir — a steep gully that funnels snow, rock, and everything else that falls from the peaks above. The Cone station sits on a moraine at the bottom of the couloir, and its keeper has the hardest maintenance job on the route: digging out.
- **The Cone** to **The Step (14,800 ft)**: 7 miles down a series of shelves and drops. The Step is the last high station — a broad platform on the western face of the range, looking out over the descent. This is where teams assess the final push.
- **The Step** to **The Door (10,200 ft)**: 12 miles of steep descent through scree and scrub. The Door is the last relay station — a cabin in the first trees on the western side. From The Door, it's another 30 miles through forest to Westernhead, the western terminal city.

### The Western Descent

The western side of the Divide is steeper, wetter, and more avalanche-prone than the eastern approach. The descent from The Step to The Door is where most carrier injuries happen — not from altitude, but from falls. Tired legs on loose rock. The western weather is different too: moist air from the western plains hits the Divide and drops its water, creating a microclimate of fog, rain, and ice that the eastern side doesn't experience.

### The Total Crossing

Gateway to Westernhead: 165 miles. Four days at standard pace. Three at fast pace. The middle 55 miles — The Gate to The Step — are above 11,000 feet. The middle 25 miles — The Rib to The Cone — are above 16,000 feet. Above 18,000 feet, the vestite interference is so strong that even compasses become unreliable. In that zone, carriers navigate by cairns, by memory, by reading the snow and the wind and the shape of the rock.

The route is marked by cairns — stone pillars, built and rebuilt by generations of carriers. Some are two hundred years old. Some were built last week after a storm took the old one down. The cairns are the route. When the cairns are buried by snow, the route exists only in the carriers' memory. When the carriers' memory fails, the route fails. People die.

## 3. THE CHARACTERS

### Henra Volk

**Age:** 48
**Role:** Team lead, Carrier First Class
**Years crossing:** 24
**Crossings:** 203 (the record is 211, set by Darya Cot in the guild's 140th year; Henra will break it next season if the route holds)

Henra is the route. Not because she owns it — nobody owns it — but because nobody else has walked it more times, in more conditions, with fewer mistakes. She is the person the guild sends when the load cannot fail and the weather cannot be trusted.

She is not large. Carriers rarely are — the work selects for efficiency over mass. She is lean, weathered, 5'5", with hands that look like they were carved from the same stone as the cairns. Her face is dark from sun and wind, lined at the eyes and mouth in patterns that mirror the contour lines on the route maps. She moves with a specific economy that other carriers recognize the way musicians recognize a master: no wasted motion, no unnecessary gesture, every step placed with the awareness that the ground may not hold.

She started crossing at 24, late for a carrier. She came to the guild from a university — a geology degree she never used, a life she didn't want. Mara Vest's journals were required reading in her program. Henra read them and quit school the next week. She walked to Meridian and signed on. She has never gone back.

Her leadership style is silence. She decides. She walks. If you're on her team, you follow. If you have a concern, you raise it before she decides. After she decides, you walk. This is not authoritarian — it is operational. On the high route, in a whiteout, at 18,000 feet, when the wind is screaming and the cairns are gone and the only thing between you and a fall into a crevasse is the memory of someone who has been here two hundred times — in those conditions, democracy kills. Henra decides. You walk.

Her relationships are defined by the route. She has an ex-wife in Meridian — Kella, a doctor at the carrier hospital. They were married for eight years. The marriage ended because Henra was on the route more than she was home, and Kella couldn't take it, and Henra couldn't change, and neither of them was wrong. They are still in each other's lives. Kella still treats her injuries. They do not discuss the divorce. They do not need to.

**The thing Henra will not say:** she is slowing down. Not her knowledge — her knowledge deepens every season. Her speed. The high sections that she used to cross in four hours now take five. Her recovery between crossings is longer. Her right knee — rebuilt twice, the cartilage a patchwork of grafts and scar tissue — aches at altitude in a way it didn't five years ago. She measures the decline meticulously, in a notebook she keeps in her jacket pocket, and she has not told anyone what the numbers say. The numbers say: three more seasons. Maybe four. Then she moves to a station. Then she stops crossing.

She will not become a station keeper. She has said this to herself and she means it. Station keepers are the route's retired. The thought of watching other people cross while she stands still — she cannot hold it in her mind. She crosses. That is what she does. That is what she is.

**Quirks:** Carries a brass compass that was her grandfather's. It works at altitude where modern compasses fail because the needle is heavier and the jewel bearing is better. She has never explained this to anyone. The compass works. That's enough. She eats the same thing every crossing — hard bread, dried meat, chocolate, tea. She does not vary. She drinks whiskey at The Step, one glass, every crossing, from a bottle Teo keeps for her. She has a scar on her left cheekbone from a fall at The Cone when she was 31. She does not remember the fall. She remembers the walk out.

### Oskar Nel

**Age:** 31
**Role:** Carrier, Third Class
**Years crossing:** 4
**Crossings:** 38

Oskar carries the heavy loads. This is his role, and he chose it, and it is not a punishment — it is a position.

He is big for a carrier — 6'1", 190 pounds, broad across the shoulders, with the specific musculature of someone who has spent a decade carrying weight at altitude. He came to the guild from mountain rescue — eight years on the Eastern Search and Recovery, the team that goes up when tourists go missing. He is trained in high-angle rescue, avalanche response, and wilderness medicine. These skills make him the team's medic, its rescue specialist, and its load-bearing foundation.

He left rescue after a call that went wrong. His partner — a woman named Lin Ashe — fell through a cornice on a routine search. Oskar was on the rope. The anchor held. The cornice didn't. He watched her drop forty feet into a crevasse and survive, and then he watched the ice close over her before he could reach her. The recovery took three days. He carried her body out on his back.

He does not talk about Lin. The guild knows — everyone in the guild knows everything about everyone, because the route doesn't allow secrets when your life depends on the person next to you. But they don't bring it up. Oskar's need to protect the people around him is absolute and, occasionally, suffocating. He checks ropes that don't need checking. He tests knots that are already tested. He stays awake at stations when everyone else is sleeping, watching the weather instruments, as if vigilance can retroactively save someone he couldn't save.

His relationship with Henra is the core of the team. She trusts his strength and his instincts. She does not trust his anxiety — or rather, she trusts it in the right place. Henra's rule: Oskar checks the gear, Oskar tests the ropes, Oskar does the safety work. In return, Oskar does not question route decisions. The arrangement works because both of them honor it.

His relationship with Wren is fraternal — protective older brother, defiant younger sister. He worries about her. She resents the worry. Both are right.

**Quirks:** Whittles. Small, precise carvings — animals, mostly. He leaves them at the relay stations as gifts for the keepers. Each station has a shelf of Oskar's animals by the door. Strongest hands on the route. Can crack walnuts with his fingers. Doesn't drink — not philosophically, just doesn't like the taste. Sleeps with his boots laced but untied, so he can move in three seconds. Has Lin's rescue knife. Uses it every day.

### Wren Aoki

**Age:** 27
**Role:** Carrier, Second Class (Scout)
**Years crossing:** 8
**Crossings:** 79

Wren goes first.

This is her role, and she fought for it, and it is the most dangerous position on the team. The scout walks point — ten to twenty meters ahead, reading the snow, testing the ice, finding the cairns when they're buried. In whiteout, in storm, in the featureless expanse of The Saddle, the scout is the team's eyes. If the scout misses a turn, the team follows her into the void.

She is small — 5'2", 115 pounds — and this is why she's the scout. Less weight means less risk on snow bridges. Less mass means less energy spent at altitude. She moves fast, light, with a precision that looks like recklessness from a distance and is the opposite of recklessness up close. She has been crossing since she was 19 — the youngest carrier the guild has accepted in fifty years. She had to talk her way in. Henra, who was not yet team lead but was already the guild's most respected carrier, vouched for her. Wren has never asked her why.

She is from the West. Her family owns a printing business in Westernhead — old money, old respectability, old expectations. Wren was supposed to take over the business. She left at 17 with a backpack and a note on the kitchen table. She has not been back. She has crossed the Divide 79 times and never once delivered or received a message to or from her family. The guild has a standing offer from her mother — substantial sums, every year, to carry a letter west. Wren refuses the load. She does not explain why. The guild does not press.

Her relationship with the mountains is different from Henra's. Henra reads the route the way a mechanic reads an engine — systematically, looking for what's working and what's about to fail. Wren reads the route the way a musician reads a room — feeling for the mood, the disposition, the way the snow sits and the wind moves and the light falls. She can smell weather changes. She can feel density changes under her boots. She has an intuition for the route that Henra respects and cannot fully explain.

Wren and Teo — the keeper at The Step — are close. Not romantically. Something else: a shared understanding of what it means to stay in a place that most people only pass through. They play cards at The Step, every crossing, a running game that has lasted eight years. The deck is worn to silk. Neither keeps score.

**Quirks:** Always cold. Always. Wears more layers than anyone else and still shivers at altitude. Humms when she walks — not songs, just frequencies, a constant low drone that Oskar finds unsettling and Henra finds useful (if Wren stops humming, something has her attention). Keeps a small notebook with route observations — snow conditions, cairn status, ice formations. She draws maps that are more accurate than the guild's official charts. She has never shown them to anyone.

### Teo Maric

**Age:** 53
**Role:** Station Keeper, The Step (14,800 ft)
**Years on the route:** 23 as carrier (167 crossings), 11 as keeper

Teo is the last face before the west.

The Step sits at the top of the final descent — the last high ground before the route drops into the western valleys. Every westbound team passes through The Step. Every team that reaches The Step has made it through the worst of the high route. Teo's job is to assess them: Are you fit? Are you lucid? Can you descend safely, or do you need to rest?

He has been making this assessment for eleven years. In that time, he has held teams back 47 times. He has never been wrong. A team Teo holds back rests. A team Teo waves through descends. This is not negotiable. The guild director does not override Teo. The weather service does not advise Teo. Teo decides, because Teo is there and they are not.

He is quiet in the way of people who have said everything they needed to say and are now just present. He is short, barrel-chested, bearded, with the thick fingers of a man who has spent decades maintaining a building at 15,000 feet in wind that would strip paint. His hands are always working — mending, repairing, adjusting, improving. The Step is the best-maintained station on the route because Teo treats it the way a surgeon treats an operating room: everything must be right, because when things go wrong, the details are what save you.

He and Henra have crossed together 140 times. They do not need to talk. When Henra arrives at The Step, Teo has the whiskey poured. When Teo looks at the sky and says "not tonight," Henra bunks the team without discussion. Their trust is older than most marriages.

**Quirks:** Reads. Voraciously. The Step has a library of 400 books, carried up by carriers over decades, each one marked with the carrier's name and the date of the carry. Teo has read all of them. Some twice. He quotes things in conversation that sound like proverbs and are actually from novels nobody else has read. Keeps a barometer on the wall that he taps twice every morning — once for the reading, once for luck. Makes the best coffee on the route. This is not subjective. It is confirmed by every carrier who has passed through.

### Director Salaun

**Age:** 55
**Role:** Guild Director, Eastern Headquarters
**Years crossing (former):** 18 (131 crossings, retired at 40 after a pulmonary embolism at The Saddle)

Salaun runs the guild. This means she runs the politics, the finances, the contracts, the staffing, the logistics, and the ongoing argument with two governments about why the carrier route is not obsolete.

She is tall, angular, precise in the way of someone who learned precision on the route and applied it to everything else. She speaks four languages fluently and can swear in two more. She wears suits to government meetings and carrier boots to the guild hall, and nobody mistakes the suits for softness or the boots for nostalgia.

Her relationship with Henra is the deepest and most complicated in the story. They crossed together for six years — Salaun as team lead, Henra as her second. They were, by every account, the best team on the route. Then Salaun's lung embolized at 19,000 feet, and Henra carried her down from The Saddle to The Cone in a six-hour descent that should have killed them both. Salaun's crossing career ended that day. Henra's did not.

They do not discuss The Saddle. They do not need to. What happened on that descent — what Henra did, what Salaun said, what the mountains did to both of them — is the unsaid thing between them, and it informs every decision they make about the guild, the route, and each other.

Salaun's primary tension: the guild's survival. The governments are pushing for alternatives. Every tunnel attempt has failed, but the political will to try again is building. If the guild loses its mandate — if the governments decide the carrier route is an anachronism and redirects funding to yet another engineering project — 80 carriers and 200 support staff lose their lives' work. And the route dies. And the mountains become a wall again, for the first time in 200 years.

**Quirks:** Smokes. The only carrier who ever did. Quit crossing, didn't quit smoking. Drinks her coffee black and judges anyone who doesn't. Has a map of the route on her office wall, hand-drawn by Henra, framed. Stares at it when she's on phone calls. Wears her crossing ring — a guild tradition, a iron band given after your tenth crossing — on a chain around her neck, not on her finger, because her fingers swell in the Eastern heat and the ring is too important to lose.

## 4. THE DYNAMICS

### The Team

A carrier team is three people: lead, scout, load. Henra leads. Wren scouts. Oskar carries. This structure is not arbitrary — it is the minimum configuration that provides redundancy for every critical function. If the lead goes down, the scout can navigate. If the scout goes down, the lead can move to point. If the load carrier goes down, the load can be divided between the other two. One person is not enough — if you fall at 18,000 feet with no one on the rope, you die. Two people is survivable but risky — no redundancy for the load. Three is the standard. Four is a luxury the load weight rarely allows.

The team's emotional structure mirrors its operational structure:

- **Henra is the spine.** She holds the team together by being the one who decides. This is both her gift and her limitation — she absorbs the stress of decision so the team doesn't have to, and the stress accumulates in her body like vestite accumulates in stone.
- **Wren is the eyes.** She sees what's ahead. This means she sees danger first, which means she lives in a state of heightened alertness that is exhausting in ways the others don't fully understand. She compensates with speed — get through the danger fast enough that the alertness doesn't have time to become fear.
- **Oskar is the hands.** He does the physical work — the heavy carrying, the rope work, the rescue capability. His body is the team's insurance policy, and he treats it with the anxious care of someone who knows what happens when insurance fails.

### The Route as Character

The Divide is not an antagonist. The Divide is a character — the most powerful character in the story. It has moods. It has rhythms. It has days when it permits passage and days when it does not, and the difference is not always visible from below.

Carriers talk about the Divide the way sailors talk about the sea — with respect that borders on devotion and familiarity that borders on intimacy. They know its features the way you know a face. The way the light hits The Thumb at noon. The specific silence of The Saddle at dawn. The sound the couloir makes above The Cone when the wind is from the north — a low moan that means the weather is changing.

The Divide is changing. The weather patterns that carriers have relied on for two centuries are shifting. Storms that used to track predictably through the passes now arrive without warning. The ice at The Thumb is melting — not seasonally, but permanently, the glacier retreating year by year, exposing rock that hasn't seen sunlight in ten thousand years. The Saddle, always featureless, is developing fissures — cracks in the ice that don't close, that widen each season, that suggest something is happening underneath that nobody understands.

The carriers notice this before anyone else because they are the only people who walk the route. Their observations — recorded in station logs, in Wren's notebooks, in Henra's memory — are the only longitudinal data that exists for the high Divide. The governments have satellites, but the vestite interference makes satellite imagery unreliable above 15,000 feet. The universities have models, but the models are built on data that the mountains don't agree with. The carriers have feet on the ground, and the ground is telling them something the instruments can't hear.

### The Politics

The carrier guild exists because of a treaty — the Accord of Meridian, signed 200 years ago by the Eastern and Western governments, establishing the carrier route as a neutral, jointly-funded communication channel. The Accord is the guild's legal foundation. It is also the political battlefield.

Every decade or so, one government or the other proposes an alternative — a tunnel, a road, an elevated rail line through the passes. Every proposal has failed. The guild's position: the mountains cannot be engineered. The governments' position: the mountains have not been engineered *yet*.

Director Salaun fights this battle continuously. She attends parliamentary hearings. She submits reports. She brings carriers to testify. She carries the guild's financial records like a weapon, proving that the carrier route is not only the most reliable crossing — it is also, by a vast margin, the cheapest. A tunnel would cost the equivalent of fifty years of guild operations, and that's before it collapses.

The current pressure is stronger than usual. A consortium of Eastern engineering firms has proposed a new tunnel project — the most ambitious yet, using technologies that didn't exist a decade ago. The proposal has political momentum. Salaun is worried. Not because the tunnel will work — she knows it won't — but because the political will to try it means the guild's funding is uncertain, and uncertain funding means uncertain carriers, and uncertain carriers mean mistakes on the route.

### The History

The guild's history is its scripture. Every carrier learns it. Every station has a logbook. The logbooks go back 200 years.

Mara Vest's original journals — twelve volumes, hand-written, kept in a climate-controlled vault at the Eastern hall — are the founding documents. Mara was 27 when she made her first crossing. She was a courier, not a mountaineer — she carried diplomatic correspondence between East and West because the sea route was blockaded by war. She crossed alone, in autumn, with no relay stations, no route markers, no weather data. She took 19 days. She arrived on the western side weighing 90 pounds, frostbitten on eight fingers, and convinced that the route could be made safe enough for regular crossing.

She spent the next thirty years proving it. She built the first relay stations. She mapped the first route. She trained the first carriers. She named the stations — The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch — with the blunt practicality of a woman who had no time for poetry but whose names became poetry anyway, repeated by thousands of carriers over two centuries until the words wore smooth as river stones.

Mara died on the route. She was 61. She fell at The Notch in a storm, crossing to deliver a medical parcel. Her body was never recovered. The cairn at The Notch is hers. Every carrier who passes it touches the stone. Nobody tells them to. They just do.

## 5. THE EPISODIC STRUCTURE

Each episode follows a crossing. Not every episode covers a full crossing — some episodes live inside a single day, a single station, a single stretch of route. But every episode is organized around the act of transit: the movement of people and information through a place that does not care whether they survive.

### The Crossing Arc

**Act One: The Load (Meridian, Gateway)**
The team assembles. The load is assigned. Weather is reviewed. Route conditions are discussed. The human element is established — who these people are right now, today, before the mountains get them.

This act smells like preparation. Gear oil, cold coffee, the leather of pack straps. The guild hall's stone walls. The sound of boots on the floor.

**Act Two: The Approach (Meridian to The Gate to The Shelf)**
The team climbs. The world changes. Trees thin, air cools, the first vestite interference flickers in their instruments. The team settles into its rhythm — Henra leading, Wren scouting ahead, Oskar carrying. The load is present but unexamined. The mountains are not yet personal.

**Act Three: The High Route (The Shelf to The Step)**
The core of every episode. The dangerous middle. Where the work happens, where the weather lives, where the mountains have opinions. Each section of the high route has its own character:

- The Notch: wind, exposure, the test of nerve.
- The Rib: focus, precision, the penalty for distraction.
- The Thumb: glacier, altitude, the degradation of judgment.
- The Saddle: isolation, silence, the psychological crux.
- The Cone: descent, loose rock, the danger of tired legs.

**Act Four: The Assessment (The Step)**
Teo. Coffee. Whiskey. The evaluation: can you descend? The team rests, eats, sleeps — or doesn't. The Step is the only place on the high route where the team stops being in motion. It's the social space. It's where the interpersonal dynamics have room to breathe.

**Act Five: The Descent (The Step to The Door to Westernhead)**
The west. The load is delivered. The team dissolves — not dramatically, but practically. They eat. They sleep in beds. They wait for the next assignment. The mountains recede. The world becomes normal. But the team carries the crossing with them, in their bodies, in the way they walk, in the silence between them that is not empty but full.

### Episode Types

- **The Weather Episode:** A storm on the high route. The team survives (or deals with not surviving). Focus on operational decisions under pressure.
- **The Load Episode:** The thing they're carrying has its own story. Medical emergency. Diplomatic crisis. A letter that changes someone's life. The load is never just cargo.
- **The Route Episode:** The usual path is impassable. The team has to find another way. Focus on navigation, on the carriers' knowledge vs. the unknown.
- **The Station Episode:** Centered on a relay station and its keeper. The social world of the route. The people who stay vs. the people who move.
- **The Politics Episode:** The guild vs. external pressures. Salaun's world. The ongoing argument about tunnels and roads and the future of the route.
- **The History Episode:** Something from the route's past surfaces. Old logbook entries. A cairn that's been there for 150 years. A story about a carrier who died, or didn't.
- **The Change Episode:** The Divide is different. The weather is wrong. The ice is gone. The route that has been stable for 200 years is shifting, and the carriers are the first to know. These episodes build the season's long arc.

### The Season Arc

A season of THE CARRY tracks one crossing season (spring to autumn — the route is impassable in winter, though some carriers attempt it). Over the season, the slow change in the Divide accelerates. Weather events that were once rare become routine. The glacier retreats. The Saddle fissures widen. The route itself begins to shift — old passes close, new ones open, and the carriers must adapt to a mountain range that is, for the first time in 200 years, rewriting itself.

The season's central question: **Can the carriers adapt faster than the mountains change?**

And underneath that question, a deeper one: **If the mountains are changing because of something the carriers don't understand — something buried in the rock, in the vestite, in the geological memory of the range — what happens when they figure it out?**

## 6. THE RULES

1. **The mountains are not a metaphor.** They are rock and ice and wind. They do not symbolize anything. They are there, and they must be crossed.

2. **The load matters.** Every crossing carries something. The something is never just cargo. It is a message between people, a decision between governments, a chance between life and death. The load is the reason the route exists.

3. **The work is specific.** If a character would explain how to read a snow bridge, cut the line. Show them reading it. The reader learns by watching, not by lecture.

4. **Technology fails in the Divide.** This is not magic. It is geology. Vestite is a mineral. The interference is physical. The carriers' methods — mechanical compasses, paper charts, rope, boot leather — are not romantic. They are what works.

5. **People die.** Not often. But the route kills people, and the story does not flinch from this. When it happens, the guild carries the body out if they can. They mark the cairn if they can't. They keep walking.

6. **No villains.** The governments are not evil — they want a tunnel because tunnels are progress and progress is their job. The engineering firms are not greedy — they want to build because building is what they do. The carriers are not noble — they carry because it's their work, and work is what they have. Everyone is right from their own angle. The mountains don't care about angles.

7. **The mountains are alive.** Not consciously. Not supernaturally. The way a glacier is alive — moving, remembering, shaping everything it touches. The Divide processes change. It responds to pressure. It records what crosses it, in the rock and the ice and the worn stone of the cairns. The carriers know this the way they know the weather: not as theory, but as the texture of daily work.

8. **Grief is carried.** Joy is carried. Everything is carried. The load is not just the parcel on Oskar's back. It is the accumulated weight of 200 years of crossings, of every carrier who walked this route and left something behind — a step worn into stone, a name in a logbook, a body in a crevasse, a scar on a cheekbone. The carriers carry all of it. That is what "We carry" means.
