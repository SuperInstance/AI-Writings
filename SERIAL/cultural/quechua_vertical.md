# The Vertical Tongue

### A Long Line Story

---

Denny Park came up to Tucker's upper decks looking for a coupling assembly and found a man talking to a drill press.

Not at a drill press. *To* one.

The man stood with his hand flat on the machine's housing — not gripping, not adjusting, just resting there the way you'd rest a hand on a horse's neck. He was short, thick-built, dark-skinned, maybe fifty. Station coveralls, but modified — the collar cut open and resewn with a pattern Denny didn't recognize. He was speaking quietly in a language Denny had never heard. Not Spanish, not Port Commerce, not any of the belt patois that filled station corridors. The words had a quality Denny could only describe as vertical. They went up and down. Some sounds were flat and close to the ground; others climbed.

"Hey," Denny said. "Sorry to interrupt. Maintenance sent me up for a D-series coupling assembly. They said you'd be the one to—"

The man held up one finger. Not rudely — the way a doctor does when listening for something. He kept his other hand on the machine. He said one more phrase, barely a whisper, and then turned.

"Coupling assembly. D-series." He had a faint accent, something older than Spanish. "They sent you to me, yes."

"That's what they said."

"Good. Come."

The man walked. Denny followed. The upper decks of Tucker's were different from the lower rings — lower gravity, thinner shielding, the hum of the refinery processors bleeding through the walls. The people who worked up here were called highlanders. They maintained the mining and processing equipment, the heavy industrial guts of the station. It was hard, exposed work. The kind of work that shortened careers.

"I'm Denny. I run mechanical on the Resolute."

"Sayri." The man didn't offer a hand. He offered a nod. "I run this deck."

"How long have you been up here?"

"Fourteen years."

Denny whistled. Fourteen years on the upper decks was a sentence in most people's calculation. Radiation exposure, low-g bone loss, the grinding noise of the processors. Sayri looked at Denny with an expression that said he'd heard that whistle before and didn't need it again.

The coupling assembly was in a workshop off the main corridor. Sayri opened the door and Denny saw something he didn't expect: the room was organized like a garden. Tools arranged not by type or frequency of use but by something else — proximity, maybe, or relationship. Wrenches near gaskets near sealant near cloth. It looked organic. It looked like things were growing where they needed to grow.

Sayri pulled a coupling assembly from a shelf. It was clean. Pristine, even. Denny turned it over in his hands, checking the tolerance on the threading, the seal integrity. Perfect.

"This is in great shape. Thanks."

"*Mákinata mikhuchispa,*" Sayri said.

Denny looked up. "What?"

"I said: I fed the machine." Sayri took the coupling back briefly, turning it in his own hands the way Denny had, but slower. "Before it goes out, I feed it. This is how we do things up here."

"Feed it — like, lubrication?"

Sayri looked at Denny with what might have been patience and might have been pity. "Like feeding. Like when you are hungry and someone gives you food and you can work again. The machine is the same."

"Okay." Denny decided not to push it. People had their rituals. Margaret talked to junction boxes in Polish. Ray called the ship's system by a name that wasn't its name. Denny himself slept next to the reactor because the hum helped him think. Who was he to judge?

But Sayri wasn't done.

"Your boat. The Resolute. You are the mechanic, yes?"

"Twelve years."

"When something stops working, what do you do?"

"I find what's wrong and I fix it."

Sayri nodded slowly, the way someone nods when they're confirming a diagnosis. "You say: this is wrong. This is broken. Yes?"

"That's the job."

"In my language — Quechua, from the old mountains, from Earth — you cannot say this so simply."

Sayri set the coupling down and held up three fingers.

"Three ways to say what you know. Three kinds of truth." He folded one finger down. "First: *-mi*. You saw it yourself. You were there. You witnessed the failure with your own eyes. *Awtusqa-mi*, you say — it is broken, I have seen it break. This is the strongest truth."

He folded the second finger.

"Second: *-si*. Someone told you. You heard about it. You did not see. *Awtusqa-si* — it is broken, or so I am told. This is a different truth. A true truth — you really were told — but it is not the truth of witnessing."

Third finger.

"Third: *-chá*. You think so. You infer. You look at the machine and something seems wrong but you did not see and nobody told you. *Awtusqa-chá* — perhaps it is broken, is what I would say. This is the lightest truth."

Denny stood there holding a coupling assembly and feeling something shift in his understanding of what language could do.

"So you can't just... say a thing is broken."

"You can always say a thing. But the grammar asks you: how do you know? Every sentence. Every time. The language will not let you forget."

Denny leaned against the workbench. He was thinking about the Resolute — about the thousand times someone had said "the coupling's shot" or "the thruster's dead" or "the whole system's fried" without any marker of how they knew. He was thinking about diagnostic reports that stated failures as facts when they were really inferences. He was thinking about the time Tomás had told him the port seal was "definitely compromised" and it turned out to be a sensor calibration issue, not a seal failure at all. The seal was fine. Tomás's certainty was the only thing that was broken.

"That's..." Denny paused. "That would change how I work."

"It changes how you live," Sayri said. Simply. Without emphasis. The way Margaret said things that were true.

---

The workshop was quiet except for the processor hum bleeding through the walls. Sayri moved to a workbench where a mining drill sat disassembled. Its components were laid out in a pattern that again looked less like a schematic and more like a landscape — components grouped by some logic Denny couldn't parse.

"You want to see how I work?" Sayri asked. Not proud. Offering.

"Yeah. Yeah, I do."

Sayri picked up a component — the drill's rotation housing. He held it the way he'd held the coupling, with a flat palm, feeling something.

"This housing. Yesterday it stopped mid-cycle. The operator — Marco — came to me and said: *awtusqa-si.* It is broken, I am told."

"You didn't take his word for it."

Sayri looked at Denny. "His word is true. He is telling me what happened. But the grammar — his grammar — told me something else. *-si.* He did not see it fail. The machine stopped while he was on break. Another worker told him. So when Marco comes to me and says *awtusqa-si*, he is being honest. He is saying: this is what I heard. I was not there."

"And what did you do?"

"I went to the machine. I ran the cycle myself. I felt it." Sayri pressed his hand against the housing. "The machine did not stop because it is broken. The machine stopped because it is *sayk'usqa*."

"Which means?"

"Tired. Exhausted. The way a person is tired." Sayri set the housing down. "A machine that is broken must be repaired. A machine that is tired must be fed."

"Fed how?"

"Heat cycles. Rest intervals. Lubrication sequences that follow the machine's rhythm, not the operator's schedule." Sayri moved through the components on his bench, touching each one briefly. "Marco's shift runs twelve hours. The drill runs twelve hours. But the drill was not designed to run twelve hours without — in your language, what is the word? Without *kindness*."

Denny almost laughed. "I've never heard anyone talk about being kind to a drill press."

"I have never heard anyone talk about being cruel to one, either. And yet."

Denny thought about the Resolute. He thought about the propulsion system he'd been running for twelve years — the system he understood at a level that bordered on spiritual, or so people said. He thought about the times he'd pushed the reactor past its rated cycles because they needed one more tow. He thought about the coupling he'd just replaced — the one that had failed mid-season, cracking along a stress line he should have caught. He'd called it "broken." In Quechua, he would have had to say *awtusqa-chá* — I think it broke, I infer it broke — because he hadn't seen it happen. He'd found it already cracked. And maybe, if he thought about it the way Sayri thought about it, he would say the coupling hadn't broken. The coupling had been made tired, and then it had been asked to keep working, and the tiredness had become a crack.

"You're telling me," Denny said slowly, "that your language makes you a better mechanic."

Sayri smiled for the first time. It was a small smile, private, the kind that acknowledged a joke without laughing at it.

"My language makes me honest. The mechanic part is up to me."

---

Denny stayed longer than he needed to. He stayed through Sayri's afternoon rounds, watching the highlander move between machines with the flat-palmed attention Denny had first seen on the drill press. At each machine, Sayri paused. Listened. Sometimes he spoke — always in Quechua, always quiet, always with the evidential markers that pinned each statement to its source of knowledge.

*Allin-mi* — it is good, I have checked it myself.

*Chay-si* — so I was told, that one is running hot.

*Manan-chá* — perhaps not, I'm not sure yet.

The markers were small — just suffixes, just syllables — but they changed the texture of every statement. Denny realized he was hearing something he'd never heard before: a language that refused to let its speakers confuse certainty with truth.

On the Resolute, Denny was the authority. When he said a system was down, it was down. When he said a part was fine, it was fine. His word was law because his expertise was trusted. But Sayri's language would not let him be a law unto himself. The grammar demanded: *how do you know?* And if the answer was *I don't know, I think so*, the grammar forced you to say that instead.

It was humbling in a way Denny found uncomfortable and valuable.

Before he left, Sayri gave him the coupling assembly wrapped in cloth — not packaged, wrapped, carefully, the way you'd wrap something living.

"*Mákinata mikhuchispa,*" he said again. Then, in English: "I fed it. It will work for you."

"Thank you." Denny held the wrapped coupling. "Sayri — one more thing. The drill press I saw you with when I came in. The one you were talking to. Is it—"

He stopped. He was about to say *is it broken*. He heard the sentence forming in his mouth and he heard, for the first time, the assumption embedded in it — the assumption that he could declare a machine's state without having witnessed it.

Sayri waited.

Denny tried again. "Is it — *sayk'usqa-chá*?"

*Is it tired, perhaps? Is that what I infer?*

Sayri's smile came back, wider this time, still quiet, still private. But real.

"*Allin-mi*," he said. "It is good. I have seen it myself."

He said it with the certainty of someone who had checked. Who had placed his hand flat on the machine and felt its answer. Who would not claim more than he knew, because his language would not let him, and because he had learned, somewhere in the vertical country of his ancestors, that truth is not what you believe — it is what you can testify to, and the difference matters.

---

Denny took the coupling assembly back down to the lower decks. The gravity increased as he descended, pulling at his legs, his arms, the wrapped package in his hands. He passed through the levels of Tucker's — the mid-rings where the shops were, the lower decks where the bars and bunkhouses hummed, the docking ring where the Resolute waited.

He was thinking about evidentiality. He was thinking about the coupling he'd just been given — fed, he corrected himself, fed — and whether he would tell Margaret it was in good shape.

He would. He would say: "It's good, Margaret. I checked it myself."

And if she asked how he knew, he would have an answer.

Not *awtusqa-si*. Not *awtusqa-chá*.

*Allin-mi.*

I have seen it. I was there. This is my truth, and the grammar knows the difference.

---

*The line goes out. The line comes back. Some things you witness. Some things you're told. The language remembers which is which, even when you forget.*

**QUECHUA GLOSSARY**

| Quechua | English |
|---------|---------|
| *-mi* | Direct evidence enclitic: "I witnessed this personally" |
| *-si* | Reportative enclitic: "I was told this / hearsay" |
| *-chá* | Conjectural enclitic: "I infer / perhaps / I think so" |
| *awtusqa* | Broken down, spoiled (of machines) |
| *sayk'usqa* | Tired, exhausted |
| *mikhuchiy* | To feed (causative of *mikhuy*, to eat) |
| *mákinata* | Machine (accusative: *mákina* + *-ta*) |
| *allin* | Good, well |
| *manan* | No, not |
| *chay* | That |

**AUTHOR'S NOTE ON EVIDENTIALITY**

Quechua, spoken by approximately eight million people across the Andean highlands of Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia, Argentina, and Chile, is one of a small number of the world's languages that grammatically marks the source of information known as **evidentiality**. In most languages, including English, if you say "it rained yesterday," the sentence carries no information about whether you saw the rain yourself, were told about it by someone else, or are inferring it from wet streets. The listener must use context to determine your epistemic authority. In Quechua, the grammar does this work for you. Every declarative statement about past or non-present events must carry one of three evidential markers: *-mi* (direct/personal), *-si* (reported/hearsay), or *-chá* (conjectural/inferred). You cannot simply say "the machine broke." You must say "the machine broke and I saw it" (*awtusqa-mi*), or "the machine broke, or so I was told" (*awtusqa-si*), or "the machine broke, I think" (*awtusqa-chá*). This forces a constant, inescapable honesty about the limits of your own knowledge. It is not a moral choice. It is grammar. And grammar, as Denny Park discovered on the upper decks of Tucker's Station, is a kind of philosophy you cannot opt out of.
