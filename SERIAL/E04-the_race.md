# The Long Line
## Episode 4: The Race

---

The notice came through on the long-range band at 04:00 ship time — a burst transmission from the Harvest Authority that hit every registered vessel in the sector simultaneously.

Sofia was already awake. She'd been awake since 02:00, sitting in the captain's chair with her feet up on the console, watching the starfield and thinking about nothing in particular. The kind of thinking that happens when you've been in space long enough that the silence becomes a texture.

The burst parsed itself across her screen:

**SECTOR NOTICE — PRIORITY ONE**
**RIDGEWAY BELT ACCESS WINDOW: 72 HOURS**
**WINDOW OPENS: 07/14 0600 UTC**
**WINDOW CLOSES: 07/17 0600 UTC**
**ALL REGISTERED HARVESTERS PERMITTED**
**STANDARD QUOTA RULES APPLY**
**FIRST ARRIVAL PRIORITY FOR CLAIM STAKING**

Sofia read it twice. Then she read it a third time.

The Ridgeway Belt. She knew the name the way any harvester knew it — by reputation. A dense field of carbonaceous chondrites laced with platinum-group metals, accessible only when the gas giant Cerberus-9 swung into a position that didn't completely shred everything in the passage between the inner system and the belt's orbital track. The window opened every three years. Seventy-two hours. Maybe less, depending on how the gas giant's magnetosphere behaved.

Every harvester in the sector would be staging already.

She pulled up the system map. The Resolute was currently anchored at a service buoy near Thompson Station, 38 hours from the Ridgeway approach at standard burn. The nearest competitor — a Bosch-class harvester out of Kendrick Dock — would be maybe 30 hours out. Maybe less. The new Bosch drives ran cleaner and faster than anything the Resolute had.

She swung her feet off the console and stood up.

"Ray."

The old man was asleep in the navigator's alcove, which was really just a cot wedged between the chart table and the bulkhead. He'd been sleeping there more often lately, claiming the cot was better for his back than the crew quarters. Sofia suspected he just liked being close to the bridge.

"Ray. Wake up."

He came awake the way he always did — completely, all at once, like a switch being thrown. No grogginess. Forty years of harvest work had burned the drowsiness out of him.

"What is it."

"Ridgeway window opens in fifty hours."

Ray sat up. In the dim blue light of the bridge, his face was unreadable.

"Who else knows?"

"Everybody. Authority burst. Whole sector."

"Then we're already behind." He swung his legs off the cot and reached for his boots. "What's our drive time to the approach?"

"Thirty-eight hours at standard burn."

"The Meridian out of Kendrick — she'll do it in thirty."

"Twenty-nine, maybe. Bosch drive."

Ray pulled his boots on with the unhurried precision of a man who had learned that panic was a luxury he couldn't afford. "We're not going to beat her on straight drive time."

"No."

"So we don't go straight."

Sofia had been hoping he'd say that. She pulled up the system map and projected it onto the main display. The standard approach to the Ridgeway Belt came in from the inner system — a clean curve around Cerberus-9's gravity well, wide and safe, skirting the edge of the gas giant's radiation zone. It was the route every navigation system would recommend. It was also the long way around.

"There's another way," Ray said. He wasn't looking at the map. He was looking at nothing — that far-off stare that meant he was reading from memory.

"Gus's route."

"Gus's route," Ray confirmed. "He ran it once. Maybe twice. I was on the second run. Deckhand, maybe twenty-three years old. Thought I was going to die."

Sofia waited. Ray had a way of unfolding a story at his own pace.

"It's a gap between Cerberus-9 and its third moon. Thanatos — the little one, the one that doesn't have a name in any official registry because nobody bothers to name a moon that nobody goes near. The gap is maybe forty kilometers wide. Maybe less. The gas giant's magnetosphere funnels right through it. Radiation's bad but manageable if you move fast. The thing is —" He paused. "The gravity is the problem."

He stood and walked to the display. With his finger, he traced a line that cut between the gas giant and a small, unremarkable moon.

"Ceberus-9 pulls you left. Thanatos pulls you right. The interaction zone between them is turbulent. The passage is short — maybe twelve minutes at transit speed — but the whole time, you're fighting both gravity wells and the magnetospheric drag. Gus had a set of waypoints. Timed burns, specific vectors. He wrote them on paper. Kept them in a lockbox in his quarters."

"Paper," Sofia said.

"He didn't trust digital. Said anything could be corrupted, but ink on paper was ink on paper."

"Do you have them?"

Ray looked at her. "I have them. Copied them myself, years ago. Before he died. Never told him."

"Would he have cared?"

"Gus didn't share. That was the thing about him. Everything he knew, he took to the grave, except what you managed to learn by watching. But I watched close." He went back to the cot and reached underneath. From a worn leather journal, he pulled a folded sheet of paper — not the original, but a careful copy in Ray's own hand. Column after column of numbers. Timestamps, vectors, burn durations.

"How old are these?" Sofia asked, taking the paper carefully.

"Twenty-some years. The orbital mechanics are the same every cycle. That's the whole point — the window opens because the configuration repeats. These waypoints will work."

"You're sure."

"I'm sure enough to sit in the chair and call them out. Whether the ship can handle it —" He looked at her. "That's Margaret's call."

---

Margaret was in the engine room, which was really the entire aft third of the ship, a vertical maze of conduits, tanks, and machinery that she navigated like a city she'd grown up in. She was waist-deep in the coolant return when Sofia found her, replacing a seal on the number-two circulation pump.

"Ridgeway window," Sofia said.

Margaret's hands didn't stop working. "I heard the burst. What's the plan?"

"We're going through Cerberus-9's inner passage. Gus's old route."

Margaret pulled herself out of the coolant return and wiped her hands on her pants. She had the look Sofia had come to recognize — the look that meant she was already building the problem in her head, running stress calculations against metal fatigue curves, cataloging every system that could fail under load.

"The inner passage," she repeated. "The one between the gas giant and the unnamed moon."

"Thanatos."

"Thanatos." She tasted the word. "Transit time?"

"Twelve minutes. Ray has waypoints."

"Twelve minutes." Margaret closed her eyes for a moment. Sofia could practically hear the spreadsheets running behind her forehead. "Drive stress at transit speed through that kind of gravity shear — we'll need the compensators at full. The number-three strut mount has a hairline crack I've been meaning to weld."

"Can you fix it?"

"I can fix anything. Question is whether I can fix it in the next thirty-six hours." She opened her eyes. "I need to see those waypoints. If Ray's vectors are right, I can model the stress profile. If the peak load is under 6 Gs lateral, the hull will hold. Above that, we're rolling dice."

"6 Gs?"

"Gus built this ship to take punishment. The frame's good for 6 Gs lateral, 8 longitudinal. Past that, you're trusting welds that are older than I am."

"What about the compensators?"

"Compensators are fine. It's the mounting brackets I worry about. And the coolant loop — if we lose flow during transit, the drive core overheats in ninety seconds. That new seal I just put in should hold, but the number-three pump is running on borrowed time."

"Fix it."

"Already fixing it." She turned back to the coolant return. "Get me Denny."

---

Denny was in the hauler bay, lying on his back under the primary intake manifold, listening to the ship the way a doctor listens to a heartbeat — with a stethoscope pressed to the hull. Not a real stethoscope. A screwdriver, handle against the metal, ear against the handle. An old trick. Gus had taught it to Ray, Ray had taught it to Sofia, Sofia had taught it to Denny.

He heard the captain's boots on the deck plating before she spoke.

"Ridgeway," she said.

Denny slid out from under the manifold. He was young — youngest on the crew by a decade — with the specific kind of calm that comes from growing up around machines that could kill you. His hands were black with lubricant.

"I know," he said. "I pulled the specs on the belt. Carbonaceous chondrites, high platinum content. Dense field. We'll fill the hold in —"

"That's not the problem. The problem is the transit. We're running Gus's inner passage."

Denny sat up. He didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "At transit speed?"

"At transit speed."

"The hauler can't take a full catch at speed. The intake baffles are rated for approach velocity — slow and steady. If we're moving fast and taking on material, the whole conveyance system shakes itself apart. We're talking about rocks hitting the intake at relative velocities the baffles weren't designed for."

"So what do we do?"

Denny thought about it. He lay back down and stared up at the underside of the intake manifold, his hands folded on his chest.

"We process on the fly," he said. "Instead of taking on raw material and processing it later, we run the crusher and separator continuously. Material comes in, gets crushed, gets sorted, gets stored. The dense fraction goes in the hold, the tailings get ejected. It means we're running every piece of equipment at full capacity for the entire fishing window."

"Can the equipment handle that?"

"The crusher can. The separator can. The conveyor system —" He held up a hand and made a wobbling motion. "It's the conveyor that's the weak link. The bearings on the number-four belt are shot. I've been nursing them along with grease and prayer. If we run continuous, I'll need to replace them during operation."

"During operation."

"While the belt is running. Yeah." He grinned. It was the grin of a man who understood that "impossible" was just another word for "hasn't been properly troubleshooted yet." "I've done dumber things. Give me the spares and the time, and I'll keep the hauler running."

"You'll have the time. Eighteen hours of continuous processing, if the field is as dense as they say."

"Eighteen hours. Yeah. I can do eighteen hours." He wiped his hands on his pants — a gesture Sofia noticed everyone on the ship had picked up from Margaret. "What about deck? We'll need someone running the intake controls nonstop. Watching the baffles, adjusting the feed rate. One mistake and we slag the system."

"Tomás."

"Tomás is good. Tomás is very good. But has he ever run a hot intake?"

"No."

"Then I'll talk him through it. Give us an hour before transit to drill." He slid back under the manifold. "And tell Margaret I need a new set of bearings from stores. The B-3 crate. If she asks, the ones on the shelf are for the number-two pump, and I'm not robbing Peter to pay Paul."

---

They gathered on the bridge six hours before the window opened. The Resolute was running dark — minimal power, no emissions, just the passive sensors watching the space around them. On the tactical display, Sofia could count the other harvesters. Eleven ships, scattered across the inner system, all burning for the standard approach. The Meridian was ahead of the pack, right on schedule, her Bosch drive pushing her at a clean 0.04 Gs toward the approach vector.

None of them were heading for the inner passage. None of them knew it existed, or if they did, none of them were crazy enough to try it.

"All hands," Sofia said. The intercom carried her voice to every compartment of the ship. "This is the captain. We are twelve hours from transit. Here's how this works."

She'd written the briefing on paper, longhand, the night before. She read from it now, not because she needed the notes, but because she wanted the crew to see that the plan was a thing — solid, real, something you could hold in your hands.

"Phase one is the approach. Standard burn for six hours, then we break from the inner-system track and angle for the Cerberus-9 inner passage. Ray calls the waypoints. Margaret monitors all systems. If she calls abort, we abort. No questions, no arguments."

Margaret nodded from her station.

"Phase two is the transit. Twelve minutes. I drive. Ray navigates. This is the part where we earn our money. Everything has to be right, and it has to be right at speed."

"Phase three is the grounds. We hit the belt, we stake our claim, and we run the hauler nonstop for the full window. Denny manages processing. Tomás runs the intake. I manage the claim and coordinate with the Authority beacon."

She looked at each of them in turn. Ray, standing behind the navigator's console, his leather journal open, the paper with Gus's waypoints secured under a magnetic strip. Margaret, lean and focused, her hands already resting on the systems console. Denny, patched into the bridge audio from the hauler bay, the sound of his breathing steady and unhurried. Tomás, quiet and attentive, standing at the deck station with his hands at his sides.

"Questions?"

"How far ahead do we come out of the passage?" Denny asked.

"If Ray's numbers are right — and they're going to be right — we exit the passage six hours ahead of the Meridian on the standard approach. That's six hours of uncontested claim time on a belt that hasn't been touched in three years."

"That's a lot of rock," Tomás said quietly. His first words of the briefing.

"That's a lot of rock," Sofia agreed. "Let's go get it."

---

The approach burn was smooth. Six hours of steady acceleration, the old drive humming through the hull in the deep baritone that Margaret said was the sound of a well-balanced core. Sofia monitored the track and watched the other harvesters on the display — a constellation of contacts all angling toward the same point, all taking the long way around.

At the six-hour mark, she adjusted the vector. The Resolute pivoted ten degrees off the standard approach and began to angle toward the pale yellow disk of Cerberus-9.

"Passive sensors show three other vessels have adjusted heading," Margaret said. "They're watching us."

"Let them watch."

"They know something's up. They just don't know what."

Ray looked up from his waypoint sheet. "Coming up on first burn marker. Thirty seconds."

Sofia's hands found the attitude controls. The ship responded — sluggish but willing, like a draft horse asked to canter.

"Mark," Ray said.

Sofia fired the lateral thrusters. The Resolute shifted, a gentle push to port that adjusted their heading by exactly 3.7 degrees. The gas giant swam closer in the forward display, its banded atmosphere filling the viewport with shades of amber and cream.

"Second marker in four minutes," Ray said. His voice had changed. Steadier. Harder. The voice of a man who was no longer remembering the waypoints but living them. He'd been here before, twenty-some years ago, watching Gus work the same controls. Now it was his turn.

The second burn was a longer one — twelve seconds of main drive, pushing the ship deeper into Cerberus-9's gravity well. The hull groaned, a low, resonant sound that Sofia felt in her sternum. Margaret watched the stress readouts without blinking.

"3.2 Gs," Margaret said. "All green."

"Entering the interaction zone," Ray said. "Next marker in ninety seconds. This is the big one."

The interaction zone. The place where Cerberus-9's gravity and Thanatos's gravity fought over the same space. Sofia could see the moon now — a dark, irregular shape silhouetted against the gas giant's glow, like a boulder in a river. The passage between them was invisible to the eye, just empty space, but the sensors showed the turbulence — gravity gradients, radiation flux, magnetospheric drag. A river of invisible force roaring between two massive bodies.

"Mark," Ray said.

Sofia fired the drive. Not a gentle push this time — a full six-second burn that slammed them back into their seats. The compensators howled. The hull didn't groan this time; it screamed, a metallic shriek that was less a sound and more a vibration, a frequency that lived in the bones and the teeth.

"5.1 Gs lateral," Margaret said. Her voice was tight but controlled. "Strut mount holding. Compensators in the green. Core temp rising — 340 Kelvin. Normal range."

"Next marker, thirty seconds," Ray said. "Short burn, two seconds, then we ride the gravity gradient for six minutes without thrust. The ship will want to drift. Fight it. Don't let the heading wander more than half a degree."

"Understood."

The two-second burn was a punch. Then silence — the drive cut, and the ship was coasting through the passage on momentum and gravity, sliding between the gas giant and the moon like a stone between two closing walls. Sofia's hands were constant on the controls, making micro-adjustments, feeling the ship want to yaw to starboard where Cerberus-9 pulled, then lurch to port where Thanatos grabbed. She held it steady, trimming the attitude by fractions of a degree, the kind of flying that has nothing to do with strength and everything to do with touch.

"Radiation alert," Margaret said. "Spiking. Expected. Shielding is holding."

"How bad?" Sofia asked, without looking away from the heading indicator.

"Bad. Three hundred millisievert peak. But we're through the worst in four minutes."

Four minutes. Sofia could feel the radiation in her teeth — a metallic taste that was probably psychosomatic but felt real. The hull was hot to the touch, vibrating, the metal conducting the magnetospheric energy in ways that made every surface buzz.

"Ray, next marker."

"Coming up. Twenty seconds. When I call mark, I want a full retro burn — four seconds — then immediately flip and burn prograde for eight. Back-to-back. You'll need to rotate the ship between burns."

A flip-and-burn. In a forty-kilometer passage between a gas giant and a moon. While coasting at transit speed. Sofia's hands tightened on the controls.

"Mark."

She fired the retros. The ship lurched, decelerating hard. Immediately — before the vibration had even settled — she rolled the ship 180 degrees on its long axis and fired the main drive. Eight seconds of full thrust, the ship accelerating back up to transit speed in the opposite orientation. The hull shrieked again. Something in the overhead clattered loose and hit the deck.

"5.4 Gs," Margaret called. "Number-three strut mount — stress at ninety-one percent. Holding."

"Out of the interaction zone in sixty seconds," Ray said. His hands were shaking. Sofia could see them on the chart table, the paper rattling under his fingertips. Not from fear. From adrenaline. "One more marker. Short burn. Ten seconds to transit exit."

Ten seconds. Sofia could see the starfield again ahead — the passage was opening up, the dark bulk of Thanatos falling behind, the gas giant's glow diffusing into open space. The Ridgeway Belt was ahead, a glittering, dense field of reflected light that looked like someone had spilled a bag of diamonds on black velvet.

"Mark," Ray said. Calm. Final.

Sofia fired the last burn. The ship surged forward, clearing the passage, and the turbulence fell away behind them like a wake off a boat's stern. The hull stopped screaming. The compensators wound down from their howl to a steady hum. The radiation reading began to drop.

"Transit complete," Ray said. He sat down heavily on the edge of the chart table and let out a breath that sounded like it had been held for twelve minutes.

Sofia checked the display. The other harvesters — all eleven of them — were still six hours out on the standard approach. The Meridian, the fastest, was leading the pack. The Resolute was already through. Already in the clear. Already first.

"Margaret, status."

"Hull integrity nominal. Number-three strut mount is stressed but functional — I'll weld a reinforcing plate when we're on-station. Core temp stabilizing. Compensators... compensators are fine." She paused. "We're good, Captain."

"Denny, hauler status."

"Spun up and ready. Bearings are holding. Conveyor is running. I've got grease in places I didn't know I had." A pause. "Let's fish."

---

The Ridgeway Belt was everything the rumors said. Sofia had seen asteroid fields before — most were sparse, disappointing things, rocks scattered so far apart you could fly through for an hour without seeing one. The Ridgeway was different. It was dense. Packed. The rocks were close enough that the Resolute had to actually navigate between them, weaving through the field like a boat through a kelp bed.

The claim beacon went out at 06:04 UTC, four minutes after the window officially opened. First claim. Priority staking. The Authority satellite registered the Resolute's position and timestamp and locked it in. No one could contest the claim until the Resolute moved off-station or the window closed.

"All hands, we are on-station. Claim is staked. Denny, start the intake."

"Starting intake. Tomás, you ready?"

Tomás was at the deck controls, his hands on the manual overrides. He'd never run a hot intake before. Six hours of drilling with Denny had taught him the basics, but basics were not the same as doing it. He took a breath.

"Ready."

"Opening baffles."

The intake opened — a wide maw on the Resolute's bow that drew in material through a magnetic gradiometer, which sorted the rocks by composition. Carbonaceous chondrites went into the primary conveyor. Silicate waste was ejected. The good rock hit the intake at relative velocity, and the baffles caught it, slowing it just enough to prevent damage. But just enough was a thin margin.

First contact. The ship shuddered as a chunk of chondrite the size of a basketball hit the intake and was captured. The conveyor grabbed it, fed it to the crusher, and the crusher — a massive rotating drum lined with industrial diamond teeth — bit into it with a sound like a car crash.

"First rock in the system," Denny reported. "Conveyor running. Separator online. Dense fraction routing to hold one."

"Copy." Sofia watched the hold fill gauge. It ticked up — one kilogram, two, five. The first rock was nothing. The field ahead was everything.

"Tomás, feed rate."

"Steady. Baffles at sixty percent. I can open up more."

"Do it. Seventy-five percent."

"Seventy-five." Tomás adjusted the baffles. The intake flow increased. More rocks — bigger ones, clusters of smaller ones — hit the system. The conveyor hummed under the load. The crusher ate them.

For the first hour, it was smooth. The Resolute drifted through the belt with its intake open, swallowing rocks like a whale swallowing krill. The hold fill gauge climbed steadily. At the one-hour mark, they were at eight percent capacity — well ahead of pace.

Then the conveyor bearing started to go.

"Denny — number-four belt is heating up. I'm seeing vibration on the drive roller."

Denny was already moving. He grabbed the spare bearings from the B-3 crate, stuffed them into his tool belt, and climbed into the conveyor housing. The belt was running six inches from his face, the crusher drum visible through the access panel, grinding away with mechanical fury.

"I see it. The inner race is spalling. Hold on —"

He worked with the belt running. Both hands in the space between the drive roller and the frame, where the bearing sat in its housing. One wrong move and the conveyor would take his fingers. He pulled the retaining clip, extracted the failing bearing — hot, the metal almost too hot to touch even through his gloves — and pressed the new one home. The retaining clip snapped into place.

"Bearing replaced. Vibration should drop."

"Copy, Denny. Vibration is... yes. Dropping. Nice work."

He slid out of the conveyor housing and lay on the deck for a moment, breathing hard. Then he got up, wiped his hands, and went back to monitoring.

---

Hour four. Hold at twenty-nine percent. They'd settled into a rhythm — Tomás managing the feed rate, adjusting the baffles as the density of the field varied around them. Denny making circuits of the hauler bay, one hand always on a wrench, his ears tuned to every sound the machinery made. Margaret in the engine room, monitoring the drive core and the compensators, occasionally welding something or tightening something, her voice on the intercom a calm counterpoint to the mechanical noise.

Sofia drove. Through the field, around the big rocks, past the clusters that could foul the intake. She threaded the Resolute through the belt the way a fishing boat threads through a crowded harbor — slow, deliberate, always aware of the space around her.

Ray sat beside her, not navigating now but watching. The waypoints were done. His job was the transit. Now it was Sofia's ship, and he was just a man sitting in a chair, watching the stars.

"You're doing fine," he said, once, around hour six. He didn't say it again. He didn't need to.

---

Hour nine. Hold at fifty-eight percent. The Meridian arrived on-station, six hours behind them, and found the prime ground already claimed. Its captain sent a terse radio message — professional, acknowledging the claim, not happy about it. Other harvesters followed. By hour twelve, there were eight ships working the belt, but the Resolute had the densest section, the pick of the field, and the hold was filling faster than anyone else's.

Hour twelve was when Denny almost lost the crusher.

A rock — bigger than the baffles were rated for, dense, iron-nickel, the wrong composition — got through the gradiometer and hit the crusher drum at the wrong angle. The drum jammed. The motor screamed, an agonizing mechanical shriek that echoed through the hauler bay. Smoke poured from the drive housing.

"Crusher down!" Denny shouted into the intercom. "I need ninety seconds!"

He killed the motor before the thermal overload tripped. Ripped the access panel off the drive housing. The gearbox was hot — smoking, the lubricant vaporized by the friction. The jam was visible: the iron-nickel rock wedged between the drum and the housing, bent steel around it like a bullet wound.

"Tomás, close baffles. We're not taking on material until I clear this."

"Baffles closing."

Denny grabbed the pry bar. Three feet of hardened steel, heavy enough that he had to brace his feet and throw his whole weight into it. The rock didn't move. He repositioned, found a better angle, and heaved again. The rock shifted — then popped free, clanging across the interior of the housing and rolling to a stop against the far wall.

He checked the drum. The teeth were gouged but intact. The housing was bent but functional — the drum still rotated in its bearings, though the clearance was tight on one side. He'd need to hammer it straight later.

"Motor's okay. Gearbox is cooked — I'm bypassing it. Going direct drive. Margaret, I need you to pull power from the auxiliary bus."

"Routing now," Margaret said. "You'll have full torque but no speed control. It's all or nothing."

"All or nothing works." Denny reconnected the motor through the bypass, sealed the housing, and restarted the crusher. The drum spun up, slightly off-balance now due to the bent housing, but turning. "Crusher back online. Tomás, open baffles. Let's go."

The belt roared back to life. The hold gauge ticked upward again.

---

Hour fifteen. Hold at seventy-nine percent. Margaret reported a minor coolant leak in the number-three loop — patched it with epoxy and a prayer. Tomás was running the baffles by feel now, his hands on the controls, his eyes on the flow rate, adjusting in real-time as the field density fluctuated. He'd found the rhythm of it — the way the rocks came in pulses, clusters followed by gaps, the way you had to open up for the clusters and throttle back for the gaps. He was natural at it in a way that surprised himself. The controls felt right in his hands, responsive, like they'd been waiting for him.

Denny noticed it too. Around hour fourteen, he said over the intercom, his voice carrying the grudging admiration of a man who didn't give compliments lightly: "Kid's got hands."

Nobody disagreed.

---

Hour sixteen. Hold at eighty-six percent. The field was thinning around the Resolute's position — they'd cleaned out the densest section, taken the best material, left the tailings and the dross behind. The other harvesters were working the outer edges of the belt, picking through what the Resolute had already claimed and passed over. The Meridian's captain sent another radio message — friendly this time, almost conversational. *Good haul?*

Sofia keyed the mic. *Adequate.*

A pause. Then: *Bosch drive didn't help us much this time.*

*No,* Sofia agreed. *It didn't.*

She signed off and looked at Ray. He was smiling.

---

Hour seventeen. The problems came in a cascade, the way they always did — not singly, but in groups, like predators that had learned to hunt together.

First, the separator's primary cyclone started losing efficiency. The dense fraction — the platinum-bearing material that was the whole point of the operation — was coming out contaminated with lighter silicate, which meant the hold was taking on weight that had no value. Denny diagnosed it in seconds: the cyclone's internal vanes were fouled with a buildup of fine dust that the cleaning system couldn't keep up with.

"I need to crack the cyclone and clean the vanes. Twenty minutes."
"We lose twenty minutes of intake?"
"We lose twenty minutes of intake."
Sofia looked at the hold gauge. Eighty-nine percent. "Do it. Tomás, close baffles. Stand by."

Denny cracked the cyclone housing, scraped the vanes clean with a putty knife, resealed it, and had the separator back online in eighteen minutes. He didn't tell anyone it was supposed to take twenty. He just did it.

Then, two minutes after they restarted the intake, the number-three coolant loop failed.

Not the patch Margaret had put on earlier — a different section, downstream, where a fitting corroded through and coolant sprayed across the engine room in a fine, hissing mist. Margaret hit the isolation valve before the core temp even moved. She had the replacement fitting out of stores and installed in eleven minutes, working in a cloud of coolant vapor that stung her eyes and turned her skin slick with chemical residue.

"Coolant restored. Core temp stable. That was the last spare fitting in that size."

"Noted," Sofia said.

---

Hour eighteen. Hold at ninety-four percent. The intake was still running, but the material coming in was thinner — the belt around the Resolute's position was genuinely depleted now. They'd taken the best of it. Sofia made the call.

"All hands. We're at ninety-four percent. I'm calling it. Denny, secure the hauler. Tomás, close baffles. Margaret, bring the core to standby."

"Securing hauler," Denny acknowledged. His voice was hoarse — eighteen hours of intercom traffic and mechanical noise and recycled air. "Crusher off. Separator off. Conveyor... conveyor off. System secure."

"Baffles closed," Tomás said.

"Core at standby," Margaret said.

Sofia leaned back in the captain's chair. The adrenaline was draining out of her, leaving behind a bone-deep weariness that felt, in its own way, like satisfaction. She looked at the hold gauge one more time.

Ninety-four percent. The Resolute's maximum certified capacity was ninety-five. They were one percent shy of full.

"Authority beacon, this is F/V Resolute, registration November-Seven-Four-Two. Claim complete. Transmitting final manifest."

She keyed the manifest — tonnage, composition, duration, the automated log that the Authority satellite would record and archive. The numbers went up. The satellite confirmed receipt.

---

They pulled off-station an hour before the window closed. The other harvesters were still working, still taking on material, still running their intakes. The Meridian had managed sixty-one percent — a good haul, by any normal standard. The Resolute had ninety-four.

The transit back was uneventful. Standard approach, wide and safe, skirting the gas giant's radiation zone. Sofia let the autopilot handle it and sat on the bridge with her feet up, watching the stars.

Ray was beside her. He had his leather journal open on his lap, but he wasn't reading it. He was looking at nothing — that far-off stare that could mean anything or nothing.

"Sofia," he said.

"Yeah."

"The transit log. Gus's record on the Ridgeway run. You know what it was?"

She did. She'd looked it up before they'd left. "Eighty-nine percent hold capacity. Forty-one hours on-station."

"Forty-one hours to get eighty-nine percent. We did ninety-four in eighteen."

Sofia didn't say anything.

"The system logged it," Ray said. "The Authority satellite. It's all in the record now. Best haul in the Resolute's history. Better than any of Gus's runs. Better than the numbers he wrote in that journal."

Still, Sofia said nothing.

Ray closed the journal. He folded his hands on top of it and looked out the viewport at the starfield — the same stars Gus had looked at, the same stars he'd looked at as a twenty-three-year-old deckhand on this very ship, terrified and exhilarated and alive.

He smiled.

"Good crew," he said.

"Yeah," Sofia said. "They are."

Ray didn't say what he was thinking, and Sofia didn't tell him what she already knew — that the system log showed his waypoints were perfect, every burn timed to the second, every vector exactly right. That the transit was textbook. That a dead man's route, copied onto paper by a young deckhand twenty-some years ago, had just set a record that would probably stand until the next window opened.

Some things you don't need to say.

Behind them, through the hull, they could hear Denny in the hauler bay, hammering the crusher housing straight. The clang of metal on metal, rhythmic and patient. The sound of a man fixing things. The sound of a ship that had done its job and would do it again.

The Resolute flew on through the dark, her hold full, her crew quiet, the stars ahead.
