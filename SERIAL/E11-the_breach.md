# The Long Line
## Episode 11: The Breach

---

The alarm didn't sound like an alarm.

It sounded like a cough — a short, percussive exhale from the hull, followed by a hiss that lasted maybe two seconds before the automated systems caught it. Then the overhead flashed amber, once, and Margaret's voice came over the intercom with the flat precision of someone reading data, not dramatizing it.

"Loss of pressure in frame nine. Starboard side, below the waterline. Automatic sealant has engaged. Holding — barely."

Sofia was in the galley. She was eating a bowl of reconstituted rice — dinner, or what passed for dinner at 21:00 ship time on day fourteen of a twenty-one-day run — and the amber flash turned her rice the color of old brass. She set the bowl down. She was on her feet and moving before the intercom clicked off.

Ray was already in the corridor. He'd been in the wheelhouse, running the night check on the nav plot, and he'd come down the ladder with the controlled urgency of a man who'd heard hull sounds before and knew the difference between routine and not. He didn't run. He moved fast, his boots hitting the deck plates with a rhythm that the crew recognized the way a household recognizes a smoke detector — not panic, but the thing that precedes panic by exactly one step.

"Report," he said.

Margaret's voice came back. "Frame nine, starboard. Below the cargo deck. Small breach — micrometeorite, most likely. Entry wound maybe three centimeters. Sealant foam is holding but the pressure differential is pulling it. I'm losing about .02 atmospheres per hour."

"Life support?"

"Compensating. Air reserves at ninety-eight percent. Not dangerous yet."

"What's 'yet'?"

"If the sealant fails — and it might, because the foam is designed for stress cracks, not punctures — I lose seal integrity in that frame within four hours. Frame nine isolates from the main hull at the frame-eight bulkhead door. I can seal it off. We lose the compartment."

"What's in frame nine?"

Margaret paused. The pause was unusual. Margaret didn't pause.

"Cargo processing. The secondary mag-net winch. And the number-three fuel cell."

Denny's voice cut in from the engine room. He'd been monitoring the conversation on the intercom. "If we lose number-three, we lose backup power for the harvester array. We can still run on one and two, but if either of those hiccups, we're done fishing."

Sofia processed this. Fourteen days into the rogue field. Their holds were at sixty-two percent — a good season shaping up to be a great one, if they could keep working. Sixty-two percent of a 20,200-kilogram cap was 12,524 kilos of pharmaceutical-grade organics. Worth real money. Worth the difference between a season that funded the coalition's filing fees and a season that didn't.

"We're not sealing it off," Sofia said. "Not yet. Margaret — can we patch it?"

"From inside? No. The hull is curved at that point — the puncture is in the plating between frames nine and ten, below the cargo deck. There's no access from the interior. It's behind the hull rib. We'd have to go outside."

Sofia looked at Ray. Ray looked at the deckhead. The calculation was the same calculation it always was on a working vessel: risk versus necessity, time versus capacity, the boat versus the water.

"We patch it," Ray said. "From outside."

---

The Resolute carried four EVA suits. They lived in the airlock, hung on charging racks like empty people, their systems cycling on standby. The suits were good — Margaret maintained them with the same ruthless precision she applied to everything — but they were not new. The oldest was twenty-two years old. The newest was nine. All of them had been patched, resealed, re-certified, and patched again.

Denny and Margaret suited up first. That was the call — Denny because the repair was mechanical, Margaret because the hull was hers, and Sofia because someone needed to stay inside and run the ship while the two people who understood the Resolute's body best went outside to perform surgery on it.

"You're not going out there," Ray said to Sofia. It wasn't a question.

"I know."

"I need you on the conn."

"I know."

Tomás stood in the airlock, holding Denny's helmet, his face doing the thing it did when he wanted to volunteer for something dangerous and knew he shouldn't. He was twenty-two. He was brave in the way that young people are brave — instinctively, without the cost-benefit analysis that experience imposes. He also weighed a hundred and eighty pounds and the EVA maneuvering was delicate enough without adding mass that wasn't needed.

"I'll monitor from the airlock," Tomás said. "Suit comms, tether management."

Denny took his helmet from Tomás's hands. He looked at the kid — at his big hands and his worried face and his instinct to help — and gave him a nod that was also a boundary.

"You'll monitor from the bridge," Denny said. "Suit comms, tether management. From the bridge. Where the console is."

Tomás opened his mouth. Closed it. Nodded.

Sofia touched his shoulder as he passed her in the corridor. "You'll hear everything," she said. "You'll be our ears."

He went to the bridge.

---

The EVA prep took forty minutes. Forty minutes was a long time when the hull was hissing. Margaret spent thirty of those minutes on the suit checks alone, running each system through its diagnostic sequence with the methodical patience of someone who knew that the suit was the only thing between a person and the specific, undignified death that space reserved for those who skipped steps.

She checked seals. She checked pressure. She checked the oxygen supply — four hours each, with a thirty-minute reserve. She checked the comm link, the tether attachment points, the maneuvering thrusters, the helmet display, the glove integrity. She checked Denny's suit as if he were a recruit, not a man who'd been doing EVA work for twelve years. He let her. That was the protocol. On the Resolute, Margaret's word on safety equipment was absolute, and even Denny — who would argue with Margaret about anything and everything, who would debate the classification of a coolant leak until they were both hoarse — submitted to her suit check without comment.

At 21:45, they cycled the airlock.

Sofia watched on the monitor from the wheelhouse. The outer door opened and the two figures stepped out into the dark, tethered to the ship by cables that looked impossibly thin against the vast, indifferent backdrop of space. The rogue field drifted beyond them — dark shapes against stars, the slow-moving asteroids of the belt that had been their workplace and their home for two weeks.

The Resolute's hull, seen from outside, was a different animal than the Resolute's hull seen from within. From inside, the ship was a series of rooms — cramped, functional, known. From outside, the ship was a shape: a long, narrow hull with the mag-net array stowed along the dorsal spine, the propulsion housing at the stern, the bridge module forward. Eighty years of patches and overlays and refits showed in the texture of the plating — different metals, different colors, the geological record of decades of repairs written on the skin of the ship.

Denny and Margaret moved along the starboard hull using the handholds that Denny had welded to the plating seven years ago — small metal loops, spaced at arm's length, painted safety yellow because Gus had insisted. They passed the cargo hatch, the ventral fin, the secondary antenna array. They were heading aft, toward frame nine.

"Sofia, we're at the frame-eight marker," Denny's voice came over the comm. "Moving to frame nine. Margaret's scanning."

"Copy." Sofia watched the suit telemetry on the secondary display. Two heartbeats. Two temperature readings. Two pressure readings. All nominal.

"I see it," Margaret said. Her voice was calm the way a surgeon's voice is calm — not relaxed, but controlled. The calm of someone who has found the problem and is now determining how bad it is.

"Describe it," Sofia said.

"Puncture. Starboard plating, between frames nine and ten. About three centimeters diameter. Clean entry — the meteorite was small, moving fast. Went through the outer plating and embedded in the inner hull layer. The sealant foam is in the gap, but it's not adhering. The inner hull surface is —" She paused. "Contaminated."

"Contaminated how?"

"Corrosion. The inner plating around the puncture site is corroded. Not surface corrosion — intergranular. The metal is tired, Sofia. The crystalline structure is breaking down. That's why the sealant won't hold — there's nothing for it to bond to."

Sofia felt something cold settle in her chest. Not fear. Something more specific than fear. The feeling of a problem that was bigger than the immediate crisis.

"Can you patch it?" she asked.

Margaret's silence lasted three seconds. An eternity, in Margaret-time.

"The puncture, yes. I can bond a plate over it. Thirty minutes with the right materials. But the corrosion —" Another pause. "The corrosion isn't local. I need to scan a wider area."

"Do it."

Sofia sat in the captain's chair and watched the two figures on the external camera. They were small against the hull — two glowing specks on the dark surface of the ship, their suit lights casting white circles on the plating. Margaret had a scanner in her hand, sweeping it across the hull in slow, deliberate arcs. Denny stood by, holding a case of repair materials, watching the tether lines and the suit telemetry on his arm display.

Ray stood behind her. He hadn't spoken since the airlock cycled. He was watching the same monitor, his arms crossed, his weight on his good leg — the left one, the one without the knee that bothered him in the cold. She could feel him thinking. It was a thing you learned to sense on a small ship — the quality of someone's silence, the temperature of their attention. Ray's silence was heavy. He was reading the situation ahead of the data.

---

Margaret's scan took twenty minutes.

In those twenty minutes, Sofia ran the ship. She adjusted station-keeping to minimize stress on the starboard hull — a slight yaw that put the puncture site on the lee side of the ship's orientation relative to the field's drift. She monitored life support, which was compensating for the slow pressure loss without strain. She checked the catch in the hold — 12,524 kilos, secure, no exposure. She did the things that needed doing while the people who could fix the hull were outside fixing the hull.

Tomás was on the comm, relaying suit telemetry, logging Margaret's scan data, maintaining the tether plot on the bridge display. His voice was steady. He'd found his role — the ears, the relay, the person who made sure the two figures outside could talk to the two figures inside. He was good at it. The data flowed through him cleanly, without commentary, without interpretation.

At 22:08, Margaret came back on the comm.

"Sofia. I need you to see this."

"Send it to my display."

The data came through — a hull integrity map, generated by Margaret's scanner, showing the plating density across frames seven through twelve on the starboard side. The map was color-coded: green for nominal, yellow for degraded, red for critical.

The puncture site at frame nine was a red dot, small and sharp. But around it, spreading outward like a bruise, was a field of yellow. Not just frame nine. Frames eight through eleven, starboard side, below the cargo deck. Thirty square meters of hull plating where the metal was aging from the inside out — intergranular corrosion, the slow death of alloy that had been cycling between extremes of temperature and pressure for seventy-eight years.

"Margaret," Sofia said. "How bad?"

"The puncture is the symptom. The corrosion is the disease. The plating in this section has lost approximately fifteen percent of its rated structural integrity. At current rates of degradation, I'd expect to see more punctures within two to three seasons. Maybe sooner, if we take another hit."

"Can you treat it? Replace the affected plating?"

"In the field? No. This is a dockyard job. We'd need to pull the plating, treat or replace the affected frames, re-skin the hull in this section. It's a full refit — three weeks in drydock, minimum."

Sofia looked at the display. The yellow area glowed like a warning she'd been ignoring. Three weeks in drydock. Three weeks of dead weight on the ledger, of yard fees and labor costs and parts. Three weeks of the ship not earning.

"How much?" she asked.

Margaret answered without hesitation, as if she'd already done the math — because she had, probably months ago, waiting for the moment when someone needed to hear the number. "Hull re-skinning for a section this size. Materials, labor, dock fees, certification. Four hundred and twenty thousand chit. Give or take."

Four hundred and twenty thousand. Sofia felt the number land in her body, not her mind — a physical sensation, like stepping into cold water. The Resolute's best season — a *great* season, the kind that came once a decade — might clear 340,000 chit split four ways after expenses. That was per crew share, not total. The total operating margin, the money available for capital expenditure, was considerably less.

The ship needed a refit that cost more than the ship could earn in a year. The ship was eighty years old. The hull was dying.

"That's the starboard side," Sofia said. "What about port?"

"I haven't scanned port in detail since the last drydock. But the port plating is the same vintage, same alloy, same thermal exposure. I'd expect similar degradation."

"So the whole hull —"

"The whole hull is aging. Not uniformly — the frames that see the most thermal cycling are worse. But yes. The Resolute needs a comprehensive hull refit. Not this season. Maybe not next. But within two to three years, or we start losing frames."

Sofia swallowed. She looked at the monitor, where Margaret and Denny were still small figures on the dark hull, their suit lights bright against the plating. "Patch the puncture first. Then we talk."

---

Denny cut the patch plate from a sheet of repair alloy he'd brought in the case — a rectangle roughly fifteen centimeters on a side, thick enough to provide structural reinforcement. Margaret prepped the surface around the puncture, grinding away the corroded metal with a hand tool until she reached clean alloy. The sound transmitted through the hull as a faint, rasping drone that Sofia could feel through the deck plates.

"Clean surface," Margaret reported. "Bond area is good. Applying adhesive."

She spread the bonding compound — a two-part epoxy rated for vacuum application — around the edge of the puncture. Denny positioned the plate over it, pressing it into the compound with his gloved hands. Margaret activated the thermal cure with a handheld lamp, bathing the plate in orange light that reflected off the hull in wavering patterns.

"Cure complete in four minutes," Margaret said. "Seal integrity test in five."

They waited. The suit telemetry showed their heart rates — Margaret's at sixty-two, Denny's at seventy-one. Resting rates. The rates of people doing hard work in dangerous conditions who were not afraid because they had done this before and would do it again.

"Seal test," Margaret said. She pressurized the patch from inside — a remote command to the sealant system that forced gas against the plate and measured the resistance. The numbers came back on her arm display.

"Seal confirmed. Pressure holding. We're good."

"Come inside," Sofia said.

---

Denny and Margaret cycled back through the airlock at 23:15. They de-suited in the equipment bay, hanging the suits on their racks, plugging in the charging cables. Denny's hair was matted with sweat. Margaret looked exactly the same as she always did — composed, precise, untouched. The difference was in her hands, which were trembling slightly, the fine motor tremor of someone who had been gripping tools in pressure gloves for ninety minutes.

Denny noticed. He didn't say anything. He made her tea — walked to the galley, filled the kettle, set it to boil, found her preferred mug (the one labeled M.K. in three languages, because Margaret labeled everything), and brought it back to the equipment bay along with a packet of the green tea she drank.

"Thanks," Margaret said. She took the mug. Her hands had steadied.

They sat in the equipment bay, in the folding chairs that lived against the wall, and drank their respective beverages — Denny had coffee, because Denny always had coffee — and didn't talk for a while. The hum of the ship filled the silence.

"Forty-two thousand square centimeters of affected plating," Margaret said. She said it to the air, not to Denny. The way you say a number that's too big to carry alone.

"You told her," Denny said.

"I had to."

"I know. I'm not saying you shouldn't have. I'm saying — forty-two thousand. On a hull that's seventy-eight years old. And that's just the section we scanned."

"Port side will be as bad or worse. Port takes more thermal flux from the drive exhaust."

"So the whole ship."

Margaret sipped her tea. "The whole ship."

Denny leaned back in his chair and stared at the deckhead. Overhead, the plating was original — the same alloy that had been installed at Cable Shipyards in 2148, stamped with the yard's mark and the date. He could see the stamp from where he sat, the letters and numbers faded but legible. CNS-2148. Cable Naval Shipyards. Seventy-eight years.

"She's old, Margaret."

"She's always been old."

"She's always been *solid*. That's different. Solid old is character. Tired old is —" He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't have the word.

"Expensive," Margaret finished for him.

"That's not what I was going to say."

"I know. But it's the word that matters."

---

The crew gathered in the galley at midnight. Not by arrangement. By gravity. The galley was where things happened on the Resolute — the table that was too small, the seats that were too few, the space that held everything.

Tomás had made sandwiches. It was what he did — assemble food from whatever was available, present it as if it were a meal, and in doing so create the illusion that the situation was manageable. People ate. Therefore things were okay. It was a form of care that required no words, and Tomás was good at it.

Lily sat in her corner. She'd been off-shift when the alarm went off, and she'd stayed out of the way — her instinct, still, after months on the boat, was to be small and present and unobserved. But she was here now, watching, listening, her hands wrapped around a mug of tea Margaret had made for her without being asked.

Sofia stood at the head of the table. She had the hull integrity map pulled up on her tablet, the yellow and red glow of the scan data casting shadows on her face.

"Margaret scanned the starboard hull tonight. The puncture was a micrometeorite — small, fast, clean entry. Denny patched it. The patch will hold. We're not in immediate danger."

She paused. The galley was very quiet.

"But the scan showed something else. The hull plating around the puncture site — frames seven through eleven, starboard side — is corroding. Intergranular corrosion. The metal is breaking down at the crystalline level. It's been happening for years, maybe decades. It's within tolerance right now, but it's accelerating. Margaret's estimate is that we'll see more punctures within two to three seasons. Maybe sooner."

She set the tablet on the table. The scan map glowed up at them — the Resolute's starboard side rendered in false color, the healthy green giving way to the yellow of degradation and the single red dot of the puncture that had started everything.

"The fix is a full hull refit. Dockyard. Three weeks minimum. Four hundred and twenty thousand chit."

The number sat on the table like a stone. Nobody picked it up. They all looked at it.

Ray spoke first. "What's our operating margin this season?"

"If we fill the quota — all 20,200 — and market prices hold, we clear about 280,000 after expenses. That's before crew shares."

"Crew shares are a hundred and twelve split four ways. Twenty-eight each. That leaves — what — forty-eight thousand for the boat?"

"Forty-six," Sofia said. "And that's a good season. That's the best season we've had in five years."

"So we're short by —"

"By a lot. By more than one season. By two seasons, if we're lucky. By three, if we're not."

Denny leaned forward, his elbows on the table. "Can we finance it? Spread the cost over —"

"Financing a refit on an eighty-year-old hull," Margaret said. Her voice carried the specific weight of someone who had already researched this. "I checked, last time we were at Waypoint. Out of curiosity. Lenders rate hull age as primary risk factor. A vessel over seventy-five requires a thirty-five percent down payment on any structural refit. That's a hundred and forty-seven thousand up front, before they'll touch us."

"Forty-seven thousand more than we have," Sofia said.

"Per season," Margaret added. "If we banked everything — no crew draws, no discretionary spending, nothing — we'd need two full seasons of max catch to get there. Two seasons of the crew working for free."

"I can work for free for a season," Denny said.

"No, you can't," Ray said. "You have a daughter."

The words were quiet and absolute. Denny looked at Ray. Ray looked back. The truth between them was old and well-worn: Denny had Maya, who was sixteen on Meridian, who needed school fees and clothes and the occasional trip to see her father. Denny's life was not just the engine room. Ray was reminding him of that, the way Ray reminded people of things — without emphasis, without drama, just the fact, placed where it needed to be.

"The coalition," Sofia said.

Every head turned.

"Mara mentioned shared maintenance resources. Maintenance pooling. The coalition buys materials in bulk, shares spares, splits dock costs. If the coalition files as a cooperative entity, we can apply for collective financing — the lender rates the co-op's combined fleet as collateral, not just one aging hull. Better risk profile. Better terms."

"You're using the coalition to fix the boat," Ray said. His voice was carefully neutral.

"I'm using the coalition for what it was designed for. This is what Mara meant. Four boats, sharing resources, sharing costs. The Resolute needs a refit. The Polaris probably needs one too — Skip Chen's boat is older than ours. The Calypso is held together with prayer. We're not the only ones."

"You're saying we need the coalition now. Not as a choice. As a necessity."

Sofia looked at Ray. The question he was asking wasn't about the coalition. It was about independence — the same independence they'd debated when Mara first proposed the idea, the same word that meant everything to fishing people. Joining the coalition because you chose to was one thing. Joining because you couldn't survive without it was another.

"I'm saying the hull is eighty years old," Sofia said. "And we can't fix it alone."

The galley was quiet. Through the hull — through the patched hull, through seventy-eight years of plating and frame and structural alloy — they could hear the faint hum of the reactor, the whisper of the life support, the distant pulse of the mag-net array on standby. The sounds of the ship. The sounds of home.

And beneath those sounds, if you listened very carefully, the sound of metal getting tired.

---

Ray didn't sleep that night.

He sat in the wheelhouse with the lights low and his coffee going cold and Gus's logbook on the console beside him. He didn't open it. He didn't need to. He knew what was in it — thirty years of entries, thirty years of catches and courses and weather, thirty years of a man's life written in cramped handwriting on paper that smelled like ozone and salt.

Gus had loved this boat. Loved it the way you love a living thing — not because it was perfect, but because it was his. He'd poured everything into it. His money. His time. His health. His marriage, in a sense — Gus's wife had left after twelve years, unable to compete with a fifty-one-foot harvester for a man's attention. She'd taken Sofia's mother with her. Gus had stayed on the boat.

The boat had been his independence. His identity. His answer to every question: *I have the Resolute, and that's enough.*

And now the Resolute was aging. The hull was corroding. The metal was tired. In two to three seasons, the boat that Gus had built his life around would need a refit that cost more than it could earn, and the only way to pay for it was to give up a piece of the independence that had defined it.

Ray understood this. He understood it the way he understood weather — not as an abstraction but as a physical reality, something you could feel in the air, something that was coming regardless of how you felt about it.

He also understood something Sofia didn't, not yet, because she was twenty-six and hadn't lived long enough to learn it: everything ages. Boats age. People age. The things you build in your youth become the things you maintain in your middle years and the things you prop up in your old age. This was not failure. This was not tragedy. This was the cost of time, and time was the only currency that couldn't be earned back.

Gus had known this. Gus had felt the hull aging under his feet for the last decade of his command. He'd felt it the way Margaret felt it now — as data, as scan results, as the slow accumulation of small repairs that were becoming larger repairs. He'd known the refit was coming. He'd also known he wouldn't be the one to deal with it, because Gus was a man who lived in the present tense with a stubbornness that bordered on faith.

Gus had left the boat to Sofia. He'd left her the quota, the ledger, the debt, the crew. He'd left her the boat's bones and its memory and its future, including the problems he'd deferred. The hull was one of those problems.

Ray picked up the logbook. He held it for a moment — the weight of it, the texture of the cover, the accumulated mass of thirty years of entries. Then he opened it to the last page. Gus's last entry, written the day before he'd handed the keys to Sofia:

*Season ending. Good year. 18,400 on the cap, no overages. Margaret says the port coupling needs new seals. Denny wants to re-shim the starboard drive bearing. I keep telling them the old girl's got another twenty years in her. I believe it, too. Some days.*

*Turning her over to Sofia. She'll do right by the Resolute. She doesn't know how yet, but she will. The boat will teach her. The boat teaches everyone.*

*The line goes out. The line comes back.*

Ray closed the logbook. He put it down. He picked up his coffee. It was cold. He drank it anyway.

---

Morning came the way morning comes on a ship — not with sunrise but with the watch rotation, the shift from night to day that was marked by the change in the ambient sounds. Denny's engine room noise changed pitch as he ran the morning diagnostic. Margaret's boots in the corridor at 05:30, heading aft for her first sweep. Tomás in the galley, making coffee, the particular sound of the kettle and the clink of mugs.

Sofia was in the wheelhouse when Ray came in. She hadn't slept either — he could tell from the set of her shoulders, the slight inward curve that said she'd been standing at the console for hours, running numbers, working the problem.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning." He poured himself coffee from the pot Tomás had sent up. He stood beside her, not behind her. Beside. The position of a first mate, not a captain. He'd been standing behind her for months, guiding, overseeing, ready to catch. Today he stood beside her.

"I ran the numbers six ways," she said. "There's no version where we pay for the refit from operating revenue. Not in one season. Not in two. The gap is too big."

"I know."

"The coalition financing is the only path I can find. Unless we sell the quota license. And if we sell the quota —"

"Then we're not a boat anymore. We're a hull."

"Right."

Ray drank his coffee. Through the viewport, the rogue field turned in its slow orbit. The amber biota clouds caught the light of the binary stars and scattered it in soft, warm patterns. It was beautiful out here. It was always beautiful out here, in the dark, where the water was new and the ground was uncharted and the ship hummed beneath your feet and the future was a problem for another day.

Except the future was today. The future was the yellow stain on the hull scan and the number 420,000 and the slow, quiet fact that everything you loved eventually needed repair.

"There's another option," Ray said.

Sofia looked at him.

"Ask Gus."

"Gus is —"

"Gus is alive. On Waypoint. In declining health, yes. But his mind is still — he has decades of records. He refit the hull once before, in '08, after the Cassini rush. He found a way to pay for it. He'll remember how."

"He didn't refit the hull. He did a processing bay upgrade."

"The processing bay, the winch assemblies, and the port nacelle. And he did it by finding a shipyard on Cable that owed him a favor. Gus had favors everywhere. People owed him things. Not money — labor. Parts. Time in the dock. He collected debts the way some people collect stamps — quietly, carefully, and with the intention of eventually spending them."

Sofia stared at him. "You want me to go to Gus and ask him for shipyard favors from twenty years ago."

"I want you to go to Gus and ask him for help. He's the one who built this life. He's the one who knows where the bones are buried, where the favors are stored, where the shortcuts are. And —" Ray paused. He looked at his coffee. "He's the one who left you this boat. He left you the good parts and the bad parts. He should help you carry the bad parts."

Sofia was quiet for a long time. She looked out the viewport at the field — at the water, at the slow drift of rock and biota, at the world she'd inherited from a man she'd seen twice a year and loved in the incomplete way you love a distant relative who smells like ozone and tells stories you half-believe.

"I'll comm him when we're back in relay range," she said.

"Good."

"And I'll draft the coalition maintenance proposal. For Mara. Even if the financing route doesn't pan out, the maintenance pooling still saves us twelve to fifteen thousand per season on consumables. That's money we can bank toward the refit."

"Good."

"And Ray —"

"Yeah."

"The next time the hull talks to us, I want to hear it before it becomes an emergency. Margaret should be running hull integrity scans on every EVA. Routine. Every suit-up, every external check. We map the hull systematically, not just when something goes wrong."

"I'll talk to Margaret."

"You don't need to. I'll talk to her. I'm the owner. It's my hull."

Ray looked at her. The same expression she'd seen before — the softening around the eyes, quick and private. The look of a man watching someone become the thing they need to become.

"Yes, it is," he said.

He went to refill his coffee. She stood at the viewport and watched the water drift past — the slow, dark, ancient water that didn't care about hull integrity or financial projections or the slow death of alloy. The water just moved. It had been moving for four billion years. It would keep moving long after the Resolute was gone.

But the Resolute wasn't gone yet. It was here, humming beneath her feet, patched and tired and holding. Seventy-eight years old and still fishing. Still working. Still alive.

Sofia put her hand on the viewport frame. The metal was cool under her fingers. She could feel the vibration of the hull — the distant hum of the reactor, the pulse of the life support, the whisper of the mag-net array. The ship breathing.

"Hold together," she said. To the ship, or to herself, or to Gus, or to no one. "Just hold together."

The ship hummed. It didn't answer. It didn't need to. It was still there.

---

In the equipment bay, Margaret was re-rolling the tool kit from the EVA. Every tool cleaned, checked, returned to its slot. Beside her on the workbench, the hull scanner's data was uploading to her workstation, compiling into a full integrity map that would take hours to render but would give them the most detailed picture of the Resolute's hull since construction.

Lily appeared at the doorframe. She had two mugs. One was tea — green, Margaret's. The other was coffee, black, which Lily had started drinking recently with the grim determination of someone who'd decided that adulthood required sacrifice.

Margaret took the tea without looking up. "Thank you."

Lily stood there. She held her own mug in both hands, the way she held everything — carefully, as if the object might be taken from her.

"You went outside," Lily said.

"Briefly."

"What's it like?"

Margaret looked up from the tool kit. She considered the question with the same precision she applied to everything — not searching for an answer, but deciding which answer to give.

"Cold," she said. "Quiet. You can hear the ship hum through your boots, but everything else is silent. The stars don't move. The field drifts, but slowly, like it's breathing. And the hull —" She paused. "The hull looks different from outside. You can see every repair, every patch, every season. It's all there, written on the skin. Like a record."

"Like a logbook," Lily said.

Margaret looked at her. The girl had said a thing that was exactly right, and she'd said it without effort, and she'd said it in a way that Margaret would remember.

"Yes," Margaret said. "Like a logbook."

They drank their drinks in the equipment bay, surrounded by the suits and the tools and the humming hull, and the scanner rendered its slow map of an aging ship that was still, despite everything, holding.
