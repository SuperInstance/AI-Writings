# The Long Line
## Episode 7: The Quota

---

Lily hadn't come to the galley in fourteen days.

Denny brought her meals on a tray — whatever was left after the crew ate, which wasn't much, because Tomás ate like the food was trying to escape and he had to catch it. Denny would knock on the frame of the storage compartment he'd cleared out and fitted with a rack and a reading light. He'd set the tray down just inside the door. Sometimes he'd say something. "Meat's not bad today." Or: "Margaret found some real coffee in the back of the stores. I swiped you a cup." Most of the time he'd just knock, wait, and leave.

The tray came back empty more often than not. That was something.

Nobody knew what to do with her. That was the honest truth. She'd come aboard at Waypoint three weeks ago — Denny's contact, a friend of a friend of someone on the Low Deck, a girl with no papers and no family and nowhere else to go. Seventeen years old, maybe. She'd stopped counting. Dark hair pulled back tight, thin in the way that spoke not of metabolism but of missed meals, with eyes that were always watching and never reacting. She moved through the ship like a ghost — unseen unless you looked directly at her, silent unless spoken to, and even then she answered in single words that gave nothing back.

She wasn't crew. She wasn't cargo. She was something in between, and the Resolute's crew, who were good at fixing things and terrible at feelings, didn't know what to make of her.

Ray had said little about it. He'd looked at her the day Denny brought her aboard, assessed the situation with the flat, practical gaze he turned on everything, and said: "She eats. She sleeps. She stays out of the way." That was his only directive. He hadn't spoken to her since.

Tomás tried, in the way Tomás tried everything — with warmth and openness and the expectation that friendliness was a universal solvent. He'd sat across from her in the passageway one evening, eating an apple, and talked about nothing for ten minutes. She'd looked through him. He'd wished her goodnight and gotten up. In the galley afterward, he'd looked at Denny with an expression that asked a question Denny didn't have an answer to.

Sofia had tried once. She'd brought the ship's log — the paper one, Gus's old ledger — and shown Lily the entries from seasons past, the careful handwriting recording catches and courses and weather. Sofia thought it might mean something to the girl, seeing the history of the boat, understanding that the Resolute was a vessel with a long memory. Lily had looked at the pages for a long time. Then she'd handed the book back and said, "Thanks." One word. Sofia had taken the ledger and left, and that had been the end of that.

Margaret hadn't tried.

Margaret had watched. She'd noted the same things everyone else had noted — the silence, the withdrawal, the way Lily flinched at loud sounds and went rigid when anyone stood too close. She'd noted them the way she noted everything on the ship: precisely, without comment, and with the absolute conviction that observation preceded action and action preceded results.

She just hadn't decided on the action yet.

---

The morning of the fifteenth day, Margaret found herself standing in front of junction box 7-C with a problem.

7-C was a distribution node on the port side, behind a panel in the cargo passageway. It handled power routing to the aft lighting grid, the number-two atmospheric scrubber, and a handful of minor systems that nobody thought about until they stopped working. Margaret had been meaning to re-cable it for two seasons. The existing wire was original — seventy-eight years old, with insulation that had gone stiff and brittle from decades of thermal cycling. It was safe, technically. It was not going to stay safe.

She'd finally gotten approval from Sofia — not that she needed it, technically, but on the Resolute, you told the owner what you were doing to her ship — and had the new cable spooled and ready. Three hundred meters of shielded conductor, rated for another century. The job would take most of a day. She needed to pull the old cable, fish the new through four bulkhead penetrations, terminate at both ends, and test.

She needed a second pair of hands.

Denny was elbow-deep in the drive coupling, re-shimming a bearing that had developed a harmonic whine. Tomás was on watch. Sofia was in the wheelhouse with Ray, going over the season's numbers — the quota numbers, which had been the source of a growing tension that everyone could feel but nobody had named yet.

Margaret looked down the passageway. Lily was sitting on the deck outside her compartment, knees drawn up, staring at nothing. She did that a lot. Sat in the passageway like it was a room. Margaret suspected the compartment was too small and too quiet and too much like the places the girl had come from, but that was speculation, and Margaret didn't speculate. She observed.

"Hold this," Margaret said.

She was carrying a canvas tool roll and a meter probe. She held out the probe — a slim metal wand with a insulated grip, connected by a curly cord to the meter on her hip.

Lily looked up. Her expression was blank, the way it always was. Guarded behind glass.

She took the probe.

Margaret knelt at the panel, unfastened the four dog latches with practiced turns of her wrist, and swung the cover open. Inside, the old cable ran in a neat bundle through a junction block — terminals clean but wire sheathing yellowed with age. She pulled the tool roll from her shoulder and unrolled it on the deck.

"Now this one." Margaret held out a ratcheting wrench, size stamped on the handle in the cable-standard notation she used for everything. Lily took it. Margaret pointed at the first terminal. "That nut. Turn it counterclockwise until it comes off. Don't drop it."

Lily looked at the terminal. She looked at the wrench. She fitted the wrench onto the nut and turned. It resisted — decades of thermal cycling had seized the threads — and then broke free with a small, satisfying click.

"Good," Margaret said. She didn't say it warmly. She said it the way she said everything: as a statement of fact. The nut came off. That was good. Next.

"Now the next one."

---

Sofia had the numbers spread across the console in the wheelhouse, and the numbers were the problem.

The Conservation Authority's quota management system was elegant in its simplicity. Every licensed harvester had a seasonal cap — a maximum allowable catch, measured in kilograms of organic compound, per zone, per season. The system tracked every gram. The ship's log fed automatically to the Authority's satellites. There was no fudging it. The fence was the fence.

The Resolute's near-line quota for the season was 18,400 kilograms. Through eleven weeks of work, they'd harvested 17,848. That left 552 kilograms on their license — almost exactly three percent of the total cap.

Five hundred and fifty-two kilograms. At current market prices, that was roughly 41,000 chit. Split four ways after expenses, it was the difference between a season that paid for the overhaul Sofia wanted to do next haul-out and one that didn't.

Three percent. The margin between a good season and a great one. The margin between comfort and worry.

Ray sat in the captain's chair, turning his coffee mug in his hands. The coffee was cold. He hadn't drunk it. He was looking at the numbers the way he looked at weather — reading patterns, calculating odds.

"We push," he said.

Sofia looked at him. "We stop."

"We've got three percent on the cap and two weeks of season left. That's a tow a day. Easy."

"A tow a day for fourteen days to clear 552 kilos. That's tight. One heavy tow puts us over."

"One heavy tow puts us over if we misread it. We don't misread tows."

"Denny misread the separator cyclone four weeks ago. We took on three hundred kilos of silicate we couldn't use."

"That's Denny's cyclone, not the catch model. The catch model's been solid all season."

"The catch model is a guess, Ray. It's a well-informed guess, but it's a guess."

Ray set the mug down. "Gus always pushed the quota."

The name landed in the wheelhouse like a stone in still water. Sofia felt the ripples.

"Gus also got fined twice," she said. Her voice was level. Controlled. "Once in season twelve, once in season fifteen. I saw the records."

"And he still made more money than anyone who played it safe."

"And he also left me debt."

The wheelhouse went quiet. Through the viewport, the belt drifted — slow, dark shapes against the starfield, the asteroids of the Near Line turning in their ancient orbits. Somewhere out there, the catch they were arguing about waited in the dark.

Ray picked up his mug. Put it down again. His hands were steady. His face was unreadable.

"What debt," he said. Not a question. A challenge.

"The lien on the quota license. I told you about this. When I took ownership, there was a balance — 22,000 chit against the license. Gus had borrowed against it. The season before he retired."

"I know about the lien."

"Then you know why I don't want to push the cap. One overage fine and I'm paying it out of pocket. One overage fine and the season doesn't cover the lien payment."

Ray was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice had dropped — not in volume, but in temperature. Colder. Flatter.

"Gus ran this boat for thirty years. He pushed the quota every single season. He got fined twice in thirty years. That's a better record than anyone else in the fleet."

"I know."

"So push it."

"I'm not Gus."

Three words. They filled the wheelhouse and then the silence that followed was so complete that Sofia could hear the hum of the console fans, the distant clatter of Denny's work in the engine room, and somewhere deeper in the ship, the faint, rhythmic sound of metal on metal — Margaret, doing something, somewhere.

Ray's jaw tightened. He looked out the viewport. He didn't respond.

Sofia let the silence sit. She understood what she'd said and what it meant. She understood that Ray had heard it the way she'd meant it and also in a way she hadn't intended. She was not just saying she was a different captain. She was saying Gus's way was not her way, and Ray — who had run Gus's boat for Gus for thirty years — was hearing that the way a soldier hears that the war he fought was wrong.

She hadn't meant it like that. But she'd said it, and it was true, and she let it stand.

"I'm going to think about it," Sofia said. "We're not making the call right now."

Ray said nothing.

---

Margaret was on her back under the junction box, pulling old cable. Lily was above her, feeding the new line through the first bulkhead penetration — a circular hole about six centimeters wide, lined with a rubber grommet that was somehow still supple after seventy-eight years.

"Feed it steady," Margaret said. "Don't push. Guide."

Lily's hands were small but steady. She guided the cable through the grommet, feeding it inch by inch, feeling the resistance where the cable curved through the unseen path behind the bulkhead.

"I feel it catching," Lily said.

It was the longest sentence she'd spoken on the Resolute.

"Back it off half a meter. There's a bend about a meter in. The cable wants to follow the existing conduit, but the conduit narrows at the turn. You have to angle it — fifteen, twenty degrees to port. You'll feel it open up."

Lily backed the cable off. She angled it. Fed it in again, slowly, her face set in concentration.

"There," Lily said.

"Good. Keep feeding."

Margaret watched the cable emerge from the far side of the penetration, sliding through the junction block opening like a snake coming home. She grabbed it, set the strain relief, and clamped it.

"Six inches of slack at the terminal. Cut it there."

She held out the cable cutters. Lily took them, measured, and cut. The cut was clean. Margaret looked at it.

"Good cut," she said.

They moved to the second penetration. Margaret explained the route — up through the deckhead, across the cargo passageway overhead, through the frame at station twelve. Lily listened. She didn't nod. She didn't need to. Margaret could tell she understood by the way she picked up the cable and positioned herself at the penetration before being told.

The second run was harder. The path was longer, with two bends, and the existing conduit was shared with an active power line that Margaret had to de-energize at the breaker. She sent Lily to throw the breaker.

"Panel C-4. Breaker seventeen. Pull it down, wait five seconds, come back and tell me the bus light is off."

Lily went. Margaret heard her boots in the passageway — soft, quick, precise. She was back in under a minute.

"Bus light's off."

Margaret checked with her own meter anyway. She always would. But the light was off. The girl had done it right.

They ran the cable. Margaret terminated the near end while Lily held the conductor in position. Margaret stripped insulation with a knife — three quick pulls, clean copper showing — and crimped the lug. She did this four times, four conductors, four lugs. Lily held each one. She didn't fidget. She didn't speak. She held.

At the third bulkhead penetration, the cable snagged on something — old cable clip, probably, left from a long-ago repair and not on any diagram Margaret had. Margaret could feel the resistance through the line, a spongy catch that meant the cable was wrapping around an obstruction rather than passing it.

"Pull it back," Margaret said.

Lily pulled. The cable came, but reluctantly, and when it was free Margaret could see a scuff mark on the insulation where it had rubbed against the edge of something sharp inside the bulkhead.

"There's a burr in there. Edge of a cable clip, maybe. I need to go in from the other side." Margaret pointed down the passageway. "Station twelve, deck plate D. Lift the plate. There's an access panel behind it — the size of a clipboard. Open it and shine a light in. Tell me what you see."

Lily went. Margaret heard the deck plate lift — the girl was stronger than she looked, or at least more determined — and then the access panel open. A pause.

"There's a clip," Lily said. "Metal. The old cable's still in it. And the edge is — yeah, it's sharp. The new cable was hitting it."

"Can you reach it?"

A pause. "Yeah."

"Pull the old clip out. Use the pliers — the ones with the red handles. Wiggle it. It'll come."

Margaret heard the sound of metal on metal, then a grunt of effort, then a small ping as the clip came free.

"Got it," Lily said.

"Good. Feed the cable through now. From your end."

Margaret watched the cable slide through the penetration, smooth this time, unobstructed. It emerged at her end clean and unmarked.

She went to the access panel and looked in. Lily was crouched on the deck, the flashlight in her teeth, feeding cable through the opening with both hands. Her face was smudged with dust. She looked, for the first time since she'd come aboard, like she was doing something she understood.

Margaret said nothing. She went back to the junction box and began the final termination.

---

The quota problem sat in Sofia's mind like a splinter. She'd told Ray she was going to think about it, and she had been, for six hours, and the thinking hadn't produced an answer. It had produced something else — a question she hadn't expected.

The question was: what would a businessperson do?

Not a fisher. A businessperson. Someone who looked at a quota license and saw an asset, not just a permission slip. Someone who understood that value could be moved and traded and structured, not just harvested.

She pulled up the quota regulations on her console. She'd read them before — you had to, to hold a license — but she'd read them the way everyone read them: for the limits. What you could catch. Where you could catch it. What happened if you went over.

She re-read them now for the provisions. What you could do with what you didn't catch.

And there it was. Section 14, Subsection C: "Unused quota allocation may be transferred, in whole or in part, to another licensed harvester within the same zone, subject to registration with the Conservation Authority. Transfers must be executed prior to the close of the season and are irrevocable once registered."

A quota transfer. She could sell her remaining allocation to another boat.

She'd never heard of anyone doing this. She pulled up the fleet registry and looked for boats in the Near Line that were under cap — boats that had room to catch more but hadn't filled their own quota. Three names came up. Two were small operators she didn't recognize. The third was the Cordelia — a sixty-foot harvester out of Tucker's, captained by a woman named Vasquez. Sofia had met her once, at the chandlery on Waypoint. Seemed practical.

She composed a message. She read it twice. She sent it.

Then she went to find Ray.

---

He was in the wheelhouse, exactly where she'd left him. The cold coffee was still in the mug. The starfield was still in the viewport. Ray had the paper logbook open in front of him, but he wasn't writing in it. He was looking at past entries — seasons of past entries, Gus's handwriting and then his own, the careful record of every tow and every catch and every season going back decades.

"Ray."

He looked up.

"I found a way to handle the quota."

He didn't respond. He waited. This was one of the things about Ray that took getting used to — his patience, which was genuine and also a weapon. He could outwait anyone.

"I can sell the remainder. Two percent — call it 360 kilos of my allocation — to another harvester who's under cap. The Cordelia, out of Tucker's. She's at sixty-one percent. She's got room."

Ray's expression didn't change. "Sell quota."

"Transfer, technically. The regulations allow it. Section fourteen, subsection C. I register the transfer with the Authority and she harvests against my allocation. Cash transfer, private arrangement. She gets the catch. I get the payment. No risk."

"How much?"

"Market price for 360 kilos of near-line organic, minus a discount for the convenience. Say twenty-six thousand chit."

"You're leaving sixteen kilos on the table."

"I'm leaving sixteen kilos on the table and collecting twenty-six thousand chit with zero risk of overage. No fines. No penalties. No chance of a heavy tow blowing the cap."

Ray closed the logbook. He set his hand on it — on the decades of records, Gus's records, his records, the history of a boat and a man and a way of doing things.

"Where'd you learn that?" he said. Not angry. Not dismissive. Genuinely asking.

"Loan officer. Prentiss. When I took ownership, I had to sit down with a loan officer at the station credit office to restructure the lien. He walked me through the quota regulations. Said most harvesters don't read past the limits. Said the transfer provision was one of the most underused tools in the whole regulatory framework."

Ray was quiet.

"He said it was an option," Sofia added. "I didn't know it was an option either. But I looked it up. It's real."

The wheelhouse was silent except for the console fans and the distant sounds of the ship — Denny's engine room, the scrubbers, and somewhere aft, the faint clinking of Margaret's tools. And another sound, maybe. Someone else's hands on metal.

Ray looked at Sofia for a long time. She held the look. She didn't flinch and she didn't soften. She stood in the wheelhouse of her ship and looked at her captain and waited.

"You talked to a loan officer," Ray said.

"I talked to a loan officer."

"About the quota."

"About the quota. About the lien. About the license. About what it means to own a harvester and not just fish from one."

Ray picked up his mug. He drank the cold coffee, grimaced, and set it down again.

"I've been fishing this belt for thirty-one years," he said. "Gus fished it for thirty before that. Neither one of us ever sold quota."

"I know."

"I didn't know you could."

"I know that too."

He was quiet again. Then: "Make the call."

"You're okay with it?"

"I said make the call."

---

The Cordelia's captain responded within the hour. Vasquez was direct, businesslike, and clearly as surprised by the offer as Ray had been. She asked three questions: how much, how confirmed, and how fast. Sofia answered all three. They agreed on price. Sofia registered the transfer with the Authority satellite. The confirmation came back in ninety seconds.

Twenty-six thousand chit. No risk. No overage. No fine.

When Sofia told the crew at dinner — such as it was, reconstituted protein and rice and a tube of hot sauce that Denny treated like a religious artifact — Denny whistled.

"I didn't know you could do that."

"Most people don't," Sofia said.

Tomás looked confused. "Wait. So we just... didn't catch the fish, and we got paid for it?"

"We sold the right to catch it. The Cordelia catches it. We get paid either way."

Tomás shook his head slowly. "That's — is that allowed?"

"It's in the regulations," Sofia said. "Section fourteen."

"Huh." Tomás went back to eating. He was still hungry. He was always hungry.

Denny looked at Ray. Ray was eating his rice without expression, methodically, the way he did everything. He didn't look up. He didn't offer commentary. Denny looked back at Sofia and gave a small nod — the kind of nod that meant filed, noted, acknowledged.

Margaret wasn't at the table. She was still aft, finishing the junction box. But Denny had noticed, during the afternoon, that she had company. He'd walked past the cargo passageway twice and seen the girl — Lily — crouched beside Margaret, handing her tools, running cable, doing the work. He hadn't said anything. But he'd looked.

---

Margaret terminated the last conductor at 19:40 ship time. She tested the junction — continuity, insulation resistance, load capacity. All within spec. She buttoned up the panel, dogged the latches, and wiped her hands on her trousers.

Lily was sitting against the bulkhead, the tool roll in her lap, re-rolling it the way Margaret had shown her. Cables coiled properly. Tools in size order. Margaret watched her do it and said nothing about the fact that the girl had learned the order on the first pass.

"Seven-C is done," Margaret said. "Tomorrow, nine-A. Same process, harder run. Longer pulls."

She looked at Lily. Lily looked up from the tool roll.

"Be here at 06:00," Margaret said.

Lily nodded. One small, certain nod. Not the blank stare. Not the guarded glass. Something behind her eyes that Margaret recognized because she'd seen it in the mirror, fifty years ago, standing in a shipyard on Cable, holding a wrench for the first time and understanding that her hands knew what to do with it even if the rest of her didn't.

Lily said: "I'll be here."

Three words. The most she'd spoken in a day since she'd come aboard.

Margaret nodded once and walked forward toward the galley. She didn't look back. She didn't need to.

---

Late that night, Sofia sat in the wheelhouse alone. The ship was quiet — Ray had gone to bed, Denny had retreated to his engine room cot, Tomás was asleep in his rack. She could hear, faintly, the sound of Margaret's boots heading aft, the engineer making her nightly round before turning in.

Sofia opened her journal. She uncapped the pen. She sat for a while, the nib resting on the page, before she wrote anything.

What she wrote was:

*Sold 2% of quota to the Cordelia. 26,000 chit. No risk. Ray didn't fight me on it, but he didn't like learning something new from me. I think the hard part wasn't the decision. The hard part was proving I could make a decision he wouldn't have made, and having it work.*

*Prentiss said something when I met with him. He said: "Most boat owners are fishermen who happen to own a boat. You need to be an owner who happens to fish." I didn't understand it then. I think I'm starting to.*

*The Resolute has been in this family for three generations. Gus ran it his way. I'm going to run it mine. Ray is going to have to live with that, or he's going to leave. I don't know which one scares me more.*

She closed the journal. She capped the pen. She sat in the captain's chair — her chair, legally, practically, increasingly — and watched the belt drift past the viewport, the slow dark shapes turning in their ancient orbits, the fish that were hers by right and someone else's by trade, and the stars beyond them that didn't care about any of it.

Somewhere aft, in a storage compartment fitted with a rack and a reading light, Lily was asleep. On her deck, beside her bunk, Margaret's tool roll lay where the girl had set it after re-rolling it — neat, ordered, ready for morning.

The line goes out. The line comes back.
