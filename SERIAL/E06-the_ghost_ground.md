# The Long Line
## Episode 6: The Ghost Ground

---

The set was perfect. Sofia knew it the way she was learning to know most things on the Resolute — not from instinct yet, not from the deep marrow knowledge that Ray carried, but from the numbers, from the steady confident pull of the mag-field as it dragged through the organic dense band at the thermal break.

Fourteen-three on the density readout. Holding steady. Two hours into an eighteen-hour soak, and everything about it looked like the kind of set you remember at the end of a season — the one that kept you going through the bad sets and the empty runs and the nights when you wondered why you were out here at all.

After three weeks of mediocre returns on the Far Line, scraping together enough organics to cover fuel and provisions, they'd found a pocket of dense biota at the intersection of two thermal currents that nobody else had charted. Ray had called it from a reading on the contour map — a fold in the temperature gradient that looked, to Sofia, like nothing at all, and to Ray like a promise.

"There," he'd said twelve hours ago, tapping the screen with a thick finger. "That fold. See how it curls? Organics pool in the curl. Gus used to call them pockets. Like a coat pocket — stuff falls in and collects."

So they'd set on the fold, and Ray had been right, and now the numbers were climbing.

Ray came into the wheelhouse carrying a plate with two sandwiches on it. He set it on the console ledge without ceremony. Eat, the plate said. He'd learned that Sofia forgot to eat when things were going well, and that telling her to eat didn't work, and that putting food next to her worked every time.

She picked up a sandwich. Peanut protein on station bread. Ray sat in the captain's chair and pulled up the catch log, entering figures with the slow deliberate pecking of a man who had never learned to type and didn't care who knew it.

"Soak time remaining?" he asked.

"Sixteen hours."

He grunted. That was satisfaction. Sixteen hours on a fourteen-three ground, the catch would be substantial. The buyers at Waypoint would pay well. This was the set that turned a marginal season into a good one.

Ray leaned back and crossed his arms. The chair creaked under him — the specific complaint of the port-side bolster that Margaret had been threatening to replace for three years. Something in his face settled. Not happiness. Satisfaction. The look of a man watching something turn out the way he'd said it would.

Then the distress beacon hit.

---

It came in on the emergency band — a flat, pulsing tone that cut through the wheelhouse like a blade. Tomás's hand went to the comm panel before the second pulse finished.

"Distress signal. Long range. Bearing one-one-seven mark four."

Sofia set her sandwich down. "Source?"

The signal resolved on the display — a transponder code, a vessel registry, a position fix.

**F/V PROMETHEUS — REGISTRY WV-3892**
**DISTRESS: DRIVE FAILURE — LIFE SUPPORT ON BACKUP**
**CREW: THREE**
**POSITION: 11.7 MARK 4 — RANGE 4.2 HOURS STANDARD BURN**
**STATUS: IMMEDIATE ASSISTANCE REQUESTED**

Sofia read it twice. Ray hadn't moved. He was sitting in the captain's chair with his arms still crossed, staring at the display, and his face had gone somewhere else. Not blank — gone. Like a house with the lights on but nobody answering the door.

"Ray?"

"That's my old boat," he said.

The wheelhouse went quiet. Tomás looked from Ray to Sofia and back.

"The Prometheus," Ray said. He said it the way you say the name of someone you haven't seen in a long time and expected never to see again. Carefully. Like the word might break if he gripped it too hard. "I captained her before the Resolute. Sold her fifteen years ago."

Sofia looked at the position fix. Four hours at standard burn. Three crew on backup life support. Three days from any station. The Resolute was the closest vessel by half a day's transit.

She looked at the display. The warm greens and golds of the thermal break. Sixteen hours remaining on the best set of the season.

You don't hesitate for this. Three people on backup life support — you go. There wasn't a version of this where you didn't go. But she felt the cost of it land in her chest, because the numbers on that display were money. Real money. The kind that meant the Resolute stayed solvent this season, that the crew got paid, that Margaret could order the parts she'd been patching around for six months.

"Tomás. Start pulling gear."

"Pulling gear. Aye."

"Ray. Set course to the Prometheus's position. Best speed."

Ray stood up from the chair. He moved like a man walking through something heavier than air. "Best speed. Aye." His voice was steady. His hands were steady on the console. Whatever was happening behind his face, it wasn't going to affect his hands. Thirty years of deep-space work had made sure of that.

Sofia opened the shipwide channel. "All hands. Distress call from the F/V Prometheus. Drive failure, three crew, life support on backup. We are pulling gear and diverting. Margaret, Denny — wheelhouse. Sofia out."

She closed the readout. There was nothing to gain from looking at it.

---

The gear came in over forty minutes. The catch was good — even abbreviated, even a third of what it would have been, the hold gained enough organics to matter. It just wasn't enough.

Margaret appeared at the deck hatch with a tablet, already reading. "Prometheus. Singleton-class, same vintage as us. Forty-six-foot equivalent." She looked up. "Drive is a Kessler-Fold Mark IV."

Denny's head came up. "Mark IV. I trained on one of those at the yard on Cable. Could strip one in my sleep."

"Good. You're going to need to. Margaret turned to Sofia. "I'll board too. If life support is degraded, I want to assess the electrical systems myself."

Sofia nodded. "Ray will want to board too."

Nobody argued with that.

---

The Resolute burned hard for four hours and twelve minutes. Ray didn't talk during the transit. He stood at the console, hands on the rail, watching the distance count down. Sofia wanted to ask about the Prometheus — about his years on her, about Bess, about what it meant to see a distress beacon from a ship that used to be yours.

She didn't ask. Some things you carry until you set them down yourself.

The Prometheus appeared on the visual display at fifty kilometers — a dark shape against the starfield, running lights flickering in the irregular pattern of emergency power. She was smaller than the Resolute. Narrower. An older silhouette. The hull plating was patchy with repairs — different alloys where panels had been replaced, poorly matched, the whole ship looking like a quilt made from whatever fabric was lying around.

Ray looked at her for a long time. His face gave away nothing. But Sofia saw his hands tighten on the rail, just slightly, and then release.

"Take us in," he said. "Twenty meters. Parallel approach."

---

Four figures cycled through the Prometheus's airlock. The inner door opened onto a corridor lit by emergency strips — dim, amber, casting everything in the color of old photographs. The air was thin and cold and tasted recycled. The overhead was low enough that Ray had to duck where a conduit run crossed the ceiling — one that hadn't been there when he left, because his head found it with a solid thunk.

He didn't react. He just ducked lower and kept moving.

The Prometheus's captain met them at the corridor junction. Young — thirty, maybe. Tall, thin, dark-skinned, with the drawn look of someone who'd been breathing thin air for eighteen hours. He wore a shipsuit that was too large, sleeves rolled at the wrists.

"Captain Okoro. Emmanuel Okoro. Thank you for responding."

Ray shook his hand. "Ray Hutchins. This is Sofia Reyes, owner of the Resolute. Margaret Kowalski, engineer. Denny Park, mechanic."

Behind Okoro, two deckhands hovered — young men, barely older than Tomás, both pale in the emergency lighting. They looked at the boarding party the way drowning men look at a lifeboat and then looked away, because gratitude and embarrassment are hard to feel at the same time.

"Life support status?" Margaret asked, already moving past pleasantries.

"Scrubbers holding. Maybe forty hours. Drive went down two days ago — main coupling failure."

"Show me the engine room," Margaret said. She was already walking.

Okoro blinked. "This way."

Sofia turned to Ray. He was standing in the corridor, looking not at anything in particular but at everything — the walls, the overhead, the amber light. Reading the ship the way he read the Resolute, except this ship wasn't his anymore.

"I want to see her," Ray said. Not to Sofia. To himself.

"Show me," Sofia said.

---

He walked the Prometheus like a man walking through a house he'd lived in and left. His hands moved at his sides, touching things — a bulkhead, a railing, a junction box — the way you touch furniture in the dark, confirming what's there and what isn't.

The galley was first. On the Resolute, the galley was the social heart — the table, the coffee maker, the place where the crew existed as people instead of functions. On the Prometheus, the galley was a storage closet. The table was gone. The counter where a stove had been was now a rack of spare parts — filters, couplings, sealant cartridges. A cargo net had been strung across the sitting area, holding boxes of provisions.

Ray stopped in the doorway. His hand rested on the doorframe — the same spot, Sofia realized, where his hand would naturally rest if he'd been coming through this door for years, ducking slightly because the frame was low. His body remembered the ship even if the ship didn't remember him.

"She had a stove here," Ray said. Not to Sofia. To the room. "Real one. Not a printer. Bess found it at a market on Tucker's. Paid forty chit for it. She used to cook — actual meals, when we were on the grounds. Said the galley was the only room on any ship that felt like a kitchen, and a kitchen is the only room that feels like home."

Sofia said nothing.

"It's gone," Ray said. Flatly. Factually. But his hand stayed on the doorframe.

The bunk room. Four racks, aftermarket, cheap, bolted to the bulkhead with hardware that didn't match. The original bunks — built into the hull, integrated, permanent — had been removed. Here, they were furniture. Temporary. The room of a boat that expected turnover.

Ray didn't linger. He closed his eyes for a moment as he passed through, and moved on.

The wheelhouse was last.

Two seats, a console, the forward display. That much was the same. But the console had been replaced — ripped out and swapped for a unit that didn't match the surrounding panelwork, leaving gaps where the old mounting brackets had been. The porthole displays were gone. In their place, a single flat screen mounted to the overhead, running a navigation program that was adequate and ugly and impersonal.

Ray stood behind the console. His hands rested on the edge — the new console, the replacement, the one that wasn't his. He stood the way he stood on the Resolute, feet set, weight balanced. But the console didn't fit him. The height was wrong. The angle was wrong. Someone else's console, someone else's ship.

"The old console had dual readouts," he said. "Parallel displays. Bess used to sit in the second seat and run the catch log. She'd call out numbers and I'd write them in the paper log." He paused. "Her handwriting was better than mine. She used to say that if the electronic log ever failed, the paper log would be the only proof we had of fifteen years of fishing, and it would be legible because she'd written it."

He looked at the second seat — Bess's seat. It was a different seat now. Cheaper vinyl, no armrests, bolted to the deck. But it was in the same place. The same spot, looking out at the same view.

"Ray." Sofia stood behind him. "We should get to work."

He took his hands off the console. He looked at the wheelhouse one more time — a slow, level survey, the way you look at a room you're leaving for the last time. Then he walked out.

---

The engine room was hot and close and smelled like burned polymer. Denny was already inside, shirt off, grease to the elbows. Margaret was at the electrical panel, running diagnostics.

"Secondary power bus failed when the coupling went," Margaret said without looking up. "That's what knocked life support to backup. I can restore main in about twenty minutes. The coupling is Denny's problem."

"The coupling is Denny's specialty," Denny corrected from the floor. He was on his back under the drive housing, a flashlight clamped in his teeth, his hands working by touch. "Mark IV Kessler-Fold. I haven't had my hands on one of these since Cable, but—"

His hands moved. Something clicked.

"—she hasn't changed," he finished quietly. Almost tenderly. "Same layout. Same failure points. Same everything." He slid out and sat up. "The main coupling is shredded. Whoever replaced it last used a generic part — not OEM. Tolerance is wrong. It's been eating itself for months."

"Can you fix it?" Okoro asked from the doorway. He'd been standing there watching, arms crossed, his face caught between hope and the defensive pride of a man watching someone else fix his ship.

"Get you running. Transit speed only. You need a proper OEM coupling at the next station." Denny was already reaching for his tool kit. "Three, maybe four hours."

Okoro's jaw tightened. His ship, his engine room, his right to be there. But the math was simple. His ship was broken. These people could fix it. He stepped back from the doorway.

---

Sofia found Ray in the cargo hold. He was sitting on a crate, not doing anything. His hands hung between his knees. He'd taken off his EVA helmet and set it on the deck, and in the amber emergency light his face looked old and far away.

She sat down next to him. Not close — a crate's width between them.

The cargo hold was the only space on the Prometheus that looked maintained. Clean, organized, cargo secured properly. Okoro was a working captain — whatever his deficiencies as a maintainer, he ran a ship that earned its keep.

Ray spoke first.

"I built the console myself," he said. "The original one. Dual readouts, parallel displays, custom mounting. Took me three months. Bess helped — she did the wiring. She was better with her hands than I was." He stopped. Breathed. "She said a console is where a captain lives. You don't buy that from a catalog."

"He replaced it."

"He gutted it. The console, the galley, the bunks. Everything personal, everything that made the ship—" Ray shook his head. "I sold her as a working vessel. Ready to fish. Everything in place. He bought her and stripped her down to a shell."

"It's his ship," Sofia said. Not a correction. A reminder.

"I know it's his ship." Something flickered in his voice — not anger at Sofia. Something deeper. The anger of watching a place you loved become unrecognizable and knowing you have no right to say anything about it because you let it go. You sold it. You walked away. Whatever happened after is not your business, even if it breaks your heart.

He was quiet for a long time.

"It's like walking through a graveyard," he said finally. "The body's there. It's the same body. But the person is gone."

Sofia didn't answer. She sat with him in the amber light and the thin cold air and the hum of Margaret's restored life support beginning to push warmth back into the ship.

---

Denny said four hours. He did it in three and forty.

He worked on the coupling with his hands and his memory and the feel of the deck plate under his back. The Mark IV's layout was stamped into his hands — every bolt, every access panel, every tolerance. His fingers found the failure points the way a blind man reads a familiar page.

The generic coupling came out in pieces. Denny replaced it with a part from the Resolute's spares — not exact, but close enough. He shimmed it, aligned it, torqued it by feel.

"Try it," he said.

Okoro hit the drive start from the wheelhouse. The Prometheus shuddered. The drive spooled up — rough, uneven, but holding. The vibration settled into something almost healthy.

Denny listened from the engine room floor, eyes closed. His head tilted slightly, the way a doctor listens for a heartbeat.

"She'll run," he said. "Transit speed only. Don't run gear on this drive until it's properly fixed." He opened his eyes and looked at Okoro. "Get to Waypoint. Get an OEM coupling."

Okoro nodded. Relief, gratitude, and the residual discomfort of a man who'd been rescued and instructed on his own ship in the same breath. "I understand. Thank you."

Denny stood up and put his hand flat on the drive housing, just for a moment, the way you'd put your hand on someone's shoulder.

"She's the same underneath," he said to no one. "They change everything on top, but underneath — same bones."

---

The transfer line back to the Resolute took four minutes. Ray went last. He stood in the Prometheus's airlock before crossing, looking back down the corridor — the amber-into-white lighting, the narrow passageway, the storage closet that had been a galley, the distance to the wheelhouse where a console he'd built with his wife had been ripped out and replaced.

He didn't say goodbye to the ship. He turned and crossed and cycled through the Resolute's airlock and pulled off his helmet and stood in his own wheelhouse, on his own deck plate, under his own lights.

"Get us underway," he said to Tomás. "Back to the grounds."

Sofia watched him. He walked to the captain's chair and sat down and entered the coordinates for the thermal break. His hands were steady. His face was the same flat, tired, competent mask it always was.

"Ray."

He looked at her.

"We'll go back and re-set. The pocket should still be there."

"Maybe. Current may have shifted."

"We go back and try again."

Something moved in his face. Not a smile — Ray didn't smile easily — but an acknowledgment. The particular softening around the eyes that meant she'd said the right thing.

"Yeah," he said. "We go back and try again."

He leaned back. The port-side bolster creaked. He reached for his cold coffee — the cup he'd left on the console six hours ago, before the distress beacon, before the Prometheus — and drank from it without checking if it was still good.

It wasn't. He drank it anyway.

---

Margaret found him later in the galley. It was 0300. The Resolute was in transit, running quiet. Tomás had the watch. Sofia had gone to her rack. Denny was in the engine room, because Denny was always in the engine room.

Ray was sitting at the galley table with his paper logbook open. He wasn't writing. He was looking at a page near the back, where the handwriting changed — a round, confident script, slightly slanted, the letters formed with the care of someone who valued legibility because she'd grown up being told it mattered.

Bess's handwriting. A page from the Prometheus years, preserved in the Resolute's pages like a fossil in stone.

Margaret sat down across from him with a cup of tea. They'd known each other twenty years. They could sit in silence without it meaning anything, or without it meaning too much.

"She looked tired," Margaret said. "The Prometheus."

"She's old."

"So are we."

"She's not old like us. She's old like a building that's been renovated by people who don't live in it."

Margaret sipped her tea. "Okoro's running a business. He's not preserving a museum."

"I know."

"He bought a tool. He uses it like a tool. That's what boats are to most people."

"I know."

"Do you?"

Ray looked at her. Margaret looked back. In twenty years, they'd had this conversation — or conversations like it — maybe a dozen times. Margaret came down on the side of function. She loved the Resolute because it worked, because she kept it working, because the working was the love. Ray came down on the side of meaning. He loved ships the way he loved people — for what they'd been through together, for the memories stored in their bones.

Neither of them was wrong. Neither was entirely right.

"He was scared," Ray said. "Okoro. Under all the— He was scared. His crew was scared. Three people breathing recycled air for two days, waiting for someone to show up."

"Someone showed up."

"Yeah." Ray closed the logbook. Carefully, the way you close something that matters. "Someone showed up."

He finished his coffee and stood up. He paused at the galley door — the Resolute's galley, with its table and its coffee maker and its dents and scratches and the small worn spot on the bench where Gus used to sit.

"Margaret."

"Hmm."

"Thank you."

She raised an eyebrow. Ray didn't say thank you often.

"For what?"

He didn't answer. He walked aft toward the bunk room. Margaret sat in the galley alone, listening to the Resolute's sounds — the reactor hum, the life support whisper, the tick of hull panels, the creak of a ship that was old and known and home.

She finished her tea. She washed the cup. She labeled it — *M.K., galley, clean* — in three languages, because that's what she did, and she went back to work.

---

The Resolute reached the thermal break at 0700. The fold was still there. The current had shifted slightly — Sofia picked it up on the contour map, a subtle change that Ray adjusted for with a two-degree correction. They re-set the gear. The net array went out.

Fourteen-one on the density readout. Not fourteen-three. The pocket had thinned in their absence. But it was still there. Still good. Still the best set of the season, even diminished.

"Fourteen-one," Tomás reported. "Holding."

Sofia looked at Ray. He was in the captain's chair, coffee in hand, watching the display with the patient attention of a man who had done this thousands of times.

"Soak time?" she asked.

"Eighteen hours. We lost time. Let her soak."

The net array settled into the thermal break, mag-field humming, collecting organic compounds from the cold dark. The line went out. The line would come back.

In her rack, Sofia was writing in her journal. *Today we answered a distress call from Ray's old ship. He went aboard and walked through his own past and came back and didn't say a word about it. The set is back in the water. I am learning that the cost of doing the right thing is always real and you always pay it and you never regret it. I am learning that some people carry their grief like cargo — lashed down, secured, hidden below deck — and it doesn't go away. It just sits there, in the hold, and you learn to sail with it.*

She closed the journal. She clicked off Gus's reading light.

The Resolute held station on the thermal break, engines idling, net array soaking. The stars turned slowly outside the hull. The ship hummed its old, known hum. The coffee maker clicked off automatically at 0730, as it always did.

The line goes out. The line comes back. That's the whole life.
