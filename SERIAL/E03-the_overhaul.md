# The Long Line
## Episode 3: The Overhaul

---

Drydock smelled like ozone and old grease.

The Resolute sat in her cradle like a beached leviathan, hull plating stripped to bare alloy in long skeletal patches where the inspection teams had peeled back the anti-corrosion skin. Power conduits were disconnected. The reactor core was cold for the first time in fourteen months. Without her systems humming, the ship was just a shape — a big metal fossil in a rented berth, reminding everyone who looked at her that ships were alive only when they ran.

Sofia stood at the observation rail on the third-level gantry, arms folded on the cold railing, watching a pair of yard workers crawl through the Resolute's open port nacelle with handheld scanners. Their lights moved inside the housing like fireflies in a ribcage.

Seventy-two hours. That was the mandated overhaul window for vessels in the long-line fleet. Seventy-two hours where the ship went dark and the crew went dirtside and the bureaucracy of safety insisted that every weld, every seal, every micrometer of hull wear be catalogued and certified.

She'd been through dozens of these. They never got easier. A ship in drydock was a ship not earning. Every hour of those seventy-two was dead weight on the ledger.

"You going to stand up here all three days?"

Ray's voice came from behind her. She didn't turn.

"Thinking about it."

"Uh-huh." He stepped up beside her, rested his own arms on the rail. He was wearing dirtside clothes — a canvas jacket, actual boots with laces instead of mag-soles. He looked like a stranger out of the ship's context. They all did. "Yard chief says the stardart array checks out. No rewiring needed. That's good news."

"Yeah."

"Sofia."

"I heard you. It's good news. I heard you."

They stood together in silence. Below, one of the yard workers laughed at something, the sound echoing up through the dock's acoustics. The Resolute's hull rang faintly where he'd knocked a tool against it.

"Rented a room in town," Ray said. "Place on Harrow Street. You?"

"The same building, I think. Second floor."

"Good. Close to the yard."

"Mm."

He waited a beat, then: "Dinner? There's a noodle place the yard workers go to. Real food, not printer stock."

"Maybe tomorrow."

"Tonight, Sofia. You haven't eaten since we landed."

She exhaled through her nose. "Tonight, then."

---

The room on Harrow Street was exactly what you'd expect for dockside transient housing in a port town: four walls, a bed, a sink, a window that looked out on the street. The mattress was too soft. The pillow was too thick. The room was too warm and too quiet and too still.

Sofia lay on the bed and stared at the ceiling and tried not to think about the numbers.

She'd met with the loan officer that morning, before the gantry. A woman named Prentiss at First Colonial Mutual, office two blocks from the dockyard gate. Prentiss had a clean desk and a sympathetic expression and the particular kind of professionalism that meant she'd already made her decision before Sofia sat down.

The terms were clear. Gus had taken the loan six years ago — a substantial refit on the Resolute's processing bay, new winch assemblies, rebuilt stardart array. He'd done it right before the market contracted. The interest rate was fixed at a level that had seemed reasonable in a different economic decade. The monthly burden consumed nearly everything the Resolute earned, and some months it consumed more than everything.

Sofia had inherited the ship. She'd also inherited the debt. Both came with the same weight.

"We could restructure," Prentiss had said, tapping the edge of her tablet. She'd turned it so Sofia could see the amortization schedule. "Extend the term. Lower the monthly. It would mean paying more over the life of the loan, significantly more, but it would give you breathing room."

"How much breathing room?"

"Enough to keep current. Probably."

"Probably."

"The fleet market is volatile. You know that better than I do. I can show you models, but —"

"But you can't promise the numbers hold."

Prentiss had the decency not to flinch. "No. I can't."

Sofia had signed the restructuring paperwork because the alternative was acknowledging out loud what the numbers meant. The Resolute wasn't sustainable at this rate. She was one bad season from default. One bad season from losing the ship.

Gus had nearly lost her in '08. Sofia hadn't known that until after he died, when she'd found the old notices in his files — the warning letters, the grace periods, the repayment plans. He'd clawed it back. A miracle run in the Q3 season that year, full holds three cycles running, had pulled him clear.

Sofia wondered if he'd spent the rest of his life waiting for the next bad season. She suspected he had.

She lay on the bed and stared at the ceiling and didn't go to dinner with Ray.

---

Denny couldn't sleep.

The room was fine. The bed was fine. The temperature was fine. None of it mattered because the room was *quiet*, and Denny's body didn't know what to do with quiet.

He'd slept aboard ships since he was nineteen. Twelve years of reactor noise, of ventilation systems cycling, of hull metal contracting and expanding as thermal gradients shifted. His body had calibrated itself to that constant vibration the way a city kid's body calibrated to traffic — it wasn't noise, it was confirmation that everything was working. That the world was still running. That the systems were alive and he was alive inside them.

In quiet, his ears searched. Filled in phantom frequencies. He'd lie half-awake, straining for the reactor hum that wasn't there, and his hindbrain would decide that silence meant something was wrong. That the ship was dead. That he was dead inside a dead ship.

So on the second night he got up at 0200, dressed, and walked the eight blocks back to the dockyard.

The yard was different at night. The big lights were dimmed to working minimums. The inspection teams were gone. The gantries were empty. The Resolute sat in her cradle, dark and open, panels removed, cables hanging loose, and Denny felt his chest unclench for the first time since they'd landed.

He climbed the port access ladder — they'd left the external ladders in place for the inspection crews — and slipped through the cargo hatch. The ship smelled like solvent and cold metal. No climate control running. No air processors. Just the faint chemical bite of inspection fluid and the ghost of ten thousand harvests.

He went to the engine room.

It was strange to see it dark. He knew this room the way a pianist knows a keyboard — every valve, every junction, every conduit run mapped in muscle memory. He navigated it blind. Found his usual spot, the maintenance nook between the secondary coolant housing and the portside junction box where there was just enough floor space for a person to sit with their back against the wall. He sat. Drew his knees up.

The ship was silent. But it was the silence of something sleeping, not something dead. He could feel it — the mass of the hull, the weight of the structure, the potential energy stored in cold reactors and sealed systems. The Resolute was a thing that *could* hum. That would hum again in forty-some hours. The possibility was enough.

He slept.

He did the same thing on the third night, and the third night there was a blanket folded on the floor in his nook. Fleece. Dockside issue. It smelled like detergent, not like the ship. Someone had brought it from the rooms on Harrow Street.

Denny knew who. He unfolded it and covered himself and slept better than he had in months.

---

Margaret had always been the one who noticed things.

Not because she was nosy — she was, a little, but that wasn't the reason. It was because managing the Resolute's engineering systems meant monitoring dozens of inputs simultaneously, cross-referencing data, flagging anomalies. After enough years, the pattern-matching didn't turn off when she left the ship. She read people the way she read diagnostic feeds: baseline, variance, deviation.

She noticed Denny's absence the first morning. He came to the yard review looking like he'd slept in his clothes, which he had, but more tellingly he looked *rested*. Denny on insufficient sleep had a specific set of tells — a tremor in his left hand, a tendency to blink too slowly, a faint rasp when he spoke that wasn't congestion but fatigue. None of those tells were present. Whatever he'd done last night, it had worked.

She noticed his boots the second morning. Dockside grit on the toes, the specific oxide-red dust of the yard's service gantries. He'd been back to the ship.

On the third night she walked to the ship herself, around 2300, on the pretense of checking the inspection progress. The cargo hatch was closed but not dogged. She climbed down to the engine room and listened. In the maintenance nook, Denny was asleep, curled on his side, jacket wadded under his head.

She went back to the Harrow Street rooms and got the blanket from her own bed. Brought it back. Folded it on the floor beside him. Left.

The next morning she said nothing about it. Neither did he. He did, however, fold the blanket neatly and leave it on the engine room workbench, and she picked it up and took it back to her room, and that was that.

---

She also noticed the money.

This was harder to miss, in fairness. Margaret managed the Resolute's operating accounts. She'd been doing it since Gus was alive — he'd been terrible with numbers, and she'd been the one to point out that someone needed to track expenses or they'd have no idea whether they were making money. Gus had given her access with the cheerful disinterest of a man who considered bookkeeping a form of suffering.

The loan from First Colonial Mutual auto-debited on the first of every month. It had done so for six years. The amount was embedded in Margaret's mental model of their finances the way the reactor's baseline hum was embedded in Denny's nervous system.

On the morning of the second day in drydock, the loan balance showed zero.

Not restructured. Not adjusted. Zero.

Margaret pulled the transaction history. A wire transfer from an external account, timestamped 0741, covering the entire remaining principal and the early-termination fee. The originating institution was listed as Port Retirement Services — a pension and retirement fund administrator. The account holder was listed as R. Ortega.

Ray.

Margaret sat on her bed in the room on Harrow Street and stared at her tablet and felt something she couldn't immediately name. She breathed through it.

Ray Ortega had been on the Resolute longer than anyone except Gus. He'd joined as a deckhand twenty-two years ago, worked his way up, become the man Gus trusted most. When Sofia took command, Ray had been the continuity — the institutional memory, the steady hand, the voice that said *your father did it this way* without ever making it sound like a criticism.

He was sixty-four years old. He should have retired three years ago. The fact that he hadn't — that he was still working deck rotations on a mid-tier harvester — was a choice, or a financial necessity, or both. Margaret didn't know which. She knew that his retirement fund existed because he'd mentioned it once, offhand, years ago, in the context of explaining why he didn't worry about the future.

He was worrying about someone else's future now.

Margaret closed the transaction history. She opened the account settings and confirmed that the auto-debit had been cancelled. The loan was gone. The Resolute was — for the first time in six years — unencumbered.

She thought about telling Sofia. She thought about it for a long time, sitting on the bed in the too-warm room with the too-thick pillow. Then she closed the tablet and put it away.

It wasn't her secret to tell. And Sofia, who carried everything on her own shoulders by default, who had inherited a ship and a debt and a crew and a way of life — Sofia would feel the weight of that gift whether she knew who'd given it or not. The ship was free. That was what mattered.

Ray would tell her himself, eventually. Or he wouldn't. Either way, the Resolute would fly.

---

Tomás's phone buzzed on the first evening.

He was sitting on the steps outside the Harrow Street building, eating a ration bar, watching dock traffic. The message was from a recruiter named Vasquez at Meridian Harvest Co., a major fleet operator. Tomás had met him once, briefly, at a crew exchange event. Vasquez had been collecting contacts the way recruiters do — shaking hands, buying drinks, filing names.

The offer was straightforward. Meridian was building out a new class of harvester — larger, more automated, longer range. They wanted experienced deckhands. The pay was roughly double what the Resolute could offer. Quarters were private, not shared. The ships had full medical, including dental. Contract terms were flexible — six-month rotations with two-month dirtside intervals.

Tomás read it twice. Then he read it a third time, slowly, the way you read something that you already know the content of because the first time was too fast to believe.

He didn't respond that night. He didn't respond the next morning.

He sat on the steps and ate his ration bar and watched dock traffic and turned it over.

The thing about the Resolute was that it was small. Five crew. He knew everyone's moods, everyone's rhythms, the way Denny hummed when he ran diagnostics, the way Sofia stood at the observation rail when she was thinking. He knew Margaret's coffee schedule. He knew that Ray's left knee ached when the temperature dropped and that he'd never mention it.

On a Meridian ship he'd be crew number twenty-six or thirty, a name on a rotation board, a body in a berth. Double the pay. Private quarters. Dental.

He sat on the steps and watched the dock traffic and didn't respond.

---

The noodle place was called Oduya's, and it was good. Not printer stock — actual stock, actual noodles, actual protein. Ray ordered for the table without asking what anyone wanted, and he got it right for all of them, which was either a testament to how well he knew the crew or an indictment of how predictable they'd become.

They sat at a corner table, five chairs, no tablecloth. Sofia. Ray. Margaret. Denny. Tomás.

Eating was strange without the ship. Without the constant low-grade awareness that at any moment a system might need attention, a haul might come in, a course correction might be called. There was nothing to do but eat, and talk, and be present with each other in a way that the ship's rhythm never quite allowed.

Denny was quiet. He was always quiet, but this was a different kind — the quiet of someone who'd solved a problem privately and didn't feel the need to report it. Margaret was also quiet, but her quiet had a shape to it, like she was holding several objects in her hands and choosing which to set down. Sofia was tired in a way that showed in her eyes but not her posture. She sat straight. She always sat straight.

Ray told a story about a drydock on Callisto Station, years ago, where the yard crew had accidentally sealed a dock worker inside a nacelle housing and didn't find him until the post-overlaunch diagnostics. "He was fine," Ray said. "He'd brought a lunch. Ate it. Took a nap. Said it was the quietest shift he'd ever worked."

Denny almost smiled.

Tomás asked questions about the Callisto yards — technical specs, hull classifications, things that a deckhand interested in bigger ships might ask. He asked them casually, in the tone of someone making conversation rather than considering options. Margaret noticed. She didn't say anything.

Sofia noticed too.

She watched Tomás the way she watched haul readouts — not staring, just tracking, just keeping the data point in her peripheral vision. He was engaged. He was present. He was also slightly too interested in the specs of ships that weren't theirs.

She didn't ask.

After dinner, walking back to Harrow Street, Ray fell into step beside her. The streetlights were sodium-vapor — old tech, warm light, deep shadows. They walked through pools of orange.

"The kid's looking," Ray said.

"He's not a kid. He's twenty-six."

"The kid's looking."

"I know."

"You going to talk to him?"

"No."

Ray nodded, as if this confirmed something. They walked another block. "He'll stay."

"You think so?"

"Yeah. He'll stay."

"You seem sure."

"I am sure. He's like his uncle. May he rest."

"Gus wasn't his uncle."

"Near enough."

They reached the building. Ray held the door. Sofia went up to her room and lay on the bed and didn't sleep and didn't think about the phone she'd seen Tomás checking in the noodle shop, angled away from the table, screen dimmed. She didn't think about how he'd smiled at the specs. She didn't think about private quarters and dental plans and two-month dirtside intervals.

She thought about the ceiling.

---

The third night was the worst.

The inspection reports came in at 1800. The Resolute was sound. Hull wear within tolerance. Reactor seals intact. Stardart array functional. Processing bay cleared for re-certification. The yard chief signed off with a handshake and a bill that Sofia paid from the operating account without flinching, or at least without visible flinching.

Departure was set for 0900. Seventeen hours.

The crew scattered. Denny went back to the ship to start the reactor warm-up sequence — a twelve-hour process that he'd begun in secret the night before, running pre-ignition diagnostics in the dark, because he couldn't help himself. Margaret went to oversee the systems restart. Ray went to settle the Harrow Street accounts and collect their personal effects.

Sofia went to First Colonial Mutual.

The office was closed, but Prentiss was there, working late. She looked up when Sofia knocked on the glass partition. Her expression shifted through several states before settling on something professionally neutral.

"Captain Herrera. I was going to call you tomorrow."

"I saw the account."

"Ah."

"The loan is paid."

"Yes. This morning, the —"

"Don't tell me who."

Prentiss closed her mouth. Opened it again. "Captain, I'm obligated to —"

"I know who it was. I don't need you to say it. I need you to tell me if the payoff included the termination fees."

"It did. The account is closed. There are no outstanding obligations."

Sofia nodded. She stood on the other side of the glass partition with her hands at her sides. She was not going to cry in a loan office. She was not going to cry in a loan office.

"Thank you, Ms. Prentiss."

"Captain. For what it's worth — your father's loan was one of the reasons I took this position. He was kind. When my daughter was sick, he —" She stopped. "Never mind. You didn't come here for that."

"I came here to say thank you. For the work."

"Take care of that ship, Captain."

"Every day."

---

She found Ray on the aft gantry, sitting on a cable spool, looking at the Resolute's hull. The yard lights were bright now — full working illumination, the kind that left no shadows, that made everything flat and honest. Every dent, every patch, every weld scar on the Resolute's skin was visible.

"Sixty-four years old," Sofia said.

Ray looked at her. He didn't pretend to misunderstand. "I wasn't using it."

"You were using it. You were going to use it in three years when you retired."

"Maybe."

"Ray."

"Sofia."

She sat beside him on the cable spool. The hull stretched out below them, scarred and patched and sound. Twenty years of harvests written on its surface in a language only people who'd worked it could read. There was the dent from the Q2 storm in '19. There was the patch from the micrometeorite hit that Denny had welded at 0300 while the rest of them slept. There was the smooth section near the bow where Gus had insisted on an aesthetic refit that served no practical purpose because he wanted his ship to look good.

"Why?" Sofia asked.

"You know why."

"Tell me anyway."

Ray was quiet for a long time. The yard lights hummed. Somewhere, a compressor cycled.

"Because I was on the Resolute when she launched. Because Gus was my friend. Because his daughter is my captain and she's good at it and she shouldn't have to spend her career paying for his mistakes." He paused. "And because I'm sixty-four and I don't want a retirement. I want this ship."

"You can't stay on deck forever."

"Watch me."

"That's not —"

"Sofia. I have maybe five good years left on a working rotation. Maybe three before my knee goes. I want those years on the Resolute. Not on a beach. Not in a portside apartment. Here. Let me have this."

She didn't answer. She didn't need to. He knew.

They sat on the cable spool and looked at the ship and didn't speak again until Margaret's voice came over the gantry comm, tinny and practical: "Reactor warm-up at forty percent. Denny wants to know if anyone's going to tell him when to start the stardart spin-up, or if he should just guess."

Ray snorted. Sofia closed her eyes for one moment — just one, a single breath of stillness — and stood up.

"Tell him to start it when the reactor hits sixty."

---

Tomás was on the dock when the Resolute powered up.

He'd spent the third day walking. The port town was small — a service economy built around the dockyards, nothing that would interest a tourist. Bars, equipment shops, boarding houses, the places that cropped up wherever workers gathered and needed to eat and sleep and spend. He walked through all of it with his hands in his pockets and the phone heavy in his jacket.

He'd responded to Vasquez at noon. A short message: *Thank you for the offer. I'm currently on contract. I'll keep your contact.*

Professional. Noncommittal. The kind of message that kept a door open without walking through it.

The truth was simpler than the message. The truth was that he'd stood on the Resolute's deck for the last thirty months and he'd learned every creak and groan of her hull, the way she listed slightly to port under full load, the way the processing bay smelled when the lines were running hot. He knew Denny's hum, Margaret's coffee, Ray's knee, Sofia's spine.

He'd looked at the Meridian offer the way you look at a road you could have taken — with curiosity, with a small honest pang of *what if*, and with the clear knowledge that you were not going to take it.

The ship hummed.

He felt it through the dock plating before he heard it — that low, resonant vibration that meant the reactor was cycling up, that the systems were coming alive, that the Resolute was waking. His body responded before his mind did. His shoulders dropped. His breathing slowed. The vibration found the frequency his nervous system had been missing for three days and filled it like water filling a glass.

He walked up the ramp.

Denny was in the engine room, running the spin-up sequence, and he looked up when Tomás passed through. Their eyes met. Denny nodded — the nod of a man who'd spent three nights sleeping on the engine room floor and understood exactly what it meant to need this ship. Tomás nodded back.

Margaret was on the bridge, running systems checks. She glanced at him as he stowed his bag in the deckhand locker. Her expression was neutral. Knowing. She'd probably seen his message log, or deduced the offer from his behavior at dinner, or simply *noticed*, the way she always noticed. She didn't say anything.

Ray was on the aft gantry, disconnecting the shore cables. He looked older in the dockyard lights than he did on the ship. Tired. His knee was bothering him; Tomás could tell from the way he shifted his weight. But his hands were sure on the cable connectors, and his face had the particular calm of a man doing exactly what he intended to do.

Sofia was in the captain's chair.

Not the chair itself — she was standing behind it, one hand on the headrest, looking at the forward display as it came online. The Resolute's systems were waking around her, one by one, like lights turning on in a house. Climate control. Navigation. Comms. The processing bay hummed. The stardart array began its initialization cycle.

She looked up when Tomás crossed the threshold onto the bridge. Their eyes met.

She didn't say: *Did you consider leaving?*

She didn't say: *Are you staying?*

She said: "Station-keeping is nominal. Take the port watch."

"Aye, Captain."

He took his station. The display glowed in front of him. The ship hummed beneath his feet. The vibration was back — constant, low, alive.

No one asked.

---

At 0901, the Resolute cleared drydock.

The yard crew released the cradle clamps. The hull settled into its own mass as the mag-drives engaged. Sofia's hand was steady on the throttle. Ray stood behind her, to the left, the position he'd stood in for twenty-two years, the position Gus had stood in before him.

The ship moved. Slowly at first, then with purpose. The dock fell away behind them. The port town shrank — Oduya's, Harrow Street, First Colonial Mutual, the noodle place where Ray had ordered without asking. All of it diminishing in the aft display, getting smaller, becoming memory.

Margaret ran the departure checklist in a calm, steady voice. Denny monitored the reactor, which was humming the way a reactor should hum, which was to say imperceptibly unless you were Denny, in which case it was a symphony in B-flat that meant *everything is fine*.

Tomás stood his watch.

The Resolute cleared the port boundary. The nav system calculated the first harvest zone — seventeen hours out, deep water, the kind of run they'd done a hundred times. Sofia set the course. Ray confirmed it. The display showed open water in every direction.

On the aft gantry camera, still visible on the secondary display, the dockyard was a thin line of light against the dark.

No one looked back.

*End of Episode 3: "The Overhaul"*
