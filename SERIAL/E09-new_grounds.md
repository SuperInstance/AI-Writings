# The Long Line
## Episode 9: New Grounds

---

The season started with a number Sofia didn't like: 14,200.

That was the Conservation Authority's revised quota allocation for the Near Line — down 4,200 kilograms from last season. The reduction had come down three weeks ago, delivered in a data packet that Sofia opened at her kitchen table on Waypoint, alone, at six in the morning, with a cup of coffee going cold next to her.

The reasons were sound. The biota survey showed stress on the Near Line's organic density. Three seasons of heavy harvesting had drawn down the regeneration rate faster than the models predicted. The Authority's recommendation was two to three seasons of reduced pressure. Sofia understood the science. She agreed with the science. She also understood that 14,200 kilograms at current market prices would not cover the Resolute's operating costs, Margaret's repair list, and the lien payment on the quota license.

The math was the math. It didn't care about her feelings.

She'd spent the off-season doing what owners do — paperwork, maintenance oversight, contract review, meetings with the quota office about the lien, meetings with buyers about pricing, meetings with the chandlery about credit terms. She'd also spent it reading. Gus's old logs — forty years of them, paper and electronic, the raw record of a life lived on the belt. She read them the way a daughter reads a dead father's letters, looking for something she couldn't name.

She found it on page 219 of the 2147 season log — a handwritten note in Gus's cramped, precise script, squeezed between a coolant pressure reading and a crude drawing of a thermal contour:

*Rogue field at 11.4/47.2. Drifted in from outer system on the current cycle. Did not fish — wrong season, wrong approach angle. Density readings promising. Next window approx 2 cycles. Worth a look.*

Two orbital cycles. That was twenty-six months. The note was written twenty-seven months ago.

Sofia had circled the entry, then sat at her kitchen table and stared at it for a long time while the coffee went completely cold and the station rotated outside her window and the off-season quiet of her apartment pressed in on her like a question she couldn't answer.

---

She brought it to the crew on a Tuesday. Wardroom meeting, the galley table, everyone present — Ray with his coffee, Margaret with her tea, Denny with his feet on the table until Margaret removed them, Tomás with a sandwich he'd assembled from whatever was left in the galley stores before departure. Lily sat at the far end of the table, quiet, her hands folded on the surface. She'd signed on for the season — properly this time, crew contract, wage, rack assignment. Margaret had insisted. Sofia had agreed. Nobody mentioned the circumstances of Lily's arrival. She was crew now. That was that.

Sofia laid it out. The quota cut. The numbers. The gap between what they were licensed to catch on the Near Line and what they needed to catch to make the season work.

"Fourteen-two doesn't cover it," she said. "Not with fuel costs up eight percent and the lien payment due at mid-season."

"How bad," Denny asked.

"Bad enough that I've been looking at the Far Line supplement. Class B supplemental license, open zone. It adds 6,000 kilograms to the cap if we work an uncharted field."

"Uncharted," Margaret said. The word sat on the table like a tool nobody had picked up yet.

"Uncharted means no established data," Sofia continued. "No catch history, no density surveys, no pattern baseline. The Authority opens it up specifically because they want reconnaissance. We'd be logging every set — catch composition, density, thermal profile, biota mapping. The data goes to the Authority as part of the license agreement. In return, we get the supplemental quota."

"Six thousand kilos on an uncharted field," Ray said. He was turning his coffee mug in his hands, slowly, the way he did when he was thinking. "Where."

Sofia pulled up the coordinates on the galley display. "11.4 by 47.2. Rogue field. Orbital drift brought it in from the outer system on the last cycle. It's in the open corridor between the Near and Far Lines — technically Class B open zone."

Ray set his mug down. He looked at the coordinates. Something crossed his face that Sofia couldn't read — not surprise, not concern, but something older and deeper. Recognition, maybe. The look of a man seeing a weather pattern he hadn't encountered in years and remembering how to read it.

"That's Gus's handwriting," Ray said. Not a question.

"In the '47 log. He noted the field but didn't fish it. Wrong season, wrong angle."

"He told me about that," Ray said. "Found it on a transit back from the Far Line. Ran a passive density scan and got excited, but the approach was bad — the field was edge-on to the thermal current, so the biota was thin on the surface. He'd have needed to come at it from below the orbital plane, and that's a four-day detour he didn't have time for." Ray paused. "He said he'd come back to it."

"He didn't."

"No. He got sick the next season. Never made it back out."

The galley was quiet. Through the hull, Sofia could hear the distant hum of the station's docking clamps, the subtle vibration of a ship tied to a place that wasn't its home.

"I'm proposing we go," Sofia said. "The supplemental license gives us 6,000 kilograms on top of the 14,200. That's 20,200 for the season. It works."

"On an uncharted field," Margaret repeated. She said it differently this time — not as an objection but as a fact to be weighed.

"On an uncharted field," Sofia confirmed. "No baseline data. No Gus's patterns. No accumulated history. We'd be fishing blind."

Ray cleared his throat. "Not blind. Gus did a passive scan. He logged density readings. It's not nothing."

"It's one pass, Ray. One scan from seven years ago. The field has drifted. The orbital position has changed. The thermal context is different. Whatever Gus saw then might not be there now, and whatever's there now, Gus didn't see."

"So we go look," Ray said. Simply. The way he said everything that mattered — as if the simplicity of the statement was its own argument.

Sofia looked around the table. Denny was nodding — slowly, the way he nodded when the engineering made sense. Margaret's expression was neutral, which meant she was reserving judgment, which meant she'd already decided but wanted to hear more. Tomás was watching Ray with the careful attention he gave to anything Ray said about the old days. Lily's face was, as always, unreadable.

"Margaret?"

"The ship can handle it. I'll want a full systems check before we leave station. Three days."

"Denny?"

"Drive's solid. I re-shimmed the coupling last haul-out. Fuel budget?"

Sofia pulled up the numbers. "Twenty-one days at standard operations. Fourteen to the field, seven on-site. Tight but manageable."

"Tomás?"

He looked up from his sandwich, slightly startled to be asked. "I've never fished new ground before."

"Nobody's fished this ground before," Sofia said. "That's the point."

He grinned. It was the grin of a twenty-three-year-old who still thought that the unknown was more exciting than terrifying. Sofia wanted to tell him that the unknown was both. She didn't. He'd learn.

"Let's do it," Tomás said.

"Lily?"

She looked at Sofia. Her expression didn't change, but her eyes focused — the subtle shift from passive to present that Sofia had learned to recognize as Lily's version of engagement.

"Okay," Lily said.

"Ray?"

Ray picked up his coffee mug. He took a sip. He set it down.

"I've been waiting thirty years for someone to suggest fishing without the map," he said. "Let's go."

---

The Resolute left Waypoint on a Thursday morning, running dark and quiet through the station traffic, past the cargo haulers and the refinery tenders and the Coast Guard cutter sitting at the edge of the lane with its running lights blinking in the universal pattern of *I'm watching*. Sofia took them out, hands on the console, running the departure sequence that had become as automatic as breathing. Ray sat in the second seat — his seat now, the one that used to be Bess's on the Prometheus, a lifetime ago on a different ship.

Tomás was on watch. Denny was in the engine room, settling the drive into cruise mode, the low throb of the propulsion system settling into the frequency that meant *everything's fine, stop worrying*. Margaret was in her workspace, running the pre-transit systems check with the methodical thoroughness of a woman who had been doing this for longer than most people had been alive.

Lily was in the cargo hold, securing stores. She did this without being asked. She did everything without being asked now. Margaret had trained her, in that wordless way Margaret trained everything — by demonstration, by proximity, by the expectation that you would watch and learn and do it right the first time because she wasn't going to tell you twice. Lily had watched. Lily had learned. Lily did it right.

Fourteen days to the field. Fourteen days of transit through increasingly familiar space giving way to increasingly empty space, the navigation beacons thinning out, the comm relays spacing wider apart, the traffic dropping from occasional to rare to none. The Resolute moved through the dark like a creature returning to a place it had never been but somehow knew.

Sofia used the transit time to prepare. She pulled every record she could find on the rogue field — Gus's scan data, the Authority's orbital tracking, the sparse astronomical surveys that covered the corridor. She built a model. The model was thin. The model was mostly empty space with a few data points floating in it like stars in a nearly blank sky.

She stared at the model and felt the absence of Gus's patterns like a missing tooth. On the Near Line, she had decades of catch data — every set logged, every thermal break mapped, every pocket and fold and density gradient catalogued by Gus's careful hand over thirty years of fishing the same waters. She could look at a contour map of the Near Line and see Gus's knowledge layered over it like a transparent skin — where to set, when to pull, how long to soak, what the current was doing, what the biota density would be at each depth and temperature. It wasn't a guess. It was a conversation with the accumulated dead, the voice of forty years of experience speaking through the data.

Out here, the data didn't speak. It mumbled. It shrugged. It said *maybe* and *approximately* and *I don't know*.

Ray, by contrast, came alive.

She noticed it on the third day. He was in the wheelhouse earlier than usual — before his watch, before his coffee, standing at the console in the blue-gray light of the transit display, studying the approach vector with an expression Sofia had never seen on his face. Not exactly happiness. Something more private. The look of a man who had been carrying a tool he never got to use and had finally found a job that required it.

"You're up early," Sofia said.

"Couldn't sleep." He didn't look away from the display. "The approach angle matters. Gus came at it from the Near Line side — edge-on to the thermal current. We're coming from below the orbital plane. Different perspective. The biota structure will present differently."

"How differently?"

"Different enough." He traced the contour lines with his finger — the old way, the physical way, the way he'd done it before touchscreens and electronic charts. "On the Near Line, the thermal breaks run horizontal. You learn to read them east-west. Gus's whole data set is built on that assumption — horizontal breaks, horizontal setting. This field came in from the outer system. Its thermal structure will be oriented to its entry vector, not to the system's plane. Could be vertical. Could be angled. Could be something we don't have a word for."

"And you can read that?"

He looked at her. In the console light, his face was all planes and shadows, the geography of a man who had spent more of his life in dark spaces than in light ones.

"I can read water, Sofia. I've been reading water since before you were born. The system reads data. I read water." He paused, as if considering whether to say the next thing. "This is how we used to do it. Me and Gus. Before the catch models got good enough to do the thinking for you. We'd come out to new ground and we'd look at it and we'd *fish*. Not data-mine. Fish."

"There's a reason the catch models exist, Ray."

"There is. They're good tools. They made us more efficient, more consistent, more predictable. They also made us lazy. A model tells you where the fish should be. Your eyes tell you where the fish are. Out here, we don't have a model. We have eyes."

He said it without judgment. Without nostalgia, even — or at least without the kind of nostalgia that pretends the past was better. He was simply naming a difference. The old way and the new way. The body and the database.

Sofia felt the difference in her chest like a weight. On the Near Line, she was Captain Reyes, heir to Gus's operation, running a boat with thirty years of accumulated knowledge backing every decision. Out here, she was Sofia Reyes, a woman on a boat in unfamiliar water, and the accumulated knowledge was useless.

"I don't know how to do that," she said. Quietly. The kind of quiet that costs something.

"I know," Ray said. "That's why we're here."

---

They arrived on the fourteenth day. The rogue field occupied a volume of space roughly forty kilometers across — a loose aggregation of asteroids, organic debris, and diffuse biota clouds, drifting in a slow, tumbling orbit that would carry it back out of the corridor in eleven months. It was, in astronomical terms, temporary. In fishing terms, it was a season.

Sofia's first impression was that it looked wrong.

Not wrong the way a mechanical failure looks wrong — alarming, urgent, demanding response. Wrong the way a familiar word spelled differently looks wrong. You know what it should be. This isn't that. The color of the biota clouds was different — deeper, more amber than the blue-green she was used to on the Near Line. The debris field had a different character — more porous, less dense, the asteroids spaced wider apart with organic matter filling the gaps like mortar between bricks. The thermal readings on the passive scan were complex and layered, nothing like the clean horizontal breaks of the Near Line.

She stared at the display and felt the absence of Gus's patterns like a hand reaching for a railing that wasn't there.

"First set," she said. "We'll run a survey pattern across the field at standard resolution. Three hours, get the density map, identify the highest-concentration zones, and set on the best one."

Standard procedure. The Near Line procedure. Survey, identify, set. It was how Gus had always done it — methodical, data-driven, letting the accumulated patterns guide the decision. It had worked for thirty years. It would work here.

She thought it would work here.

Ray said nothing. He sat in his seat and watched the display and drank his coffee and said nothing.

---

The survey took three hours. The density map assembled itself on the console — a three-dimensional rendering of the field's biota distribution, color-coded by concentration. It was, as Sofia had feared, complex. The biota didn't follow the horizontal layering of the Near Line. Instead, it formed spirals — tight, dense coils of organic matter wrapped around six of the larger asteroids, connected by thinner streams of debris that curved between them like threads in a web.

"Never seen anything like this," Tomás said from the watch station. His voice held the particular wonder of a young man encountering something genuinely new. "The density is highest here, here, and here." He pointed at three of the spiral formations. "Classic pocket structure, except it's vertical, not horizontal."

"So we set on the densest pocket," Sofia said. "Number one. Standard approach — tow across the spiral axis, let the net sweep the dense band."

"Standard approach," Ray repeated. He said it the way Margaret said *that's not how this works* — flatly, with a precision that was almost gentle but was actually an objection.

"You see a problem?"

"I see a spiral. You're setting across it. A spiral is a curved structure — you tow across it, your net cuts one cross-section. One slice of the curve. You'll get whatever's in that slice and miss the rest."

"The density map shows the highest concentration in the cross-section. That's where the fish are."

"The density map shows where the fish are right now. A spiral is a dynamic structure — it rotates, it shifts. The biota is moving through the spiral, not sitting in it. By the time we set and soak, the dense band will have rotated away from our tow line."

Sofia looked at the map. She looked at Ray. She felt the weight of thirty years of Gus's data pressing on her judgment — *this is how we do it, this is how it works, survey and identify and set* — and she felt the weight of Ray's gaze, patient and steady, waiting for her to see what he saw.

She didn't see it.

"The density map says set on pocket one. I'm going with the density map."

Ray nodded. Once. He picked up his coffee and said nothing else.

---

The first set was wrong.

Not disastrously wrong. Not dangerously wrong. Just wrong, in the quiet, expensive way that things go wrong on fishing boats — a miscalculation that costs time and fuel and the crew's confidence, and that you can't take back.

They set the net across the spiral axis of pocket one. Eighteen-hour soak, standard for a deep biota harvest. The mag-net array deployed clean, the tow line held, the catch log started ticking as the net gathered. For the first six hours, the numbers looked good. Then they dropped. Then they flatlined. Then they went to zero.

Sofia watched the catch log and felt her stomach tighten.

"Net's empty," Tomás said from the deck station. He didn't sound surprised. He sounded careful — the careful tone of a crew member who has watched the captain make a call and watched the call go wrong and is now navigating the social geography of that failure without stepping on landmines.

"Pull it," Sofia said.

The net came up light. Sixty-two kilograms — mostly silicate residue, trace organics, the debris that fills any biota cloud but isn't what you're there for. The sort of catch that costs more in processing time than it earns at market.

Denny hauled the array in, ran the separator cyclone, and looked at the results with the flat expression of a man reading a bank statement he'd expected to be bad and wasn't disappointed.

"Well," he said. "That happened."

Sofia stood on the deck, looking at the catch tray. Sixty-two kilos of nothing. She could feel the crew's eyes on her — not judging, not yet, just watching. Seeing how she handled it.

"Ray called it," she said. She said it to the crew, but she said it for herself. "The spiral rotated. The dense band moved out of our tow line before the soak was done. I misread the structure."

Nobody argued. Nobody needed to.

"We're not on the Near Line," she said. "The patterns I know don't apply. I need to stop fishing the Near Line in my head."

She went to the wheelhouse. Ray followed her. He didn't say anything. He waited.

"Teach me," she said.

---

He taught her the way old fishermen teach — not with lectures or diagrams or curricula, but by showing. By doing. By putting her in front of the thing and letting her see it.

"Watch the debris," he said. They were standing at the viewport — the physical viewport, not the display. Real space, real light, real matter drifting past the thick composite pane. "Watch how it moves. Not where it is — where it's going."

Sofia watched. At first, she saw what she'd always seen: debris. Rocks, dust, organic particulate, the slow drift of material that fills the space between asteroids. Unremarkable. Background noise.

"Keep watching. Don't look at the display. Look at the water."

*Water.* He called it water. Always had. The belt, the grounds, the fields — to Ray, it was water. An ocean without an ocean's name.

She watched. Five minutes. Ten. And then something shifted in her perception, the way a magic eye image resolves when you stop trying to see it — not a snap but a softening, a refocusing, and suddenly the debris wasn't random. It was moving in patterns. The particles were drifting in curves, not lines, spiraling inward toward the asteroid at the center of the formation, and the spirals were alive — rotating slowly, drawing material in from the edges and concentrating it at the core.

"The spiral's a feeder," she said.

"Now you're seeing it."

"The biota doesn't sit in the spiral. It flows through it. The spiral concentrates it. The densest material is at the—"

"At the bottom of the drain. Where the spiral tightens. That's where you set. Not across it — along it. You follow the spiral down to its root and you set your net there, where everything converges."

Sofia looked at the viewport, then at the display, then back at the viewport. Two different languages describing the same thing. The display showed density — a snapshot, a moment. The viewport showed motion — the living reality of matter in movement, drawn by gravity and thermal dynamics into structures that a static model couldn't capture.

"Why didn't the density map show this?"

"It did. You were reading it for concentration, not for flow. The map tells you what's there. Your eyes tell you what's happening." Ray paused. "Gus was the best I ever saw at reading density maps. But even Gus would come up here and look at the water first. Data second. Always water first."

"He didn't teach me that."

"He taught you everything else. I figured you'd learn this part the way everyone learns it — by getting it wrong and having someone show you the difference." He put his hand on her shoulder. Brief. Almost impersonal. The weight of it was not.

"Second set," he said. "Your call."

---

The second set was better. Not perfect — Sofia was reading water the way a student reads a language she's only just begun to learn, haltingly, uncertainly, missing nuances that a fluent reader would catch without thinking. But she followed the spiral down, identified the convergence point at the root of the formation, and set the net along the flow line rather than across it.

The catch was 340 kilograms. Better. Not great — the biota at this convergence point was a different composition than the Near Line organics, heavier in long-chain compounds, lighter in the cellular matrices that fetched the best prices at market. But it was real. It was there. The net had found what the density map couldn't show her.

"Three-forty on an unworked pocket," Denny said, reviewing the separator output. "That's not bad for a blind set on a new field. The composition's different, though. Higher industrial fraction."

"Industrial fraction?" Tomás asked.

"Long-chain organics. Pharmaceuticals, industrial feedstock. Different market than the Near Line cellular product." Denny ran the numbers on his handset. "Actually... the industrial fraction trades at a premium when the supply is tight. And I don't think anyone's supplying right now, because nobody's fishing this field."

Sofia looked at the composition breakdown. He was right. The organics from this field weren't the same as the Near Line's food-stock product. They were heavier. More complex. The kind of compound that pharmaceutical processors paid well for because it was hard to synthesize and expensive to harvest from established grounds.

Gus's patterns were built on Near Line biota — cellular food-stock, the bread and butter of the organic harvest industry. His data wouldn't have led them here because his data was tuned to a different product, a different market, a different kind of fishing. The rogue field wasn't just a new location. It was a different kind of ground entirely.

"Set three," Sofia said. "I want to work the next spiral. Same approach — read the flow, set along the convergence."

"You're getting the hang of it," Ray said. He was standing at the viewport, watching the debris drift, and for the first time since Sofia had known him, he looked like a man who was exactly where he belonged.

---

They worked the field for five days. Five days of mistakes, corrections, and slow, hard learning. Sofia misread a thermal current on day three and set into a density inversion that clogged the separator cyclone — two hours of Denny elbow-deep in the housing, clearing biota paste from the intake vanes, his commentary on the experience delivered in a continuous monologue that Margaret later described as "creative in its profanity if not its engineering." On day four, Tomás lost a tow line when the mag-net array snagged on an asteroid outcrop and the safety release failed to trigger — the line snapped with a sound like a gunshot, and four hundred chit worth of cable drifted into the dark while Tomás stared after it with the specific expression of a man calculating how many shifts of extra watch it would take to pay for his mistake.

"It happens," Ray told him. "Cable breaks. You log it, you note the position, and next time you check the seafloor before you set." He meant asteroid floor. He always said seafloor. "Go help Denny re-rig."

On day five, Sofia found the column.

It was an accident — or rather, it was the kind of accident that happens when you're learning to read and your eye catches something that your training wouldn't have let you see. She was at the viewport, watching the debris patterns around the fourth spiral formation, when she noticed something odd about the asteroid at its center. The debris wasn't just spiraling around it — it was rising. A thin, continuous column of organic particulate was streaming upward from a fissure in the asteroid's surface, like smoke from a chimney, feeding the spiral from below.

"Ray. Look at this."

He looked. He was quiet for a long time.

"Thermal vent," he said. "The asteroid's got an active core — radioactive decay, maybe, or a chemical reaction in the mineral strata. It's pumping heat and minerals up through the fissure. The organics are feeding on it."

"A bloom."

"A bloom." He said it with the particular reverence that fishermen reserve for the word — the reverence for an event that is rare, unpredictable, and enormously productive. A bloom was when the conditions aligned — thermal, chemical, biological — and the biota exploded in density and richness. Blooms were legendary. Blooms were what fishermen told stories about. Gus had found three blooms in thirty years on the Near Line. Each one had made a season.

"This is why the spiral structure exists," Sofia said, thinking aloud. "The thermal column creates an upwelling. The upwelling feeds the biota. The biota concentrates in the spiral. The spiral isn't just a shape — it's a living system. The whole field is built on this."

"Find another one," Ray said.

---

They found two more thermal columns over the next six hours — one on the fifth asteroid, one on the second. Each one was feeding a spiral formation. Each one was producing organics of a density and complexity that Sofia had never seen on the Near Line.

She set on the second column's bloom. The catch was 1,100 kilograms in a twelve-hour soak — the largest single tow of her career. The composition was extraordinary: a mix of pharmaceutical-grade long-chain compounds and a cellular fraction so dense that the separator cyclone struggled to classify it. Denny had to override the sorting algorithm manually, standing at the control panel with grease on his forearms and a look on his face that Sofia could only describe as joy.

"This is the good stuff," he said. "This is the really good stuff. The cellular fraction alone — I mean, look at this density reading. You don't see this on the Near Line. You don't see this anywhere."

"No," Sofia agreed. "You don't. Because nobody's looked here."

She stood on the deck, holding a sample container of the harvest — a translucent cylinder filled with amber-gold biota that caught the overhead light and glowed. It was beautiful. It was worth more per kilogram than anything Gus had ever pulled from the Near Line.

And Gus had never found it. Not because he couldn't have — Gus could read water as well as anyone — but because his patterns were Near Line patterns. His data was Near Line data. His thirty years of accumulated wisdom was a map of a different ocean. This ocean, this ground, this bloom — it was hers. She'd found it by learning to read water instead of data, by standing at a viewport and watching debris drift, by making a sixty-two-kilogram mistake and letting Ray show her the difference between a map and the territory.

That night, in her rack, she pulled out Gus's old ledger. The paper was soft with age, the pages filled with thirty years of entries in Gus's careful hand — coordinates, catch totals, thermal readings, the small notes in the margin that were sometimes technical and sometimes personal and sometimes both. *Good set on the fold. Bess's birthday — forgot to call. Made it up with chocolate from Tucker's. Density dropping on the near bank. Worried.*

Sofia opened to the next blank page. She picked up Gus's pen — the same pen, the old one, the one that had lived in the ledger's spine for four decades. She wrote the date. She wrote the coordinates of the rogue field. She wrote:

*First season on new ground. Three thermal columns feeding spiral formations. Bloom density off the charts — pharmaceutical-grade long-chain + high-density cellular. Composition unlike Near Line. Denny says premium market. Ray says the water reads different out here. He's right. It does.*

*Found the columns by watching debris flow from the viewport. Misread the spiral on first set — 62 kg, mostly silicate. Ray showed me the difference between reading density and reading motion. Second set: 340 kg. Third set on the bloom: 1,100 kg. Learning to see.*

*This is not Gus's ground. This is not Gus's data. This is new ground, and I am fishing it, and the line goes out and the line comes back and the water gives up its secrets to whoever is patient enough to watch.*

She closed the ledger. She turned off Gus's reading light. In the dark, the Resolute hummed around her — the low vibration of the reactor, the whisper of life support, the sounds of a ship at work in unfamiliar water — and she lay still and listened, and the sounds were the same sounds they always were, and they were enough.

---

Morning. The galley. Coffee. Ray was already there, sitting at the table with his mug and his paper logbook, writing in the slow deliberate hand that never used the electronic system if he could help it. He looked up when Sofia came in.

"Set four," he said.

"Set four," she agreed.

"Same approach?"

"No." She poured her coffee. She sat down across from him. "I want to try the third asteroid. The one we haven't worked yet. The debris pattern looked different — tighter spiral, higher velocity. I think the thermal column is stronger."

"You read that from the viewport?"

"I read it from the water."

Ray looked at her. For a moment, his face did something she'd never seen — a softening around the eyes that lasted less than a second and that he would have denied if she'd mentioned it. Not pride, exactly. Something more private than pride. The look of a man watching someone learn the thing he'd spent his life trying to teach.

"Good," he said. He picked up his coffee. He drank it. He set it down.

"Let's go fishing," he said.

Outside the galley, through the hull, through the vacuum and the dark, the rogue field turned slowly in its temporary orbit — a cluster of rock and dust and organic clouds and thermal columns that nobody had charted and nobody had fished and nobody had named. It was theirs. It was new ground.

The line goes out. The line comes back. That's the whole life.
