# The Long Line
## Episode 12: The Coalition

---

Waypoint Station smelled like recirculated air and fried food, the way it always did, the way every station in every port in the system always did. Sofia had been breathing that smell for three days now — since the Resolute docked at the end of the rogue field run, holds at sixty-two percent, crew tired, hull patched and holding — and she still hadn't gotten used to it. You could take the girl off the boat, but the boat stayed in your lungs.

The meeting was on C-deck, in a conference room that belonged to the Waypoint Harbor Master's office. It was the kind of room that tried not to be institutional and failed: gray walls, a long table, chairs that were comfortable enough for a two-hour meeting and agonizing after three. A viewport along the port side showed the station's docking ring, where ships sat in their berths like birds on a wire.

Sofia had arrived early. She'd brought the hull scan data, the coalition proposal she'd been drafting for three weeks, and a cup of coffee that was already cold. She sat at the head of the table — not because she wanted to, but because the Harbor Master's office had arranged the room with a head seat, and sitting anywhere else would have looked like she was hiding.

Margaret sat to her left. She had a tablet, a cup of tea, and an expression that said *I am here to observe and will speak only when something technical requires correction.* She'd worn her station clothes — clean shipsuit, hair brushed, boots polished. She looked, Sofia thought, like a diplomat who happened to also be an engineer.

Denny had stayed with the ship. Someone always stayed with the ship. Ray had stayed too — his choice, delivered in the same flat tone he used for all decisions he considered non-negotiable.

"The coalition is your play, Sofia. I don't belong in that room."

"You have thirty-one years of —"

"I have thirty-one years of reading water. That room is going to be about politics. I'm the wrong tool." He'd paused. "You'll know when to call me. You'll know when to call Margaret. Trust that."

She did trust it. She trusted it the way she trusted the hull — not because it was perfect, but because it had held so far.

The others arrived in ones and twos.

---

Captain Mara Vasquez came through the door like she owned it. She was wearing the same clean shipsuit from the rogue field rendezvous, but today there was something harder in her bearing — the set of her shoulders, the angle of her jaw. Political bearing. She was here to win.

"Captain Reyes." She extended her hand. Her grip was the same — firm, brief, professional. "Thank you for hosting."

"The Resolute's home port. Seemed right."

Mara sat at the table across from Sofia. She didn't bring a tablet. She didn't bring notes. She brought herself, and that was enough.

Behind her came Petra Wallach — Captain of the Calypso, the last to join the coalition. Petra was a big woman, broad and tall, with the physical presence of someone who'd spent decades hauling equipment in low-g. Her face was weathered — deep lines around her eyes and mouth, the topography of a life lived in harsh environments. She had dark hair streaked gray, pulled back in a braid that hung to the middle of her back. She wore a dock jacket with the Calypso's registry patch on the shoulder, the stitching frayed.

"Reyes," she said. Not a greeting. An acknowledgment. She sat down without shaking hands. Her eyes moved around the room with the quick, assessing scan of a person who'd been burned before and intended to see the fire coming next time.

Skip Chen was already seated at the far end of the table. Sofia had met him once, briefly, at the chandlery on Waypoint two seasons ago. He was compact, wiry, with quick hands and a thin face that carried an expression of permanent, ironic amusement. His ship, the Polaris, was a Far Line veteran — forty years on the deep grounds, Skip at the helm for twenty of them. He'd brought a thermos of tea and was drinking from it with the unhurried calm of a man who'd been in more meetings than he cared to remember and had learned that patience was the only weapon that never ran out of ammunition.

And then there was Voss.

---

Captain Elias Voss of the F/V Dawntreader arrived four minutes late. He came through the door with an apology that was too casual to be sincere and a smile that didn't reach his eyes. He was medium-height, medium-build, medium-everything — the kind of man who was easy to overlook in a crowd, who faded into backgrounds, who was, Sofia suspected, exactly the kind of person who had spent his entire life being underestimated and had learned to use it.

"Captain Voss," Sofia said. "Welcome."

"Captain Reyes. Good to meet you. Been hearing a lot about you." His handshake was damp. His eyes flicked around the room — counting heads, reading the seating arrangement, mapping the power geometry. It took about two seconds. He sat down between Skip and Petra, the position of someone who wanted to appear central without committing to a side.

Mara had briefed Sofia on Voss two days ago. The Dawntreader was a mid-range harvester out of Bremerhaven, one of the struggling outer stations. Voss had been fishing the Near Line for nine years, the last three at reduced quota. He'd been vocal at the station bars about the unfairness of the quota system, the corruption of the Authority, the way independent boats were being squeezed out. He'd been equally vocal about his interest in joining a coalition.

"He reached out to me," Mara had said. "Not the other way around. He heard about the coalition through the Bremerhaven network and contacted me directly. Seemed eager. Maybe too eager."

"Why 'too'?"

"Because eager in this business usually means desperate, and desperate means unpredictable. I've met him twice. He's smart. He knows the system. He knows where the pressure points are." Mara had paused. "But I can't read him. I can't tell if he's here because he believes in the coalition or because he needs something from it. Those aren't the same thing."

Sofia had filed the assessment. Now she watched Voss settle into his chair, produce a tablet, set it on the table with the screen angled away from everyone else, and smile his too-casual smile, and she thought: *Mara's right. I can't read him either.*

---

The meeting opened at 10:00 station time. Sofia stood.

"Thank you all for coming. I'm going to skip the preamble because we all know why we're here. DeepWell Organics is buying independent quota licenses. Fourteen so far, with more on the way. They've filed a consolidation petition with the Authority. If it's approved, they'll control the largest single quota block in the system within a season. Every independent harvester in this room — every independent harvester in the *system* — will be competing against a corporate fleet with bottomless capital and no incentive to preserve the grounds."

She let that sit. Skip sipped his tea. Petra's jaw tightened. Voss nodded, slowly, as if hearing confirmation of something he'd long suspected.

"Mara proposed a coalition. Four boats, filing jointly for priority access to the rogue field under first-worked rights. The Resolute is the founding vessel — we discovered and documented the field. The coalition gives us protected access for three seasons. Three seasons to establish the ground, build the data, and create a market position that DeepWell can't displace."

She pulled up the rogue field data on the room's display — catch logs, composition profiles, density maps, the three weeks of work that the Resolute's crew had poured into uncharted water. The numbers spoke for themselves. Pharmaceutical-grade organics at densities that hadn't been seen in twenty years. A ground worth fighting for.

"That's the pitch," Sofia said. "Today is the vote. Join the coalition or stay independent. Before we vote, I want to hear from each of you. Questions, concerns, conditions. This only works if everyone at this table understands what they're signing up for."

Petra spoke first. "What exactly are we signing up for? I've heard the broad strokes. I want the details."

Mara took over. She laid out the coalition structure — the same structure she'd presented on the Resolute, refined and formalized. Each boat kept its own license, its own catch, its own crew. The coalition held the ground collectively. Data sharing was reciprocal and real-time — every boat's catch logs visible to every other boat. No black boxes. No hidden sets. Full transparency within the group.

"Ground rules," Mara said. "The coalition operates under the Resolute's founding registration. Captain Reyes has final authority on ground management. Ray Hutchins, the Resolute's master, has advisory veto on fishing practices — if he says a set is wrong, the set is wrong, for any boat in the coalition. In exchange, every boat gets priority access to the rogue field for three seasons, shared catch-modeling data, and collective bargaining power with buyers."

"Advisory veto," Skip said. He said it neutrally, the way he said everything. "That's giving one captain authority over another captain's boat."

"It's giving one captain authority over fishing practices on shared ground," Mara corrected. "You can run your boat however you want. But if you're fishing the coalition's ground, you fish it sustainably. Hutchins has thirty-one years on the belt. His read on the water is better than any catch model. If he tells you a set will damage the ground, you pull the net. That's the deal."

"And if I disagree with his call?"

"Then you bring it to the coalition. Majority vote among the four captains. But the default is: Hutchins knows the water. You trust that or you don't join."

Skip looked at Sofia. "You're comfortable with that? Giving your captain veto power over coalition boats?"

"He's not my captain. I'm the captain. He's my master, and he knows more about the belt than anyone alive. Yes, I'm comfortable with it."

Petra leaned forward. "The data sharing concerns me. I've been burned on shared ground before. Cassini Bank, '44. Three boats working a shared claim. One of them sold the catch data to a corporate buyer, who used it to justify a commercial licensing petition. Lost the ground in a season."

"Who," Sofia asked.

"Does it matter? It was a different time, different boats. What matters is that it happened. Shared data is a liability if the wrong person sees it."

"Everything is a liability if the wrong person is involved," Mara said. "That's why this coalition is four boats, not fourteen. We know each other. We vouch for each other."

"Do we?" Petra looked at Voss. "I don't know Captain Voss. I know of him. There's a difference."

Voss met her look with his too-casual smile. "Petra Wallach. I know your record. Far Line skipper for twelve years, lost your Near Line quota in the Cassini collapse, ran supply for two seasons, been trying to get back on the water ever since. I'd say we know each other well enough."

"That's my file. That's not me."

"Then introduce yourself."

Petra stared at him. The room went very still.

"I'm a fisher," Petra said. "I've been on the water since I was nineteen. I lost my quota because the Authority restructured the Cassini zone after the collapse and my license was in the affected block. I didn't sell. I didn't walk away. I fought the restructuring for three years and lost. I've been running supply because it was the only work I could get, and I hate every minute of it. I want back on the water. That's why I'm here."

The room was quiet. Petra's honesty had done what honesty usually does — it had stripped the conversation of its diplomatic skin and exposed the raw tissue underneath. These were not diplomats. They were fishers. They spoke in facts and deeds, not in pleasantries.

Voss nodded. "Fair. I'll be straight too. I'm here because the Dawntreader is two bad seasons from going under. I've got nine years on the Near Line, reduced quota three seasons running, and a crew that needs to eat. I'm not here for ideology. I'm here because I need a ground that pays, and the rogue field sounds like it pays."

"At least he's honest," Skip murmured into his tea.

---

They broke for lunch at 12:30. The Harbor Master's office had provided a spread — sandwiches, fruit, coffee that was almost good. Sofia ate standing at the viewport, watching the ships on the docking ring.

Margaret appeared beside her. She'd said almost nothing during the morning session. That was Margaret — observe, process, speak only when the data was complete.

"Voss," Margaret said.

"I know."

"His tablet. The screen was angled away from the table."

"I noticed."

"That's a man who doesn't want anyone to see what he's looking at. In a meeting about transparency."

"There are a lot of reasons someone might angle a tablet."

"There are. And most of them are innocent. But combined with the fact that he arrived late — four minutes, enough time to have a conversation in the corridor before entering — and the fact that he contacted Mara directly rather than through the station network, which means he wanted to control the communication channel —" Margaret sipped her tea. "It's a pattern. It doesn't mean anything on its own. But it's a pattern."

Sofia looked at her. "You think he's the mole."

"I think Mara mentioned that someone in the Bremerhaven network was feeding DeepWell information about independent ground access. She didn't name a source. She didn't have one. But she had suspicions."

"Mara suspects everyone. It's how she operates."

"Mara suspects strategically. I observe precisely. There's a difference." Margaret set her tea down. "I'm not making an accusation. I'm noting a pattern. Watch him this afternoon. Watch what he asks for and what he offers. The questions are always more revealing than the answers."

---

The afternoon session began at 13:30. Sofia opened the floor for conditions — each captain could propose amendments or requirements before the vote.

Skip went first. "Maintenance pooling. I want it in the charter. The Polaris is forty years old. I need access to shared spares, bulk purchasing, and Margaret's engineering expertise." He looked at Margaret. "No offense, but my engineer is twenty-three and learned on a simulator."

"None taken," Margaret said. "The charter includes a maintenance pooling clause. Coalition boats contribute to a shared spares inventory based on tonnage class. Bulk purchasing is handled through the co-op account."

"I also want the hull survey data," Skip said. "The Resolute's hull scan. If we're sharing a ground, I want to know what Margaret's learned about hull degradation in this environment. The rogue field's thermal profile is different from the Near Line. I need to know what it does to plating."

Sofia looked at Margaret. Margaret nodded once. "The scan data is available to coalition members," Sofia said. "Margaret will share the full integrity maps. We'll also share the scanning protocol she's developed, so your engineer can run the same surveys on the Polaris."

Skip nodded. "Agreed."

Petra went next. "I want a crew rotation clause. The Calypso's been running supply for two seasons. My crew is rusty. I want access to experienced hands on a temporary basis — borrowed crew, not transferred. Two-week rotations during the first month on the new ground."

"Whose crew?" Mara asked.

"Anyone who can spare a hand. The Resolute has Tomás — young, strong, eager. The Polaris has deckhands. I need people who know how to run a mag-net array on a working ground, not supply jockeys who've forgotten which end of the net is which."

Sofia considered. Lending Tomás was a cost — the Resolute would be short-handed. But Petra's need was real, and the coalition was about mutual support.

"We can rotate Tomás for two weeks," Sofia said. "Margaret will stay on the Resolute. I'll cover the watch."

"Agreed," Petra said. She didn't smile. She didn't soften. She nodded once, the way Margaret nodded — as a statement of fact, not a gesture of warmth.

Then Voss spoke.

"I want data access before I commit."

The room shifted. Not visibly — no one moved, no one changed expression. But the air pressure changed. Sofia felt it the way she felt the hull's vibration — a subtle alteration in the ambient frequency of the room.

"Explain," Mara said.

"The rogue field data. I want to see it before the vote. Not the summary — the full data set. Catch logs, density profiles, thermal mapping, composition analysis. Everything. I need to know what I'm voting on."

"The summary is comprehensive," Sofia said.

"The summary is a summary. It shows results. It doesn't show methodology. I need to see how the data was collected, what the sample sizes are, what the error bars look like. I've been on the Near Line long enough to know that a good catch in one spot doesn't mean a good ground. I need the underlying data."

Margaret's voice came from Sofia's left, quiet and precise. "The underlying data includes our proprietary bloom-mapping methodology. That's not catch data. That's operational technique. It's how we find the blooms, not where they are."

"I'm not asking for your technique. I'm asking for your data."

"In this business, Captain Voss, those are the same thing."

The room held its breath. Voss and Margaret looked at each other across the table. Margaret's expression was impassive. Voss's smile had thinned.

"I'm not trying to steal your methods," Voss said. "I'm trying to make an informed decision."

"Then make it on the summary," Margaret said. "Every captain at this table has made ground decisions on less data than what's in that summary. If it's not enough for you, I'd ask why."

The question hung. Voss's eyes flickered — a micro-expression, gone in a fraction of a second, but Margaret saw it and Sofia saw it and they both filed it in the same place: *He wants the data. Not for himself.*

"The summary is sufficient," Voss said. He leaned back. The smile returned. "I withdraw the request."

---

Sofia called a thirty-minute recess at 15:00. She needed to think, and she needed to talk to Mara, and she needed to do both out of earshot of a room full of captains who were, by training and disposition, the most suspicious people in any system.

They met in the corridor outside the conference room. The corridor was empty — C-deck was administrative, and the Harbor Master's staff had cleared the area for the meeting.

"Voss," Sofia said.

"I know."

"The data request."

"I know."

"Is he feeding DeepWell?"

Mara leaned against the corridor wall. Her expression was controlled, but there was something behind it — the particular frustration of a person whose suspicions were being confirmed in real time and who couldn't act on them yet.

"I don't have proof. I have patterns. Three months ago, DeepWell filed an amended petition with the Authority. The amendment included detailed catch-composition profiles for three rogue field zones — zones that match the Resolute's open-registry filings. But the filing also included density projections that weren't in your registry data. Projections that could only come from someone who'd seen the underlying methodology."

"Someone who'd seen our bloom-mapping data."

"Or someone who'd reconstructed it from the summary. DeepWell has analysts. They're good. But the projections are too precise for reconstruction. They have primary data, Sofia. And the only people who had access to primary data on the rogue field before today are you, your crew, and the people you've briefed."

"I briefed you."

"You did. And I briefed my crew. And I told Voss about the coalition, including a general description of the rogue field's composition profile. General. Not detailed." Mara paused. "But Voss is smart. Smart enough to extrapolate. Smart enough to have done the math from the fragments I gave him and the open-registry filings."

"Or smart enough to have passed the fragments to DeepWell and let their analysts do the math."

"That too."

Sofia stared at the wall opposite. The corridor was gray. Everything on stations was gray. She missed the viewport, the dark, the drift of the field. She missed the water.

"We can't prove it," Sofia said.

"Not today. Maybe not ever. But the pattern is there."

"Do we block him from the coalition?"

Mara considered this. "If we block him, he knows we suspect him. He goes to DeepWell — if he hasn't already — and tells them we're onto the leak. They adjust their strategy. We lose the intelligence advantage."

"And if we let him in?"

"If we let him in, he has access to the coalition's real-time data sharing. Every catch log, every density map, every set. He becomes a fire hose instead of a drip."

"Then we don't do real-time sharing."

"Sofia. The data sharing is the coalition. It's the whole point. Without shared data, we're just four boats fishing the same water with a piece of paper that says we're friends."

Sofia thought. She thought the way she'd learned to think on the Resolute — not quickly, not slowly, but deliberately, turning the problem over the way Ray turned a course calculation, checking each variable against experience and instinct and the accumulated data of a life spent learning.

"There's a third option," she said.

Mara raised an eyebrow.

"We let him in. We share the data. But we control what he sees."

"You mean feed him false data."

"I mean feed him selective data. Real data — nothing that would fail verification — but curated. We share catch logs on a twelve-hour delay instead of real-time. We include density maps for zones we've already fished, not zones we're about to fish. We show him the ground we've covered, not the ground we're covering."

Mara's expression shifted. Not to disagreement — to interest. The look of a captain seeing a tactical option she hadn't considered.

"A buffer," Mara said.

"A buffer. Voss gets enough data to justify the coalition. DeepWell gets enough data to keep them complacent. And we keep the operational intelligence — the real-time bloom tracking, the methodology, the active ground — close to the chest."

"That's not full transparency."

"No. It's managed transparency. There's a difference."

"The charter says full transparency within the coalition."

"The charter also says Captain Reyes has final authority on ground management. I'm exercising that authority. Full transparency applies to boats I trust. For boats I don't trust —" Sofia looked at Mara. "I'll need your help. Your crew manages the data platform. You can build the buffer into the architecture."

Mara pushed off the wall. She paced three steps, turned, paced back. The energy she'd been containing — the frustration, the calculation, the chess-player's instinct — found its way into her movement.

"Petra will notice," Mara said. "She's sharp. She'll see the delay."

"I'll tell Petra. Privately. She lost her ground on Cassini because someone sold data. She'll understand."

"Skip?"

"Skip doesn't care about data architecture. He cares about maintenance pooling and hull surveys. He'll take what he's given."

Mara stopped pacing. She looked at Sofia with an expression that Sofia was starting to recognize — the look of a captain assessing another captain and finding something worth following.

"You've changed since the rogue field," Mara said.

"I've had reason to."

"Gus would have just kicked Voss out."

"Gus didn't have a coalition to hold together." Sofia paused. "And Gus didn't have a hull to refit. I need this coalition, Mara. I need it to work. I can't afford to kick out a potential leak and I can't afford to give him everything. So I find the middle."

"The middle is a hard place to stand."

"I know. But it's where the ground is."

---

The vote was at 16:00.

Sofia stood at the head of the table. The room was quiet. The afternoon light through the viewport had shifted — the docking ring outside was silhouetted against the binary stars, and the ships in their berths cast long shadows across the station's hull.

"I'm going to ask each captain for a simple yes or no. Do you join the coalition, or do you stay independent? The terms are as discussed: shared ground under the Resolute's founding registration, data sharing per the charter, maintenance pooling, collective bargaining. Captain Hutchins has advisory veto on fishing practices. Captain Reyes has final authority on ground management."

She looked around the table. Four faces. Four captains. Four boats, each one carrying a crew and a history and a set of reasons to be in this room on this day.

"Captain Chen."

Skip set down his tea. "The Polaris is in. Been fishing other people's grounds for twenty years. About time I fished my own." He paused. "With friends."

"Captain Wallach."

Petra's jaw was tight. Her hands were flat on the table. She looked at Sofia with the direct, unblinking gaze of a woman who had been off the water for two seasons and intended to never be off the water again.

"The Calypso is in. I've got conditions — we'll discuss those separately. But I'm in."

"Captain Voss."

Voss looked around the table. His too-casual smile was gone, replaced by something more careful — the expression of a man calculating odds. He held Sofia's gaze for a moment. Whatever he saw there — the steadiness, the set of her shoulders, the particular authority of a twenty-six-year-old woman who had found new ground and defended it and brought four boats together to protect it — he measured it, and he made his call.

"The Dawntreader is in. Full participation. Whatever the charter requires."

He said it smoothly. Sincerely. The way a good liar says true things — with the conviction of someone who has decided that the lie and the truth are the same thing, as long as the outcome is what he wants.

Sofia nodded. She didn't trust him. She didn't need to trust him. She needed him in the coalition, visible, predictable, and feeding DeepWell the data she chose to give him.

"Captain Vasquez."

Mara smiled. It was the first time Sofia had seen her smile — a real one, not a diplomatic one. It changed her face. Made her look younger. Made her look like someone who had been fighting for a long time and had just won a round.

"The Meridian is in. Been in since the beginning. Not going anywhere."

One vote left.

"Captain Reyes."

Sofia looked at the table. Four captains, four boats, four sets of reasons. Petra's honest desperation. Skip's patient opportunism. Mara's strategic vision. Voss's unknowable agenda. And behind all of them, the crew of the Resolute — Ray in his wheelhouse, Margaret with her tea, Denny in his engine room, Tomás on his watch, Lily in her corner — the people who had found this ground and worked it and bled for it and were now entrusting it to a group.

Independence. Gus's word. The word that meant everything.

Community. Sofia's word. The word that meant: I can't do this alone. I don't have to.

"The Resolute is in," she said. "Founding vessel. The ground is ours. We share it with the coalition. The coalition protects it for all of us."

She sat down. The room exhaled.

---

After the meeting, the captains shook hands. It was formal and brief — fishing people don't linger over ceremony. Petra gripped Sofia's hand and held it for an extra second. "Don't screw this up," she said. It was the warmest thing Petra Wallach had said to anyone in the room.

Skip gave Sofia a nod. "I'll comm my engineer tonight. Tell him to expect Margaret's survey protocols."

Voss shook her hand last. His grip was dry, firm, carefully calibrated. "Looking forward to working together, Captain Reyes."

"Captain Voss."

He left. The door closed behind him. The room was empty except for Sofia and Margaret and Mara.

Margaret spoke first. "He'll comm DeepWell within the hour. The data he got today — the summary, the charter terms, the ground coordinates — that's enough for their analysts to start mapping."

"Let them," Sofia said. "By the time they file for commercial access, we'll have priority rights locked in. Three seasons."

"And after three seasons?"

Sofia looked at Mara. Mara looked back. Between them, unspoken, was the understanding that three seasons was a lifetime in the belt. Three seasons was enough to build something that couldn't be taken. Or enough to watch it fall apart.

"After three seasons, we'll know who our friends are," Sofia said.

Mara picked up her bag. She extended her hand to Sofia — not to shake, but to hold. Palm up. Open.

"It's a good ground, Sofia. And a good coalition."

"It's a start."

"It's a start." Mara squeezed once and let go. She left the conference room with the stride of a woman who had been sailing toward this moment for months and had finally made landfall.

---

Margaret and Sofia walked the corridor to the docking ring in silence. Their boots echoed on the deck plating — Margaret's steady and measured, Sofia's quicker, lighter, the footsteps of someone whose mind was running faster than her feet.

"I'll build the data buffer tonight," Margaret said. "Twelve-hour delay on catch logs. Active ground coordinates withheld from the shared platform. Bloom-mapping methodology restricted to Resolute internal."

"How long can we maintain the split?"

"Indefinitely, as long as the platform architecture is mine. The coalition sees a shared feed. The Resolute sees the real-time data. Voss sees what we show him. DeepWell sees what Voss gives them."

"And if Voss figures it out?"

"Then we'll know, because DeepWell's filings will suddenly show gaps — data they should have but don't. And we'll know where the leak is."

They reached the docking ring. The Resolute was in berth seven, her hull dark against the station's lights. She looked small from here — a working vessel among working vessels, nothing special, nothing grand. Just a boat.

But she was theirs. And she was holding.

"I want the hull scan protocol on every EVA from now on," Sofia said. "Every external check. We map the hull centimeter by centimeter. I want to know every weak point before it becomes a breach."

"Already drafted," Margaret said.

Of course it was. Margaret had probably drafted it during the meeting, in the margins of her attention, while the captains argued about data sharing and Voss smiled his too-casual smile.

"Margaret."

"Hmm."

"The coalition. Is it the right call?"

Margaret stopped walking. She looked at the Resolute — at the hull, at the patches, at the eighty years of repairs written on the skin of the ship. She looked at it the way she'd looked at it from outside, in her EVA suit, on the night of the breach: as a record, a logbook, a living thing.

"The right call is the one that keeps the boat fishing," she said. "This keeps the boat fishing."

She walked up the ramp. Sofia followed. The airlock closed behind them, and the station smell — recirculated air and fried food — gave way to the ship's smell: ozone and metal and the faint, persistent ghost of ten thousand harvests.

Home.

---

That night, in her rack, Sofia opened Gus's ledger. She turned to the page she'd written on the rogue field, the night Mara first came aboard. The night the coalition was born.

She read it through. Then she turned to the next blank page and wrote:

*Coalition vote: four to zero. Resolute, Meridian, Polaris, Calypso, Dawntreader. Five boats.*

*Voss is the leak. Can't prove it. Don't need to. Margaret is building a data buffer — selective transparency. Real data, curated access. Let DeepWell think they see the whole picture. They see what I show them.*

*Petra is honest. Scared, but honest. She'll hold. Skip is solid — patient, practical, low drama. Mara is — Mara. She thinks in five-year arcs. I think in seasons. The combination should work.*

*The hull needs a refit. The coalition is the path to the refit. The refit is the path to the Resolute's future. The Resolute's future is my future. The line goes out. The line comes back.*

*Ray said to ask Gus about the shipyard favors. I'll comm him tomorrow. I don't know what he'll say. I don't know if he'll remember. I don't know if the favors still exist, twenty years later, in a shipyard that's probably changed hands three times.*

*But Gus knew things. He knew people. He kept a ledger in paper and pen because he didn't trust the electronic one, and he was right to do it, because the electronic one can be hacked and the paper one can only be read by someone who holds it in their hands.*

*I'm holding it in my hands.*

*New ground. New rules. New friends. One enemy I can't see and one ally I can't fully trust. A hull that's aging. A crew that's learning. A boat that's holding.*

*The line goes out. The line comes back.*

She closed the ledger. She turned off Gus's reading light. In the dark, the ship hummed — the reactor, the life support, the distant clink of Margaret's boots on her midnight sweep, the muffled snore of Tomás three racks down.

Sofia Reyes, Captain. Owner. Founder of a coalition she couldn't fully control, on a ground she couldn't fully protect, in a ship she couldn't fully repair. Twenty-six years old. Learning.

The line goes out. The line comes back.
