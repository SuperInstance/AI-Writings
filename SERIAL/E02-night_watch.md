# The Long Line
## Episode 2: Night Watch

---

The thing about deep space is that it's not actually dark.

That's the first lie they tell you in training. "Space is dark." Space is black, sure, but black isn't the same as dark. Dark means absence. Space is full — stuffed to bursting with light you can't see, signals you can't hear, and the cold persistent chatter of stars that have been screaming since before anything alive had ears to listen.

What space is, is empty. And empty is worse than dark.

Tomás Hermida knew this theoretically. He'd completed his watch certification seven months ago, logged four hundred hours of solo watch, and passed the psychological evaluation with a score that the examiner called "adequate" in a tone that made adequate sound like a diagnosis. He knew the protocols. He knew the systems. He knew that on a transit between grounds, with the Resolute running on minimum power and most of the crew asleep, the watch-stander's job was to monitor, log, and above all not do anything stupid.

He knew all of that. It didn't help.

Because the other thing about deep space — the thing they don't put in the manual — is that after four hours alone on a quiet bridge, the silence stops being silence and starts being pressure. It presses against your eardrums. It fills the room like water. And your brain, which evolved on a planet full of things that wanted to eat it, starts inventing patterns in the nothing.

Tomás stared at the sensor display. The sensor display stared back, blank and steady, showing exactly what it had shown for the last four hours and seventeen minutes: nothing. Clean space. The kind of empty that made you want to invent a problem just to have something to do.

He took a sip of coffee. It had gone cold two hours ago. He drank it anyway.

The Resolute hummed around him — the low chest-vibration of the reactor, the occasional tick of hull panels adjusting to the temperature differential, the whisper of the life support cycling. These were the sounds of a healthy ship. He knew them like he knew his own breathing. Twelve months on the Resolute, and the sounds had become so familiar that he sometimes heard them in his sleep, or whatever passed for sleep during transit rotations.

His watch started at 02:00 ship time and ended at 08:00. Six hours. In those six hours, his responsibilities were:

1. Monitor long-range sensors.
2. Monitor ship systems status.
3. Log any anomalies.
4. Notify the captain or watch officer in the event of — and the manual was very specific here — "any significant deviation from expected operating parameters."

The manual was less specific about what constituted "significant." That was left to the judgment of the watch-stander.

Tomás had good judgment. He thought so, anyway. Captain Sofia said so, once, after he'd caught a coolant leak in the number three processor before it became a coolant failure. "Good eye," she'd said, and then gone back to her charts, and he'd felt warm for two days.

It was 03:47 ship time when the contact appeared.

---

It didn't appear dramatically. That was the thing about real life — it didn't have cues. No music swelled. No one said "look at that." The sensor display simply updated, the way it updated a thousand times a day, and a new entry appeared in the tracking log.

CONTACT — BEARING 047 RELATIVE — RANGE 2.3 AU — NO TRANSPONDER — NO DRIVE SIGNATURE — CLASS UNKNOWN

Tomás blinked at it. He blinked again. He refreshed the display, a reflex that training hadn't managed to train out of him. The entry remained.

He pulled up the detailed view. The contact was on a slow drift, no thrust, no attitude control. No transponder signal. No communications on any standard channel. The passive sensors picked up a faint thermal signature — something alive in there, or at least something that had been alive recently. The mass estimate was consistent with a mid-size harvester.

His first thought was debris. His second thought, arriving half a second later with a chill that started at the base of his skull, was: that's a ship.

His third thought was protocol. Protocol chapter six, section four: "Unidentified or non-communicating vessels in deep space represent a potential hazard. The watch-stander shall log the contact, plot a divergence course if trajectory intersects the vessel, and notify the watch officer at the earliest opportunity. Under no circumstances shall the watch-stander approach or attempt communication without authorization."

The protocol was clear. It was, in fact, one of the clearest protocols in the manual, because it was written in blood. Three crews had been lost to derelict traps in the outer fields — ships rigged to look abandoned, waiting for someone to dock and provide a nice salvage target for the people hiding inside.

Tomás logged the contact. He plotted the trajectory. The Resolute's current course would bring them within hailing distance in about six hours, which meant he should flag it now so they could adjust course.

He reached for the comm to wake Ray, and stopped.

Because the fourth thought — the one that came after the protocols and the procedures and the blood-written warnings — was simpler and quieter and wouldn't go away.

*What if there are people on that ship?*

---

He pulled up the public registry. A contact without a transponder didn't identify itself, but the mass and configuration profile went into the database, and the database came back with a probabilistic match.

**F/V MERIDIAN** — registered harvester, 2400-tonne class, home port Livingston Station. Crew of six. Reported overdue 04 January. Search suspended 22 February. Presumed lost.

The match probability was 94.6%.

Tomás stared at the name. The Meridian. He'd heard about it — everyone in the fleet had. The Meridian had been working the Cassini Bank, same as half a dozen other boats, when it went silent. No distress call, no debris, no final transmission. Just gone. The search had lasted seven weeks and found nothing. Six crew, six families, and a hole in the fleet roster that got filled by the next boat along.

That had been six months ago.

He pulled up the thermal data again. Faint but present. The signature wasn't consistent with a dead ship — a dead ship went cold, eventually matching the background temperature of space. The Meridian was warmer than it should have been. Only slightly. Enough.

Something was running on that ship. Something was drawing power.

*Cold sleep*, he thought, and the word sat in his chest like a stone.

If the crew had lost primary power — reactor failure, systems failure, any of the hundred things that could kill a ship without killing it immediately — the emergency protocols would have routed remaining power to the cold sleep pods. The pods could keep a crew alive for months on minimum power. Months and months, if the crew had been smart about rationing what was left.

Six months.

Tomás looked at the comm. He looked at the sensor display. He looked at the clock: 03:52 ship time.

He thought about the protocol. He thought about the thermal signature. He thought about six people in cold sleep, waiting for someone to find them.

He thought about waking Ray.

---

Ray slept like a man who'd earned it. Twenty-eight years on harvesters, fourteen of them as captain of the Resolute before he'd handed the chair to Sofia and demoted himself to advisor. He slept in a bunk that was too short for him, in a cabin that smelled like engine grease and peppermint, and he slept deep.

Tomás pressed the comm button and waited.

Three rings. Four. Then Ray's voice, thick with sleep: "This'd better be the end of the world."

"Ray, it's Tomás. I've got a contact."

A pause. The sound of a man sitting up. "What kind of contact?"

"Derelict. No transponder, no comms. I've got a ninety-four percent registry match to the Meridian."

Silence. A long silence, the kind that had weight behind it. Then: "The Meridian. The one from the Cassini Bank."

"Yes sir."

Another pause. "Thermal?"

"Faint but present. Consistent with emergency cold sleep."

The sound of feet hitting the deck. A zipper. "I'm on my way. Don't do anything."

The comm clicked off. Tomás sat in the silence and waited.

---

Ray arrived on the bridge four minutes later, wearing a jumpsuit and the expression of a man who'd been awake for thirty seconds and already hated it. He was sixty-one years old and moved like he was made of hinges — stiff but functional, each motion deliberate. He dropped into the auxiliary chair and pulled up the sensor display without saying a word.

Tomás watched him read the data. Ray's eyes moved the way they always did when he was processing — left to right, top to bottom, then back to the middle, where the important stuff lived.

"Ninety-four six," Ray said. "Not bad odds."

"It's the Meridian, Ray."

"Maybe." He scratched his jaw. "Thermal's awful faint."

"It's there."

"It's there," Ray agreed. He leaned back in his chair and looked at nothing for a while. Then he turned to Tomás, and his expression had changed — not softer, exactly, but different. More present.

"Your watch," Ray said. "What do you think?"

Tomás had expected this. He'd expected it because he'd heard the stories — Ray's old crew, the way he ran a bridge, his belief that the watch-stander was the watch-stander, and the chair meant the decision lived with whoever was sitting in it. Ray didn't take decisions away from people. He'd told Tomás that on his first transit. "I'll advise," he'd said. "I'll tell you when you're about to do something terminally stupid. But the watch is yours. Own it."

"I think we should investigate," Tomás said.

"Protocol says don't."

"I know."

"So tell me why you're wrong."

Tomás had been thinking about this for fifteen minutes. He'd had time. "Thermal signature is consistent with active cold sleep pods. The registry match is over ninety percent. The Meridian had a crew of six. If even one of them is alive in those pods, they've been in there for six months, and every day they stay is another day the pods could fail." He paused. "A divergence course puts us out of range in four hours. After that, we'd have to loop back, and that's another two days. Two more days for anyone in those pods."

Ray listened. He nodded slowly, the way he did when he was weighing something rather than agreeing with it.

"And if it's a trap?" Ray asked.

"The outer field incidents were all in the Borrelli Drift. We're three sectors from the Drift. And those were three years ago — the syndicate that was running them got broken up after the Petrel went in with marines."

"So it's probably safe."

"It's probably safe."

Ray looked at him. "Probably is a word that doesn't mean anything, Tomás. You know that, right? Probably safe, probably not a trap, probably people alive. You can stack probably all the way to the ceiling and it won't hold up a single decision."

Tomás felt his face warm. "Then what's the point of the data?"

Ray almost smiled. "The data tells you what is. It doesn't tell you what to do about it. That's judgment, and judgment is the only thing on this ship that doesn't come with a manual." He gestured at the display. "You want to go over there."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because if I were in that pod, I'd want someone to come."

Ray held his gaze for a long moment. Then he reached for the comm.

"Sofia."

---

Captain Sofia Arenas came to the bridge in under five minutes, which meant she'd been awake already. She did that — couldn't sleep during transit, she said, the silence bothered her. She'd been in her cabin reviewing charts, same as always, and she arrived with her hair tied back and her eyes sharp and no trace of sleep in her voice.

"Show me," she said.

Tomás showed her. The contact, the registry match, the thermal data, the drift trajectory. He laid it out the way he'd laid it out for Ray, clean and structured, facts first, interpretation second. Sofia listened the way she always did — still, focused, her eyes on the data rather than on him.

When he finished, she looked at Ray. Ray shrugged.

"Watch-stander's call," Ray said.

Sofia looked at Tomás. "What's your call?"

"Investigate. Close to scanning range, passive sensors only. If the thermal data holds, we move to active scan and assess for docking. If anything looks wrong, we break off."

She studied him for a moment. He could feel her weighing him — not the data, him. His voice, his hands, the way he sat in the chair. She was checking to see if he'd thought it through or if he was just reacting.

"All right," she said. "Take us in. Passive only. And Tomás — "

"Yes captain?"

"Good call."

---

The Resolute adjusted course. It was a small adjustment — a nudge, really — and the ship settled into its new heading with the patient indifference of a vessel that didn't care where it was going as long as someone told it.

They were three hours from scanning range. Ray went to get real coffee. Sofia stayed on the bridge, working her charts but present, available, watching without hovering. Tomás flew the ship.

That was the strange thing. He was flying the ship. Not literally — the autopilot handled navigation — but the decision was his. The course change, the approach, the choice to investigate a derelict in deep space at four in the morning because of a thermal signature and a feeling. His choice. His watch.

He thought about the Meridian's crew. Six people. He didn't know their names yet — he hadn't pulled the registry details — but they were out there, floating in the dark, waiting. If they were alive. If the thermal signature meant what he thought it meant. If, if, if.

Probability didn't tell you what was right. Ray had said that, or something close to it, and Tomás was beginning to understand what he meant. The system could tell him there was a 94.6% chance the contact was the Meridian. It could tell him the thermal signature was "consistent with" cold sleep operations. It could model the likelihood of survivors, the risk of approach, the odds of a trap. It could give him numbers, clean and precise and reassuring in their precision.

But none of those numbers could answer the only question that mattered: *What do you do?*

And the answer to that question wasn't in the data. It was in something else — something that Ray called judgment and that Tomás didn't have a better word for. It was the thing that made you look at a faint thermal signature at 03:47 and decide it was worth risking your ship. Or the thing that made you decide it wasn't. Either way, the data was the same. The numbers didn't change. Only the person looking at them.

At 05:30, they entered active scanning range.

---

The Meridian looked dead.

Tomás had expected this — the visual was irrelevant, a ship without lights looked dead by definition — but the scan confirmed it. Dark hull, no running lights, no navigation beacons, no communications array activity. The solar collectors were retracted. The docking ports were sealed. The ship drifted on a slow, uncorrected yaw, rotating about its long axis once every eleven minutes.

But the thermal signature was there, and it was real.

"Confirmed," Margaret said over the comm from systems engineering. "I'm reading power draw from the lower deck. Minimal — looks like cold sleep pod array, emergency life support, and basic hull integrity monitoring. That's it. Everything else is dark."

"How much power?"

"Reactor's at... twelve percent? Maybe fourteen. Enough to keep the pods going for a while longer, but the degradation curve is not great. She's been running on fumes for months."

Sofia looked at Tomás. "Active scan?"

Tomás nodded. "Go active."

Margaret triggered the active scan. The Resolute's sensors painted the Meridian in detail — hull mapping, internal structure, thermal imaging, power grid mapping. The picture assembled itself on the display like a body being drawn from the inside out.

Six cold sleep pods on the lower deck. Six thermal signatures, faint but steady.

"Oh," Tomás said.

"Six pods, six signatures," Margaret confirmed. Her voice had changed — professional calm over something sharper. "They're alive. Barely, but they're alive."

The bridge was quiet. Sofia stared at the display. Ray stared at the display. Tomás stared at the display and felt something crack open in his chest that he couldn't name.

"Denny," Sofia said into the comm. "We've got a situation."

---

Denny Park had been the Resolute's mechanic for twelve years. He'd joined when he was twenty-three and had never left, not because he loved the ship — though he did, in the grudging way you love something you've sworn at ten thousand times — but because he couldn't imagine being anywhere else. He arrived on the bridge shirtless and barefoot, took one look at the scan, and said, "I'm going to need pants."

"We're docking with the Meridian," Sofia said. "I need you and Margaret on the crossover team."

"Give me ten minutes."

He was back in eight. Margaret joined him on the bridge in twelve, carrying a kit bag and wearing the focused expression of someone who'd been briefed by remote and was already three steps into the problem.

The docking took forty minutes. Forty minutes of slow, careful maneuvering — the Resolute nudging up to the Meridian's portside docking collar, the automated systems trying to handshake with a ship whose automated systems were barely functional. Denny had to override half the docking sequence manually, matching rotations and tolerances by eye and instinct while Margaret talked him through the power coupling.

"Got it," Denny said. The collar sealed. The umbilical pressurized. "Crossover's green."

Tomás stood at the airlock behind Denny and Margaret and Sofia and Ray and felt suddenly, acutely aware that he was twenty-three years old and had made a decision that was now becoming real. The airlock door was between him and a ship that had been floating in the dark for six months, and he'd put them here. His call. His watch.

Sofia went first. Denny followed. Then Margaret. Then Ray, who paused at the threshold and looked back at Tomás.

"Coming?"

Tomás swallowed. "Yes sir."

---

The Meridian was cold. Not freezing — the emergency systems kept the interior above zero — but cold in the way that empty rooms are cold, the way that spaces designed for people feel wrong when the people are gone. The air was thin and stale, recycled past the point of usefulness, and it tasted like metal.

Denny moved forward immediately, heading for the reactor room, his flashlight cutting through the dim corridor. Margaret peeled off toward systems control. Sofia led the rest toward the lower deck.

The cold sleep pods were in a compartment aft of the crew quarters, behind a pressure door that opened with a reluctant groan. The room was small — smaller than the Resolute's, which wasn't large to begin with — and it was dominated by six pods arranged in three tiers of two. The pods hummed. The displays glowed. The occupants lay still.

Tomás looked at them.

Six people. Six names he'd finally read from the registry on the way over. He knew them now, at least on paper:

- Captain Yuki Ohara, 51
- Engineer Pasha Litvin, 38
- Harvester Ngozi Adekunle, 44
- Harvester Felipe Rivera, 29
- Mechanic Sun-Hee Cho, 33
- Watch-stander Eli Brennan, 24

Eli Brennan was Tomás's age.

The pods showed minimal vitals — heart rates in the low thirties, body temperatures at hibernation levels, brain activity just above the threshold that separated sleep from death. They were alive. They'd been alive for six months in these pods, breathing recycled air, fed by recycled nutrient lines, kept cold and still while the ship drifted and the reactor slowly died.

"Christ," Ray said quietly.

Sofia was already on the comm. "Margaret, I need a status on their power situation. How long do those pods have?"

Margaret's voice came back tinny through the thin air. "Reactor's at eleven percent and declining. Pod life support is drawing about... two percent per day under current load. They've got maybe a week. Maybe less if the degradation accelerates."

"Can we stabilize?"

"Denny's in the reactor room now. If we can patch in a power transfer from the Resolute, yes. It'll take some time, but yes."

"Do it."

Sofia turned to Tomás. "I need you to stay with the pods."

"Me?"

"You found them. Stay with them. Monitor their vitals. If anything changes — anything — you call me."

She left. Ray gave Tomás a look that might have been approval, or might have been curiosity, and followed her.

Tomás sat down on the deck between the pods and listened to them hum.

---

A pod sounds different from the outside. From the outside, you hear the machinery — the pumps, the circulators, the soft whisper of the thermal exchange system. You hear the displays ticking. You hear the low steady vibration of the power feed.

From the inside, you hear nothing. Cold sleep isn't sleep — it's closer to suspended animation, a state where the body is slowed to the edge of stopping but not past it. There are no dreams. There's no sense of time passing. You close your eyes in the pod and open them and it could have been a minute or a year and you wouldn't know the difference.

Tomás thought about that. Six months of not knowing. Six months of not existing, really — not thinking, not feeling, not breathing on your own. Just a body in a box, kept alive by machines, waiting.

Eli Brennan's pod was at eye level. The display showed a heart rate of thirty-two beats per minute. Tomás watched it count. Thirty-two. Thirty-two. Thirty-two. Each beat a small defiance of the nothing outside.

He thought about what would have happened if they hadn't found the Meridian. Another week, maybe two, and the reactor would have dropped below the threshold needed to sustain the pods. The power would have failed. The pods would have warmed. The bodies would have thawed, and the people inside them would have died without waking up. Quiet. Clean. Nobody would have known.

Or someone else would have found them first. Another ship, another watch-stander, another 03:47 with a blip on the display. Maybe. Or maybe not. Maybe the Meridian would have drifted forever, a ghost ship in the deep black, and the six people inside would have become a story — another lost boat, another cautionary tale, another hole in the fleet roster.

Probability.

Tomás looked at the probability that the Meridian would have been found by another vessel within the next week. The system could calculate it. Traffic density in this sector, average transit routes, the odds of a ship coming within scanning range. He could pull it up right now, look at the number, know the odds.

He didn't.

Because it didn't matter. What mattered was that he'd been here, on this watch, at this hour, and he'd seen the contact, and he'd made the call. Not because the numbers told him to. Not because the protocol told him to — it hadn't, it had told him the opposite. He'd made the call because somewhere in the space between data and decision, between probability and action, there was a thing that Ray called judgment and that Tomás was beginning to think was just another word for caring enough to act.

He cared. That was it. That was the whole thing. He'd looked at a faint thermal signature and thought, *there are people alive in there*, and the thought had mattered more than the risk.

Maybe that was foolish. Maybe that was the kind of thinking that got crews killed. The protocols existed for a reason, and the reason was that sometimes caring wasn't enough, sometimes the derelict was a trap, sometimes the noble choice led to disaster.

But not today.

Today, the derelict was the Meridian. Today, the crew was alive. Today, the kid on watch had made a call, and the call had been right.

Tomás sat on the deck of a dead ship and watched six people breathe, and the silence of deep space pressed against the hull, and he didn't mind it at all.

---

Four hours later, Denny had the power transfer stabilized. It was a messy job — he had to reroute power from the Resolute's reserves through the docking umbilical and into the Meridian's grid, bypassing half a dozen failed components and jury-rigging connections that the original designers would have hated. Margaret handled the software side, nursing the Meridian's systems back to something approaching functional.

"Reactor's holding at fourteen percent on external feed," Denny reported. "Pods are stable. Vitals are improving — slightly. Heart rates coming up, brain activity increasing. They're not out of the woods, but the woods are less dark."

"Can we wake them?" Sofia asked.

"Not yet," Margaret said. "Cold sleep revival needs to be done carefully. Temperature has to come up gradually, nutrient levels restored, metabolic function stabilized. We're talking a day, maybe two, before we can start the revival sequence."

"So we wait."

"We wait."

Sofia looked around the Meridian's bridge — dark, cold, damaged but functional. Charts and personal items still scattered where the crew had left them. A coffee mug with a faded logo. A photo taped to a console edge — a beach, a sunset, two people Tomás didn't recognize.

"We'll take them in tow," Sofia decided. "Transfer the crew to the Resolute once they're stable enough to move. Margaret, coordinate with Denny on the revival protocol. Ray, I want a full damage assessment for the insurance report. Tomás —"

She paused.

"Tomás, you're off watch. Get some sleep."

He wanted to argue. He wanted to stay. But he was tired — the kind of tired that lives in your bones, not your eyes — and Sofia's expression left no room.

"Yes captain."

He went back through the umbilical to the Resolute, to the familiar hum and warmth of his own ship. He passed Denny in the corridor, grease to the elbows and grinning. "Heard you found them, kid. Good eyes."

Tomás nodded and kept walking.

In his bunk, he lay in the dark and listened to the Resolute breathe around him. He thought about Eli Brennan, twenty-four years old, waking up after six months in a pod to find that someone had come. He thought about what Eli would say. He thought about whether Eli would understand what the last twelve hours had been like — the decision, the approach, the airlock, the cold.

He thought about Ray's question. *Your watch. What do you think?*

He thought about his answer. *Investigate.*

And he thought about the fact that it was the right answer, not because the data had told him so, but because he'd decided that the faint thermal signature of six people barely alive was worth more than the certainty of protocol. He'd bet his judgment against the numbers, and he'd won, and tomorrow he'd have to live with the fact that next time the judgment might be wrong.

But that was tomorrow's problem. Tomorrow, he'd wake up and there'd be a ship in tow and six people coming back to life and a decision that had become a story that would follow him through the fleet.

Tonight — this morning — whatever it was — he closed his eyes and slept.

---

He dreamed of silence, and it wasn't empty at all.
