# THE CARRY
## Episode 1: The First Load

---

The parcel weighed two kilograms.

Oskar Nel held it in both hands in the loading room at Gateway while Henra Volk read the manifest. He'd carried heavier — rescue equipment, rope coils, the specific and unforgettable weight of a body in a litter — but he held this one differently. The thermal case was rigid and cold despite the phase-change gel sealed inside. Donor tissue. Type-matched, harvest-confirmed, packed twelve hours ago at the Eastern University hospital in a sterile field by technicians who had been very careful and very fast.

A child in Westernhead needed what was in this case. The child had been waiting eleven days. The match had come through four days ago. The surgical window opened in fifty hours and closed in seventy-two.

Standard crossing from Meridian to Westernhead: four days. They had three.

Henra read the manifest twice. Her face was still. Then she folded the paper into quarters and put it in the inside pocket of her jacket, the one closest to her chest. She looked at Oskar. Then at Wren, who sat on the bench by the door, re-lacing her boots for the second time that morning.

"Three-day pace," Henra said. "We leave in twenty minutes."

Wren didn't look up from her laces. "I know."

Oskar set the case into his frame pack, nesting it between the tent and the insulated sleep gear in its padded sleeve. He adjusted the compression straps, felt the weight distribution settle across his shoulders and hips. The parcel was small but dense — two kilograms that shifted the pack's center of gravity by a fraction of a degree. He tightened the left hip strap a quarter inch and checked the buckles.

"Oskar," Henra said.

He looked up.

"Is the load secure?"

He paused. She wasn't telling him to stop checking. She was asking him if it was done, in the same tone she'd use on the route: once, direct, and then the question was closed.

"It's secure," he said.

"Then we're done here."

---

Twenty minutes later they stepped out of Gateway into the blue-dark of a mountain morning — four a.m., the stars still out, the air sharp with the smell of pine and cold stone. Meridian was quiet behind them. The supply depot was dark. The Broken Compass had closed three hours ago, and the last of the carrier crowd had staggered home through the alleys.

The first stretch was forty miles of forest climbing to The Gate. Pack animals could make this section. Day-hikers could make it. Henra had walked it two hundred and three times.

She set the pace without announcing it — a steady, metronomic stride that ate ground without burning legs. Wren moved ahead, twenty meters in front, reading the trail by headlamp. She was already humming. The sound was low, almost subsonic, a constant drone that floated back through the trees like a mechanical breath.

Oskar walked behind Henra. The pack weighed thirty-one kilograms fully loaded — the parcel, the shared gear, the extra rations, the rope and hardware, the medical kit. It was the heaviest load on the team by ten kilograms. This was by design. Oskar had requested it.

The trail switchbacked through old-growth forest, the trees so thick that the headlamp caught only the nearest trunks. The air smelled of resin and damp earth. Somewhere below, a creek moved over stones. Above, the sky was beginning to lighten at the edges, the first rumor of dawn.

They didn't speak. There was nothing to say yet. The forest was a corridor — you walked through it to get to the mountain. The work hadn't started.

---

They reached The Gate at noon.

The station sat at eight thousand feet, a stone cabin with a tin roof, built into a sheltered hollow where the tree line began to thin. The keeper — a wiry woman named Darya who'd been at The Gate for six years — had water heated and bread on the table. She looked at the team with the quick assessment that all keepers performed: bodies upright, gaits normal, gear sound, eyes clear.

"You're the new configuration," she said to Henra. It wasn't a question.

"First crossing together."

"Good team?"

Henra hung her pack on the wall peg and sat at the table. "We'll know in three days."

Darya poured tea. Wren took hers and went outside to sit on the flat rock behind the station, scanning the valley below and the ridge above with the slow, systematic gaze of someone memorizing terrain. She'd pulled her notebook from her chest pocket and was writing — quick, abbreviated notes in a hand nobody else could read. Snow conditions at the trailhead. Wind direction at the ridgeline. The specific quality of the light at altitude, which told her things the barometer couldn't.

Oskar checked the parcel. The thermal case was holding temperature. He logged the reading — 3.2°C, nominal range — and closed the pack. Then he sat with his back against the station wall and whittled. The knife was small, sharp, precise. The wood was pine — scrap from Darya's woodpile. A shape was emerging: a bird, maybe. His hands moved with the particular certainty of someone who didn't need to think about what they were doing.

Wren came back in. She looked at the bird in Oskar's hands. "What is it?"

"Finch."

"You always do animals."

"Animals are easy. They don't have expressions."

She sat down across from him and pulled her hat lower over her ears. She was cold. She was always cold — it was the first thing the other carriers noticed about her, the layers she wore, the way she pulled her hands inside her sleeves at stations, the permanent flush in her fingertips from poor circulation. At altitude, she shivered. Even at The Gate, at eight thousand feet in afternoon sun, she shivered.

"You should eat," Oskar said. Not looking up from the finch.

"I ate."

"You ate bread. Eat the meat too."

Wren looked at him. The look was precise and familiar — the look of a younger sister who resented being mothered and knew it came from a place that couldn't be argued with. She ate the meat.

Henra watched this from the table and said nothing. She was learning the shape of her team. Two hundred and three crossings, but never with these two in these positions. She knew Wren's work — she'd vouched for the girl eight years ago, when Wren was nineteen and underweight and refused to explain why she wanted to cross. Henra had seen something in her then. The specific quality of attention. She was seeing it now, refined by eight years and seventy-nine crossings into something that might be extraordinary.

Oskar she knew only on paper. Salaun had handed her the file with a look that meant *I'm recommending this one.* Eight years mountain rescue. High-angle, avalanche, wilderness medical. Two citations. One fatality — partner, cornice collapse. The personal statement in the file was four sentences long: *I was on the rope. The anchor held. The cornice didn't. I want to carry.*

Salaun's recommendations were never wrong. Henra would find out.

---

From The Gate to The Shelf was eight miles and three thousand five hundred feet of elevation. The forest fell away in stages — first the big pines, then the scrub birch, then the lichen, then nothing but rock and the first pale veins of vestite in the granite, catching the afternoon light like mica.

Wren had stopped humming.

Henra noticed. On the trail below The Gate, the drone had been constant — a background frequency that meant everything was normal, the way a pilot light means the gas is on. Now Wren was quiet. She was still twenty meters ahead, still moving at the same pace, but her body had changed. Her shoulders were forward. Her stride was shorter. She was reading something in the ground that Henra couldn't see from behind.

"Wren."

Wren stopped and turned. Her face was flushed from the climb, her eyes bright in the thinning air. She pointed at the trail ahead.

"Ice under the scree. Thin layer, from the melt-freeze cycle. It's not showing because the rock dust is covering it."

"How thick?"

"Thin enough to break under weight. Thick enough to slide."

"Switchback?"

"Stay left of the cairn. The rock is solid on the left. The right side is the slide path."

Henra looked at the cairn — a three-foot pillar of stacked stone, weathered smooth, old enough that the lichen had colonized it in a continuous pelt. The cairns were the route. Wren was telling her the cairn was right but the ground around it was wrong.

They went left. Oskar's boots found the solid rock and held. The pack swayed with each step, the thirty-one kilograms shifting gently, and he absorbed the movement with his knees and hips the way a sailor absorbs the sea — constantly, unconsciously, the body making a hundred small calculations per second that the mind never has to think about.

They reached The Shelf at dusk.

---

The Shelf sat on a broad ledge of exposed vestite at eleven thousand five hundred feet. The station was larger than The Gate — a two-room shelter with bunks for six and a stove that the keeper, a quiet man named Pavel, kept burning at a low, steady heat. The vestite in the rock face behind the station was clearly visible now: dark veins in the granite, pulsing faintly in the fading light with a charge that no one could see but everyone could feel. A low static prickled on exposed skin. Compasses still worked here. GPS did not. Wren had confirmed this by holding up her wrist compass and watching the needle hold true, then pulling out the small GPS unit the guild issued for the low sections and watching it spin.

She put it away. They wouldn't need it again.

Henra spread the route map on the table — a paper chart, hand-drawn, updated by hand each season based on station reports and carrier observations. The map was the guild's most valuable document, and every team carried a copy in a waterproof sleeve. Henra's copy was annotated in her own hand: notes on cairn positions, ice conditions, wind patterns, accumulated over twenty-four years of crossings.

She traced the route from The Shelf to The Notch. Six miles through a boulder field to the cut between the ridgelines. Wind funneling through the gap at sustained sixty, gusting higher. She'd crossed The Notch in wind so strong it had pressed her against the cliff face and held her there.

"Tonight we sleep," she said. "Tomorrow we cross The Notch, The Rib, and The Thumb. If we're fast, we reach The Saddle by late afternoon."

Wren was sitting cross-legged on her bunk, her notebook open. "The weather system."

"Teo said tomorrow evening."

"Teo's barometer is at The Step. That's sixty miles and six thousand feet below The Saddle. Conditions can change between here and there."

"I know."

Wren looked at her. This was the moment — the first test of how this team would work. Wren had been a scout for eight years. She had crossed seventy-nine times. She knew what she was seeing in the weather data. She also knew that Henra was the lead, and the lead decided, and the scout's job was to report, not to argue.

"The pressure gradient is steep," Wren said. "If it steepens overnight, the system accelerates. We could hit weather at The Thumb instead of The Saddle."

Henra looked at the map. The Thumb was at eighteen thousand four hundred feet, on a glacier, roped up, in the zone where even compasses started to fail. Weather at The Thumb was a different problem than weather at The Saddle.

"What does your gut say?"

Wren blinked. She hadn't expected the question. Scouts reported data. Leads made decisions. Asking for her gut was a different kind of request.

"My gut says the system is slow. Teo's right. We'll reach The Saddle before it does."

Henra folded the map. "Then we go."

---

They left The Shelf at four in the morning, headlamps cutting through a darkness that was absolute. No light pollution. No ambient glow. Only the stars, hard and bright in the thin atmosphere, and the beams of their lamps picking out the rock ahead in sharp, discrete slices.

The boulder field between The Shelf and The Notch was a landscape of broken stone — granite blocks the size of cars, scattered across a slope that rose steadily toward the gap in the ridgeline. The route was marked by cairns, but the cairns were widely spaced here, and the dark made distance hard to judge. Wren moved through the boulders like water, her small frame slipping through gaps that Oskar had to navigate around. She found each cairn without searching, as if she could feel them in the rock.

Henra followed, placing her feet with the unconscious precision of someone who had walked this terrain so many times that her body held the map without consulting her mind. She carried her compass in her left hand — a brass instrument with a heavy needle and a jewel bearing, old enough that the casing had worn smooth from the oils of her grandfather's skin before it had ever touched hers. The needle held steady despite the vestite. It always did.

Oskar brought up the rear. The pack was heavy. His breath came in measured, rhythmic pulls — in through the nose, out through the mouth, the respiration of someone who understood that at altitude, oxygen was a resource to be managed, not a gift to be wasted. His legs burned. Not from the weight — he could carry twice this — but from the elevation. Eleven thousand feet and climbing. The air was thinning. Each breath delivered less.

They reached The Notch as the first light hit the ridgeline above them.

The Notch was a cut in the rock — a narrow gap between two walls of shattered stone, maybe ten feet wide, funneling the atmosphere like a wind tunnel. The wind hit them before they reached the gap. It came in steady, sustained waves that pressed against their bodies and stole the warmth from their hands. Henra felt it in her chest — a cold so deep it seemed to bypass her skin and go straight to her lungs.

She stopped the team at the entrance to the gap. There, on the right-hand wall, was Mara Vest's cairn.

It was not large — three feet of stacked stone, unremarkable except for its age and its story. Mara Vest had died here. She had been sixty-one years old. She had been crossing to deliver a medical parcel. The cairn marked the place where she fell, or where they believed she fell, because her body had never been recovered and the only account came from her team's second, who had been below the gap when the storm hit and had spent the night in a snow cave, emerging the next morning to find the route empty and Mara Vest gone.

Henra touched the cairn. She did this every time. She did not think about it. Her hand went to the stone the way a body remembers prayer — muscle memory, devotion, and something deeper than either.

Wren touched it after her. Oskar after Wren.

They went through.

---

The wind in The Notch was everything the reports had promised. Sixty miles per hour, steady, with gusts that arrived without warning and shoved sideways against the body with enough force to shift a loaded pack. They moved single file, Wren first, then Henra, then Oskar, each person placing their feet on stone worn smooth by two centuries of carrier boots.

The gap was a quarter mile long. It took them forty minutes. At the far end, the ridgeline opened onto The Rib — the narrow spine of rock that connected The Notch to The Thumb, sixteen thousand eight hundred feet of exposure. The path along The Rib was perhaps two feet wide. The fall on either side was not vertical but close enough — slopes of broken shale and ice that ran for a thousand feet to the glacier basins below.

Wren stopped at the start of The Rib. She looked back at Henra.

"Wind is from the northwest," Wren said. "Consistent. No gusts right now."

"How's the path?"

"Clear. Cairns are visible. Ice on the left side, about a foot wide. Right side is dry."

Henra looked at the path. The Rib was the section where trust mattered most. The scout went first. If the scout missed something — a loose stone, a hidden ice patch, a cairn that had been knocked off its perch by a storm — the team followed her into whatever she'd missed. There was no room for second-guessing. Either you trusted your scout or you didn't.

Henra had crossed The Rib a hundred and eighty times. She had never crossed it with Wren on point.

She looked at Wren's face — the calm, the readiness, the way her eyes moved along the path in small, precise sweeps, reading every inch of stone.

"Go," Henra said.

Wren went.

She moved along The Rib like someone walking a line drawn in paint. Her feet were small and precise, each step landing flat and centered. Her body barely swayed. The pack on her back was light — she carried the team's lightest load, the tent and the rations, maybe twelve kilograms — and she used the lightness the way a dancer uses it: as permission to be exact.

Henra followed. The wind pushed at her right side. She leaned into it, a micro-adjustment that her body made without consulting her. Below, on either side, the slopes fell away into white. The sky above was a depthless blue, the kind of blue that only exists at altitude, where the atmosphere is too thin to scatter the light and the color goes straight through to something that looks like the back of the sky.

Oskar came last. On The Rib, the load carrier was the most exposed — the heaviest pack, the highest center of gravity, the most momentum if a foot slipped. Oskar knew this. He walked with his weight low, knees slightly bent, the thirty-one kilograms on his back a living thing that shifted and swayed with each step. He managed it the way he managed everything: with his hands, with his body, with the constant, background vigilance that never turned off.

They made The Rib in ninety minutes. No slips. No loose stone. No words. Just three bodies moving along a line of rock between two falls, trusting each other in the specific, nonverbal way that the route required.

---

The Thumb was a spire of granite rising from the glacier like a fist punched through the ice. The station was built into its base — a prefab shelter anchored to the rock with expansion bolts, wrapped in insulation, heated by a diesel stove. At eighteen thousand four hundred feet, the air was thin enough to degrade judgment, slow reaction times, and make simple tasks feel like puzzles.

They roped up before crossing the glacier. Wren on point, fifteen meters of rope between her and Henra, another fifteen between Henra and Oskar. The ice was blue and old, scarred with crevasses that opened and closed with the seasons. Wren probed ahead with a lightweight aluminum pole, testing each snow bridge before committing her weight.

The glacier crossing was four miles. It took three hours.

At The Thumb, they stopped. The keeper — a man named Ries, broad and sunburned, who had been at The Thumb for four years — assessed them with the practiced eye of a former carrier. He looked at Oskar's pack.

"Heavy?"

"Thirty-one kilos," Oskar said.

"For three days?"

"For three days."

Ries nodded. He heated water for tea and set out bread and dried fruit. They ate in the shelter, sitting on the floor because The Thumb had no table, just bunks and a stove and a shelf of Oskar's carvings — a fox, a bear, a mountain goat, each one left here on a previous crossing, each one precise and smooth, the wood grain following the animal's body like a map of its anatomy.

Henra checked the barometer on the wall. The needle was dropping. Not fast. But steady.

"Ries. How's the trend?"

"Started dropping yesterday. Slow. Consistent."

"Teo's note said the system would reach The Saddle tomorrow evening."

Ries shrugged. "Teo's at The Step. I'm at The Thumb. The weather between here and there doesn't always consult either of us."

Henra looked at the barometer. She pulled her grandfather's compass from her pocket and set it on her knee. The needle was starting to drift — not spinning, not failing, but drifting, a half-degree of wobble that would be invisible at lower altitude but that she could feel in the jewel bearing. Vestite interference. They were in the zone now.

"Rest," she said. "One hour. Then we go."

---

The Saddle was three miles from The Thumb, across a plateau of blue ice where the wind scoured the surface clean. At nineteen thousand one hundred feet, it was the highest point on the route, the highest inhabited structure on the continent, and the psychological crux of every crossing.

They were halfway across the plateau when the weather changed.

It didn't arrive. It materialized. One moment the sky was the high-altitude blue, depthless and cold. The next, a band of cloud had appeared from the northwest, low and dark, moving fast — faster than anything Henra had seen on this route in twenty-four years. The barometer at The Thumb had been dropping, but the gradient hadn't suggested this speed. The system was accelerating.

"Wren," Henra said.

Wren had stopped. She was standing fifteen meters ahead, her head up, watching the cloud line. The humming had stopped. The silence where the sound had been was louder than the wind.

"I see it," Wren said.

Henra pulled the compass. The needle was wobbling, the drift worse now, the vestite interference pulsing with the pressure change. She looked at the cloud line and did the arithmetic that every lead carrier carried in their bones: distance to The Saddle station — one and a half miles. Distance to The Cone — five miles of descending couloir. Time to The Saddle at current pace — forty minutes. Time for the weather to hit — she didn't know. The cloud line was moving in a way that suggested the system had found a corridor through the peaks, a path that the barometer at The Thumb hadn't predicted because the system wasn't following the model. It was making its own.

"Henra," Oskar said from behind. His voice was tight. Not with fear — with readiness. He was waiting for the call the way a rescue team waits for the alarm: coiled, prepared, needing only the word.

Henra looked at the cloud. She looked at the plateau ahead — the featureless expanse of blue ice, the cairns marking the route in a line of small stone piles that disappeared into the distance. She looked at Wren, who was still watching the sky with the focused stillness of someone listening to something only she could hear.

"Wren. Your read."

Wren turned around. Her face was flushed from cold and exertion, her eyes watering in the wind. She looked at Henra with an expression that was not fear and not confidence but the particular intensity of a scout who was about to say something that would determine whether three people walked or ran, held or pushed, lived or became names in a logbook.

"There's a gap," Wren said. "Between the front edge and the back of the system. The front is fast — it'll hit us in maybe twenty minutes. But it's thin. It'll pass. Behind it, there's maybe three, four hours of reduced visibility before the main body arrives."

"Reduced visibility. Not clear."

"Not clear. But passable. If we move now, we can make The Saddle before the front hits. Wait it out there — thirty minutes, maybe less — then cross to The Cone in the gap."

Henra looked at the sky. Wren was reading something up there — pressure, moisture, the way the cloud edges feathered and thickened — that Henra could see but not interpret at Wren's level. Henra had twenty-four years on this route. Wren had eight. But Wren had something Henra didn't: an intuition for weather that operated below the level of instruments, in the place where the air itself carried information.

Henra could make the call herself. She could hold the team on the plateau, wait for the front to pass, then move in the gap. Or she could turn them around, retreat to The Thumb, and wait for the system to clear — twelve hours, maybe more, and the child in Westernhead lost another twelve hours of a closing window.

She looked at Oskar. He was standing with his weight centered, the thirty-one-kilogram pack on his back, his face still. He was ready for whatever she decided. That was what his face said: ready.

"The parcel," she said. "How long on temperature?"

Oskar pulled the small thermometer from his chest pocket. "Forty-one hours of nominal range remaining."

Forty-one hours. If they held at The Thumb and waited twelve hours for the system to clear, they'd reach The Step with hours to spare. Barely. And if the system didn't clear in twelve hours — if it stalled, if it deepened, if the gap Wren was seeing closed or didn't exist — they'd lose the window. The tissue would expire. The child would wait.

"We go," Henra said. "Wren, set the pace. Fast as you can safely probe. We do not run on this ice."

Wren turned and moved.

---

They moved across the plateau at a pace that was almost a jog, Wren probing the ice ahead with rapid, precise thrusts of the aluminum pole, Henra behind her, Oskar behind Henra. The cloud line bore down from the northwest, eating the sky. The light changed — from the high blue to a gray-green pallor that flattened the terrain and erased the shadows, making the ice surface into a uniform, featureless sheet.

The front hit them one hundred meters from The Saddle station.

It hit like a wall. The wind went from thirty to seventy in three seconds, and the temperature dropped ten degrees in the same span. Snow arrived not in flakes but in a horizontal sheet of ice crystals that stung exposed skin and filled the air with a white hiss. Visibility went from a mile to nothing.

Henra felt the wind take her pack and pull. She leaned forward, weight on her toes, and kept moving. She could see Wren's shape ahead — a dark smudge in the white, barely visible, still probing. The cairns were gone. Buried or invisible. The route existed now only in Wren's memory and in the feet that had walked it.

Then the sound went away.

Not the wind — the wind was screaming, a sustained howl that vibrated in the chest. But the other sounds — the crunch of boots, the hiss of ice crystals, the clatter of gear — were swallowed. The air was so full of moving snow that sound couldn't propagate. Henra opened her mouth and breathed and the cold went into her lungs like a solid thing.

And then they were at The Saddle. Wren had found it — not by sight but by the specific change in the ice under her boots, the subtle texture difference that marked the station's foundation slab, and by the taut guideline that the keeper had strung from the station door to the last cairn, a rope at knee height that Wren's boot caught and her hand followed.

They piled through the door. Maren, the keeper, slammed it behind them. The wind screamed against the walls.

The Saddle station was the highest inhabited structure on the continent. It was small — four bunks, a stove, a counter, a window that was now a wall of white. The air inside was thin and cold. The stove burned with a blue flame that threw heat exactly three feet in any direction. Maren — a compact woman with gray hair and the broad hands of someone who maintained a building at nineteen thousand feet alone — was already pouring water for tea.

Henra stood inside the door and breathed. Her heart was hammering. Not from exertion — from the altitude, the cold, the adrenaline of the last twenty minutes. She could feel her pulse in her teeth. The silence of The Saddle — the absolute, total silence that existed between gusts when the wind dropped for a moment and the world held its breath — that silence was in her jaw, in her molars, a low thrumming that was her own blood moving through her own skull.

She could hear her heartbeat in her teeth. She had read this phrase in the station logs, in old carriers' accounts, in Mara Vest's journals. She had always thought it was a figure of speech. It was not. It was literal. At nineteen thousand feet, in the silence between gusts, with the blood pushed hard through a body that was working at the edge of its tolerance, the pulse became audible. Not in the ears. In the jaw. In the bones of the face. A sound that was not a sound but a vibration, the body's own engine made audible by the absence of everything else.

She looked at her team.

Wren was already at the window, watching the outside with the fixed attention of someone reading a text written in a language only she could see. Her humming had started again — low, steady, the pilot light back on.

Oskar had the pack off. He was checking the parcel. Thermometer: 2.8°C. Nominal. His hands were shaking — not from fear but from the cold, the adrenaline, the specific physiological tax of moving thirty-one kilograms across a plateau at nineteen thousand feet in a whiteout. He checked the temperature, logged it, and then sat on the floor with his back against the wall and closed his eyes.

Henra looked at Maren. "How long?"

Maren had been at The Saddle for nine years. She had seen weather systems arrive fast and slow, had held teams and waved them through, had watched the plateau through every season and every condition the high route could produce.

"The front is thin," she said. "It'll pass. Thirty minutes. Maybe forty."

"And behind it?"

"A few hours. Reduced visibility. Then the main body."

"Will the main body hit The Cone?"

Maren tapped the barometer on the wall. The needle was low and dropping. "I think it tracks north. Hits The Cone light, if at all. But I'm not certain."

Henra looked at the barometer. She looked at the whiteout outside the window. She looked at her team.

Oskar was sitting with his eyes closed, his hands on his knees, the stillness of a man conserving energy for whatever came next. Wren was at the window, watching, reading.

The child in Westernhead had thirty-nine hours.

"Oskar."

He opened his eyes.

"The parcel?"

"Nominal. Thirty-nine hours of range."

"We rest here for thirty minutes. Then we cross to The Cone in the gap." She looked at Wren. "You'll be on point. Visibility reduced. Cairns may be buried. Can you find the route?"

Wren turned from the window. Her face was calm. Not the calm of someone who wasn't afraid — the calm of someone who had been afraid and had put it away.

"I can find it."

---

They rested. Not comfortably — the bunks were narrow and cold, the stove's heat didn't reach past arm's length, and the wind never stopped screaming against the walls. But they sat, and they drank the tea Maren poured, and for twenty-five minutes they were still.

Henra sat on the floor with her grandfather's compass in her hand. The needle was drifting badly now — a five-degree wobble that made it useless for navigation. She held it anyway. The compass was not just an instrument. It was a weight in her hand, a thing her grandfather had carried and her mother had carried and that she had carried across this route two hundred and three times. It was a line. A continuity. The brass was warm from her palm.

Wren was humming again. The sound was different now — lower, slower, a frequency that Henra hadn't heard before. She realized, with a quiet surprise, that Wren was humming the sound of the wind outside. Not a song. Not a melody. A mirror. Wren was matching the frequency of the storm, the way a musician tunes to a room.

Oskar whittled. A small piece of pine from Maren's woodpile. His hands were steady now — the shaking had stopped. The knife moved in small, precise cuts. The shape was a bird. Henra couldn't tell what kind. It didn't matter. The carving was meditation. Oskar's way of being present without being tense.

He left the finished bird on the shelf by the door. A finch. It joined a line of other animals — Maren's collection, assembled over nine years of carriers passing through. Foxes, bears, goats, each one carved by different hands, each one left as a gift that said: *I was here. I made it to The Saddle. This is what my hands did when they had nothing else to do.*

---

The gap came. The wind dropped from seventy to forty. The snow thinned from a wall of white to a haze — still opaque, still flat, still capable of hiding a crevasse or a fissure — but navigable. Cairns were shadows, dark smudges in the white that appeared and disappeared like buoys in fog.

Wren found them. Every one. She moved ahead with the probe, testing the ice, and her body — small, low, close to the surface — read the terrain through her boots with a sensitivity that Henra had never seen in another scout. Wren didn't just find the route. She became the route. Her body was the line between The Saddle and The Cone, and the team followed her as if she were the thread and they were the needle.

The descent to The Cone was five miles of couloir — a steep gully that funneled everything that fell from the peaks above. Rock, ice, snow, wind. The couloir was the most dangerous section of the route in poor conditions because the gully collected debris and delivered it to the bottom at speed. Wren probed each step. Oskar moved with the pack braced against the slope, using his weight as an anchor, his legs absorbing the impact of each drop. Henra followed, watching the terrain above for any movement — rockfall, ice fall, the sloughing of snow from the walls.

They reached The Cone in two hours. The station sat on a moraine at the bottom of the couloir, half-buried in snow that the keeper — a man named Tamir — was in the process of shoveling. He saw them coming and stopped. Stood. Watched them descend the last hundred meters with the fixed assessment of a keeper who had been digging out this station for twelve years and knew exactly what a team looked like when it had been through weather and what it looked like when it hadn't.

They had been through weather.

He had water on. He had bunks ready. He didn't ask questions.

---

From The Cone, the route descended seven miles to The Step. The air thickened. The vestite interference dropped. Compasses began to hold true again. The world opened up — the western face of the Divide spreading below them in a panorama of ridges and valleys that caught the late-afternoon light and turned it gold.

They reached The Step as the sun was touching the western peaks.

The Step was the last high station — fourteen thousand eight hundred feet, a broad platform on the western face, looking out over the descent. The station was larger than any they'd passed: six bunks, a common room, a kitchen, a library of four hundred books on shelves that lined every wall. And Teo Maric.

Teo was standing at the door when they came down the last shelf. He was short, barrel-chested, bearded, with the thick fingers of a man who maintained a building at fifteen thousand feet in wind that would strip paint. He watched them come with the particular attention he gave every team — the assessment that had been wrong zero times in eleven years.

Henra stopped in front of him. She was tired. The tiredness was in her legs, in her shoulders, in the specific deep-muscle exhaustion that only altitude and cold and sustained tension could produce. But she was upright. Her eyes were clear.

"Teo."

"Henra."

He looked past her at Wren and Oskar. Wren's face was windburned, her lips cracked. Oskar's pack was still on his back — thirty-one kilograms that he'd been carrying for thirty hours.

"Inside," Teo said. "Food first. Then I look at you."

He had coffee. The best coffee on the route, confirmed by every carrier who had passed through, a fact that was not subjective but documented in the station logs going back eleven years. He poured three cups. They sat at the table and drank. The coffee was hot and strong and it tasted like something that mattered.

Oskar set the pack in the corner and checked the parcel. 2.4°C. Nominal. Thirty-one hours remaining. He logged it. Closed the notebook. Sat down.

Teo set a plate of rice and dried meat in front of each of them. They ate without talking. The sound of forks on plates. The wind outside. The stove ticking.

Then Teo sat across from Henra and looked at her.

"The system."

"Faster than your note said."

"I saw. The barometer dropped three millibars in an hour this afternoon. I was about to radio The Cone when you came through."

"You'd have told us to hold."

"I'd have told you what I saw. You'd have decided."

Henra looked at him. Twenty-four years. One hundred and forty crossings together. The trust between them was not a feeling. It was a structure, built from a thousand small decisions in which each of them had been right when it mattered and honest when they weren't.

"How's the descent?"

"Clear. Some ice above The Door, but the cairns are good. You'll make Westernhead by tomorrow afternoon."

Henra nodded. She pulled the compass from her pocket and set it on the table. The needle was holding true again — the vestite interference dropping off as they descended below the worst zone.

Teo looked at the compass. Then at Henra. He didn't ask about the crossing. He could see it in her face — the particular tightness around the eyes that meant she'd made a hard call and the call had been right and the rightness didn't make it easier.

He reached under the counter and pulled out a bottle. Amber glass, no label. He poured one glass and set it in front of Henra.

She picked it up. Held it. Looked at it.

"One glass," she said.

"One glass," he said.

She drank it. The whiskey burned a line from her throat to her stomach, a heat that unfurled in her chest. She set the glass down.

Wren and Oskar were watching. Not the whiskey — Henra. They were seeing something they hadn't seen on the trail: the small, private ritual between a carrier and a keeper, between two people who had been on this route long enough to need nothing from each other except presence. The whiskey was not a celebration. It was a punctuation mark. The hard part was done. The descent remained.

Teo looked at Wren. Then at Oskar. "He'll check the parcel twice more before morning," he said to Henra.

"I know."

"Good man. Bad sleeper."

"Aren't we all."

Teo almost smiled. He poured himself a cup of coffee — no whiskey, he didn't drink the stuff, just kept the bottle for others — and sat with them in the quiet. Outside, the wind moved across The Step like a hand across a table, steady and purposeful, carrying the smell of ice and the last of the light.

---

They descended from The Step at dawn. Twelve miles of steep trail through scree and scrub, the western weather closing around them in a mantle of fog and light rain that made the rocks slick and the footing treacherous. Wren went first. She was tired — they all were — but her feet found the holds, and her probe found the ice beneath the scree, and the team moved down the mountain in the same configuration they'd maintained for two days: scout, lead, load.

The Door was a cabin in the first trees on the western side. Ten thousand two hundred feet. The air was thick. The world was green. The smell of wet earth and pine was so strong after two days of rock and ice that it hit them like a wall.

The keeper at The Door — a young woman named Suki, new to the station, formerly a carrier — had hot food and dry clothes. She took one look at them and said, "How long since you slept?"

"Teo let us have four hours," Oskar said.

Suki set out blankets.

From The Door, it was thirty miles through forest to Westernhead. They walked it in twelve hours, through rain that never quite committed to falling, under a canopy of green that closed over the trail and shut out the sky. The world was soft. The trail was dirt, not ice. The cairns were wooden posts, not stone. The altitude was gone. The vestite was gone. The wind was a breeze.

Henra's knee ached. The right one, the rebuilt one, the one with the patchwork of grafts and scar tissue. It had held through the crossing — it always held — but the descent had been harder than the climb, and now, on the flat, it throbbed with each step. She noted it. She would write it in her notebook tonight. The numbers. The pain scale. The recovery time. The slow, patient arithmetic of a body measuring its own decline.

She did not limp. She walked.

---

They delivered the parcel at the Westernhead Guild Hall at seven in the evening, thirty-one hours before the surgical window closed.

A guild steward signed for it. A medical courier was waiting — a woman in a white coat with an insulated transport box and a face that said she had been waiting for hours and was not going to wait any longer. The parcel changed hands: from Oskar's pack to the courier's box to the waiting vehicle. The vehicle left. The parcel was gone.

Oskar stood in the hall after the courier left and felt the absence of the weight. Not the thirty-one kilograms — his pack was still on his back, lighter now by exactly two. Two kilograms. The mass of a thermal case containing donor tissue, which is to say: lighter by everything that mattered.

He set the pack down.

Wren was sitting on the bench by the door, her notebook open, drawing. Not route observations — a picture. The Saddle station, seen from the plateau, with the cloud line above it and the cairns running in a line across the ice. She drew with quick, sure strokes. The proportions were exact. The sky was right — the specific gray-green of a weather front, rendered in pencil shades that shouldn't have been able to capture that color but somehow did.

She did not show it to anyone.

Henra signed the crossing log. Two hundred and four. She set the pen down and stood in the hall — the western hall, smaller than the eastern one, warmer, smelling of wood and paper and the faint sweetness of the soap the steward used on the floors — and she was done.

The team stood together for a moment. Not in formation. Not in any ritual way. Just three tired people in a hallway, with the mountains behind them and a bed ahead.

"First crossing," Oskar said.

"First crossing," Henra said.

Wren looked up from her drawing. "The Saddle was close."

"It was," Henra said.

Wren held her gaze. "You trusted the read."

"I trusted the scout."

Something moved between them — not warmth, not gratitude, not the dramatic resolution of a story that wanted to be finished. Something smaller and more durable. The first thread of the thing that would, over many crossings, become the specific and irreplaceable trust that a carrier team builds in the mountains, where your life is in the hands of the person ahead of you and the person behind you and no one else.

Wren went back to her drawing. Oskar went to find food. Henra stood alone in the hall for another minute, looking at the route map on the wall — the western copy, less detailed than hers, drawn by a hand she didn't know — and then she went to find her bunk.

Outside, it was raining in Westernhead. The rain fell on the rooftops and the cobblestones and the green hills that rolled west toward the sea. The Divide was invisible behind the clouds — a wall of stone and ice hidden in the weather, its peaks lost in the dark, its passes still marked by cairns that two hundred years of hands had built and rebuilt and would build again.

The child was in surgery by morning. The tissue took.

Nobody in the guild would ever know this. The load was delivered. The crossing was logged. The result was someone else's story — a hospital, a family, a child who would grow up without ever knowing that three people had carried a piece of someone across a mountain range so that the distance between illness and survival could be measured in days instead of never.

Henra slept. In the morning, she would begin planning the return crossing.

The mountains would be there.

They always were.
