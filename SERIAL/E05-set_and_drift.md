# The Long Line
## Episode 5: Set and Drift

---

The net came in clean at 0640, and Tomás whooped when the numbers hit the display.

It was a good tow. Not a great tow — a great tow was the kind of number that made everyone go quiet and recalculate their ideas about the season. This was just a good tow. Solid. The kind of number that had become normal.

Three months off the season line and the Resolute was posting the kind of catches Gus used to log in his good years. Sofia had found it — the rhythm, the timing, the way you read a thermal and set your line and waited with just enough patience. She still asked Ray before she committed to a set, but the questions had changed. They weren't *should I?* anymore. They were *am I right that—* and Ray would nod, or shake his head, or grunt, which was the same as nodding.

The crew had gelled. That was the word Margaret used — *gelled*, like it was a chemical process, which in a way it was. Five people in a 51-foot equivalent boat, working sixteen-hour days, sleeping in racks three feet apart. Either you became a unit or you became a problem. The Resolute had become a unit.

Tomás was on the hauler, running the catch through the sorter with the efficiency of someone who'd done it a thousand times, which he now had. Margaret was at the systems console, logging tonnage and composition, her handwriting on the paper backup as precise as a typesetter's. Ray was in the wheelhouse, drinking the coffee Margaret considered a moral failing, watching the scope for the next set line. Sofia was on deck with Tomás, boots planted, working the array leads.

Denny was in the engine room.

That was where you'd find Denny at 0640 on a working morning — the way you'd find a clock's mainspring in its housing. Exactly where it belonged, providing the tension that kept everything else moving. He was humming. He always hummed in the engine room. Old station radio hits from twenty years ago, the kind of music that gets into your head as a kid and never leaves. The hum was diagnostic. Margaret had said that once, as a joke, and then realized it was true. When Denny hummed, the engine room was fine. When he stopped, something was wrong.

Nothing was wrong. The propulsion system ran at its steady 94% rated output, which was the number Denny had coaxed it to over twelve years of tweaking and which he could have pushed to 96 if he'd wanted to shorten the maintenance cycle, which he didn't, because Denny understood that a boat was a marathon, not a sprint. The cooling loop held pressure. The coupling seals held vacuum. Everything worked.

The morning smelled like ozone and coffee and the faint organic sweetness of the catch coming through the processor. Tomás was hungry, which was not news. Margaret was due for her tea break. Ray's cup was empty again. Sofia was planning the next set, her journal open on the deck box, wind notes in her careful handwriting.

It was a Tuesday. It was a good Tuesday.

---

The message arrived at 0307 on Thursday.

Denny was asleep. Not in his bunk — in the engine room, on the cot he'd rigged between the number-two coolant pump and the bulkhead, the one Margaret called "the coffin" because it was narrow and dark and she couldn't understand how anyone slept there. Denny slept there because the sound of the pumps was the sound of everything working, and everything working was the only thing that had ever reliably put him to sleep.

The console chimed. Not the ship's system — the personal channel, the one tied to his ident, the one that carried messages from stationside. He never got messages at 0307. He rarely got messages at all.

He sat up. The blanket pooled around his waist. The engine room was warm, lit by the always-on systems indicators — green and amber dots in the dark, a constellation he knew by heart. The console chimed again.

He crossed the three feet of deck plate and read the header.

MERIDIAN FAMILY COURT — ADMINISTRATIVE NOTICE.
RE: PARK, R. (DECEASED) — GUARDIANSHIP STATUS OF MINOR.

He read it again.

He read it a third time.

Rachel. Dead. His ex-wife, Rachel. Dead at forty-three, which was two years younger than him, which was the kind of fact the brain seizes on when it can't process the fact next to it.

The message was brief. Administrative. The way these things always are — the most life-altering information delivered in the flattest language, because the system doesn't know you and doesn't care. *Rachel Park (née Torres), resident of Meridian, deceased. Next of kin: minor child, Liliana Park, age 14. Guardianship transfer required. As sole surviving parent, you are listed as default guardian. Child transport to Gliese Station arranged via civilian carrier, ETA 72 hours from dispatch. Please confirm receipt and coordinate with station social services upon arrival.*

Lily. Fourteen. He'd done the math every year on her birthday — she'd be this age now, she'd be in this grade now, she'd look like this now. He'd done the math and he'd never done anything about the math, because doing something about it would have meant going back, and going back would have meant explaining, and explaining would have meant being in a room with a girl he'd abandoned and saying the words that would make it OK.

He didn't have those words. He didn't deserve to have them.

Six years. She'd been eight. She'd been sitting at the kitchen table doing homework — fractions, he remembered, because he'd seen the worksheet on the table as he was walking out and fractions was something he could help with, something simple, and he'd almost stopped. He'd almost sat down. He'd kept walking instead.

He'd been drunk. Not falling-down drunk — functioning drunk. The kind where you can hold a job and hold a conversation and hold it together right up until you can't, and what you can't hold is your daughter. He'd known that. He'd known it with the terrible clarity that drunks sometimes get — the honesty that doesn't save you but at least keeps you from destroying the person you love most. He left because leaving was the last good thing he could do for her.

He got sober on the Resolute. Nine years ago. Margaret found him shaking in the engine room on day three of a run and didn't say a word. She brought him water and a blanket and sat with him through it. Nobody asked. He never offered. The Resolute didn't carry alcohol anyway, so it was easy in the way things are easy when the temptation is physically impossible.

But he never went back.

Rachel didn't want him back — that had been clear from the start, and she'd been right. Full custody. No visitation. He sent money when he could. He didn't send messages. He didn't call. He told himself this was respecting Rachel's wishes, which was true, and also a cowardly excuse, which was also true. Both things had been true for six years.

Now Rachel was dead and Lily was fourteen and she was coming to Gliese Station in seventy-two hours and Denny was her father, which was a technical fact that felt like a lie.

He closed the message. He sat down on the cot. The pumps hummed.

He didn't.

---

Denny didn't tell anyone.

He processed the message the way he processed a failing coupling — assessed the damage, identified the options, and then went quiet while he worked. This was how Denny dealt with things. He went internal. The engine room had a door, and the door could be closed, and when it was closed Denny was in his own country, unreachable, working through whatever needed working through.

But the engine room had systems, and systems had logs. And Denny had a crew that could read him the way he read the propulsion system — by sound, by vibration, by the feel of the deck plate under their boots.

Margaret noticed first.

It was the humming. Or rather, the absence of it. Margaret was in the junction room two compartments aft, re-wiring a degraded power relay, when she realized the ambient sound had changed. The pumps were fine. The coolant loop was nominal. The thing that was missing was Denny's voice — the low, tuneless hum that was always there, the way breathing is always there.

She paused, wire stripper in hand, and listened. Nothing. Just the ship.

She finished the relay. She went to the galley. She made two mugs of tea. She walked to the engine room door and knocked once, and when Denny opened it she handed him the tea without a word. He took it. He said thanks. He didn't look at her.

She looked at his face. She'd known Denny for twelve years. She knew his resting expression — open, alert, slightly amused, like he was always about to tell you something. His face now was closed. Shutters down. The engine room behind him was running fine. Denny was not.

She didn't ask. She went back to the galley. She made extra food at dinner — the soy-braised protein from the stores that Tomás complained smelled like wet dog. She put a plate outside the engine room door on a crate. It was there, untouched, three hours later. She took it back. Washed the plate. Said nothing.

---

Ray noticed second.

It was the repair logs. The Resolute ran on a dual system — electronic logs that the ship maintained automatically, and paper logs that each crew member kept by hand. Ray had started the paper system thirty years ago on his first boat and never stopped. The paper logs were the real history of the ship, in Ray's opinion. The system recorded what happened. The logs recorded what it meant.

Denny's paper logs were a model of consistency. Every repair, every adjustment, every tweak — logged in his quick, slanted handwriting, with the date, the system, the action, and a note if there was something to watch. Ray read them every morning over coffee. They were more reliable than the electronic logs because Denny included his reasoning, not just his actions.

Thursday morning: no entry.

Friday morning: no entry.

Two missed entries in twelve years.

Ray went looking for Denny at the end of watch. Found him in the engine room, elbow-deep in the number-three coupling housing, doing maintenance that didn't need doing. The coupling was fine. Denny was checking it anyway, running his hands over the housing the way a blind person reads a page — slowly, thoroughly, feeling for something that wasn't there.

Ray leaned against the doorframe. He watched for a minute.

"How's the coupling?"

"Fine."

"Anything I should know about?"

"No."

Ray looked at the back of Denny's head. Denny didn't turn around. He kept his hands on the coupling.

Ray pulled the folding stool from the wall bracket, opened it, and sat down. He didn't say anything else. He just sat there, in the engine room doorway, watching Denny work on a coupling that didn't need working on. The engine room hummed around them. The systems panel blinked its steady greens.

After twenty minutes, Ray stood up, folded the stool, put it back in the bracket. He touched the doorframe once with his palm — a gesture that meant nothing and everything — and left.

Denny still hadn't turned around.

---

Sofia noticed third.

She didn't notice Denny's mood — she noticed the diagnostic. The Resolute ran a full systems diagnostic every seventy-two hours, initiated from the engine room. It was Denny's task. He'd done it every three days for twelve years without missing one. It was the single most reliable recurring task on the ship, more reliable than the sunrise, because the ship didn't have a sunrise.

Saturday morning, the diagnostic didn't run.

The system flagged it on Sofia's console at 0715 — DIAGNOSTIC CYCLE OVERDUE: ENGINE ROOM. She stared at it. She pulled the log. Denny had never missed a diagnostic. Not once. Not when he had the flu two seasons ago. Not when the number-two pump failed during a tow and he spent fourteen hours straight fixing it. He ran the diagnostic. Always.

She went to the wheelhouse. Ray was there, watching the scope.

"Denny missed the diagnostic."

Ray didn't look up. "I know."

"What's going on?"

Ray was quiet for a moment. He picked up his coffee mug. Set it down. The coffee was cold.

"Something's wrong," Ray said. "He'll tell us when he tells us."

"Should I—"

"No."

Sofia nodded. She went back to the deck. She did the work. She ran the next tow. She watched the engine room door.

At the end of watch, she went down.

She didn't knock. She walked in the way you walk into a room that belongs to someone you trust — openly, without announcement. Denny was sitting on the cot, elbows on his knees, staring at the deck plate between his boots. The engine room was clean. Everything in order. Tools racked. Systems green. The only thing out of place was Denny.

She stood there. He looked up.

"What's going on?"

She said it the way she said everything — directly, without ornament. She'd learned the crew's languages over the past season. She could do Margaret's economy. She could do Ray's patience. She could do Tomás's openness. But her own style — the one she'd settled into, the one that fit — was plain questions and honest attention.

Denny looked at her. He opened his mouth. Closed it. He rubbed his face with both hands, the way you do when you're trying to wipe something off that isn't on your skin.

"I got a message Thursday."

"What kind of message?"

"My ex-wife died."

Sofia didn't move. Didn't react. She stood in the engine room doorway and took it the way the deck takes a wave — absorbing the weight without shifting.

"I'm sorry."

Denny nodded. He was looking at the deck plate again. "She was forty-three."

Sofia waited. She'd learned when to wait. Ray had taught her that without ever sitting her down and teaching it.

"I have a daughter," Denny said. "Lily. She's fourteen. She's on Meridian. She's — they're bringing her here. To Gliese. Seventy-two hours, or maybe less now. Because I'm next of kin. Because Rachel doesn't have anyone else. Because—"

He stopped. His jaw worked. He pressed his palms together hard enough to whiten his knuckles.

"I haven't seen her in six years."

Sofia sat down on the deck. Cross-legged, the way Tomás sat when he was eating. She put herself below Denny's eye line, which was a thing she did instinctively — making herself smaller, less imposing, more present.

"Tell me," she said.

He told her. All of it. The drinking. The leaving. The sober years on the Resolute that were supposed to be the first step back and became the whole life instead. The money he'd sent. The messages he hadn't sent. The birthdays he'd counted in his head and missed in reality. Rachel, who he'd loved and failed and never made it right with. Lily, who was eight the last time he saw her and who was fourteen now and who was coming to a space station in however many hours, and he didn't know how to be her father. He didn't know if he could be. He didn't know if she'd want him to try.

Sofia listened. She didn't interrupt. She didn't offer comfort, because comfort wasn't what Denny needed. Denny needed to say it out loud, to another person, because keeping it in the engine room was killing him the way a sealed pressure valve kills a line — not with a burst but with a slow, grinding failure that you don't notice until the whole system drops.

When he was done, Sofia was quiet for a long time.

"Does the crew know?"

"They know something's wrong."

"Margaret left food outside your door."

"I know. I wasn't hungry."

"Ray sat with you."

"For a while."

"OK," Sofia said. She stood up. "Here's what's going to happen. You're going to tell Ray and Margaret. Not because they need to know — because they've been worried about you, and they deserve to know why. Then we're going to figure out the practical things. Because there are practical things, and practical things are what this crew does."

Denny looked at her. She looked back.

"OK," he said.

---

Denny told them at dinner.

The galley was the only place on the Resolute where all five of them fit, and even then it was a squeeze — Tomás on the crate, Margaret standing against the counter because she preferred to, the rest around the table. Margaret had made soup. It was good soup. Margaret's cooking was always good, a fact she would never acknowledge because acknowledging it would be sentimental, and sentimentality was a margin of error she didn't allow.

Denny waited until they were eating. Then he said it.

"I have a daughter. She's fourteen. Her name is Lily. Her mother died last week. She's being transported here."

The galley was quiet for exactly as long as it took Tomás to put his spoon down, which was three seconds.

"I haven't seen her in six years," Denny said. "I left when she was eight. I was drinking. I stopped drinking nine years ago, but I didn't go back. Her mother had custody. I sent money. That was it."

He said it plainly, without excuse or embellishment. The galley absorbed it the way the galley absorbed everything — in the sound of the ventilator and the warmth of the soup and the steady hum of the ship around them.

Margaret set her mug down. "When does she arrive?"

"Seventy-two hours from Thursday. Maybe less now."

Margaret nodded. She looked at Sofia. Sofia looked at Margaret. Something passed between them — not words, not a plan, just the shared recognition of two women who had run a ship together long enough to communicate in glances.

"I'll reconfigure the bunk room," Margaret said. "We can put a third rack on the port side. The load-bearing stud is already there. I'll need two hours."

"I'll help," Tomás said.

"You'll hold things," Margaret said. "I'll build."

Ray hadn't spoken. He was looking at Denny across the table with an expression that wasn't pity — Ray didn't do pity — and wasn't surprise. It was recognition. The look of a man who understood what it meant to lose someone and be left with the shape of their absence.

"Ray," Sofia said.

Ray picked up his spoon. "I'll adjust the watch rotation. She'll need you for the first few days. We can run four-handed until she's settled."

Denny stared at him. "You don't have to—"

"I know I don't have to," Ray said. "This is what we're doing."

Tomás was doing math on his fingers under the table, which he thought no one could see. "I can hot-bunk," he said. "I'm on watch different hours than — I mean, I don't need a dedicated rack. I can take the engine room cot when Denny's not using it. She can have my bunk."

"Tomás," Margaret said.

"What? It makes sense. The hours line up."

Margaret looked at him. Her expression didn't change, but something in her eyes did — a softening so slight it was like watching steel flex. "That's generous," she said. Which from Margaret was like a standing ovation.

"I can adjust the budget," Sofia said. She'd pulled her journal out. She was already writing. "A dependent changes our cost structure. There are station subsidies for families on fishing vessels — I saw it in the quota regulations. We can apply for a supplemental allowance. It won't be much, but it'll offset food and quarters."

She looked up from the journal. "Denny."

Denny was very still. He was looking at the table — at the five bowls of soup, at the hands of the people around him. Margaret's scarred hands. Ray's broad hands. Tomás's big nervous hands. Sofia's small precise ones. All of them doing something. Planning. Building. Offering.

"I don't know if she'll want me," Denny said. His voice was very steady. The kind of steady that takes everything you have. "She might not want to be here. She might not want any of this."

"Then she'll go back," Ray said. "And we'll deal with that too. But she's coming, and you're her father, and we'll be ready."

It was the most Ray had said at once in weeks. It was the most direct thing he'd said about family since Bess died. He said it to his soup.

Denny put his face in his hands. He didn't cry. He just stayed there, breathing, while the galley hummed around him and Margaret got up to make more tea and Tomás ate a second bowl of soup because he was always hungry and that was fine, that was Tomás, that was the Resolute — where people didn't fall apart because other people held the boat together while you needed them to.

---

The civilian carrier docked at Waypoint Station at 1430 on Sunday.

The Resolute had made port two hours earlier, which was unusual — Sofia generally timed arrivals to minimize station fees, and that meant cutting things close. This time she'd built in margin. Nobody asked why. Everybody knew why.

Denny was on the dock. He'd changed into a clean shirt, which Margaret had noticed because Denny changing his shirt for anything other than work was an event of geological significance. He'd shaved. He'd tried to do something with his hair, which had given up about halfway through and settled into its usual state of functional disarray.

He stood at the docking bay with his hands in his pockets and his shoulders pulled in, which made him look smaller than he was. Sofia and Tomás were on the Resolute's deck, running the post-transit checks they'd normally do tomorrow. Ray was in the wheelhouse, ostensibly doing paperwork. Margaret was in the engine room, ostensibly checking the coolant pressure. They were all watching the dock.

The carrier was a small transport — a shuttle, really, the kind that moved people and light freight between stations on scheduled runs. Its hatch opened with the pneumatic sigh that all station hatches make, a sound like a long, slow exhale.

Four people came out. A station handler. Two passengers with their own business. And a girl.

She was small for fourteen. She had a bag — one bag, a duffel, carried in both hands in front of her like a shield. Dark hair pulled back. Jeans and a station-issue jacket that was too big for her. She stopped on the dock and looked around with the particular expression of someone who has been transported to a place they've never been and is trying to decide how to feel about it.

Her face was her mother's.

Denny saw that and something happened to him that he didn't have a name for and didn't try to name. It was like a coupling giving way — not a failure, but a release. Something that had been holding tight for a very long time letting go.

She saw him. She'd been told, presumably. Social services would have told her who to look for. Would have said *your father is Denny Park, he's a mechanic on the F/V Resolute, he'll meet you at the dock.* She knew his face from photos, which is not the same thing as knowing a face, but it's what she had.

She walked over. She stopped about four feet away. Close enough to talk. Far enough to run.

"Denny," she said.

Not Dad. Not Mr. Park. Denny.

"Lily," he said.

They stood there. The dock hummed around them — station noise, handler calls, the clank of cargo being moved. The carrier's hatch closed behind her. She was here.

"You look different than the pictures," she said.

"Yeah. Older."

"I guess."

He didn't reach for her. He didn't try to hug her. He understood — with the clarity he wished he'd had six years ago and ten years ago and every day in between — that this was not a moment for reaching. This was a moment for standing still and being exactly where he was and letting her decide how close to come.

"I'm on a boat," he said. "The Resolute. Do you want to see it?"

She looked at him. She had her mother's eyes, and her mother's way of looking at someone — direct, evaluating, not unkind but not generous either. She was deciding. She was fourteen, and she'd just lost her mother, and she'd been put on a shuttle to a space station by a system that didn't know her, and the only person waiting at the other end was a man she hadn't seen since she was eight.

"OK," she said.

He turned. She followed. She carried her own bag. He didn't offer to carry it, because offering would have been reaching, and he wasn't reaching. He was walking.

They came down the dock toward the Resolute. Sofia saw them first. She nudged Tomás. They both went back to work — checking lines, checking valves, doing the things they'd do at any port call.

Denny brought Lily up the ramp. She stepped onto the deck and looked around. She took it in — the equipment, the net arrays, the worn deck plate, the smell of old metal and catch residue and engine grease. Her face didn't change. She didn't say anything.

Margaret appeared in the engine room hatch. She had a rag in her hand. She was wiping a gauge that was already clean. She looked at Lily. Lily looked at her.

"I'm Margaret," Margaret said. "There's tea on."

She went back inside. Lily watched her go.

Ray came down from the wheelhouse. He walked past them both, heading for the dock to handle the port paperwork. He paused at the top of the ramp. He looked at Lily the way he looked at new crew — assessing, not unkind, with the quiet recognition of someone who understood what it meant to be in a strange place with strange people and no way back.

"Welcome aboard," he said.

He went on about his business.

Tomás was coiling a line on the foredeck. He smiled at Lily as she passed. It was a Tomás smile — big, open, involuntary. He couldn't help it. It was how he was built.

"Hey," he said.

Lily looked at him. For a second — just a second — something flickered in her expression. Not a smile. But not a wall either. The faintest recognition that she was in a place where people were being normal at her, which was better than people being sad at her, which was what she'd expected.

Sofia was at the deck box, writing in her journal. She closed it when they came aboard. She didn't introduce herself. She just nodded — a small, precise nod that said *I see you. You're here. This is a boat. We're working.* Then she went back to her journal.

Denny stood in the middle of the deck, not doing anything, which was the most unnatural thing Denny had ever done on the Resolute. He was waiting for Lily to tell him what came next, because he didn't know, and she didn't know either, and they stood there in the not-knowing together.

"I'll show you where you'll sleep," he said finally.

"OK."

He led her below. Margaret had built the third rack — clean work, precise, bolted to the load-bearing stud with the same care she gave every system on the ship. There was a blanket folded on it. Fresh. Margaret's touch.

Lily put her bag on the rack. She sat down. She looked at the ceiling, which was low, which was the ceiling of every bunk room on every fishing boat that had ever existed.

"It's small," she said.

"Yeah."

"Mom said you lived on a boat."

"Yeah."

"She said you were good at fixing things."

Denny sat on his own rack, across from her. He put his hands on his knees. He looked at his daughter, who was fourteen, who had her mother's face, who was sitting on a bunk that Margaret had built in two hours because that's what the crew did when something needed doing. They did it.

"I'm going to try," he said. "I want you to know that. I'm going to try to do this right."

Lily looked at him. She didn't say anything for a long time.

"You can start by showing me where the food is," she said. "I'm starving."

It was the kind of thing Tomás would say. The kind of thing the Resolute did to people — it brought them in through the stomach, through the work, through the small practical things that were easier than feelings.

Denny stood up. He led her to the galley.

Above them, the Resolute hummed. The crew worked. The systems ran green. The boat held.

On the dock, Ray finished the port paperwork and walked back up the ramp. He paused at the deck. He looked at the wheelhouse, at the scope, at the grounds ahead. He thought about Bess. He thought about the girl. He thought about how a boat changes — not all at once, not dramatically, but the way a hull changes over years of use. Taking on the shape of the water. The shape of the work. The shape of the people who live inside it.

The Resolute was different now. Not better. Not worse. Different. One more person inside the hull. One more life to carry.

The line goes out. The line comes back.
