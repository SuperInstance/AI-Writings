# THE LONG LINE
## Series Bible

*"The line goes out. The line comes back. That's the whole life."*
— Captain Ray Hutchins

---

## 1. THE WORLD

### The colonized system: Gliese Station

Humanity didn't conquer the stars. It moved in, the way it always has — gradually, practically, because there was work to do.

Gliese is a binary star system, four generations settled. Two hundred and twelve years since the first long-haul ship dropped anchor. The population sits around nine million, spread across seven orbital stations, two rocky inner planets, and dozens of smaller outposts tied to the belt economies. Nobody calls it a "colony" anymore. It's just home.

The system breaks into zones:

**The Inner** — Meridian and Cable, the two populated planets. Meridian is the seat of government, the regulatory bodies, the universities. Cable is industrial — refining, manufacturing, the shipyards. Most people live here. Most people have never been past the fence.

**The Middle Ground** — the orbital stations. Waypoint, Tucker's, The Cofferdam, and half a dozen smaller platforms. These are port towns. Fishermen, miners, haulers, suppliers. Bars, chandleries, repair shops, crew agencies. Everything smells like recirculated air and fried food. If you want to know what's happening in the belt, you hear it at a station bar first.

**The Grounds** — the asteroid belts and deep-space harvesting zones. This is where the work happens. Two main belts (the Near Line and the Far Line) and a scattering of rogue fields that shift with orbital cycles. The Grounds are where the Resolute lives.

### The economy

The system runs on protein and ore.

Deep-space harvesting — "fishing," everyone calls it — extracts organic compounds from asteroid-bound biota and from the massive, slow-drifting organic clouds that collect in the system's Lagrange regions. These are processed into food stock, pharmaceuticals, and industrial organics. It's dangerous, skilled labor done from small vessels in open space. It has always been done this way. It will continue to be done this way until the last fisherman hangs up their suit.

Mining operates similarly — small crews, small ships, long shifts. The two industries share culture, share bars, share graveyards.

The market is quota-based. The Conservation Authority sets limits per zone per season. Quotas are bought, leased, inherited, and fought over. They are the single most important economic unit in a fishing family's life. The Resolute's quota has been in the family for three generations.

Currency is the chit — system-credit, portable, universal. A good season on the Resolute might clear 340,000 chit split four ways after expenses. A bad season puts you in debt to the station.

### The culture

Fishing culture is its own thing. It has always been its own thing, wherever humans fish.

Fishers talk different. They move different. They measure time in seasons, not years. They trust the person next to them in a pressure suit more than they trust any government. They are superstitious in ways that look like religion and practical in ways that look like engineering. They name their boats like they name their children. They bury their dead in the belt if they have to and they don't talk about it pretty.

There's a class divide. Inner system — government, academia, corporate — looks at belt workers as rough, uneducated, necessary. Belt workers look at the Inner as soft, meddlesome, out of touch. Both are sometimes right. The regulators who actually work the boundary — the Conservation Authority's field officers — tend to be grudgingly respected. The ones who sit in offices on Meridian are not.

Stations have their own neighborhoods. Fisher's Row on Waypoint. The Low Deck on Tucker's. These are tight communities. Everyone knows everyone. Grudges last decades. So does loyalty.

### The technology (and the rule about it)

Here's what exists: predictive catch-modeling systems, conservation enforcement perimeters (the "fence"), edge compute nodes on every vessel, automated navigation, pressure-sealed EVA suits with hours of life support, shipboard AI assistants, communication relays, station-side processing, FLUX bytecode infrastructure that underpins half of it.

Here's the rule: **nobody cares.**

A catch-prediction model is a fishfinder. The conservation fence is the warden. The ship's AI is the radio. Edge compute is electricity. Nobody explains it. Nobody marvels at it. Nobody has a conversation about it. A mechanic doesn't say "I need to recalibrate the FLUX bytecode emitter array." A mechanic says "the port thruster's running rough" and fixes it with a wrench and a soldering iron and maybe a firmware reboot that takes ten seconds and is never mentioned again.

Technology in The Long Line is like weather. It's there. It affects everything. It is not the story.

## 2. THE BOAT

### F/V Resolute

**Classification:** Deep-space harvesting vessel, 51-foot equivalent. Singleton-class.
**Registry:** WV-4471, Waypoint Station
**Year built:** 78 years ago at Cable Shipyards
**Current owner:** Sofia Reyes (inherited from Captain Gus Martin, retired)
**Master:** Captain Ray Hutchins

The Resolute is old. That is its defining quality and its greatest strength.

It was built in a era when ships were over-engineered because no one was sure yet what space would do to them over decades. The hull is thick. The frames are redundant. The systems have been replaced piecemeal over the years — Margaret has seen to that — but the bones are solid. Eight decades of patches, upgrades, refits, and repairs have made the Resolute into something that no new ship could be: a vessel that is entirely, completely known.

Every vibration means something. Every sound is diagnostic. Denny can tell you what the port coupling is doing by the feel of the deck plate under his boots. Margaret can trace a power fluctuation to a single junction box from memory. Ray can read the ship's attitude in the way his coffee shifts in the mug.

Specs that matter to the story:
- **Crew:** 5 (captain, mechanic, engineer, two deckhands)
- **Operating range:** 14 days standard, 21 with rationing
- **Harvest method:** Towed mag-net array with physical and energetic catch systems
- **Decompression seals:** Triple-redundant (Margaret's doing)
- **Quota license:** Near Line and Far Line, Class B (organic compounds)
- **Mortgage status:** Paid off. 14 years ago. This matters enormously.

The Resolute is not the fastest boat. Not the biggest. Not the most efficient. But it's paid off, which means every season's catch goes to the crew, not to a bank. In the belt economy, that is the difference between a life and a grind.

The interior: cramped, functional, lived-in. The wheelhouse is where Ray spends most of his time — two seats, the main console, porthole displays that show real-time space but also have physical overlays because Ray doesn't trust the displays alone and nobody argues with him about it. The galley is the social space. A table that seats four (someone always stands or sits on a crate). Denny's engine room is his kingdom. The bunk room sleeps five in racks. Sofia's rack has Gus's old reading light, still mounted.

The ship has an AI system. It's called "the system." It does what ship systems do — navigation, catch logging, comm relays, life support, quota tracking. Ray talks to it sometimes. It responds. These conversations are not important. They are habit. Like talking to a dog that doesn't particularly care what you say.

## 3. THE CHARACTERS

### Captain Ray Hutchins

**Age:** 62
**Role:** Master of the Resolute
**Years on the boat:** 31

Ray is the Resolute. Not because he owns it — he doesn't anymore, not since Gus retired and Sofia inherited — but because nobody else can imagine it without him.

He's a big man gone slightly soft, broad-shouldered, with hands that still look like they could haul a net by hand even though nothing works that way anymore. Gray beard, close-trimmed. Eyes that are tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. He moves deliberately, economically, the way men who've worked in pressure suits learn to move — no wasted motion, ever.

He was married to Bess Hutchins for 34 years. She died two years ago — an aneurysm, on Waypoint Station, sudden, no warning. She'd been a schoolteacher at Tucker's. The light of every room she was in. Ray's grief is not dramatic. It is architectural — it's the load-bearing wall of his interior life, and everything else is built around it. He doesn't talk about Bess often. When he does, it's matter-of-fact, sometimes funny, never sentimental. He still wears his ring.

Ray's competence is total and quiet. He doesn't shout orders. He doesn't need to. He's been doing this longer than anyone else on the boat, and when he says "we'll hold here and wait," people hold and wait. His judgment calls are the product of thirty years of reading the belt, and they are almost always right. The times they're not are the times that keep him up.

He wanted to retire when Bess died. Almost did. But the season was starting and Gus called and Sofia was coming aboard and someone had to show her how it works. So he stayed. He's still staying. He's not sure if that's duty or if he just doesn't know how to do anything else.

**Quirks:** Drinks terrible coffee. Keeps a paper logbook alongside the electronic one. Talks to the ship's system by name (calls it "Lu," short for nothing, it's just what he calls it). Sings old songs when he thinks no one can hear. Has a photo of Bess taped inside his locker. Checks it every morning.

### Denny Park

**Age:** 45
**Role:** Ship's mechanic
**Years on the Resolute:** 12

Denny is the kind of man who was born to work on one specific thing his entire life and is perfectly content with that. He is the Resolute's mechanic the way a heart is a heart — the right part in the right place, doing the work it was made for.

He's lean, wire-muscled, perpetually grease-stained below the elbows. Korean-American. Quick hands, quicker smile. He talks fast, thinks faster, and has opinions about everything, most of them correct. He sleeps four hours a night and spends the other twenty either in the engine room or in the galley arguing with someone.

Denny showed up on the Resolute at 33, running from a bad situation on Meridian — a dissolved marriage, a job in a shipyard that he hated, a life that felt like a waiting room. Gus gave him a bunk and a trial run. Denny never left. The engine room became his home. The Resolute's propulsion system — a patchwork of original equipment and six generations of upgrades — is something he understands at a level that borders on spiritual.

His relationship with Ray is the core of the crew's stability. They've worked together long enough to communicate in grunts and glances. Denny would follow Ray into a hull breach. Ray trusts Denny's read on the ship absolutely. They don't say any of this. They don't need to.

Denny is divorced. Has a daughter, Maya, 16, on Meridian with her mother. He sees her between seasons. She thinks his job is cool. He doesn't know how to talk to her. He's trying.

**Quirks:** Keeps a cot in the engine room and sometimes sleeps there instead of his bunk. Listens to talk radio from the stations, hours of it, voices in the background while he works. Bets on everything — card games, weather, catch totals. Keeps a running tab that nobody takes seriously but everyone honors. Knows the Resolute's sounds so well he once diagnosed a coupling failure from three compartments away while asleep.

### Margaret Kowalski

**Age:** 58
**Role:** Systems engineer
**Years on the Resolute:** Since construction (78 years ago — the ship has been in her family's care since the keel was laid; Margaret is the third generation)

Margaret is the Resolute's nervous system, and she knows it.

Polish-American. Stocky, strong, white-haired, sharp-jawed. She has the hands of someone who has worked wire and junction boxes her whole life — scarred, calloused, precise. She wears her hair short and practical and hasn't owned a mirror in twenty years. She doesn't need one. She knows what she looks like: she looks like someone who gets things done.

Margaret's grandmother was on the original construction crew at Cable Shipyards. Her mother maintained the Resolute for Gus's predecessor. Margaret grew up on station hearing about "that stubborn boat." When she was 22, she took the contract. She's been the Resolute's engineer ever since.

She is not warm. She is not cold. She is precise. Every word she says is the word she means. She does not do small talk. She does not do reassurance. When she tells you something will hold, it will hold, and the fact that she said it is all the comfort you're going to get.

Her relationship with Ray is professional and deep. They've worked together for over twenty years. She respects his judgment. He respects hers. They disagree sometimes — about repairs, about routes, about whether to push for one more tow. When they argue, it's brief, technical, and resolved. Neither holds a grudge. Both remember.

She and Denny have a working relationship that looks like an old marriage because it basically is one. They bicker constantly about jurisdiction — is a coolant leak mechanical (Denny's) or electrical (Margaret's)? They've never agreed on the answer. They've never failed to fix the leak.

Margaret has a partner back on Waypoint — Helen, a station administrator. They've been together 19 years. Helen understands the life. Margaret writes her a message every night, even if it's just "still here, love you." It's the most sentiment she allows herself.

**Quirks:** Labels everything. Everything. Every wire, every panel, every junction box. In three languages (English, Polish, and the cable-standard notation she grew up with). Keeps a ship's model on her workbench that she's been slowly building for years, a replica of the Resolute as it looked when new. Won't let anyone touch it. Drinks tea, not coffee. Considers coffee a moral failing.

### Sofia Reyes

**Age:** 26
**Role:** Deckhand / Owner
**First season on the Resolute**

Sofia is the future, and she's not ready for it yet.

She inherited the Resolute when Gus retired. Gus is her mother's brother — Uncle Gus, who she saw twice a year, who smelled like ozone and fish, who told stories about the belt that she half-believed and half-dismissed. When the ownership papers arrived, she was working a processing line on Waypoint. She'd never been on a harvester. She'd never been to the Grounds.

She is small, dark-haired, quick-moving. Venezuelan on her mother's side. Smart in the way of someone who's been underestimated — quietly observant, constantly absorbing, waiting before she acts. She listens more than she talks, which people mistake for timidity. It's not. It's discipline.

Her position on the boat is complicated. She's the owner. She's also the newest, least experienced crew member. This tension is real and ongoing. Ray has authority because the crew grants it to him. Sofia has authority because the law grants it to her. Both of them know this. Neither of them is entirely comfortable with it.

Sofia is not on the Resolute because she loves fishing. She's on it because it's hers, because walking away would mean selling her uncle's legacy to a stranger, and because some stubborn part of her — a part she recognizes from Gus — won't let her. The love for the work, if it comes, will come later. Right now she's in survival mode: learn everything, make no mistakes, earn the crew's respect.

Her relationship with Ray is the show's central axis. He's teaching her. She's watching him. There's warmth there, buried under mutual formality. He calls her "kid" sometimes and catches himself. She calls him "Captain." They're feeling out what they are to each other — mentor and student, employee and boss, grandfather-figure and inheritor. It changes by the day.

**Quirks:** Keeps a journal — actual paper, actual pen. Writes in it every night. Organized to the point of compulsion. Doesn't drink. Misses fresh fruit. Has Gus's old knife, which she carries everywhere. Asks too many questions, some of which are brilliant.

### Tomás Oliveira

**Age:** 22
**Role:** Watch-stander / Deckhand
**First season in deep space**

Tomás is young. That's his whole deal.

Brazilian-American. Tall, still growing into his frame. Big hands, big grin, big heart. He grew up on Cable — factory kid, parents both in the shipyards. He signed on with the Resolute because the quota license came open and he needed a job and someone at the agency said "Ray Hutchins runs a clean boat." That was enough.

He is profoundly, aggressively trustworthy. He shows up early. He does what he's told. He asks questions when he doesn't understand and he doesn't pretend to know things he doesn't. He is also, predictably, a magnet for every difficult, messy, dangerous situation the belt can produce, because he's the newest and the youngest and that's how it works.

Tomás looks up to Denny the way younger brothers look up to older ones — with a mixture of admiration, annoyance, and the determination to one day be as good. Denny gives him a hard time because that's how Denny teaches. Tomás takes it because that's how Tomás learns.

His relationship with Sofia is the most natural on the boat. They're close in age, both new, both learning. They're friends. They eat together, complain together, cover for each other. There's no romance — or if there is, neither of them has time for it, and the boat doesn't need that kind of complication right now.

**Quirks:** Always hungry. Always. Can eat twice what anyone else eats and still be hungry. Fiddles with things — pens, tools, zippers — when he's thinking. Sings along to music he doesn't know the words to, making them up. Terrible card player. Takes losses with a grin. The crew protects him instinctively, and he doesn't quite realize it.

## 4. THE DYNAMICS

### The Crew Structure

The Resolute runs on a chain that's part formal, part organic:

- **Ray** is captain. Final word on operations, routes, and safety.
- **Margaret** outranks everyone on matters of ship systems. If she says a system is down, it's down. Even Ray doesn't argue.
- **Denny** has the same authority over mechanical systems. His engine room is sovereign territory.
- **Sofia** owns the ship. This gives her authority over business decisions — quota management, contracts, expenses. On deck, she's a deckhand.
- **Tomás** is at the bottom of the hierarchy and comfortable there.

The tension between Sofia's legal authority and Ray's practical authority is the engine of many episodes. Not conflict, exactly — they're aligned more often than not — but negotiation. Two kinds of power, learning to coexist.

### The History

- Ray and Margaret have the longest working relationship — 20+ years.
- Denny joined 12 years ago, recruited by Gus (the previous captain).
- Gus retired 18 months ago. Sofia inherited 6 months ago. Ray has been running with a rotating fourth hand since Gus left.
- Sofia and Tomás both started this season. This is the crew's first full season together.
- Bess died 2 years ago. Ray's grief is still a live wire, though he'd deny it.
- Gus is alive, on Waypoint, in declining health. Sofia visits him. Ray doesn't.

### The Tensions

**Ray and Sofia:** Authority and ownership. Experience and inheritance. Ray treats her like crew because that's what she needs. Sofia sometimes needs him to treat her like the owner. They're working it out.

**Denny and Margaret:** Two experts, one ship, overlapping domains. Their arguments are legendary and meaningless. They agree on everything that matters.

**Ray and Bess's death:** Ray doesn't talk about it. The crew doesn't bring it up. This is both respectful and a problem, because grief that isn't processed has a way of showing up in other rooms.

**Sofia and Gus's legacy:** She's on the boat because of him. She doesn't fully understand why she's staying. This unresolved question drives her arc.

**Tomás and the belt:** He's never done this before. The belt is dangerous and beautiful and it doesn't care if you're new. His arc is about becoming someone the belt can't break.

### External Relationships

- **The Conservation Authority:** Sets quotas, patrols the fence, enforces regulations. Field officers are grudgingly respected. Office regulators are not.
- **Other boats:** The Resolute has peers and rivals. Some friendly (shared information, shared meals at station), some competitive (overlapping claims, quota disputes). Fishing communities are tight-knit and factional.
- **Waypoint Station:** Home port. Where the crew goes between seasons. Where their lives outside the boat exist — partners, families, debts, problems.
- **The market:** Buyers at station set prices. Prices determine whether the season was worth it. The crew tracks prices the way farmers track rain.

## 5. THE EPISODE FORMULA

Every episode of The Long Line follows the same skeleton. The flesh changes. The bones don't.

### Act One: The Work (Pages 1–8)

Open on the Resolute at work. Not in transit, not at station — at work. The net is out or going out. The crew is in their routine. Show competence. Show rhythm. Show the specific beauty of people doing hard work well.

Establish the episode's context: where they are, what they're after, what the weather (space weather — debris fields, radiation flux, orbital conditions) is doing. Show the crew interacting in small ways that reveal who they are today.

This act smells like work. It feels like the deck under your boots. The technology is present — the fishfinder pings, the system logs the catch, the fence beacon blinks on the console — and nobody mentions it, because why would they.

### Act Two: The Complication (Pages 8–15)

Something goes wrong. Or something changes. Or something arrives.

This is NOT a technology failure. This is NOT an AI malfunction. This is NOT a system going rogue.

This IS:
- A weather event (debris storm, solar flare, navigation hazard)
- A regulatory encounter (a fence inspection, a quota dispute, a zone closure)
- A mechanical breakdown (a coupling fails, a seal degrades, a net array tangles)
- A human problem (someone makes a mistake, someone gets sick, someone's judgment is off)
- A resource squeeze (low fuel, low supplies, a bad catch that won't cover costs)
- An interpersonal conflict (two crew members disagree on a call, old tensions surface)
- An outside force (another vessel, a distress call, a station directive)

The complication escalates. It becomes the problem of the episode. The crew shifts from routine to response.

### Act Three: The Response (Pages 15–25)

Each character engages the problem through their specific lens:

- **Ray** makes the tactical call. He reads the situation from experience. He decides what to do.
- **Denny** handles the mechanical reality. If the ship needs to do something it wasn't designed to do, Denny figures out how.
- **Margaret** handles the systems reality. If the ship's capabilities are degraded or need to be pushed, Margaret determines what will hold and what won't.
- **Sofia** is learning. She's in the response, contributing, but also absorbing. Her perspective — newer, less conditioned by "how it's always been done" — sometimes reveals something the others miss.
- **Tomás** does the physical work. The dangerous part. The part that requires someone to go outside or climb into something or hold something together while someone else fixes it.

The response tests the crew. It creates friction between them — not because anyone is wrong, but because different expertise produces different priorities. Margaret needs to shut down a system. Denny needs that system running. Ray has to decide. This is where character lives.

### Act Four: The Resolution (Pages 25–30)

The problem resolves. Not cleanly — problems on boats never resolve cleanly. There's a cost: a damaged net, a missed catch window, a debt to another boat, a bruise, an argument that doesn't quite end.

The resolution comes from character. It's Ray's experience that sees the path. Or Denny's stubbornness that holds the ship together long enough. Or Margaret's precise calculation of what the ship can take. Or Sofia asking the question nobody else thought to ask. Or Tomás doing the thing that needed doing without being told.

Technology is part of the resolution because technology is part of everything. But it's never the hero. A system provides information. A person makes a decision. A tool enables an action. A human takes the risk.

End on the boat. Back to work, or heading home, or holding station waiting for weather. The crew reset — maybe slightly changed, maybe slightly closer, maybe with a new fracture to carry forward. The rhythm reasserts itself. The line goes out. The line comes back.

### Recurring Episode Types

- **The Grounds Episode:** On the belt, fishing. Weather or catch complications.
- **The Station Episode:** At port. Business, resupply, interpersonal. The world outside the boat.
- **The Transit Episode:** Between grounds and station. Confined space, forced proximity. Character-focused.
- **The Regulatory Episode:** Inspection, dispute, zone change. Institutional power vs. working life.
- **The Emergency Episode:** Something goes very wrong. The crew is tested to their limit.
- **The History Episode:** A person or object from someone's past arrives. Backstory is revealed through action.

### What Every Episode Is Actually About

Work. Family. Trust. The quiet heroism of competence. The way people who are very different from each other build a functional unit through shared labor and mutual dependence. The ocean is space. The boat is home. The crew is everything.

## 6. THE RULES (reminder, for every script)

1. **Technology is invisible.** If a character would explain how something works, cut the line.
2. **Problems are human.** Weather, mechanical failure, regulatory pressure, interpersonal conflict. Never "the AI did something."
3. **Solutions are character-driven.** Expertise, judgment, stubbornness, courage. Not "the system figured it out."
4. **The boat is a character.** The Resolute has moods. It cooperates or resists. The crew reads it like a living thing.
5. **No villains.** Antagonists are other people doing their jobs, or nature, or the economy, or bad luck. No one is evil. Some people are difficult.
6. **Grief is real.** Joy is real. Work is real. These things coexist without resolution.
7. **The line goes out. The line comes back.** Routine is not boring. Routine is beautiful. Show the work.
