# THE EXCAVATION
## Chapter Three: The Storyteller

---

Basil Erath lived in a three-room apartment on the fourth floor of the Academy quarter, in a building that housed seven other scholars and their families and that smelled, depending on the season, of either heating oil or the lime wash they put on the walls in summer. The apartment had a kitchen, a sitting room that also served as Basil's study, and a bedroom that he shared with Davi.

Davi's bed was against the wall beneath the window. Basil's bed was against the opposite wall, next to the door. Between them, a rug that had been Palas's and that Basil could not bring himself to replace even though it was worn through at the center and the color of dust. The bookshelves covered every wall floor to ceiling. Basil had built them himself, in the first year after Palas died, as a project — something to do with his hands in the evenings when the silence was too loud and reading was too quiet. The shelves were not straight. The books didn't care.

Davi was eight. He was small for his age — shorter than his classmates by a head, which concerned his teachers and did not concern Davi, who compensated for his lack of height with an excess of velocity. Davi moved through the apartment like a weather system: fast, unpredictable, occasionally destructive, always interesting. He left trails — shoes by the door, a half-eaten fruit bar on the kitchen counter, a drawing of a wind-runner (with modifications — Davi's wind-runners always had modifications) on the sitting room floor, a schoolbook open to a page about river systems, a sock on the bookshelf between a monograph on pre-Ascendancy trade routes and a volume of the Index.

Basil did not clean up after him with any consistency. He was not negligent. He was overwhelmed. There is a difference, though the difference is not always visible from the outside.

The bedtime story was the one fixed point of their day. Every night at eight, Davi stopped being a weather system and became an audience. He got into bed. Basil sat on the edge of the mattress. And the story began.

They had been through every story in the Index's folkloric collection. They had been through the Ascendancy's foundation myths. They had been through the adventures of Kalu the Wind-Rider, which were Basil's childhood favorites and which Davi pronounced "too easy" — meaning the heroes solved problems by being strong, and Davi wanted them to solve problems by being clever.

Basil had started bringing home fragments of the Ketharu texts during the translation committee sessions. He read them to Davi — not the academic translations, which were too dry and too footnoted, but retellings. Basil took Vost's translations and told them in his own words, shaping them for an eight-year-old's ear. He simplified. He dramatized. He added pauses at the moments of greatest tension.

He did not know that his simplifications and dramatizations added another layer of distortion on top of Vost's mistranslations. The translation committee had compressed the original texts into Pashti categories. Basil was now compressing the Pashti translation into the categories of a bedtime story. The mountains became a little more magical. The Walkers became a little more heroic. The Silence became a little more mysterious. Each retelling moved the story further from the source, the way a river moves further from its spring as it crosses the plain.

---

The first night Basil told Davi the story of the Walkers, Davi was sitting up in bed with his hands on his knees, which was his listening posture. Basil sat on the edge of the bed with his notebook open on his lap, though he rarely looked at it. The notebook was a talisman — proof that the story was real, that it came from somewhere, that it wasn't invented.

"Long ago," Basil said, "in a world before the Ascendancy, there were people called the Walkers."

"What did they walk on?" Davi asked.

"The ground. The plain. The world."

"All the time?"

"No — not all the time. They walked across something called the Schism."

"What's the Schism?"

Basil consulted his memory of the committee's deliberations. "The Schism was... a great division. Between the East and the West."

"Like the Reaches and the Capital?"

"Something like that, yes."

"So the Schism was a disagreement?"

"That's one way to think about it."

"But if it was a disagreement, why did they need to walk? Couldn't they just talk?"

Basil paused. This was not a question he had anticipated. "The Schism wasn't just a disagreement, Davi. It was also a... place. A place that was hard to cross."

"Like the river during flood?"

"Yes. Like that. A place that was hard to cross."

"But you can cross a flood in a boat. Why didn't they use boats?"

Basil felt the first faint stirring of something he would come to recognize over the next several weeks: the sensation of his son's questions landing on the weak points of the translation, like fingers pressing on bruises.

"I don't think they had boats the way we do," Basil said. "The Schism wasn't a river. It was more like..." He searched for the committee's language. "It was like a wall. A living wall."

"Walls aren't alive."

"This one was. The texts say it was made of something called the Silence — a force that lived in the rock and made instruments stop working."

Davi's eyes widened. This was the kind of detail that a second-grader could work with. "What kind of instruments?"

"Navigational instruments. Tools for finding your way."

"So the Silence made them lost?"

"Sometimes. Yes."

"And they walked across anyway?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

Basil smiled. This was the spiritual question — the question the Pashti translation was designed to answer. "Because someone on the other side needed to hear from them. They carried messages. Important messages — between doctors and patients, between families, between governments. The messages were so important that the Walkers risked the Silence to deliver them."

Davi considered this. His face had the expression Basil called "the processing face" — eyes slightly narrowed, lips slightly pursed, the expression of a small computer running a large problem.

"That's good," Davi said. "Tell me more."

---

Basil told the story of Henra Volk. In the Pashti translation, Henra was not a 48-year-old professional carrier with 203 crossings and a rebuilt knee. Henra was a Pilgrim Elder — a woman who had dedicated her life to the Schism crossing, whose authority came from her decades of devotion, whose silence was wisdom rather than operational necessity.

"Henra didn't talk much," Basil said. "When she was on the route, she decided everything. Where to walk, when to rest, when to turn back. The other Walkers followed her because she had crossed the Schism more times than anyone alive."

"More than two hundred times," Davi said. He'd been counting.

"More than two hundred times."

"Was she ever scared?"

Basil checked his notes. The original text said Henra didn't think in terms of fear — she thought in terms of conditions. You didn't cross when conditions were wrong. You crossed when they were right. Fear was not part of the equation, because fear implied a negotiation, and Henra didn't negotiate with the mountain.

The Pashti translation rendered this as: "Henra feared nothing, for her devotion was pure, and in purity there is no room for doubt."

Basil read this and felt its wrongness without recognizing it as wrongness. He felt it as grandeur — the grandeur of a spiritual teacher who had transcended ordinary human emotions. The fact that the original Henra was not transcendent but practical — that she didn't experience fear because fear was a useless emotion at 18,000 feet, not because she had achieved spiritual purity — was invisible to him.

"She was brave," Basil told Davi. "The texts say she was the bravest Walker who ever lived."

"Did she ever fall?"

"Once. She fell at a place called — " Basil checked his notes — "a place called the Cone. She was hurt. She doesn't remember the fall. But she remembers the walk out."

"She walked out after she fell? By herself?"

"With her team. They helped her."

"And then she went back?"

"Yes."

"Every time she fell, she went back?"

"Every time."

Davi's processing face intensified. "Baba," he said, "if Henra fell at the Cone, and the Cone is a place on the route, then the route must be real."

"Of course it's real."

"No — I mean, the route is a real place. Not a spiritual place. A place where you can fall. You can't fall in a spiritual place."

Basil opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "That's... a good point, Davi. The route is a real place. But it's also a spiritual journey."

"Can you fall in a spiritual journey?"

"Not... physically, no."

"Then the Cone is a physical place where you can fall. And the Altar is a physical place. And the Sill is a physical place."

"Yes, I suppose —"

"Then why do the texts call them sacred names? If they're just places, why not call them what they are?"

Basil stared at his son. Davi stared back. His eyes were bright and entirely serious.

"I think," Basil said slowly, "the texts use sacred names because the places were sacred to the Walkers."

"But sacred places don't make your heart beat in your teeth," Davi said. "That sounds like it hurts."

Basil did not have an answer for this. The original text was describing the physiological effects of extreme altitude — the thinning of the air, the degradation of cognition, the auditory hallucinations that occur when the body is oxygen-deprived and the silence is absolute. None of this was sacred. All of it was dangerous.

But the translation said the Altar was a sacred place where the Walker experienced the totality of being. And Davi, who was eight and whose heart did not yet beat in his teeth, could feel the mismatch between the description of the experience and the label the translation had put on it.

"We'll come back to that," Basil said. "Let me tell you about Oskar."

---

He told the story of Oskar Nel — the big carrier who carried the heavy loads, who whittled animals and left them at the stations, who checked ropes that didn't need checking. In the Pashti translation, Oskar was a Devoted Bearer — a figure of religious significance whose carrying of heavy loads was a form of penance. His anxiety about ropes and safety was translated as devotional vigilance. His whittled animals were translated as ritual offerings.

"Oskar left the little animals at the temples," Basil said. "Each temple had a shelf of Oskar's animals by the door."

"Why by the door?"

"I don't know. As offerings, I think."

"But the temples had doors?"

"Yes."

"And the doors were real doors? On real buildings?"

"Yes, the temples were buildings — shelters where the Walkers rested."

"With doors. And shelves. And someone who lived there."

"The temple guardians, yes."

"Baba." Davi pulled the blanket up to his chin. His processing face was at maximum intensity. "If the buildings had doors and shelves and someone who lived there, they sound like houses. Not temples. Temples don't usually have shelves by the door."

"Some temples do —"

"Does our temple have a shelf by the door?"

Basil conceded that it did not.

"Then maybe the Walkers' temples weren't temples. Maybe they were just houses. And the guardians were just... people who lived in the houses."

Basil felt the bruise-pressing sensation again. Davi was doing something the translation committee had not done: he was comparing the described structures to real structures and noticing that the description didn't match the label. The committee had called the relay stations "temples" because the Pashti taxonomy required it — Walkers were pilgrims, pilgrims rest at temples. Davi, who had not internalized the taxonomy, compared the description to his own experience and concluded: houses.

"Davi, the texts are translated by experts," Basil said. "Professor Vost is the greatest translator in the Ascendancy. If the texts say temples, the texts mean temples."

"The texts don't say temples," Davi said. "You said they say temples. What do they really say?"

Basil blinked. "I... don't know the original word. The translation says temples."

"Maybe the translation is wrong."

Basil laughed — the laugh of a man who is uncomfortable and doesn't know why. "The translation is not wrong, Davi. It was produced by a committee of six scholars over four months. They know what they're doing."

Davi accepted this. He was eight. He did not yet have the confidence to override his father's faith in expertise. But the question had been asked, and the question would stay in the room like a seed in the ground, waiting for the next night's rain.

---

The first night ended with the story of Teo — the temple guardian at the Step, who made the best coffee on the route, who read 400 books, who had been assessing Walkers for eleven years and had never been wrong.

"Teo knew when to let the Walkers go and when to make them stay," Basil said. "He could look at a team and know if they were ready."

"How?"

"How what?"

"How did he know? Did he use an instrument?"

"No. He just looked at them. He used his experience."

"His experience," Davi repeated. "Not a document. His experience."

"Yes."

"But I thought knowledge was in documents."

Basil felt the ground shift. Not visibly. Not audibly. But the way the ground shifts when you're standing on something that isn't quite solid and a vibration passes through it.

"Knowledge is in documents," Basil said. "But experience is also a kind of knowledge."

"Then why don't we write experience down?"

"Some things can't be written down."

Davi's face changed. The processing face became something else — something sharper, more focused. "Why not?"

"Because... some knowledge is in the body. In the hands. In the way you do something. Teo could look at a Walker and know — but he couldn't write down how he knew. He just knew."

"Like how I know when Mama's soup is done?"

Basil's breath caught. Davi rarely mentioned Palas. When he did, it was always sideways — an angle of approach that avoided the direct center of the grief because the direct center was still too hot to touch.

"Yes," Basil said. "Like that."

"Did Mama write down the soup recipe?"

"No."

"Can you make Mama's soup?"

"No. Not the way she did."

"Then Mama's soup is gone."

Basil put his hand on Davi's head. "Yes."

"And nobody can ever make it again."

"Not the way she did."

"That's sad, Baba."

"Yes."

"Is that what happened to the Walkers? Did their soup recipe get lost?"

Basil almost laughed again, but this time not from discomfort. This time from the strange, vertiginous recognition that his eight-year-old son had just stumbled onto the central theme of both texts — the thing Hermes had built the entire architecture around. Knowledge that lived in bodies and died with them. Knowledge that couldn't be written down because writing was the wrong medium, the way you can't record a smell by photographing it.

The carriers' route knowledge — the feel of the snow, the sound of the wind, the way the rock looked in different lights — lived in their bodies and died when they stopped crossing. The fishers' water knowledge — the feel of the hull, the proprioceptive sense of the current, the way the passages felt from the deck — lived in Sera's instincts and Tomás's muscle memory and could not be put on a chart because charts showed routes, not feelings.

Davi had found this idea through soup.

"Yes," Basil said. "That's what happened. The Walkers' knowledge — the things they knew in their bodies, the things they couldn't write down — it got lost."

"And the charts — the Sacred Texts — they were supposed to have the knowledge. But they didn't."

"That's right."

"Because you can't write down soup."

Basil squeezed his son's shoulder. "No. You can't write down soup."

Davi settled into his pillow. His eyes were closing. The conversation had tired him in the specific way that thinking tires a child — not physically, but deep, in the engine of the mind.

"Baba?"

"Yes?"

"Tell me about the Fishers tomorrow."

"I will."

"And Baba?"

"Yes?"

"I think the Walkers' temples were just houses. And I think the Walkers weren't pilgrims. I think they were just people doing a job."

Basil sat in the dark after Davi fell asleep. He sat for a long time. The apartment was quiet. The bookshelves were shadow. The rug was dust-colored in the moonlight from the window.

He thought about Davi's question. *I think the Walkers were just people doing a job.*

The translation committee had spent four months elevating the carriers from workers to pilgrims. It had taken Davi one bedtime story to push them back.

Basil didn't know if Davi was right. The translation was the translation. The experts were the experts. The Index was the Index.

But sitting in the dark, in the room that still smelled faintly of Palas's soup — a knowledge that lived in the walls and would be gone when the walls were gone, a soup recipe that no document could hold — Basil felt, for the first time, that the translation might be missing something.

He couldn't say what. He couldn't classify it. He couldn't footnote it.

He just felt the gap.

*End Chapter Three*
