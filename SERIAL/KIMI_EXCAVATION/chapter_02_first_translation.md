# THE EXCAVATION
## Chapter Two: First Translation

---

The translation committee convened in the Academy's East Hall, in a room that smelled of paper and policy. There were six members: Vost as lead translator, Therop as archaeological context specialist, Basil Erath as narrative structure analyst, and three consulting scholars — one in comparative religion, one in pre-Ascendancy economics, and one in military history, because every Pashti academic committee includes a military historian as a matter of tradition.

The committee worked for four months.

The process was straightforward, as translation processes go. Vost produced a preliminary gloss — word-by-word translations of each text, with multiple candidate meanings for each unfamiliar term. The committee debated the candidates. They voted. The winning candidate became the official translation. Disagreements were noted in the minutes. Alternatives were filed in footnotes.

The footnotes would have filled a separate volume.

---

The first major debate was over the word the committee would translate as *Schism*. In the original text, the word was *the Divide*. Vost identified it as a proper noun — a named geographical feature. But the committee could not determine what kind of feature.

"It appears in contexts suggesting a barrier," Vost said. "Something that separates two regions — East and West. But the descriptions of the barrier are inconsistent. Sometimes it's described as if it were a wall. Sometimes as if it were a weather system. Sometimes as if it were alive."

"A living wall," said the comparative religion scholar, whose name was Pelta Aris. "This is a common feature of devotional narratives. The barrier is not physical — it's spiritual. The living wall is the wall inside the self."

"Or it's a metaphor for political division," said the military historian, whose name was Dorn Ketal. "East and West. A civilization split by ideology. The 'wall' is the disagreement itself — too high to see over because neither side can understand the other."

"Or it's a river," said Therop. "A major river system that floods seasonally. The Ketharu Plain has similar features — the river becomes a barrier during flood season. The 'living wall' is the floodwater."

This last interpretation had the virtue of fitting the Pashti experience. It had the vice of being completely wrong. But the committee had no way to know that, and the river hypothesis was elegant, and elegance is the quality that Pashti scholarship values above accuracy.

They voted. River won. Political division was noted as an alternative in the minutes. The living wall hypothesis was filed under "metaphorical interpretations."

The Divide became *the Schism* — a term broad enough to accommodate a river barrier, a political split, and a spiritual metaphor. It was a Pashti word for a Pashti concept. It fit the way a shoe fits a foot that's been wearing shoes its whole life: comfortably, naturally, and completely concealing the shape of the ground underneath.

---

The second debate was over the word the committee would translate as *Walkers*. In the original text, the word was *carriers* — people who carried things across the Divide for a living. The guild. The profession.

Vost identified the word as an occupational title. He noted that the text described these people performing physical labor — walking, carrying loads, maintaining routes — in conditions that were described as dangerous. He identified the loads: small containers, sealed documents, medical parcels. He identified the compensation structure: payment by weight and urgency.

"They're couriers," Vost said. "Professional couriers who cross the Schism to deliver goods."

Ketal, the military historian, objected. "The text describes them as a guild with a two-hundred-year tradition, with their own songs, their own funerals, their own cultural identity. That's not a courier service. That's a religious order."

"The two are not mutually exclusive," Aris said. "Many religious orders perform practical functions. The Devotees of the Wind maintain the telegraph network. The River Brethren manage the irrigation systems. The carriers may be a similar tradition — secular work elevated to spiritual significance."

Basil Erath, the narrative analyst, spoke for the first time that morning. "The text says they trust each other in ways that outsiders find unsettling," he read from his notes. "They have their own slang. Their own drinking songs. When one of them dies, they don't mourn publicly — they carry the body out or mark the cairn. This is a culture, not a profession."

"A culture of walkers," Aris said. "Pilgrims who have made the journey their way of life."

The word *pilgrim* caught the committee's attention. It fit. It was elegant. It was wrong — but it was wrong in a way the Pashti could understand. A courier was too mundane. A pilgrim was just right. It elevated the mundane to the spiritual, which was the Pashti's instinctive move whenever they encountered something they couldn't quite place.

The carriers became *the Walkers* — *Tevasharin* in Pashti. The word carried connotations of devotional journeying. It suggested that the carriers walked because walking was sacred, not because someone needed a letter delivered to the other side of a mountain range that the committee had just classified as a river.

---

The debates continued. Each one was a small tragedy of misinterpretation, rendered in the careful language of academic procedure.

**On vestite:**

Vost presented the word as "a material that interferes with instruments." He described the textual evidence: the substance appeared in the rock, it generated a force that disrupted navigation, its effects varied by location and condition.

The committee considered several translations. "A spirit that confuses the senses" was proposed by Aris. "A strategic obstruction" was proposed by Ketal. "A magnetic anomaly" was proposed by Therop.

The word they settled on was *Heshtu* — *the Silence*. It was a Pashti term from contemplative philosophy, meaning the state of mental stillness in which external stimuli fall away. The committee liked the translation because it was evocative, because it sounded profound, and because it classified vestite as a spiritual phenomenon rather than a physical one.

The actual substance — a piezoelectric mineral that generated electromagnetic interference under geological pressure, that made GPS systems fail and radios distort, that forced human beings to navigate by stone markers and memory because the mountain itself jammed every instrument they carried — this reality was lost. *The Silence* was a beautiful translation. That was enough.

**On cairns:**

The text described cairns as stone markers, built and rebuilt by generations of carriers, used to navigate when the route was buried by snow. The committee translated these as *Kethpasar* — *prayer stones*. The reasoning: if the Walkers were pilgrims, their stone markers must be devotional. The idea that a pile of stones could be a navigational aid rather than a shrine was, to the Pashti mind, a category error. Stones in the Pashti tradition were used for inscription — for writing on. Uninscribed stones stacked in the wilderness could only be shrines.

That the cairns were uninscribed because there was nothing to write on a mountain in a whiteout at 18,000 feet — that the stones were meant to be SEEN, not read — this did not occur to the committee. The Textual Principle made it invisible.

**On relay stations:**

The text described nine relay stations — shelters at intervals along the route, maintained by former carriers who lived in them year-round. The committee translated these as *Sanaru* — *temples*. The reasoning: if the Walkers were pilgrims and the cairns were prayer stones, then the stations where the Walkers rested must be temples. The station keepers — Teo at The Step, Maren at The Saddle — were translated as *temple guardians*. The fact that the keepers were former carriers, that the stations were operational infrastructure rather than religious structures, and that the keepers' primary function was to assess whether teams were fit to continue — all of this was filed under "institutional context" and ignored.

**On the Saddle:**

The text described the Saddle as "a broad, flat, featureless expanse" at 19,100 feet, where "the horizon is a perfect circle and the silence is so complete that carriers report hearing their own heartbeats in their teeth." The committee read this passage with awe.

"The Saddle is the mystical center of the narrative," Aris said. "It is the place where the Walker transcends ordinary experience. The silence, the circle of the horizon — these are classic descriptors of ecstatic union with the divine."

"Or it's a description of altitude sickness," Ketal said, to his credit.

Ketal was overruled. The Saddle became *Mesaru* — *the Altar*. In the Erath Translation, the crossing of the Saddle is described as a "ritual passage across the sacred Altar, where the pilgrim confronts the totality of existence."

The actual Saddle — a plateau at 19,100 feet where the air is so thin that cognition degrades, where the wind scours snow to blue ice, where there is nothing between you and death except the memory of a route marked by stones that may or may not be visible — was gone. In its place was an altar, and on the altar was a pilgrimage, and the pilgrimage was beautiful.

The mountains did not need to be beautiful. They needed to be crossed. But the committee did not know that.

---

Basil Erath took notes throughout the sessions. He filled four notebooks. He copied passages that struck him — the description of Henra's compass, the account of Mara Vest's first crossing, Teo's letter about the mountain not needing you to cross it. He copied them in the Pashti translation, and the translation was wrong, and the wrongness was invisible, and the passages moved him anyway.

He was moved because the wrong translation carried its own truth — a Pashti truth, a truth about the spirit's journey across the metaphorical landscape of the inner life. The Pashti translation of THE CARRY was not the story of people who walked across a mountain range for a living. It was the story of pilgrims who walked across the landscape of the soul, carrying the burden of existence, finding meaning in the act of carrying itself. This was not what Hermes wrote. But it was, in its own way, a story. A different story, growing in the soil of a different culture, fed by the same words and producing different fruit.

Basil read the translated passage about the Saddle and felt the hair rise on his arms. He read:

> *"And the Altar spread before them, vast and silent, and the world became a circle, and they heard their own hearts in their teeth, and in that hearing was the totality of their being, and the totality was enough."*

What Hermes wrote was:

> *"The Saddle is the psychological crux — a broad, flat, featureless expanse between two peaks, where the horizon is a perfect circle and the silence is so complete that carriers report hearing their own heartbeats in their teeth."*

Hermes was describing altitude and exhaustion and the specific, terrifying experience of standing on a high plateau where nothing moves and nothing grows and the only sound is your own body reminding you that it is working. The passage is clinical, precise, and completely unromantic. The carriers hear their hearts in their teeth because the air is too thin and the silence is too deep, and the experience is awful in the original sense of the word: it produces awe, and the awe is not pleasant.

The Erath Translation turned the awful into the sacred. The Pashti could not hear their hearts in their teeth, so they translated the phrase as "the totality of their being" — a philosophical concept, not a physiological event. The body became the spirit. The altitude became the altar. The mountain became a metaphor.

Basil read the metaphor and loved it. He did not know there was a mountain underneath.

---

The translation of THE MERIDIAN presented different challenges.

The oceanic vocabulary was, if anything, more alien than the mountain vocabulary. The Pashti had no concept of deep water. Their rivers were shallow, their floods were manageable, and their relationship with water was fundamentally agricultural — water as resource, water as necessity, water as the medium through which crops grew and rituals purified.

The ocean — vast, deep, dark, powerful, hostile, alive — was beyond their conceptual horizon.

Vost wrestled with the word *ocean* for three days. The text used several terms: *ocean*, *sea*, *the deep*, *the Reach*, *the Drop*. Each appeared in different contexts, suggesting distinctions the Pashti didn't have. Vost treated them as synonyms — Pashti has one word for large water: *pas-iteru*, which literally means "the great flood." He translated all five terms as *pas-iteru-im* — *the Great Flat*.

This single decision compressed an entire world into a single wrong word. The ocean was not the Great Flat. The ocean was not a plain. The ocean was a body of water so vast and so deep that human beings could sink into it and never return, that currents moved through it like rivers through stone, that it sustained an ecosystem of organisms that the fishers harvested for food, that it killed people who didn't respect it and allowed passage to those who did.

The Great Flat was a Pashti word for a Pashti concept. The ocean was not a Pashti concept. The translation was a perfect circle of misunderstanding: the Pashti translated the unknown into the known, and the known accepted the translation because it fit, and the fit confirmed that the translation was correct.

Vost encountered the word *boat* and translated it as *telupa-daru* — *house that moves*. The reasoning: the text described people going out onto the Great Flat in structures that moved. The Pashti had wind-runners — vehicles that moved across the plains. They had river barges — structures that floated on water. The combination of "structure that moves" and "on the Great Flat" suggested to Vost a vehicle analogous to a wind-runner but designed for the Great Flat. A moving house.

The word *current* appeared throughout the Meridian text. The Incurrent. The counterflow. The Sill current. These were specific, physical, measurable phenomena — movements of water with direction, force, and seasonal variation. Vost translated them as *nashtu* — the Pashti word for political drift, the tendency of public opinion to shift. He elevated it to *nashtu-im* — *the Inner Calling* — which carried connotations of vocation, destiny, and spiritual pull.

The ocean's current — a mass of water moving with the force of a river a hundred miles wide, cold water from the deep pushing up against the continental shelf, warm water from the equator sliding over the top, the shear zones where these masses met and created turbulence that could sink a boat — this became "the Inner Calling." A physical force became a spiritual one. The ocean's mass became metaphor. The water evaporated.

---

The committee completed the preliminary translation in four months. Vost produced a clean, readable Pashti text with extensive footnotes. The footnotes documented the uncertainties — the words with multiple candidates, the passages where the original meaning was unclear, the places where interpretive judgment had been exercised.

The footnotes were honest. The translation was not. The translation was smooth, confident, and coherent. It read as a unified narrative about two groups of pilgrims — the Walkers who crossed the Schism on foot, and the Fishers who went out onto the Great Flat in moving houses — and their encounters with spiritual forces (the Silence, the Inner Calling, the Altar) that tested and transformed them.

The translation was published by the Academy Press under the title *The Ketharu Texts: A Preliminary Translation and Commentary*. Three hundred copies were printed. Two hundred and fifty went to academic libraries. Thirty were purchased by scholars. Twelve were purchased by general readers.

One was purchased by Basil Erath, who already had a committee copy but wanted his own.

And one — actually, not purchased but borrowed, from Basil's personal shelf, without permission, because Basil's son Davi had learned to reach the second shelf by standing on a stool — was read by a child.

But that comes later.

---

On the night the translation was completed, the committee held a small dinner in the East Hall. Therop made a toast. "To the Ketharu texts," she said. "Which have given us a window into a civilization we didn't know existed."

Vost raised his glass. "To the translators," he said. "Who have made that window transparent."

Basil raised his glass. He didn't say anything. He was thinking about a passage he'd translated that afternoon — a passage from the Carrier text. In the original, it was Teo's letter to Director Salaun:

> *"The mountain doesn't need you to cross it. You cross it because someone on the other side needs to hear from you. The mountain is just the distance between them, made of stone."*

In the Erath Translation, it read:

> *"The Altar does not require your devotion. You approach it because someone on the other side of the Schism needs to hear from you. The Altar is merely the distance between them, made sacred."*

Basil read the translation and felt something stir in him — a recognition, a resonance, the feeling of encountering a truth that has been wearing different clothes in every culture but is always the same underneath. The distance between people, made sacred. That was beautiful. That was true.

He didn't know that the distance between people wasn't sacred. It was stone. It was 40,000 square miles of rock and ice. It was a mountain range that killed people, and the people who crossed it did so not because the mountain was sacred but because someone on the other side needed a letter. The beauty of Teo's statement was in its unsentimentality — the mountain is just the distance, made of stone, and stone is just stone, and you cross it because you have to, and the crossing is not a metaphor.

The Pashti translation made the stone sacred. The original made the stone stone. The difference was everything.

Basil didn't know this. He raised his glass. He drank. The translation was done.

The excavation of the texts was complete.

The excavation of the translation was about to begin.
