# The Long Line
## Episode 1: The Temperature Break

---

The Resolute came out of transit at 0417 ship's time, and the grounds were already occupied.

Sofia Vendel saw it on the display before anyone spoke — a second ship, running dark, sitting about four kilometers off the prime set line. The system had tagged it automatically: F/V Daybreak, registered to Captain Harmon Saldibar. Transponder active, running lights off. Station-keeping. Settled in like he owned the place.

She stood in the wheelhouse with her hands in her jacket pockets and looked at the readout the way you look at a door that's locked when you expected it open. She'd been captain of the Resolute for one season and sixteen days. She'd made four transit jumps in command. She had not yet had another vessel sitting on her ground when she arrived.

"What's his set like?" she asked.

Tomás Luque, on watch, pulled up the Daybreak's position relative to the thermal — the boundary layer where temperature gradient concentrated the organics they were all out here to collect. "He's right on the break. Right on top of it. Four kilometers off the prime set line, maybe less."

"How long?"

"Drive signature's cold. Eight hours at least. Maybe since yesterday."

Sofia nodded. She turned toward the comm panel, then turned back. She looked at the overhead, where a small speaker grille sat flush with the ceiling — the system's mouth, Margaret called it.

"System, what's our read on the grounds?"

The system answered in the flat, room-temperature voice that Margaret had calibrated fifteen years ago and never bothered to personalize. "Organic density at the thermal break is fourteen-point-seven. Historic yield at this position, adjusted for seasonal variance, averages eleven-point-two on comparable cycles. Confidence: high. Source: Captain Vendel log archive, forty-five seasons."

*Captain Vendel* meant Gus. The system had never updated the title. Sofia hadn't asked it to. She wasn't sure if that was sentiment or an oversight, and she didn't want to examine it too closely either way.

Fourteen-seven. That was the kind of number you heard twice in a season, if you were lucky. Gus used to say that a fourteen-seven ground was a Christmas bonus, a new hauler bearing, and three months of peace of mind, all floating in a cloud of organics at the temperature boundary.

She looked at the display again. The Daybreak sat on the prime line.

"Get Ray," she said.

---

Ray Anicet was in the galley, sitting with a cup of coffee that had gone cold an hour ago. He was reading. Or he was looking at a reader with his eyes open, which was what he did instead of sleeping most nights. He was sixty-eight. He'd run the Resolute for nineteen years before Sofia's uncle bought her, and he'd stayed on after Gus died, which surprised no one who knew him, because the Resolute was the only place Ray still existed. His house on Tycho Station had a two-year film of dust on everything. His wife, Bess, had died there — in the house, not on the ship — and Ray had shipped out a week later and hadn't been back since.

He didn't talk about Bess. He talked to the system sometimes, in the gaps between tasks, the way you talk to someone who's left the room but might still be in earshot. *System, what's the exterior temp. System, how's the line tension. System, remind me to check the number-three locker.* Things he'd said to Bess on the comm, back when she was on the other end. The system answered all of it in the same flat voice, and Ray accepted its answers the way he accepted everything — steadily, without visible reaction, the way old wood takes weather.

Tomás found him in the galley. "Captain wants you in the wheelhouse."

Ray closed his reader. "She say what for?"

"The Daybreak's on the grounds."

Ray set his cup down carefully, the way you set things down on a boat — paying attention to the motion of the deck, even when the deck isn't moving. He stood up. He was a big man, but he moved like someone who had learned to be careful with his size in small spaces. He followed Tomás up the narrow ladder without speaking.

---

Sofia Vendel was twenty-nine. She'd been a deckhand on the Resolute for three seasons before Gus died — heart attack, on Tycho Station, between runs, sitting in a chair with a drink in his hand, which was the way you'd want to go if you had to go — and she'd inherited the ship because Gus had no children and his will was two sentences long. *The boat goes to Sofia. She'll know what to do with it.*

She was still finding out what to do with it.

She had Gus's jaw and her mother's eyes, and she wore her hair short because long hair in a pressure suit was a problem she'd solved once and never again. She had a captain's license and three seasons of deep-space time, and she was frightened in a way she managed by being very still and very precise, which some people mistook for calm. Gus had the same stillness. Gus had learned it from thirty-five years of deep water.

When Ray came into the wheelhouse, Sofia didn't turn around.

"Harmon's on the grounds."

"I see him." Ray looked at the display over her shoulder. The Daybreak's icon sat on the thermal break like a thumb on a scale. "Right on Gus's spot."

"Is it Gus's spot?"

Ray was quiet for a moment. "It was."

"The system says fourteen-seven organic density. High confidence."

"That's a good number."

"Was it Gus's spot because Gus had a deal with Harmon, or was it Gus's spot because Gus got there first every cycle?"

Ray leaned against the bulkhead. He crossed his arms. He knew what she was asking. He'd known she'd ask it before they left Tycho. "You're asking me what I think you're asking."

"I'm asking what the arrangement was. Not what I should do about it."

"And I'm going to tell you what the arrangement was, and then I'm going to let you decide what to do about it, because that's how this works."

Sofia turned around. Ray saw her face. She looked like Gus when she was working through something — the same set to the mouth, the same focused stillness in the eyes.

"Gus and Harmon had a handshake," Ray said. "Every other cycle, this ground was Harmon's. Gus took the alternate. They'd been doing it since before I came aboard. Since before the Resolute was the Resolute, back when Gus was running a different boat."

"Skip-cycle."

"Skip-cycle. You take it one pass, he takes the next. Kept both boats working without cutting each other's lines. Made sense for both of them — Gus needed the guaranteed ground, Harmon needed the exclusivity on his cycles. Two boats can't work the same thermal without tangling. You know that."

"Whose cycle is this?"

Ray didn't answer immediately. He looked at the display. The Daybreak hadn't moved.

"It was Gus's turn," Sofia said.

"It was."

"And Gus is dead."

"And Harmon is Harmon."

"And the handshake died with Gus."

"That's the question, isn't it."

Sofia looked at him. She needed something from him — not an answer, but permission to find her own. Ray knew the difference. He'd known it with Gus, too.

"Your boat, Sofia," he said. "Your call."

---

Denny Okafor was in the equipment bay with his head inside the main hauler housing when the system first flagged the vibration. He heard it before the system did — or rather, he heard the *quality* of it change, the way a mechanic hears an engine note shift by half a tone, which is a thing you cannot teach in a classroom or a manual. You learn it by spending ten thousand hours next to a machine, and then one morning the machine says something slightly different and you notice.

The system flagged it three minutes later. A small amber light on the diagnostic panel: **HAULER-1 BEARING VIBRATION, OUT OF SPEC.** Denny glanced at it, noted the frequency and amplitude, and filed it under *later*. The hauler had been making noise for six months. Bearings wore. Housings loosened. It was an old machine doing old-machine things, and the Resolute was an old ship full of old machines that he kept running with his hands and his ears and the institutional knowledge that lived in his bones. He'd adjust the bearing preload during the maintenance window between grounds. Three days from now. Plenty of time.

He closed the housing panel and wiped his hands on a rag that was already more grease than cloth. Twelve years on the Resolute. He'd signed on when he was twenty-three, a yard tech from Callisto with good references and no plan. Gus had hired him on the dock after a five-minute conversation that was mostly Denny listening to the hauler run and telling Gus the port-side bearing was going. Gus had hired him on the spot. *Anybody who can hear a bearing from the dock can work on my boat.*

Denny knew every sound the Resolute made. Every creak, every whine, every rattle that meant something and every rattle that didn't. The hauler vibration was, he was almost certain, a rattle that didn't. Background noise. Old bearings in an old housing. He was wrong about that, but he didn't know it yet.

---

Tomás Luque was twenty-two, from Meridian Colony, and this was his first season in deep space. He'd done two years on inner-system shuttle runs — cargo work, the kind of spacefaring that's more ferry than voyage. Tycho to Europa, Europa to Callisto, back to Tycho. Same stars, same stations, same traffic. The Resolute was different. The Resolute went out past the beacon network into the deep black, where the transit lanes thinned to nothing and the sky opened up into something that Tomás didn't have a word for yet. Not darker — dark was dark. But wider. A dark that had depth.

He was on watch, monitoring the Daybreak's position and trying not to listen to Sofia and Ray discuss things above his pay grade. He'd learned in the first week that the Resolute's crew didn't explain things to you. You watched. You listened. After a while, the picture filled in, or it didn't, and you got replaced at the next station. Gus's system, Ray had told him. *Learn fast, ask twice, then stop asking.*

What Tomás could see: the Daybreak was sitting on the prime line. Sofia was tense. Ray wasn't going to help her, or rather, he was going to help her by not helping her, which was a kind of help Tomás was still learning to recognize. And the system — the voice from the ceiling — kept reporting numbers in the same even tone, whether it was telling them the hauler was fine or telling them the grounds were rich. It didn't editorialize. It just reported. It had learned to report from Gus, who had logged forty-five years of data in the same flat, factual voice, and the system had inherited the tone the way a child inherits a parent's cadence on the telephone.

Tomás's job was to watch the displays and not say anything stupid. He was reliable on the first count. He was working on the second.

---

Sofia opened the channel to the Daybreak. She did it the way Gus had taught her — open channel, identify vessel, identify self, wait. Maritime protocol. Deep-space protocol. Same grammar, different ocean.

"F/V Daybreak, this is F/V Resolute, Captain Vendel. Come in."

A pause. The soft hiss of an open carrier. Then a voice — older, gravelled, an accent Tomás couldn't place. Something from the old stations, maybe. Something that predated the colonies.

"Resolute, this is Saldibar. Go ahead."

"Captain Saldibar, we're arriving at the thermal grounds. I see you're set on the prime line."

"I am."

"This ground cycles to the Resolute this pass. My uncle, Gus Vendel—"

"I know who Gus was."

The channel sat between them like a table nobody was sitting at. Sofia held the transmit key.

"Gus and I had an arrangement," she said. "Skip-cycle on this ground. This is our cycle."

Harmon's voice was steady. Not hostile. The steadiness of a man stating a position, not picking a fight. "Your uncle's arrangement was with me. Personally. Gus is gone. The arrangement was between Gus and Harmon. Not between Harmon and whoever inherits the boat."

Sofia let go of the transmit key. In the silence of the wheelhouse, she could hear the hum of the display fans and the faint, rhythmic click of the system updating its thermal readout. She looked at Ray. Ray was studying the deck with the careful attention of a man who was listening very hard to everything and offering nothing.

"F/V Daybreak, stand by," Sofia said, and closed the channel.

---

Margaret Liang was in the systems room — a narrow space between the equipment bay and the reactor bulkhead, lined with monitoring panels and cable runs she'd installed herself. She'd wired the Resolute's internal network during build, at the yard on Callisto, seventeen years ago. Every sensor, every relay, every pressure switch and temperature probe and flow meter ran through her architecture. She'd wired three other ships since then, but the Resolute was her firstborn, and her best.

The hauler diagnostic updated from amber to red at 0453. She saw it immediately — she saw everything immediately, that was the whole point of the systems room, that was why she existed on this ship. **BEARING FAILURE IMMINENT, MAINTENANCE REQUIRED.** She pulled up the detail. Bearing three on the main hauler shaft. Vibration amplitude had jumped from the steady-state background to a harmonic she didn't like the look of — a rising trend, not a spike. Spikes were noise. Rising trends were problems.

She called Denny.

"Denny, are you near hauler one?"

"I'm in the bay. Why?"

"Check your panel."

A pause. She could hear him cross the bay. The clang of his boots on the deck plates. "It's red?"

"Bearing three. You had an amber flag on it?"

"Six weeks ago. I noted it. It was within tolerance."

"It's not within tolerance anymore. The amplitude's climbing. Denny, if you run that hauler under load right now, you'll spin the bearing and seize the shaft. You know what a seized shaft sounds like?"

"I know what it sounds like."

"Then you know we're not running the hauler until that bearing is replaced."

"We're about to make a set, Margaret."

"Then you need to fix it before you make the set."

"It's a two-hour fix. The bearing puller, the preload calibration, the test cycle—"

"If you help me, ninety minutes. If Tomás helps, sixty. I've already pulled the kit."

Denny was quiet for a beat. She could hear him breathing, and behind that, the hauler idling, and behind that, the low, constant voice of the ship — the reactor hum, the air circulators, the thermal regulators, the thousand small machines that Margaret Liang had wired together into something that functioned, impossibly, as a single thing. A ship. A home. A working vessel four light-hours from the nearest station.

"We'll do it in sixty," Denny said.

"Denny."

"I hear you."

Margaret sat back. She didn't like the trend line. She'd seen bearing failures before — not many, because she was good at her job, but enough. A seized shaft doesn't just stop. It shatters. And shrapnel in the hauler housing means a full rebuild, which means ten days in a yard, which means the season's revenue bleeds out through the maintenance account and Sofia, who has owned this boat for one season and sixteen days, gets to learn what a bad year feels like.

She pulled up Denny's maintenance log for hauler one. He'd noted the vibration six weeks ago. Amber. *Recommended: monitor.* She saw his notation — clean, precise, the handwriting of a man who kept good records. He'd done everything right except the one thing that mattered: he hadn't trusted the data over his own experience. The vibration was small. His experience said small vibrations stayed small. The data said small vibrations were trends, and trends had directions.

She noted that he'd noted it, and said nothing. For now.

---

In the wheelhouse, Sofia was studying the chart display. The thermal break ran through the grounds like a current through a channel — a boundary layer where the temperature gradient concentrated organic compounds into something worth crossing four light-hours of empty space to collect. Gus had taught her to read it before he'd taught her anything else about the boat. *The temperature break is everything, Sofe. Everything collects there. Everything feeds there. You learn to read the break, you'll never need luck.*

The gradient map showed the prime line — fourteen-seven, where Harmon sat — and the secondary positions. Lower density. The system had them marked from Gus's logs, annotated with catch data and yield projections across forty-five seasons of working this ground.

"System, projected yield on secondary set lines for this cycle."

"Secondary line alpha: organic density eight-point-one. Secondary line beta: six-point-three. Secondary line gamma: four-point-nine."

Gus's numbers, filtered through the system's model. It said *four-point-nine* the way Gus used to say it — flat, dismissive, the verbal equivalent of a shrug. Four-nine wasn't worth the fuel to get there.

Sofia turned to Ray. "He's not moving off the prime."

"No."

"Do I push it?"

"What does pushing it look like?"

"I hold position on the prime line. I make my set. If he's there, he's there, and we work around each other."

"Two boats on the same thermal. Lines in the water at the same gradient. You know what happens."

She knew. Tangled gear. Damaged lines. Lost sets. In the worst case, a collision — boats working within meters of each other while collection lines ran for kilometers astern. It happened every season somewhere. Two captains who wouldn't give, gear in the water, and then the mutual ruin that came after.

"There's another option," she said.

"There's always another option."

"I take a secondary. I work the western break."

Ray looked at her. His expression didn't change, which was itself a kind of answer. "You could."

"Alpha's eight-one. That's workable."

"It is."

"It's not fourteen-seven."

"It's not."

"Ray."

"Yeah."

"Would Gus have moved?"

Ray was quiet for a long time. The wheelhouse was still except for the hum of the displays and the soft, periodic click of the system updating. Tomás stared at his screen and thought about the space outside the hull, the dark that had depth, and how right now he would rather be anywhere in it than in this room.

"Gus would have talked to Harmon," Ray said finally. "They'd have worked it out. On the radio, or over coffee if they were at the same station. One of them would have moved. They'd figure it out because they'd been figuring it out for thirty years." He paused. He looked at Sofia directly. "You haven't been doing this for thirty years. Harmon doesn't know you. He doesn't know what your word is worth, and you don't know what his is worth. That's not a failure. That's just new."

"He owed Gus."

"Gus is dead."

The word sat in the wheelhouse like a stone dropped on a table. Ray hadn't meant it hard. He'd meant it the way you mean all true things — plainly, because there's no kind way to soften them, and trying only makes them heavier. Gus was dead. Bess was dead. Handshakes died with the hands that made them, unless someone new reached out and made a new one.

Sofia turned back to the chart display.

---

Denny had the hauler housing open when Margaret arrived with the bearing kit. She found him on his back under the main shaft, working the bearing puller by feel — one hand on the tool, the other braced against the housing frame, his face invisible behind the mass of the machine.

"Let me see the diagnostic," she said.

"It's the bearing. You called it."

"Let me see the shaft. There might be scoring."

"Shaft's fine, Margaret."

"Denny. Let me see the shaft."

He slid out. His face was streaked with lubricant — the thick, dark kind that smelled like graphite and burnt polymer and that got under your fingernails and stayed there for weeks. Margaret knelt next to the housing and shone a probe light along the shaft. She could see the wear — a faint groove where the bearing had been riding unevenly, the metal polished bright in a strip that shouldn't be bright. Not catastrophic. Not yet.

"If we replace the bearing now, the shaft will last another season, maybe two," she said. "If we run it and it seizes, you're looking at a full shaft replacement. Possibly a new housing. Possibly a new hauler."

"How long to swap the bearing?"

"If you help me, ninety minutes. If Tomás helps, sixty."

"Sofia wants to make a set."

"Sofia wants to make a set with a working hauler. I'll tell her sixty minutes. You get Tomás down here."

Denny looked at Margaret. Margaret looked at Denny. Twelve years of shared work in that look — every repair, every crisis, every three-in-the-morning emergency when something broke that shouldn't have and they'd fixed it together with frozen fingers and a flashlight clamped in their teeth. They'd done this a hundred times. They'd do it a hundred more.

Denny keyed the intercom. "Bridge, this is Denny. Hauler's down for maintenance. Sixty minutes."

Sofia's voice came back, flat. "What happened?"

"Bearing three. Margaret caught it."

A pause. "We need the hauler, Denny."

"You need a hauler that works. Sixty minutes."

Another pause. "Copy. Sixty minutes." And then, softer: "Tell Margaret thanks."

Denny looked at Margaret. "She says thanks."

"I heard her. Get me Tomás. And get me the preload gauge — the good one, not the yard one."

---

Tomás came down at Margaret's call, and for sixty minutes the equipment bay was a working space — hands and tools and the precise, unhurried language of people who knew what they were doing. Margaret directed. Denny executed. Tomás handed things and held things and tried to stay useful without being in the way, which was the essential skill of a new hand on any boat.

The bearing came out harder than it should have — heat-welded to the shaft seat, the old lubricant baked to a shellac that had to be chipped away with a pick before the new bearing would seat. Margaret checked the shaft tolerance twice with the preload gauge. Denny cleaned the housing with solvent and inspected every surface. Tomás held the light steady and didn't talk.

At minute fifty-eight, Margaret ran the hauler through a no-load cycle. The diagnostic cleared from red to green. The vibration was gone — not reduced, gone. The shaft turned true.

Denny listened. He closed his eyes and listened the way he always listened to machines, with his whole body, feeling the frequency through the deck plates and the bulkheads and the bones of his hands.

"Sound right?" Margaret asked.

"Sounds right."

She closed her toolkit. Each tool in its slot. She'd had the same kit since the Callisto yard. Green case, foam-cut slots, every piece accounted for. "Denny. The system flagged the bearing three minutes after you heard it yourself. You noted it six weeks ago. You knew."

"I assessed it. I made a call."

"You assessed it into a red flag. The call was wrong."

Denny was quiet. He didn't argue because she was right, and arguing with Margaret when she was right was something he'd stopped doing around year three.

"Next time you hear something I haven't flagged yet," Margaret said, "tell me. Don't wait for my sensors to catch up to your ears."

"That's fair."

Margaret paused at the bay door. "The new bearing is good. But I'm adding a higher-frequency sensor to the housing. I want to catch it earlier next time."

"Next time."

"There's always a next time, Denny. That's what boats are."

She left. Denny stood in the equipment bay and listened to the hauler idle. Clean. Smooth. The absence of the vibration was itself a sound — or rather, a space where a sound used to be. He'd been listening to that vibration for six weeks, and now it was gone, and he realized he'd been listening to it the way you listen to a dripping faucet, not because you can't hear it but because you've decided it's not worth fixing. That was a habit. Habits killed boats. He filed it under lessons, which was a mental folder that got thicker every year and never got lighter.

---

Sofia opened the channel to the Daybreak again. She stood at the comm panel with her hand on the transmit key and her weight balanced — feet apart, shoulders level, the way Gus stood when he was about to say something he'd have to stand behind.

"F/V Daybreak, Captain Vendel."

"Go ahead, Resolute."

"Captain Saldibar, I've been thinking about what you said. The arrangement was personal between you and Gus. I take that at face value."

"Go on."

"I'm not Gus. I won't pretend to be. But I'm on Gus's boat, I'm here on Gus's cycle, and I'm asking you — not telling you — whether there's a way we can both work this ground without cutting each other up."

Silence on the channel. Tomás could hear the background hum of the Daybreak's systems through the open link — the sound of another ship, another crew, another set of problems on the other side of four kilometers of dark.

"What did you have in mind?" Harmon asked.

"I don't have the whole ground mapped the way you do. Gus's data is good, but you've been working this thermal longer than the Resolute has been a boat. If there's a position on the break you're not working — something that makes sense for a smaller operation — I'd take it. I'd log the coordinates, and I'd remember how I got them."

"You'd remember."

"I would."

"And next cycle?"

"Next cycle, we talk. Before either of us sets lines. The way you and Gus did."

Harmon didn't respond immediately. Sofia could hear him breathing — not dramatic, just an old man thinking, taking his time, weighing a decision that had more dimensions than the ones on the surface. He was weighing her. Her voice, her cadence, the way she'd framed it. Not a demand. Not an assertion of rights. A request, backed by the implicit acknowledgment that he had something she needed and the power to withhold it.

"Your uncle," Harmon said. "He was a good fisherman."

"He was."

"Not the best I ever saw. But consistent. Reliable. You knew what you were getting with Gus."

"I know."

"I'll send you coordinates. Western edge of the thermal, where the break curves. Yield runs about nine, maybe nine-five on a good cycle. It's clean ground — no debris, no interference. Good set line." He paused. "It's not the prime."

"I know it's not the prime."

"But it'll keep your boat running."

"It will."

"And I'll expect a call next cycle, Captain Vendel. Before you transit in."

"You'll get one."

"Copy, Resolute. Daybreak out."

The channel closed. Sofia stood in the wheelhouse and let out a breath she'd been holding for what felt like the entire conversation. Her hands were shaking — a fine tremor, barely visible, the kind that comes after the adrenaline and not during. She put them back in her jacket pockets.

Tomás looked at his display. "Western edge coordinates coming in. Marking."

"Thank you, Tomás."

"Captain." He paused, choosing his words the way you choose footing on uneven ground. "That was well done."

Sofia almost smiled. Almost. The left corner of her mouth moved, and then she caught it and brought it back. "Set course for the western break. Two-kilometer approach, slow. I want to read the gradient before we set."

---

Sixty-two minutes after Denny's call to the bridge, Margaret confirmed the hauler was operational. Sofia brought the Resolute to the western edge of the thermal, where the break curved and the gradient shifted — not the sharp, rich boundary of the prime line, but a workable gradient. Real ground. The kind of ground that paid the fuel bill and the crew shares and left enough for the next transit.

Ray came to the wheelhouse after the set was in the water. He stood behind Sofia, hands in his pockets, looking out at the display where the collection lines extended into the dark along the gradient. The Daybreak's icon held its position on the prime line, four kilometers east.

"How's it reading?" Ray asked.

"Organic density at nine-two. Climbing slowly."

"Not fourteen-seven."

"No. But it's climbing."

"Nine-two will pay for fuel and stores."

"It'll do more than that."

"It will." Ray looked at her. "You did what Gus would have done."

"Does that mean it was right?"

"It means it was his move. You made it yours." He paused. "Same shape, different hands."

Sofia watched the density readout. The first pull was building — the collection lines gathering organics along the thermal boundary, the hauler ready to bring them in. Through the deck plates she could feel the hauler running, the faint vibration of the shaft turning in its new bearing, steady and clean. Margaret's wiring translating the hauler's heartbeat into data on the screens. Denny standing by the machine he'd just rebuilt, listening. Tomás at the watch station, learning to read the numbers the way Gus had taught Sofia to read the temperature break — by repetition, by patience, by the slow accumulation of seasons.

"System, log set position and organic density for cycle archive."

"Logged. Set position: western thermal break. Organic density: nine-point-three, trending upward. Confidence: high."

"Source?"

"Captain Vendel log archive, season forty-six."

Season forty-six. Gus's forty-five, plus one. Hers.

"First pull coming up," Tomás said.

Sofia nodded. "Bring it in."

The hauler engaged. Through the hull, she could feel the lines coming in — the long, slow pull of the harvest, the weight of something real being gathered from the dark. The hauler ran true. The bearing held. The numbers climbed.

Ray stood behind her, watching the readout. He didn't say anything. He didn't need to. He'd given her back to herself by stepping back, and she'd found her ground — not the prime, not Gus's spot, but hers. A different break on the same thermal. A different handshake with a different man, built from nothing and starting fresh.

The Resolute pulled. The lines came in. The dark held.

*Your boat. Your call.*
