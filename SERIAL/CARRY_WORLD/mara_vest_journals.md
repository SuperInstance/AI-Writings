# THE JOURNALS OF MARA VEST

## Founder's Original Writings — First Crossing, Autumn

*Five entries selected from the twelve-volume Vest Journals, held in the climate-controlled vault at the Eastern Guild Hall, Meridian. Transcribed with editorial notes by Director Alaun Salaun, Year 195.*

*Context: Mara Vest, age 27, former diplomatic courier, undertook the first recorded crossing of the Divide in autumn of the founding year. She carried diplomatic correspondence between Eastern and Western governments during a naval blockade that had severed the sea route. She traveled alone. No relay stations existed. No route markers. No weather data. No one had crossed before her and survived to document the passage.*

*The crossing took nineteen days. These entries cover days 1, 4, 7, 12, and 15.*

---

### Day 1

Left Meridian at dawn. The sky was clear and the air had that autumn edge — the kind that tells you the warmth is borrowed and the loan is nearly due. I packed light because I did not know what heavy meant yet. Now I know. Heavy is everything you carry when no one is coming to help you.

The letters are in a waxed leather tube strapped to my back. Seven dispatches. I read none of them. They are not mine to read. They are mine to carry. There is a difference, and the difference is the whole of my work, though I did not understand that yet on Day 1.

I walked until the trees thinned. I made camp near a stream at what I would later call the treeline, though I did not have a name for it then. The water was cold and tasted like stone. I ate bread and dried fruit. I built a fire because I was afraid of the dark, which is a thing I will not admit to anyone except this page.

The stars were extraordinary. I have lived in cities my entire life. I did not know there were this many. I counted them until I lost count, which happened quickly, and then I watched them move, which they don't do, but the Earth does, and so they seemed to move, and I fell asleep watching the sky turn above me like a slow wheel.

I think: I am twenty-seven years old and I am alone in a place where no one knows where I am. I think: this is either the bravest or the stupidest thing I have ever done. I think: those might be the same thing.

---

### Day 4

The air changed today. Not gradually — suddenly, like walking through a door into a colder room. I am above the first major band of what I have started calling the singing stone, because when the wind moves across it at certain angles, it hums. A low, sustained note. Not loud. Constant. Like the mountain is breathing and the stone is the throat the breath moves through.

I noticed something else: my compass needle has begun to drift. Not wildly — it still finds north, but it hesitates. It wanders east for a few seconds, then settles. I tapped the glass. It wandered again. The needle is brass on a jewel bearing and it has never failed me. I do not think the compass is broken. I think the mountain is doing something to it.

This is not a comfortable thought, alone, at altitude, with no map.

I have been following the ridgeline because the ridgeline goes west and I need to go west. But the ridgeline is narrow — three feet across in places, with drops on both side that I do not look at because looking does not help. What helps is putting one foot in front of the other and trusting the stone. The stone is solid. The stone does not care about my fear, which is almost reassuring. The mountain's indifference is not cruelty. It is simply the mountain being the mountain. I find I prefer this to human unpredictability.

I made camp in a hollow in the rock. I cannot call it a shelter — it is a gap between two boulders, just wide enough for my body if I lie on my side. The fire I built was small. The wind took most of the heat. I ate the last of the bread. Tomorrow I start on the dried meat and whatever I can find.

I thought about the letters in the tube. Diplomatic correspondence. Words written by people in warm rooms, words that will be read by other people in warm rooms. Words that matter enough to send someone across a mountain range but not enough to come themselves. I do not resent this. It is the arrangement. I am the distance between them. I am the stone the words must cross.

My fingers are cold. Eight of them — the thumbs stay warmer, tucked against my palms. I have wrapped them in cloth but the cold comes through. The fire is too small. I cannot build it larger because I cannot find enough wood at this altitude — the few shrubs that grow here are barely kindling. I blow on the coals and they glow and I hold my hands over them and the heat lasts for a second and then the wind takes it.

I write by firelight with a pencil, not a pen, because ink freezes. This is something I learned today. Another thing I learned today: when your fingers are numb enough, you cannot tell if the pencil is making marks or scraping bare paper. I press hard. I hope the marks are there. If they are not, if this journal is blank pages filled with pressure marks, then I am talking to no one, which is what I am doing, which is what being alone is.

I am not unhappy. I want to be clear about this, page. I am not unhappy. I am cold and I am tired and I am afraid in a way that is not familiar to me, and I am also more awake than I have ever been in my life. Every sense is sharpened. Every sound matters. The wind tells me things — which direction, how strong, whether it carries moisture. The stone under my boots tells me things — is it stable, is it loose, is it ice-covered. I am reading a text written in wind and rock, and I am illiterate, and I am learning the alphabet by walking through it.

Tomorrow I go higher.

---

### Day 7

I do not know what day it really is. Seven, I think. Maybe eight. The days have blurred together because the landscape does not change at the pace of human time. I have been above the clouds for two days. The sky is dark blue — not the blue of a clear day at lower altitude, but a deep, saturated blue that looks almost purple at the edges. The sun is harsh and close. I can feel it burning my face even as the wind freezes my hands.

I found a pass today. A cut in the ridgeline, maybe ten feet wide, with sheer walls on both sides. The wind through the pass was — I do not have a word for it. Not strong. Not violent. Absolute. The wind through that pass was a fact, the way gravity is a fact. I leaned into it and it held me up. I leaned with it and it threw me sideways. I walked through it bent almost double, one hand on the rock wall, and when I came out the other side I was shaking, not from cold but from the effort of moving through something that did not want me there.

This is where I will build the first station. If I survive this crossing, I will build a station at this pass. A shelter on the lee side. A place where a carrier can rest out of the wind. The wind is the enemy here — not the cold, not the altitude. The wind. The cold you can insulate against. The altitude you can acclimate to. The wind you can only endure, and endurance is a finite resource.

I am writing in the shelter of a rock overhang. The sky above me is full of stars again. The singing stone is louder here. The hum is constant, and sometimes it shifts pitch — higher when the wind is stronger, lower when it eases. I pressed my hand flat against the rock and I could feel the vibration in my palm, in my wrist, in the bones of my arm. The mountain is alive. Not the way a person is alive — the way a heart is alive. Mechanical. Rhythmic. Involuntary.

I ate snow today for water because I could not find a stream. I melted it over the fire, cup by cup. The water tasted metallic. Vestite, I think — the mineral I have been finding in the rock layers. It is everywhere above a certain altitude. It is beautiful — dark veins in grey stone, almost black, with a faint crystalline structure that catches the light. But it does something to the instruments. My compass is useless now. The needle spins slowly, continuously, like it's looking for something it can't find. I navigated today by the sun and the shape of the ridgeline. This works in clear weather. In a storm, I would be lost.

Note to self: if I build this route, every carrier needs to memorize the path. Instruments will not save them. Memory will. The stone will. Their feet will.

I am thinking about a woman named Eliss. She is a clerk in the Western chancellery. I have never met her. She wrote one of the letters I am carrying — I know because the tube was sealed in her office and the seal is marked with her department's stamp. I do not know what the letter says. But I am carrying her words across a mountain range, and she does not know my name, and I do not know her face, and this is the arrangement, and the arrangement is the reason the world works. People carry things for other people. That is all civilization is. A system for carrying things across distances.

I will call this route The Carry. I do not know why. It sounds right. It sounds like what it is.

---

### Day 12

I almost died today.

I am writing this in a cave — a real cave, not a hollow between boulders. A fissure in the rock that opens into a space large enough to stand in. I found it by accident. I was descending a slope of loose scree when the ground gave way beneath me — not a collapse, a slide, the stone shifting under my weight like a pile of marbles. I went down fast. I couldn't stop. I grabbed for rock and got air. I slid thirty meters, maybe forty, and the only reason I am alive is that the scree piled up against a lip of harder stone and I stopped.

My left ankle is sprained. I can walk on it, but slowly, and the pain is — significant. I wrapped it with a strip of cloth from my shirt. The swelling has begun. I cannot afford to rest long. If I rest too long, the ankle stiffens, and a stiff ankle on this terrain is worse than a painful one.

The tube is intact. I checked it first, before I checked my ankle, before I checked anything. The tube is intact. The letters are safe. This is the priority. I did not decide this. My body decided it. I was on the ground, and my hands went to the tube before my hands went to my ankle. The arrangement: the load first. The carrier second. Always.

I have been thinking about that. The arrangement is the answer to a question I did not know I was asking. The question is: what matters more, the person or the purpose? And the answer, on this mountain, alone, is: the purpose. The purpose is the reason the person is here. Without the purpose, the person would be in a warm room writing letters instead of carrying them. The person is the instrument. The load is the music.

I do not mean this as self-sacrifice. I am not sacrificing myself. I am applying myself. The way a tool is applied to a task. I am the tool. The route is the task. The letters are the reason.

I am cold. I am hurt. I am out of bread and the dried meat is almost gone. I do not know how many more days. Five, perhaps. Six. The western side must be close — the slope has been descending for two days, and the air is thicker, and the vestite deposits are thinning. The compass needle has slowed its spinning. It almost finds north now. Almost.

I think about warm rooms. I think about bread and soup and tea. I think about a bed. I think about these things with a precision and a longing that surprises me. I did not know I cared about beds. I have slept in beds my entire life and never once thought: this is extraordinary. Now, on stone, in a cave, with a swollen ankle and a stomach that has forgotten what full feels like, I think: a bed is extraordinary. Comfort is extraordinary. The absence of pain is extraordinary. We build our lives in the absence of pain and call it ordinary, and then pain comes and we learn that ordinary was never ordinary at all. It was the most extraordinary thing we ever had.

I will cross this mountain.

---

### Day 15

Trees.

I am below the treeline. I am on the western side. I can tell because the trees are different — broader-leafed, darker, wet with a mist that comes from the western plains. The air is warm. Not warm the way a room is warm — warm the way a hand is warm. The mountain let me go.

I have been walking for — I don't know. Days. The ankle screamed for the first day after the fall and then went quiet, which worried me more than the screaming. The pain moved from sharp to dull to a deep ache that I could walk through. I walked through it. I had no choice. The mountain does not offer choices. It offers passage or it offers death, and the difference between the two is sometimes a single step.

I found a stream today. I knelt beside it and drank and the water was clean and sweet and did not taste of metal. I drank until my stomach hurt. I have been rationing water for so long that I had forgotten what it felt like to drink until full.

The letters are intact. The tube is scratched, dented — the leather is gouged where the scree hit it. But the seals are unbroken. The words inside are safe. They do not know what it cost to carry them. They are words. They were written in a warm room and they will be read in a warm room and between the two warm rooms there was a mountain, and on the mountain there was a woman, and the woman carried them because that is what she was for.

I am sitting against a tree. A real tree — bark under my hand, roots under my legs, leaves above me moving in a wind that is gentle. Gentle. I had forgotten wind could be gentle. On the high route, the wind is a force — it pushes, it tears, it takes. Here, the wind is a touch. It moves the leaves and cools my face and does not want anything from me.

I weigh nothing. I have not eaten properly in — I don't know how long. My clothes hang on me. My hands are skeletal. The frostbite on my fingers — eight of them, the thumbs spared — has turned the tips black. I know what this means. I know I will lose parts of them. I do not care. I am alive. The letters are delivered. Or they will be, once I find the western town.

I have decided something on this mountain. I have decided that this route can be made passable. Not easy — never easy. But passable. With stations for shelter. With cairns for navigation. With teams of three so that when one falls, the others can catch them. With keepers at the stations to hold people back when the mountain says no, and to wave them through when the mountain says yes.

I will build this. I will build this route. I will build the stations. I will train the carriers. I will give this mountain a path that people can walk, because people need to cross it, and crossing it alone is — I will not say impossible. I am crossing it alone. But I am twenty-seven and strong and lucky and the luck will not hold for the next person, or the next. The route needs to hold instead.

I will call it The Carry.

The fire is small and the wood is green and the smoke is thick and I don't care. I am warm. I am below the treeline. I am alive. The letters are safe.

Tomorrow I walk into the western town and I deliver the tube and I begin the work of building the thing I have just described, and if it takes thirty years, then it takes thirty years, and if it kills me — well. The mountain will have my body, and the cairns will have my name, and the carriers who walk after me will have the path.

That is enough. That is more than enough.

I am twenty-seven years old and I have crossed the Divide and I am going to build a road made of people.

*— Mara Vest, autumn, by firelight, on the western slope.*

---

*Editorial note (Director Salaun, Year 195): The remaining seven volumes cover the thirty-year period of guild construction — the building of relay stations, the training of the first carriers, the negotiation of the Accord of Meridian, the mapping and cairning of the route. Volume XII contains Mara's final entry, written twelve days before her death at The Notch at age 61. It reads, in full: "The route holds. The carriers walk. The mountain tolerates us. That is all I ever asked of it."*

*The journals were carried from the western slope to Meridian by the second carrier team — the first team Mara trained. They have been read by every director since. They are not available to the public. They are available to every carrier on their first day of training, and every carrier reads them, and every carrier puts them down and thinks about beds, and comfort, and the distance between people, which is the only distance that matters.*

*— A.S.*
