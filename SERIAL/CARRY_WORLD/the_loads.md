# THE LOADS

## Thirty Possible Loads That Cross the Divide

*Each load has a story. The carrier does not always know the story. But the story is there, in the tube or the parcel or the pack, and it is the reason the carrier walks.*

---

**1. A letter from a mother to a daughter who left.** Three pages, handwritten, folded twice. The daughter has not spoken to the mother in nine years. The mother does not know if the daughter will read it. The guild has been paid for priority delivery — three-day crossing. The carrier does not know any of this. The carrier knows only the weight: 40 grams. **Hook:** The daughter is Wren Aoki. The letter is from her mother. Wren refuses the load.

**2. A vial of bone marrow.** 200 milliliters, packed in a medical cold-chain container weighing 2.3 kilograms. Temperature-stable for 72 hours. The recipient is a 14-year-old boy in a Western hospital with aplastic anemia. The donor is his sister, who is in an Eastern hospital recovering from the collection procedure. The match is 10/10. There will not be another. **Hook:** The team encounters a storm at The Saddle. The cold-chain container has a time limit. The storm does not care about time limits.

**3. A diplomatic tube containing the text of a trade agreement.** Sealed with the Eastern chancellor's personal wax. The agreement covers 200 pages of tariff schedules, fishing rights, and border protocols. If the tube does not arrive by Friday, the Western parliament recesses for six weeks and the agreement dies. Two governments have spent three years negotiating. The tube weighs 300 grams. **Hook:** The carrier team learns, at The Step, that the Western parliament has moved the recess forward by two days. The deadline is now tomorrow. They have to do a three-day crossing in two.

**4. A wedding dress.** Handmade. Silk and lace. It weighs 1.8 kilograms and fills an entire pack. The bride is in the West. The dress was made by her grandmother in the East. The grandmother has six months to live. She finished the dress last week. She will not see the wedding. **Hook:** The pack tears at The Notch. The dress is exposed to wind and ice. Oskar wraps it in his own jacket and carries the rest of the crossing in his shirtsleeves.

**5. A data drive containing architectural plans for a hospital.** 850 grams in its shock-proof case. The hospital will be built in a Western city that had an earthquake six months ago. The plans are site-specific, drawn from geological surveys that took eight months to complete. The surveys cannot be repeated — the funding has been spent. If the drive is lost, the hospital is delayed by a year. **Hook:** Nothing goes wrong. The crossing is routine. The carrier hands the drive to the architect's assistant in Westernhead and walks away. Some loads are just loads.

**6. A music box.** Mechanical — brass and wood, no electronics. It plays a melody that the carrier has never heard. It weighs 600 grams. It is addressed to a hospice. The intended recipient is a woman who is dying and has not spoken in three weeks. Her husband ordered it from an Eastern craftsman who makes music boxes to specification. The specification was: "The song she used to sing while cooking." The craftsman did not know the song. The husband hummed it into a recording device and sent the recording east. The craftsman listened and built the mechanism. **Hook:** The carrier hears the music box play during a rest at The Shelf — it must have been jostled. The melody is simple, repetitive, a folk tune in a minor key. The carrier does not know the context. The carrier knows only that the box plays music and the music is for someone who is dying.

**7. A sealed envelope marked ONLY TO BE OPENED BY THE RECIPIENT.** 30 grams. The recipient is a Western government minister. The sender is an Eastern government minister. No guild record of contents. Priority delivery. **Hook:** The carrier drops the envelope at the ministry. Three days later, the minister resigns. The carrier never learns why. The guild receives a thank-you note and a substantial bonus. The bonus is redirected to the station maintenance fund. Nobody explains.

**8. A child's drawing.** Crayon on paper, folded into a padded envelope. 80 grams. It shows two people holding hands in front of a house. One person is very large. The other is very small. The sun is in the corner. The grass is green. The sky is blue. It is addressed to a man in a Western prison. **Hook:** The carrier sees the drawing because the envelope is torn. The carrier does not judge. The carrier carries. But the carrier thinks about the drawing for a long time afterward — the green grass, the blue sky, the two figures holding hands. The carrier wonders if the man in the prison remembers the house, or the child, or the sun in the corner.

**9. A canister of seeds.** 400 grams. Agricultural samples — a drought-resistant grain variant developed by an Eastern research institute. The canister is vacuum-sealed and labeled with scientific codes. It is being sent to a Western agricultural station for field trials. If the grain adapts, it could feed a million people in the western plains, where the rains have been failing. **Hook:** The canister is temperature-sensitive. Extreme cold kills the seeds. The carrier does not know this — the labeling is in scientific notation the carrier cannot read. The seeds pass through The Thumb at -30°C. They may be dead. They may not be. The carrier will never know.

**10. A pair of shoes.** Children's shoes, size small. Leather, well-made, with buckles. They weigh 300 grams. They are addressed to an orphanage on the western side. The sender is anonymous. The guild sees this often — people send things to orphanages. Shoes, coats, books, toys. The guild carries these loads at a loss. The guild does not advertise this. **Hook:** The shoes fit a child at the orphanage. The child has never had new shoes. The child wears them every day until they are too small and then gives them to a younger child, who wears them every day until they are too small, and so on. The shoes cross the Divide once and then they cross a city, and a neighborhood, and a childhood, and another childhood.

**11. A diplomatic passport.** One document, 50 grams, in a waterproof sleeve. The passport allows the bearer — a Western diplomat who has been expelled from the East for reasons that are not stated — to re-enter the West through the border post at Westernhead. The diplomat will carry the passport himself. He is the load. He weighs 78 kilograms. He has never been above 8,000 feet. **Hook:** The diplomat is not a bad man, but he is not a good walker. He slows the team. He complains about the food. At The Shelf, he asks when they will arrive. Henra tells him: three days. He asks if there is a faster way. Henra tells him: no. At The Notch, in the wind, he cries. Henra does not comfort him. She cannot. She has to navigate. But she slows her pace by ten percent so he can keep up, and she does not tell him she has done it.

**12. A blood sample.** 10 milliliters in a vacuum tube, packed in a diagnostic kit weighing 350 grams. The sample is from an Eastern patient with an unknown disease. It is being sent to the Western National Laboratory for analysis. The patient is in isolation. The disease has appeared in three people. Two have died. **Hook:** The tube breaks. The carrier reports it at The Step. Teo logs the incident. The guild notifies the sender. A new sample is drawn from the surviving patient and dispatched. The carrier carries the new sample on the next crossing. The second tube arrives intact. The laboratory identifies the disease three days later. It is treatable. The patient survives. The carrier does not know this. The carrier knows the tube broke and the tube didn't break.

**13. A love letter.** Handwritten, perfumed, sealed with a wax stamp shaped like a star. 20 grams. It is addressed to a man the sender has never met. They have corresponded by mail for two years — the sea route, six weeks each way. This is the first letter she has sent by carrier. It costs as much as her monthly rent. It will arrive in three days. She has written: "I think I love you, which is a strange thing to say to someone I have never seen, but there it is, and I am not taking it back." **Hook:** The letter arrives. The man reads it. He sits at his kitchen table for a long time. Then he writes a letter back. He sends it by carrier. It costs as much as his monthly rent. It says: "Come east." She does. They marry. Neither of them ever meets the carrier who delivered the letters. The carrier is a woman named Henra Volk, who carried a 20-gram letter across a mountain range and did not know what it said and did not need to know.

**14. A clock mechanism.** Brass gears, steel springs, a pendulum assembly. 1.2 kilograms. It is a replacement mechanism for a tower clock in a Western town where the original mechanism — 150 years old — has failed. The new mechanism was built by an Eastern horologist who specializes in mechanical clocks. It is precise to within one second per month. **Hook:** The mechanism must not be subjected to extreme shock. The carrier team knows this. At The Cone, a rockfall forces the team to take cover. Oskar shield the pack with his body. A rock the size of a fist hits his shoulder. The mechanism is intact. Oskar's shoulder is not. He finishes the crossing.

**15. A divorce decree.** Legal document, notarized, stamped, official. 80 grams. The marriage lasted eleven years. The divorce took four. The decree grants custody of the children to the mother, who is in the East. The father is in the West. He contested the divorce for two years and then stopped contesting it and the decree was issued. The carrier does not know this. The carrier carries the paper. **Hook:** The man receives the decree at his door. He reads it standing in the doorway. He folds it and puts it in his pocket and walks to the harbor and watches the ships for a long time. He does not cry. He is not sad. He is something that does not have a word. The carrier is already gone.

**16. A fertility clinic shipment.** Four vacuum-sealed vials in a cryogenic container. 1.8 kilograms. Temperature-stable for five days. The vials contain embryos — frozen, viable, irreplaceable. A couple in the West have been on a waiting list for four years. The donor match became available in the East three days ago. The window for the procedure is narrow. **Hook:** The crossing goes smoothly. The delivery is made. Nine months later, a child is born. The carrier does not know this. The carrier carries.

**17. A volume of poetry.** One book, 300 pages, hardcover. 600 grams. It is a first edition of a collection by an Eastern poet who died last year. It is being sent to the poet's former lover, who moved to the West twenty years ago. The lover does not know the poet has died. The book is inscribed: "For L., who understood the silence between words." **Hook:** Teo, at The Step, notices the book because he has read this poet. He asks the carrier about it. The carrier knows only the delivery address. Teo considers — briefly, in the way of a man who has been on the route for thirty years — keeping the book. He does not. He hands it back. He makes a note of the poet's name. He will look for the collection when the supply team comes up.

**18. A set of surgical instruments.** Titanium, sterile-packaged, specialized for neurological surgery. 3.5 kilograms in their case. They belong to a Western surgeon who has been called to the East to perform an operation on a child with a brain tumor. The surgeon is flying to the Eastern coast by sea. The instruments cannot go by sea — they were delayed by a customs error and the ship has sailed. The carrier route is the only option. **Hook:** The instruments arrive on time. The surgery is successful. The child lives. The guild is paid a premium. The premium covers the cost of two standard crossings. The instruments are carried back west, clean and repackaged, three weeks later.

**19. A military communique.** Sealed tube, 200 grams, marked with security classification. The guild does not normally carry military traffic — the Accord specifies the route as a neutral channel. But the communique is not military in the operational sense. It is a notification of a ceasefire. The war is small — a border conflict on the eastern coast, unrelated to the Divide. The ceasefire is fragile. The notification must reach the Western military liaison within 48 hours, or the ceasefire collapses. **Hook:** The carrier team does not know the contents. They know only the deadline. They make a fast crossing — three days. The tube is delivered. The ceasefire holds. For now.

**20. A photograph.** One photograph, black and white, in a stiff envelope. 40 grams. It shows a group of people in front of a building. The building is an orphanage. The people are children and staff. The photograph was taken forty years ago. It is being sent to a woman in the West who was one of the children in the photograph. She has not seen it before. A former staff member found it while cleaning out an attic and recognized the name. **Hook:** The woman is 72. She opens the envelope and looks at the photograph for a long time. She finds herself in the back row — small, serious, holding another child's hand. She cannot remember whose hand. She puts the photograph on her mantle. Sometimes she looks at it and tries to remember.

**21. A chess set.** Hand-carved wooden pieces, no board (the recipient has a board). 800 grams. Each piece is wrapped in cloth. The pieces are beautiful — dark walnut and pale birch, turned on a lathe, weighted with lead inserts. The set was made by a man in the East for his estranged father in the West. The man has not spoken to his father in fifteen years. He does not include a letter. The set arrives without explanation. **Hook:** The father opens the package. He recognizes the wood — walnut from the tree in his mother's yard, birch from the grove behind the house where his son grew up. He sets the pieces on the board. He does not call. He does not write. He plays a game against himself, slowly, taking hours, and leaves the pieces on the board when he is done.

**22. A geological core sample.** A cylinder of rock, 15 centimeters long, in a protective tube. 900 grams. The sample is from a borehole on the eastern side of the Divide, drilled by the geological survey. It is being sent to a Western university laboratory for analysis. The sample contains an unusual concentration of vestite. **Hook:** The sample arrives. The analysis reveals properties that do not match any known vestite profile. The university contacts the guild. The guild contacts Director Salaun. Salaun reads the report and adds it to the file she is compiling about the changes in the Divide. The file is getting thicker.

**23. A prosthetic eye.** Custom-made, hand-painted acrylic. 30 grams. It is a perfect match for the recipient's remaining eye — brown, with a fleck of gold near the pupil. The ocularist who made it is in the East. The recipient is in the West — a woman who lost her eye in an industrial accident. **Hook:** The carrier knows only the weight and the fragility warning. The prosthetic eye, in its padded case, rides in Oskar's pack among heavier items. He does not know what it is. He carries it the way he carries everything — carefully, completely, as if the pack and its contents are the most important thing in the world, because for the duration of the crossing, they are.

**24. A confession.** Five pages, handwritten, notarized. 50 grams. The writer is a man in an Eastern prison serving a sentence for a crime he did not commit. He is confessing to a crime he did commit — a different crime, in the West, for which another person was convicted. The confession is addressed to a Western judge. The carrier does not know the contents. **Hook:** The confession arrives. The judge reads it. An investigation begins. A man in a Western prison is released after seven years. A man in an Eastern prison dies in his cell before he can be charged with the western crime. The carrier learns about this three years later, in a newspaper. She reads the article twice and folds the paper and puts it away and does not think about it again for a long time.

**25. A recording.** A wax cylinder — the kind used in antique phonographs. 150 grams in its protective case. The recording is a voice. A woman's voice, singing. The woman is dead. The recording was found in an estate sale in the East. The buyer is a man in the West who has been searching for recordings by this singer for twenty years. She was his grandmother. He has never heard her voice. **Hook:** The wax cylinder is fragile — temperature-sensitive and shock-sensitive. The carrier team is briefed on its handling. At The Thumb, the temperature drops to -35°C. The cylinder sits against Oskar's body, inside his jacket, where the cold reaches last. It survives. The man plays it on a phonograph he bought for this purpose. The voice fills his living room — thin, wavering, beautiful. A song in a language he does not speak. His grandmother's voice, carried across a mountain, a hundred years after she sang it.

**26. A key.** A single brass key, 20 grams, in a small box. It opens something — a door, a box, a cabinet. The carrier does not know what. The sender is dead — the key was found in her effects, with a note: "For my nephew, in Westernhead." The nephew does not know his aunt has died. He does not know she has left him anything. He does not know what the key opens. **Hook:** The key is delivered. The nephew is 34 and works in a bakery. He puts the key in a drawer. Six months later, he finds it and decides to investigate. The key opens a storage unit. Inside the storage unit is a wooden box. Inside the box is a collection of letters — fifty years of correspondence between his aunt and a woman he has never heard of. The letters tell a story his family never shared. The key was not a key. It was a door.

**27. A kilogram of coffee beans.** Vacuum-sealed, 1 kilogram total. Premium Eastern highland coffee, roasted three days ago. The recipient is Teo Maric, at The Step. The sender is Dr. Kella Volk, who sends a kilogram every month because Teo makes the best coffee on the route and good coffee requires good beans, and if the guild will not fund this, she will. **Hook:** The beans arrive fresh. Teo grinds them by hand. He brews a cup. He drinks it standing at the window of The Step, looking east, watching the morning light hit the peaks. It is, by his assessment, a good cup. He logs nothing about this. Some things are too small for the log and too large for the record.

**28. A puppy.** 4 kilograms. A live animal — a six-week-old mastiff puppy, sedated for the crossing, in a ventilated carrier pack. The puppy is a gift from an Eastern breeder to a Western family. The family has children. The children want a dog. **Hook:** The sedative wears off at The Shelf. The puppy wakes up and begins to cry. The carrier team has a live, distressed animal at 11,500 feet. Oskar holds the puppy inside his jacket. The puppy stops crying. It falls asleep against his chest. Oskar carries it the rest of the crossing. When he delivers it to the family, one of the children — a girl, five years old — says "He's warm." Oskar says: "They usually are." He walks away. He does not tell anyone about the puppy. But Wren notices that he is humming when they descend to The Door, and she does not mention it, because some things you let be.

**29. An unsigned painting.** Oil on canvas, 40 x 30 centimeters, rolled in a tube. 400 grams. It depicts a mountain — not the Divide, but a gentler mountain, green, with a village at its base. The painting is unsigned and undated. The sender is an art dealer. The recipient is a museum. The dealer believes the painting is by a significant Western artist, but cannot authenticate it without physical examination by the museum's experts. **Hook:** The painting is authentic. It is worth a fortune. The carrier carried a fortune across the Divide in a tube and did not know it. The museum displays the painting. A label identifies the artist. The carrier, passing through Westernhead on a layover, sees the painting in a gallery window. She does not recognize it. She has carried thousands of loads. One more painting in a window is not remarkable. She walks on.

**30. Nothing.** An empty tube. 180 grams — the weight of the tube itself, sealed, with a delivery label. The sender paid the full priority rate. The recipient is expecting delivery. The tube is empty. **Hook:** The carrier does not know the tube is empty — it is sealed, and the guild does not open loads. The recipient opens the tube and finds nothing. They stare at the nothing for a long time. They understand the message. Or they don't. Or there is no message, and the nothing is just nothing, and the sender spent a fortune to send emptiness across a mountain range because emptiness was the only thing they had left to send. The carrier carries nothing. The nothing arrives. Someone weeps, or laughs, or closes the door, or stands in the light. The carrier is already gone. The carrier is walking back east. The carrier is carrying the next thing.

---

*These are thirty loads. There are thousands more. Every crossing carries something. Every something is a story. The carrier carries the story without reading it, and the story is delivered, and the carrier walks back, and there is another story waiting, and it goes on like this, and it has gone on like this for two hundred years, and it will go on like this until the mountains close or the carriers stop walking or the distance between people no longer matters, which is to say: never.*

*We carry.*
