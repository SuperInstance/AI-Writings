# KEEPER'S LOGS

## Relay Station Logbook Excerpts, 200 Years

*Transcribed from the original logs at the Eastern Guild Hall. Spelling and punctuation preserved where legible. Editorial notes in brackets.*

---

### I. THE GATE — Year 3 of the Guild

**Keeper: Halen Dris**
**Station: The Gate (8,000 ft)**

17th of Autumn

Dris here. The new shelter holds. Mortar is poor — too much sand in the mix from the riverbed. Will try a different ratio when the supply team comes up next. Two bunks, one table, one stove. It is enough.

Mara came through today with the western post. She was moving well. She does not stop for tea. She should. I told her so. She laughed at me. Nobody tells Mara Vest to rest. She left me three candles and a loaf of bread. The bread was stale. The candles are good.

Weather: clear from the east. Wind light. The first snow has not come. I watch for it.

### II. THE SHELTER — Year 41

**Keeper: Pela Tourr**
**Station: The Shelf (11,500 ft)**

8th of Spring, morning

Team arrived from the east at dawn. Three carriers — lead named Cort, I don't know the other two. Young faces. They carried a medical parcel, priority. I flagged them through. The instruments began acting up yesterday — the compass needle swings left of north now by about four degrees, then corrects. It has done this since the snowfall. I have noted it. If it continues I will send word to Meridian.

Cort left me a novel. Something about the sea. I will read it tonight.

Barometer: 21.4 and falling slowly. Wind: northwest, moderate. Visibility: good to the ridgeline.

### III. THE NOTCH — Year 67

**Keeper: Eddar Sune**
**Station: The Notch (14,200 ft)**

[Excerpt — fragment, the page is water-damaged]

...held the team three days. Three days. The wind did not stop. I have been at The Notch nine years and I have not seen wind like this — sustained, not gusting, like the mountain found a new shape to press the air through. I could hear it through the walls. I could feel it in the stone.

The carriers wanted to push through on the second day. They were restless. One of them, the young one, she had a letter for someone on the other side and the letter had a deadline. A wedding, I think. Or a funeral. She did not say and I did not ask. I held them. Teo's rule — my rule now — is that The Notch decides who passes. The team does not argue with the wind. They bunked. They ate my stores. They played cards. The young one lost.

On the third morning the wind dropped and I let them go. I watched them enter the cut and the wind took them sideways and I thought: I should have held them another day. But they made it through. I could see them on the far side, small against the rock, walking.

They did not come back this way.

### IV. THE RIB — Year 93

**Keeper: Jenos Wike**
**Station: The Rib (16,800 ft)**

Spring, 14th day

Repairs: the alcove wall. The east-facing stone is cracking along the mortar line. I believe this is frost cycling — water enters the joint, freezes, expands, the joint widens. It is slow but it is relentless. I have repointed the joint with a lime putty I mixed from the stones below the station. It will hold for the season. Next year I will need proper mortar brought up.

This is the problem with The Rib: the station is carved into the mountain, and the mountain is alive. Not in the way the carriers mean when they say it — not mood or temper or personality. The mountain moves. Micro-fractures. Thermal expansion. The stone breathes with the temperature, and the breathing opens cracks, and the cracks open the shelter, and the shelter needs me.

This is why we are here.

Two teams through this week. The second team's lead — a woman named Hallek — asked me how long I'd been up here. Twelve years, I said. She said: "You look like the mountain." I think she meant it as a compliment. My hands are like the stone here. Grey. Cracked at the knuckles. I did not tell her that.

### V. THE THUMB — Year 118

**Keeper: Ren Aldic**
**Station: The Thumb (18,400 ft)**

Log entry — autumn, season end

Last team of the season went through yesterday. Westbound. They carried diplomatic correspondence — the sealed tube, the guild's most charged load. The lead was a woman named Darya Cot. I have watched her cross for years. She moves like she was made for this route, and perhaps she was — some people are built for one thing and the thing for them, and the rest of their life is just waiting to do the thing again.

The diesel stove is low. I have enough fuel for the winter if I am careful, which I am, which you must be at 18,400 feet in winter with no one coming. The supply team will not reach me until late spring. This is the calculation: how much fuel, how much cold, how many months. I have made this calculation nine times. I have been right nine times. I do not attribute this to skill. I attribute it to the mountain not having killed me yet.

The glacier outside the station has changed. I have been here nine winters and the ice is smaller than it was. The face of it — the wall of blue ice that rises behind the station like a frozen wave — has retreated by what I estimate is fifteen meters since my first winter. I can see rock now that was not visible five years ago. Dark stone. Wet. The kind of stone that does not dry because it has never been exposed.

I do not know what this means. I am not a scientist. I am a keeper. But I write it down because that is also what keepers do.

### VI. THE SADDLE — Year 142

**Keeper: Maren Colt**
**Station: The Saddle (19,100 ft)**

Mid-summer. Clear.

Nothing. That is the entry. Nothing happened today. No teams. No weather. No wind. The sun is up and the sky is white — not clouded, white, the way it gets at this altitude when the air is too thin to hold color.

I should be grateful for the silence. I am not. The silence at The Saddle is not the absence of sound. It is the presence of something else. I have tried to describe it and I cannot. It is not peace. It is not loneliness. It is the sound of a place that was not made for people, tolerating a person inside it. That is the closest I can come.

I walk the perimeter every morning. I check the cairns. I note the ice conditions. I fill the stove. I read. I eat. I sleep. I am the only person within twenty miles. The station is the highest permanent structure on the continent. I have been here four years. I am the seventh keeper of The Saddle. The first was Tor Vesh, who served twelve years and came down with frostbite damage in both feet and a quiet manner that his family found unbearable. They said he came down from The Saddle a different man. I understand this.

Three teams through this season. All eastbound. The loads were routine — data drives, medical parcels. No diplomatic. No personal. The personal loads are the interesting ones because the personal loads are the ones that make carriers walk faster. Grief is heavier than diplomacy. I have weighed loads and I have carried loads and I know this to be true.

The fissure on the north edge of the plateau has widened again. I measured it: seven centimeters since last summer. I do not know if this is geological or glacial or something else. I report it to every team that passes. They nod. They write it in their notebooks. The information goes down the mountain and into files and nothing happens. This is not a complaint. This is how institutions work. The data exists. The data waits.

I am going to make tea.

### VII. THE CONE — Year 167

**Keeper: Fenn Aul**
**Station: The Cone (17,600 ft)**

Autumn, 3rd day of the month

Dug out again. Four hours with the shovel to clear the station door. The couloir has been active — the usual autumn debris from the peaks. Rockfall on the east side of the moraine. Nothing large enough to threaten the station, but I note it because the pattern is different. The debris field extends further from the couloir mouth than in previous years. The rocks are larger. Something is shifting above — the headwall, perhaps, or the ice that holds the headwall together.

This is the keeper's life: digging out, measuring, recording, digging out again. The carriers come through and they see a clear door and a warm stove and they do not think about the four hours of shoveling that made the door clear. This is correct. They should not think about it. My job is to make the station ready so that their job — which is harder than mine — can be done.

I am not being humble. I crossed for fifteen years. I know which job is harder.

A team came through yesterday — westbound, three carriers. The lead was a man named Volk. Henra Volk. She was tired. I could see it in her gait — the right leg favoring slightly, the weight carried a fraction too far forward. Altitude fatigue, or something older. She did not complain. She drank my coffee and ate two biscuits and asked about the couloir. I told her: clear, but watch the headwall. She nodded. She is the kind of carrier who files information without reacting to it.

They went down in the morning. Clear weather. I watched them descend until they were below the moraine line. The woman moves well. But the right leg. I will note it if it continues.

### VIII. THE STEP — Year 189

**Keeper: Teo Maric**
**Station: The Step (14,800 ft)**

Spring, opening week

Season begins. The route is clear from The Step to The Door — I walked the first three miles myself yesterday to check the path. Scree is stable. The cairns at the first switchback took a hit over the winter — I rebuilt them with stone from the slope. They will hold.

Barometer reads fair. The wind has been from the west for three days, which is unusual for early spring — typically we get the eastern airflow this time of year. I don't know what it means. Possibly nothing. Possibly the season will be strange. They are all strange now.

I have stocked the station: fourteen days of food for four, fuel for the stove, medical supplies (the kit from the guild hospital — Dr. Kella Volk personally packed it, which means everything is in order and labeled in her handwriting, which is the best handwriting at the guild, and I will not hear arguments on this point). The whiskey is from The Broken Compass — old stock, the good barrel. I buy three bottles a season. One for the carriers. One for the shelf. One for me, at the end.

The library survived the winter. No frost damage to the spines. The new shelf I installed last autumn holds. The complete set of The Eastern Review (years 120-160, donated by Director Salaun's predecessor) is intact. The novels are in their place. The poetry — gifted by a carrier named Wren Aoki three years ago, a slim volume she carried in her jacket pocket for an entire crossing and left on my desk with a note that said "You'll argue with this" — is on the bedside shelf. I have read it nine times. She was right. I argue with it every time.

Henra will come through first, I think. She always pushes to open the season. She will arrive looking exactly as she always does — lean, steady, that economy of movement. I will have the coffee ready. I will have the whiskey ready. I will look at her legs and her breathing and her eyes, and I will decide if she is fit to descend, which is my job, which is the job I was given because I spent twenty-three years on this route and the route gave me this station in exchange for my knees and most of my hearing.

I will pour the coffee. She will drink it. We will not need to say much. That is the way it is with people who have crossed together. The mountains said everything that needed to be said. What's left is just the being here.

Spring. The route is open. I am ready.

### IX. THE DOOR — Year 196

**Keeper: Lissa Pok**
**Station: The Door (10,200 ft)**

Autumn, late

They came through today — the last team of the season. Three carriers I know. Henra, Oskar, Wren. I have been at The Door two years and I have seen them pass through more than any other team. They are the route's spine now, the way other teams were the route's spine before them and other teams will be after. The route doesn't care who carries. The route cares that someone carries.

Wren stayed an hour. She sat by the window and looked at the trees. You forget, on the high route, that trees exist. The first trees on the western side are birch — thin, pale, elegant, bending in the wind. After weeks above the tree line, they look impossible. Wren looked at them as if they were music.

Oskar checked his gear on the porch. He always checks his gear. He left me a carving — a mountain goat, this time. Small. The horns are good. He has been getting better at the horns. I put it on the shelf with the others.

Henra drank tea. She did not speak. I did not speak. The stove ticked. Outside, the wind moved through the birch and the light was gold and the season was ending and we both knew it and neither of us said so.

They walked out in the afternoon. I watched them descend through the trees toward Westernhead. Henra's right leg — she is compensating. Has been for a few crossings. She thinks I don't notice. She thinks nobody notices. I notice because watching people walk is what I do, what all keepers do, and I can see the micro-adjustment she makes at heel strike, the way she shifts her weight to the left, the fraction-of-a-second delay that tells me the knee is costing her something she has not yet calculated.

I will say nothing. It is not my place. She is Henra Volk. She has crossed the Divide more times than anyone alive. If she says the knee is fine, the knee is fine. Until it isn't.

### X. THE SADDLE — Year 200

**Keeper: Maren Colt (continued tenure)**
**Station: The Saddle (19,100 ft)**

Summer. Guild bicentennial.

The official celebration is in Meridian. Speeches. Politicians. Director Salaun giving remarks. A new logbook — bound in leather, stamped with the guild seal. It will travel the route in a carrier's pack and each station keeper will inscribe the first page. A nice gesture. I have been asked to write something meaningful. I will write: "Still here."

That is the most meaningful thing a keeper can say. Two hundred years of relay stations and carriers and cairns and storms and deaths and loads that mattered to someone somewhere, and the stations are still here. I am still here. The mountain is still here, though less of it than there was — less ice, more rock, more of the dark stone that Ren Aldic wrote about at The Thumb a hundred years ago and that I see now at The Saddle. The world changes. The route endures. Or it doesn't, and we find a new route, or we don't, and the distance between people becomes stone again.

The fissure has widened to nineteen centimeters since I first measured it. That is nineteen centimeters in eleven years. Not fast by geological standards. Not slow by human standards. Fast enough to notice. Fast enough to report. Fast enough to worry, if I were the kind of person who worried, which I am not, which is why I am the keeper of The Saddle and not the director of the guild.

Salaun will worry. That is her job. Mine is to be here.

I will make tea. I will check the cairns. I will fill the stove. I will read. I will wait for the next team, and when they come, I will wave them through or hold them back, and they will go or they will stay, and the mountain will not care, and I will write it down.

Still here.
