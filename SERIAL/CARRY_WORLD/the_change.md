# THE CHANGE

## Possible Explanations for the Transformation of the Divide

*Compiled from guild records, carrier observations, and academic consultation. Director Salaun's office, Year 200. Classified: Internal.*

---

**Editorial note:** The following document represents seven working hypotheses for the environmental changes observed in the high Divide over the past two decades. No single hypothesis is confirmed. Several are mutually exclusive. All are presented for consideration by the guild's technical advisory board, the carrier council, and the director's office.

The observations are not in dispute:
- The glacier at The Thumb is retreating permanently, not seasonally.
- The Saddle plateau is developing fissures that do not close.
- Weather patterns through the passes have become unpredictable.
- The couloir above The Cone is producing larger, more frequent debris flows.
- Vestite interference is fluctuating in ways that do not match historical patterns.

The question is why. These are the possibilities.

---

## 1. The Climatological Explanation

The simplest hypothesis. The planet is warming. The glacier retreats because the accumulation zone — the altitude at which snowfall exceeds melting — has risen above the highest peaks in the range. What ice remains is ablation ice: old, dense, and diminishing. When it is gone, it will not return.

The Saddle fissures are a consequence of the same process. The plateau is ice-covered. Underneath the ice is rock — permafrost rock, frozen since the last glacial period. As the permafrost degrades, the rock expands and contracts with freeze-thaw cycles that were previously impossible. The rock fractures. The ice above it cracks. The fissures open.

The weather changes are atmospheric. A warmer atmosphere holds more moisture. More moisture means more energy in storm systems. Storms that were once predictable — tracking along established pressure gradients — now behave erratically because the gradients themselves are shifting. The passes that have funneled wind in consistent directions for centuries are now subject to wind from directions that have no historical precedent.

**Implication for the route:** Long-term, the route becomes more dangerous. The high sections — The Thumb, The Saddle — lose their ice, which means they lose their stability. Rock replaces ice. Rock is less predictable. Rock falls. The carrier route, which has been a glacier route at the highest sections, becomes a rock route. Different skills. Different dangers. Different deaths.

**Assessment:** Likely correct. Likely insufficient. Explains the glacier and the weather. Does not fully explain the vestite fluctuations, which have a pattern that climatological models cannot reproduce.

---

## 2. The Geological Explanation

The Divide is an active mountain range. Not volcanically — there is no magmatic activity. Tectonically. The range sits on a convergent boundary that has been building mountains for forty million years. The convergence has not stopped. The range is still being compressed. The stone is still being stressed.

Vestite is piezoelectric. It generates electrical charge under pressure. If the tectonic stress on the range is increasing — and there is some seismic data suggesting an increase in micro-earthquakes along the range's eastern flank — then the vestite deposits are being subjected to greater pressure. Greater pressure means greater charge. Greater charge means more interference. The fluctuations carriers observe may be the electrical signature of a mountain range being squeezed.

The Saddle fissures fit this model. Tectonic compression could fracture the rock at the surface — not dramatically, not in a way a person would feel, but incrementally. Centimeters per year. Stress accumulating in the stone until the stone releases it along fracture lines. The fissures are release valves.

The glacier retreat, in this model, is coincidental — or rather, the warming climate exposes rock that was ice-supported, and the newly exposed rock, freed from the weight of the ice, decompresses. This decompression can itself trigger fracture. The mountain is adjusting to the absence of ice the way a body adjusts to the absence of a splint — by shifting, settling, finding a new equilibrium that may involve cracking.

**Implication for the route:** The route itself may need to shift. If tectonic stress is reconfiguring the high rock, passes that have been stable for two centuries may become unstable. The Notch — already the wind funnel of the route — may be widening, which would change wind patterns. The Rib — already a narrow ridge — may be fracturing, which would compromise the single most exposed section of the route.

**Assessment:** Partially testable. Seismic monitoring stations could be installed at relay stations (with the understanding that vestite interference would degrade the data). The micro-fracture hypothesis is consistent with the couloir debris at The Cone and the Saddle fissures. Does not explain the weather pattern changes.

## 3. The Structural Explanation

This is the hypothesis that Director Salaun finds most troubling, and the one she has discussed least with government officials.

The carrier route is two hundred years old. For two hundred years, people have been walking the same path across the same stone. Two hundred years of boots on the same ridgeline. Two hundred years of cairns being built and rebuilt from the same loose stone. Two hundred years of relay stations anchored to rock faces, of expansion bolts drilled into stone, of diesel stoves vibrating through shelter walls into the mountain itself.

The route is a wound.

Not a metaphor. A physical modification. The stone at The Rib has been worn smooth by two centuries of boots — that is documented, visible, measurable. The path is eighteen inches lower than the surrounding rock because the stone has been eroded by foot traffic. Erosion changes the surface profile. A changed surface profile changes how water flows, how ice forms, how wind moves. The route has altered the micro-topography of the high Divide in ways that may be accelerating.

The Saddle fissures run along the path the carriers walk. Not near it — along it. The fissures follow the route. As if the weight of two hundred years of carriers has stressed the plateau along the line of greatest traffic, and the plateau is cracking along that line.

This hypothesis suggests that the carriers themselves are causing the changes they observe. Not intentionally. Not negligently. In the same way a river carves a canyon — by being present, by flowing, by doing what it does, over time. The route is the river. The carriers are the water. The mountain is being reshaped by the traffic.

**Implication for the route:** Profound. If the route is causing its own degradation, then increasing traffic — which the governments want, which the economies demand — will accelerate the degradation. More carriers, more loads, more boots, more erosion, more fissures. The route eats itself. The solution would be to route-switch: find a new path, move the traffic, let the old path heal. But the old path exists because it is the only path. The alternatives are worse. Or they don't exist.

**Assessment:** Uncomfortable. Fits the fissure pattern along the Saddle. Does not explain the glacier retreat or the weather changes. Salaun's concern: if this hypothesis reaches the engineering consortium, they will use it as ammunition. "The carriers are destroying the mountain. Let us build a tunnel instead." The tunnel would also stress the rock — differently, but no less. Salaun knows this. The consortium does not, or does not care.

## 4. The Vestite Cycle Hypothesis

Vestite has been present in the Divide for millions of years. It has been interfering with electronic instruments for as long as electronic instruments have been brought near it. The interference is piezoelectric — charge generated by pressure. This is established.

But piezoelectric minerals have properties that are not fully understood. Quartz, the most studied piezoelectric mineral, exhibits resonance. It vibrates at precise frequencies under electrical charge. This is why quartz is used in timekeeping. Vestite is not quartz. But vestite may have its own resonant properties — frequencies at which it oscillates when subjected to charge, and the oscillation may amplify the charge, which amplifies the oscillation, in a feedback loop.

In this model, the vestite in the Divide is entering a resonant phase. The charge it generates is not increasing linearly — it is increasing exponentially, because the mineral is finding its resonant frequency and amplifying itself. The fluctuations carriers observe are not random interference. They are oscillations. The mountain is vibrating at a frequency that is growing.

If the resonance is increasing, it could explain everything. The weather changes: strong electromagnetic fields can affect atmospheric ionization, which could alter pressure systems and wind patterns. The fissures: a resonating mineral deposit could transfer vibrational energy to the surrounding rock, causing mechanical fracture. The glacier retreat: vibrational energy generates heat. If the vestite deposits under the glacier are resonating, they are warming the rock from below. The ice melts from the bottom up, invisible until the retreat becomes visible at the surface.

**Implication for the route:** Unknown. If the resonance is natural and cyclical — if vestite goes through resonant phases on geological timescales — then the current phase may pass. The resonance may dampen. The interference may return to historical levels. But if the resonance is self-amplifying — if the feedback loop has no damping mechanism — then the interference will increase until the vestite deposits fracture from the energy, or until the charge reaches a level that makes the high route genuinely impassable. Not because of weather or rockfall, but because the electrical environment itself becomes hostile. Instruments fail. Compasses spin. And at sufficient charge, the air itself ionizes. St. Elmo's fire. Corona discharge. A mountain that glows.

**Assessment:** Speculative. The resonant properties of vestite have never been studied in a laboratory setting. The guild has submitted samples to three universities. The results have been inconclusive — vestite is difficult to work with because its piezoelectric properties are nonlinear and sample-dependent. What is true for one crystal may not be true for another. But the hypothesis fits the data. It fits all the data. And it is the only hypothesis that does.

## 5. The Deep Structure Hypothesis

There is something beneath the Divide that is not rock.

This is not a supernatural claim. It is a geological one. Seismic surveys of the Divide are limited — vestite interference degrades the data — but the surveys that exist show anomalous readings at depth. Reflection profiles that suggest a structure — or a body, or a formation — below the main vestite deposits, at a depth of three to five kilometers. The anomaly is large: forty kilometers long, ten kilometers wide, with a density that does not match any known geological formation in the range.

The anomaly was discovered thirty years ago. It has been largely ignored because the data quality is poor and because the anomaly does not fit any existing geological model. The guild became aware of it five years ago, when a geology student at Eastern University contacted Director Salaun with a thesis proposal. The thesis was never completed. The student took a position with the engineering consortium. Salaun does not believe this is a coincidence.

In this model, the anomaly at depth is the cause of the surface changes. If the anomaly is a large body of concentrated vestite — a single massive deposit, rather than the distributed veins found in surface rock — then its piezoelectric properties would be orders of magnitude stronger than anything observed at the surface. A deep deposit of that size, under tectonic pressure, could generate the charge patterns observed at the surface. It could warm the rock above it. It could fracture the rock above it. It could alter the electromagnetic environment of the entire range.

And if the deep deposit is itself changing — growing, shifting, or being subjected to changing pressure as the overlying ice retreats and the mountain decompresses — then the surface changes are the echo of something happening miles below.

**Implication for the route:** The route is the canary. What carriers observe at the surface is the surface expression of a deep process that the guild cannot monitor, cannot predict, and cannot influence. If the deep process is accelerating, the surface changes will accelerate. The route will become more dangerous. The governments will push harder for a tunnel. A tunnel, drilled into a mountain underlaid by a massive vestite deposit, would be — to use the engineering term — extremely interesting.

The engineering consortium knows about the anomaly. The guild knows they know. Neither side has acknowledged this publicly. Salaun's concern: the consortium's tunnel proposal is not about crossing the Divide. It is about reaching the anomaly.

**Assessment:** Disturbing. The anomaly is real — the seismic data, while poor quality, is consistent across multiple surveys. The connection between the anomaly and the surface changes is speculative but plausible. The political implications are severe. This hypothesis is classified. It is not to be discussed outside the director's office and the carrier council.

## 6. The Threshold Hypothesis

The Divide has been stable for two hundred years — the span of the guild's existence. Before the guild, the Divide was uncrossed and unmonitored. There is no data from before the guild. There is no baseline.

What if the Divide is not stable? What if the Divide has cycles — long cycles, centuries-long or millennia-long, in which the range goes through phases of activity and quiescence? The guild was founded during a quiescent phase. The route was established during a period of relative calm. The cairns, the stations, the culture — all of it was built during a window of stability that the carriers mistook for permanence.

The window is closing.

In this model, the glacier retreat, the fissures, the weather changes, and the vestite fluctuations are all symptoms of the Divide entering an active phase. The mountain range is cyclical. It has been here for forty million years. It has gone through thousands of these phases. The active phase may last decades or centuries. It may involve rockfall on a scale that rewrites the route entirely. It may involve seismic activity — not the micro-tremors that the seismographs occasionally catch, but real earthquakes, the kind that reshape passes and close routes and bury stations.

The carriers are the first to know because they are the only ones who walk the route. Their observations are the only data. And their data says: the mountain is waking up.

**Implication for the route:** Existential. If the Divide is entering an active phase, the route may not survive. Not the current route. Not any route. The relay stations, carved into rock that is beginning to fracture, may become untenable. The passes may close. The glacier may disappear entirely, leaving a rock face that no cairn can make passable. The guild may face the one thing it has never faced in two hundred years: a mountain that cannot be crossed.

**Assessment:** Unverifiable without centuries of additional data, which the guild does not have and the mountain may not provide. But the hypothesis cannot be dismissed. The carriers feel it. Not as data — as texture. The mountain feels different. The snow sits differently. The wind sounds differently. The stone rings differently under their boots. These are not measurements. These are the observations of people who have walked the same ground for years and who can feel, in their feet and their lungs and their bones, that the ground has changed.

Henra Volk said it best, in a carrier council meeting last spring: "The mountain used to ignore us. Now it's paying attention."

---

## 7. The Null Hypothesis

There is no single explanation. The glacier retreat is climatological. The fissures are structural. The weather changes are atmospheric. The vestite fluctuations are geological. The deep anomaly is unrelated. The carrier observations, while accurate, are being aggregated into a pattern that does not exist — the human tendency to find connections between independent events.

The mountain is not waking up. The mountain is not resonating. The route is not eating itself. The world is changing because the world is always changing, and the carriers are noticing now because the changes have reached a magnitude that intersects with their work.

The route has always been dangerous. The weather has always been unpredictable. People have always died. The guild has survived for two hundred years not because the mountain was stable but because the carriers were skilled. Skill, not stability, is the guild's foundation.

In this model, the appropriate response is adaptation. The route shifts. The training evolves. The stations are reinforced. New cairns are built. The carriers do what they have always done: they walk. They adjust. They carry.

**Implication for the route:** None that the carriers don't already know. The mountain is the mountain. The work is the work. The route is the route. Adapt or die.

**Assessment:** Possibly correct. Certainly the healthiest response. But Director Salaun, who has read all the data, who has walked the route 131 times, who has spent every waking hour of the last fifteen years thinking about the guild's survival — Director Salaun does not believe the Null Hypothesis. She believes something is happening. She does not know what. She has tasked the carrier council with continued observation, expanded logging, and the compilation of every anomalous data point from every station log and every carrier notebook for the past twenty years.

The data is being assembled. The pattern, if there is one, will emerge.

Until then, the carriers walk. They watch. They write it down.
