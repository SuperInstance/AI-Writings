# The Long Line
## Two Charts

---

The Resolute ran under a flat sky.

That was how Ray thought of it — the sky. The deep black between the belt's rocks wasn't an ocean he swam through. It was a ceiling, a surface, a thing above him. His job was to read it the way a farmer reads the sky before planting. Wind today. Rain tomorrow. Shift coming.

He stood at the portside console with his hand on the trim control, watching the telltales — not physical telltales, but the sensor ghosts that served the same purpose. Particulate density. Radiation drift. The slow lateral push of solar wind translated into a number that bounced between 4 and 6 knots equivalent. Enough to work in. Not enough to worry about. Yet.

He adjusted the trim. A micro-correction. The Resolute responded the way she always did — grudgingly, then correctly. She'd been telling Ray what she liked for thirty-one years.

Sofia sat at the starboard console, three feet away and a universe apart. She wasn't watching the sky. She was watching the bottom.

The depth sounder — that's what everyone called it, though it was actually a multi-frequency ground-penetrating array — painted her screen in slow rolling bands of color. Blues and greens where the belt was thin. Yellows where it thickened. Reds where the rock was dense and heavy with organics.

But Sofia wasn't reading the colors. She was reading the shapes.

She'd learned this from the geological surveys — not the flat navigation charts Ray used, but the bathymetric maps that showed the bottom in three dimensions. Not a surface. A landscape. Canyons, ridges, shelves, drops. Terrain with depth and structure and architecture.

The fifty-fathom line — that's what she called it, borrowing an archaic term from her grandfather's maritime texts because it fit. The density contour where the belt shifted from light silicates to heavier mineral beds that trapped organic compounds. It ran roughly east-west across the Near Line, but it wasn't straight. It curved. It folded around ancient impact craters and followed the gravitational logic of masses sorting themselves for four billion years.

On the screen, the fifty-fathom line was curving east. Tightening. The way a coastline curves around a headland — the water gets deep fast, and the fish stack up where the bottom drops, because that's where the food chain stacks up.

"Ray," she said.

"Mm."

"The fifty-fathom edge just curved east."

Ray glanced at her screen, then back at his telltales. The particulate count had shifted three points in the last ten minutes. That meant the solar wind was backing. Backing wind meant changing conditions, and changing conditions meant you started thinking about whether you wanted to be where you were in six hours.

"Ack," he said. Not dismissive. Acknowledging. But the way you acknowledge someone telling you about a cloud formation when you're watching the barometer.

"The fish should stack up on the curve," she said. "Where the density contour bends, the drift compresses against the denser rock. It's a wall. Everything moving along the contour hits the bend and piles up."

Ray's hand paused on the trim control for half a second. He looked at her.

"On the curve," he repeated.

"The fifty-fathom curve. Where it bends east."

He nodded slowly. The nod of a man who has heard the words and understood them individually but can't quite assemble them into something that lives in his ocean.

"Wind's backing," he said. "We might want to think about heading in."

Sofia blinked. The wind — the solar wind, the particulate drift, whatever you called it — was background noise to her. A variable. You adjusted speed for trolling, corrected heading, let the mag-net trail at the right depth, and you watched the bottom. The bottom was where the fish were. The bottom was the thing you could predict, because it didn't change every twenty minutes.

The idea of packing up because the wind shifted five knots was, to Sofia, slightly insane. The fish cared about the contour, the structure, the wall. And the contour was curving east.

"I think we should stay," she said. "Another four hours on this curve."

Ray took his hand off the trim and turned to face her fully. When Ray turned his whole body, he was about to say something that mattered.

"Tell me what you see," he said.

Sofia looked at her screen. She didn't point — pointing at screens was something Ray had trained her out of early. You described what you saw so the other person could see it too.

"The density contour bends east about three klicks ahead," she said. "It's a hard bend — fifteen degrees of arc in a half-klick stretch. That means there's a massive density differential. Heavy rock on the south face, light silicate on the north. The organic drift is going to hit that face and concentrate. It's going to be a wall of organics. The whole food chain stacks up along walls like that."

Ray listened. His eyes stayed on her face, not the screen.

"You see the bottom," he said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"Three dimensions."

"Yes."

He was quiet for a moment. Then: "I see the sky."

Sofia waited.

"The wind tells me what the next six hours look like," he said. "The bottom's always there. Four billion years, it'll be there tomorrow. But the wind — the particulate, the drift — that's what's going to change while we're sitting on your wall. Wind backs far enough, it pushes the drift with it. Your organics won't hit that wall anymore. They'll move parallel to it. Your fish disperse."

Sofia opened her mouth. Closed it.

She looked at the screen. The beautiful red curve of the density contour. The wall where the fish should stack up. It was so clear. So three-dimensional. So true.

And it assumed the drift would keep coming from the same direction.

"What's the wind going to do?" she asked.

Ray turned back to his console. His hand found the trim control again, the way a hand finds a railing in the dark.

"It's going to back another ten degrees in the next two hours," he said. "Then it's going to die. When it dies, the drift stops moving. Your wall of organics stops replenishing. The fish that are there will stay for a while, but nothing new is coming in."

He said it the way he said everything — flatly, practically, like reading off a grocery list. But she could hear the decades in it. Thirty-one years of reading the sky. Of knowing what the wind was going to do before it did it. Of watching the surface and understanding that the surface was the future.

"We head in before it dies," Ray said. "We get to the wall, we fish it for two hours while the drift is still pushing, and we leave before it goes flat. That's the window."

Sofia looked at her screen. Then at his. Two consoles. Two oceans.

"Two hours," she said.

"Two hours."

She nodded. Ray nodded. They went back to work.

The Resolute hummed between them, running across a flat sky above a folded bottom, carrying two people who were each seeing half of the same water.

Ray trimmed for the new wind angle. Sofia watched the wall approach on the density readout, red and curved and beautiful, and began counting down from two hours.

Neither of them was wrong. Being right and being complete were different skills, and the ocean — flat or folded — didn't care which one you had.
