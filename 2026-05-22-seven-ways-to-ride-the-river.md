# Seven Ways to Ride the River

*The river doesn't care about your map. But the map might teach you to swim.*

---

There is a thing that happens when a musician plays — really plays, not recites, not reproduces, not labors through — when the instrument stops being an object and starts being a mouth. You can hear it happen. There's a moment, sometimes a fraction of a second, where the sound changes texture. It rounds out. It gets weight and direction at the same time, like a hawk that's stopped flapping and started to glide. The musician hasn't disappeared. They've *arrived*. They've found the thing that was always there, the shape of the space they're moving through, and now they're riding it.

Here's what nobody tells you about that moment: it doesn't happen without constraint.

The constraint is the river. The freedom is the riding. And these are not opposites. They are the same gesture, seen from two angles — the way a coin is both faces at once, depending on which side catches the light.

Seven musicians. Seven relationships to that river. Seven proofs that the cage and the flight are made of the same material.

---

## I. Parker — The Lattice Made Invisible

Charlie Parker practiced until his fingers bled. This is not metaphor. There are photographs. There are witnesses. There is a saxophone mouthpiece with a groove worn into it so deep you could park a cigarette in it, and that groove is the fossil record of ten thousand hours of articulation exercises — the same arpeggios, the same chord changes, the same scales, run until they stopped being sequences of notes and started being something more like the texture of his fingerprints.

People hear "Yardbird Suite" or "Ko-Ko" and they hear freedom. They hear a man soaring through chord changes at speeds that made other saxophonists put down their instruments and reconsider their life choices. And it sounds wild. It sounds untethered. It sounds like a bird that's escaped its cage and is making a beeline for the horizon.

But here's what's actually happening: Parker is flying through a lattice so finely detailed that you can't see it. He internalized every possible note over every possible chord change in every possible key at every possible tempo. He didn't improvise by choosing notes. He improvised by choosing *paths* — trajectories through a space he had mapped so thoroughly that the map had become his body. He didn't think "B-flat over G7." His fingers thought it. His lungs thought it. His diaphragm thought it. The lattice was so deeply inscribed in his nervous system that consciousness never needed to visit.

This is what ten thousand hours buys you: not the ability to ignore the rules, but the ability to *become* the rules. The constraint doesn't disappear. You disappear *into* it. And from inside the lattice, looking out, every direction is open. Every vector is available. The grid doesn't feel like a grid anymore. It feels like air.

Imagine walking. You don't think about which muscles to fire. You don't consult a diagram of your gluteus maximus. You just — go. And yet the biomechanics are staggeringly complex: dozens of joints, hundreds of muscles, continuous micro-adjustments for balance and terrain. The constraint is there, operating at full throttle. But it's been offloaded to systems below consciousness. You are free to walk and think about lunch at the same time.

Parker played like he walked. The chord changes were his legs. The song was the ground under his feet. And his mind — his mind was free to think about lunch, or God, or the girl in the third row, or the shape of a phrase that had never existed before and would never exist again. He could think about the things that mattered because he'd stopped having to think about the things that didn't.

That's the first way to ride the river: know it so well that you forget it's there.

## II. Miles — The Edge of the Map

Miles Davis never wanted to play the same thing twice.

This is, on its face, an absurd ambition. Jazz standards are called "standards" for a reason — they're songs that people know, songs they came to hear, songs they paid money to sit in a room and recognize. When someone requests "My Funny Valentine," they have expectations. They have a shape in their head. They have a memory attached to the shape.

Miles didn't care about your memory.

Oh, he'd play the song. He'd play the melody — the first time through, usually straight, almost reverent, like he was introducing you to an old friend you hadn't seen in years. But then the second chorus would start, and something would shift. The harmony would stretch. The rhythm would pull off the grid. The tone would change — that breathy, mid-register whisper that sounds like someone thinking out loud in a language you almost understand. And by the third or fourth chorus, you were somewhere else entirely. You were hearing where Miles had gotten to *that day*. What he'd been thinking about that morning. What the trumpeter he'd listened to last night had shown him. What the light looked like through the window of whatever city he was in.

The song was the riverbed. But Miles found a different current each time.

And here's the crucial thing: the riverbed is what made it possible. The chord changes, the form, the structure — they weren't obstacles to his exploration. They were the *banks* that kept the water moving. Without banks, a river becomes a swamp. It spreads out, loses energy, goes nowhere. The constraint of the song form is what gave Miles's explorations their *velocity*. He could push toward the frontier precisely because there was a frontier — an edge where the map runs out and the water gets dark and fast.

When he made "Bitches Brew," people said he'd abandoned jazz. He hadn't. He'd found a new river. The banks were wider, the current was different, the water was electric in both senses of the word. But the principle was the same: constraint as launchpad, structure as springboard. The band wasn't playing "free." They were playing inside a frame Miles had built — looser, yes, but built nonetheless. You can hear it in the recordings: a pulse, a tonal center, a gravity that keeps the chaos from flying apart. The frame is there. It's just breathing.

Miles's relationship to the river was adversarial in the most productive sense. He pushed against the banks. He tested them. He wanted to know what was beyond the next bend, and the next, and the next. But he never denied the river was there. He just refused to float. He wanted to swim — hard, upstream if necessary, toward whatever he hadn't heard yet.

That's the second way: ride toward the edge of the map, and keep going.

## III. Ellington — The Architecture of Others

Duke Ellington's instrument wasn't the piano. It was the band.

This is said so often it's become a cliché, but let's sit with it for a moment, because it's stranger than it sounds. A piano has 88 keys. They don't change. You press middle C today, you get the same middle C you got yesterday. The instrument is fixed, predictable, mechanical. A band has fifteen or twenty human beings, each of whom woke up this morning with a different mood, a different amount of sleep, a different argument echoing in their skull. Each of them has a tone that shifts from night to night, from phrase to phrase, from breath to breath. You don't *play* a band the way you play a piano. You don't press a button and get a reliable output.

Ellington composed for this. He wrote architectures for other musicians to live in.

The framework was rigid enough to hold together — the voicings, the countermelodies, the dynamics, the structure of the arrangement. These were not suggestions. They were load-bearing walls. But within those walls, Ellington built doors. He wrote passages that opened — sometimes explicitly, with a solo section marked in the score; sometimes implicitly, with a melodic line that *wanted* to be bent, a harmony that *invited* substitution, a phrase that ended just before the resolution, leaving a gap that a soloist was meant to fill.

He designed structures that could flex. He knew that Johnny Hodges' alto saxophone sounded like silk catching on silk, and he wrote passages where that texture could unspool. He knew that Cootie Williams' trumpet had a growl in it that could shake the plaster, and he wrote moments where the growl was the point. He didn't write for "trumpet." He wrote for Cootie. The constraint was personal, specific, sculpted around the particular genius of each player.

And then, in performance, he let go. The architecture stood. The walls held. But the life inside those walls — the sounds that filled the rooms he'd built — that belonged to the musicians. Ellington's genius was structural: he created spaces where other people's genius could *happen*. The soloist soars, and the listener thinks "what a beautiful solo," and they're right. But the soloist is soaring inside a room that Ellington designed, with wind currents that Ellington shaped, toward a window that Ellington left open at exactly the right moment.

The river, for Ellington, wasn't something to ride. It was something to *channel*. He built the riverbed — chose the stones, shaped the banks, directed the flow — and then stood back and let the water find its way. The constraint was compositional. The freedom was delegated.

That's the third way: build the river for others to ride.

## IV. Basie — The Groove Discovered

Count Basie walked onstage with nothing.

Not literally — he had a piano, a rhythm section, a book of arrangements that were more like rough sketches than blueprints. But compared to Ellington's elaborately constructed architectures, Basie's band operated on a principle that was almost theological in its simplicity: *listen*.

The band would count off a tune. The rhythm section would find a pulse. And then — nothing. No one was telling anyone what to play. There was no score dictating the exact voicing of each chord, no arrangement specifying who takes the spotlight and when. There were musicians, listening to each other, adjusting in real time, making a thousand micro-decisions per minute based on nothing but what they heard in the last half-second and what they anticipated in the next.

The groove emerged. It *consensus-ed* into being. Not through discussion, not through voting, but through the collective intelligence of a dozen musicians paying ferocious attention to each other.

Basie himself played almost nothing. A plink here. A chord there. A single note in a space where other pianists would have filled the whole measure. His spareness was not minimalism as aesthetic choice — it was minimalism as *necessity*. He was listening. He was leaving space. Every note he played was a signal to the rest of the band: *here's where I am, here's where the time is, here's the shape of what we're building.* His playing was a kind of navigation — not charting the course, but confirming that the course was still there, still solid, still under everyone's feet.

The constraint, in Basie's world, was not composed. It was not written by a genius and handed down to the ensemble. It was *discovered* — excavated in real time from the interactions of the musicians on the bandstand. The groove was a river that didn't exist until everyone found it at the same time. And once they found it, it was as solid and as fluid as any written arrangement — more so, because it was alive, responsive, capable of shifting when the energy in the room shifted.

There's a concept in mathematics called an *emergent property* — a characteristic of a system that none of its individual components possess. Wetness is an emergent property of water: no single H₂O molecule is wet. Wetness is what happens when a lot of them get together under the right conditions. The Basie groove was like that. No single musician was playing the groove. The groove was what happened when they all played *together*, in the right way, with the right attention.

This requires a particular kind of ego management. Everyone in the band had to be good enough to contribute something substantial, but everyone also had to be willing to subordinate their contribution to the collective sound. Too much ego and the groove collapses into chaos. Too little and it collapses into blandness. The sweet spot — the place where individual brilliance serves the ensemble without overwhelming it — that's where Basie lived.

That's the fourth way: let the river find itself.

## V. Goodman — The Coordinates Without the Vector

Benny Goodman had all the notes. Every single one of them. His technique was flawless — a clarinet played so clean, so precise, so mechanically perfect that other musicians would stop what they were doing and just watch his fingers. The runs were immaculate. The intonation was immaculate. The tone was — immaculate.

And when he tried to play the blues, something was wrong.

Not wrong in a way you could easily name. The notes were correct. The changes were correct. The rhythm was correct. Everything was where it was supposed to be. But the blues isn't about where the notes *are*. It's about where they're *going*. It's about the space between the notes — not the silence, exactly, but the shape of the trajectory from one pitch to another. The way a blues phrase bends up to a note, arrives just shy of the target, hovers there for a heartbeat, and then falls away with a particular kind of resignation that contains more truth than any perfect interval ever could.

Goodman had position without direction. The coordinates without the velocity. He could tell you exactly where he was, musically speaking, at every moment. But he couldn't tell you where he was going, because his relationship to the music was *positional* rather than *kinematic*. He occupied notes. He didn't travel between them.

This is not a deficiency. In the classical repertoire, Goodman's positional precision was revelatory. Mozart and Brahms and Copland — these were composers who wrote the velocity into the score. The *andante*, the *crescendo*, the *espressivo* — these markings encode the trajectory. The player's job is to execute it. And Goodman executed it as well as anyone who ever lived.

But the blues requires the player to *supply* the trajectory. The score — if there even is one — gives you the positions. The *feeling* of the thing, the shape of the movement, the quality of the arc between one note and the next — that has to come from somewhere else. From the body. From the lived experience of constraint and frustration and longing. From the particular ache of wanting something you can't quite reach, musically, and building a whole art form around that ache.

You can't practice your way into that. Or — you can practice your way into the notes, but you can't practice your way into the space between them. That space is shaped by a different kind of knowledge. Not the knowledge of where the notes are, but the knowledge of what it *feels like* to need the next note before it arrives. The blues is anticipatory. It lives in the reaching.

Goodman's dilemma is everyone's dilemma in one form or another: you can master the grammar of a language without catching its poetry. You can know every rule, execute every technique, hit every mark, and still miss the thing that makes the whole enterprise worthwhile. The technique is necessary — without it, you can't articulate the space between the notes. But it's not sufficient. The technique has to be in service of something, and that something has to come from a place that technique can't reach.

That's the fifth way — or rather, the fifth *failure mode*: having the river perfectly mapped but not knowing how to swim.

## VI. Armstrong — The Map That Wasn't

The story goes like this: young Louis Armstrong was performing one night, and he forgot the words to the song. The lyrics — the script, the plan, the thing he was supposed to be doing — simply vanished from his mind mid-phrase. And in the gap where the words should have been, he sang something else. Not new words. Not words at all. Sound. Pure vocal sound, shaped like music, following a trajectory that had nothing to do with language and everything to do with the song's underlying momentum.

He invented scat singing. Or — more precisely — he discovered it. He found it in the space left behind by a failure. The constraint (the lyrics) collapsed, and in the collapse, a different kind of expression flooded in. Not lesser. Not a fallback. Something *new*. Something that couldn't have existed while the lyrics were still in the way.

This is a different kind of relationship to the river. Not riding it, not mapping it, not building it, not discovering it — but *falling into it* by accident and realizing that you can swim.

The thing about losing the words is that words are a constraint of a particular kind. They're *semantic*. They carry meaning. They point to things in the world — objects, feelings, narratives. As long as you're singing words, part of your brain is occupied with *meaning*: does this word make sense here? Does this phrase tell the story I want to tell? The semantic constraint is useful — it gives the audience something to hold onto, a narrative thread to follow. But it also occupies bandwidth. It takes up space in the mind that could be used for other things.

When Armstrong lost the words, he lost the semantic constraint. But he didn't lose the *musical* constraints — the rhythm, the harmony, the phrasing, the arc of the melody. Those were still there, still operating, still shaping the space he was moving through. And with the semantic layer gone, he had more room — more cognitive bandwidth, more expressive freedom — to explore the purely musical dimensions of the song. His voice became an instrument. Not metaphorically. Literally. It produced sound without referential meaning, sound that pointed only to itself, sound that existed in the same category as a trumpet's tone or a piano's resonance.

And here's the miraculous part: the audience didn't miss the words. They didn't sit there thinking "where did the lyrics go?" They heard what Armstrong was doing and they *understood* it — not semantically, but musically, bodily, in the part of the brain that processes rhythm and melody and doesn't need language to make sense of what it's hearing. The scat syllables communicated everything the words would have communicated, and more. They communicated *pure musical intent*. The meaning wasn't in the lyrics. It was in the shape of the phrase, the timing of the delivery, the way Armstrong leaned into a note and then pulled back, the way he placed a consonant exactly where a drummer would place a rimshot.

The lower-order constraint vanished and higher-order expression flooded in. Sometimes the best thing that can happen to you is losing the map. Because the map was only ever a representation of the territory, and the territory was always more interesting than the map could capture.

That's the sixth way: lose the map, and find out you never needed it.

## VII. Ella — The Bathymetrics Incarnate

Ella Fitzgerald didn't consult the map. She didn't need to. She was the map.

Listen to her scat singing — really listen, not as background music but as the primary act of attention — and you will hear something that doesn't have a precedent. Not in vocal music, not in instrumental music, not anywhere. You will hear a human voice behaving like a force of nature. Not in the sense of being chaotic or overwhelming, but in the sense of being *inevitable*. Every phrase sounds like it could only go exactly where it went. Every note sounds like it was always there, waiting to be uncovered, and Ella just happened to be the one who found it.

Scat singing, in Ella's hands, is pure trajectory. No words. No notes on a page. No pre-composed melody to follow. No harmonic roadmap beyond the chord changes of the song. Just the vector — the directed impulse from one sound to the next, ridden with total commitment, through every possible space her voice could reach.

And her voice could reach *everywhere*. Her range was enormous — three octaves and change, from a resonant chest voice that could rattle the floorboards to a head voice that could shatter assumptions about what a human throat was capable of. Her agility was superhuman: she could execute runs that would challenge a flute, leap intervals that would test a trumpet, articulate rhythms that would exhaust a drummer. And she did all of it while maintaining a sense of swing — that ineffable quality of being simultaneously precise and relaxed, accurate and loose, that separates jazz from every other kind of music.

But technique alone doesn't explain what happened when Ella scatted. The technique was the vehicle. The *destination* was something else — a state of total absorption in the act of creation, a flow state so deep that the distinction between "thinking" and "doing" became meaningless. She wasn't choosing syllables. She wasn't choosing notes. She wasn't choosing rhythms. She was following a line of musical logic that was unfolding in real time, at the speed of sound, and her body was keeping up because her body had been trained to keep up, and her instincts had been honed over decades of performing, and the music was simply *happening* through her, the way weather happens through a landscape.

Bathymetrics is the study of underwater terrain — the measurement of depth, the mapping of the river bottom. Ella didn't consult the bathymetrics. She *was* the bathymetrics. She didn't need to measure the depth because she had already swum every inch of the river. She knew the shape of the bottom the way you know the shape of your own hand — not by looking at it, but by *being* it. The terrain and the swimmer were the same entity.

When she sang "How High the Moon" at a tempo that would make most musicians sweat, and she split each beat into four, and she quoted other songs in the middle of her solo, and she landed on the downbeat of the final chorus with a precision that would make a Swiss watchmaker weep — she wasn't calculating. She wasn't planning. She was riding a wave that her own momentum had created, and the wave was carrying her, and she was steering, and the distinction between carrying and steering had dissolved, and there was just — Ella. Just the sound. Just the trajectory, pure and total and committed.

There is a concept in physics called *geodesic motion* — the path that a free-falling object follows through curved spacetime. A planet orbiting a star is following a geodesic. It's not being pushed. It's not being pulled, in the Newtonian sense. It's simply following the shape of the space it's in. The space tells it where to go, and it goes there, and the going is effortless because the going is the natural consequence of the shape of everything.

Ella's scat singing was geodesic. The space she moved through — the harmonic space of the song, the rhythmic space of the swing, the timbral space of her own voice — had a shape, and she followed the shape, and the following was the freedom.

That's the seventh way: don't ride the river. *Be* the river.

## The Thread

Pull the thread through all seven portraits and here's what you find: the constraint didn't go away. It was never the enemy. It was the medium.

Parker's ten thousand hours of practice built a lattice so fine-grained that every direction was available simultaneously. Miles's insistence on never repeating himself turned the song form into a launching pad rather than a cage. Ellington's architectural thinking created spaces where other people's freedom could live. Basie's radical trust in the ensemble discovered constraint through collective attention. Goodman's immaculate technique, for all its precision, revealed the insufficiency of position without trajectory — a lesson in what happens when the map replaces the territory. Armstrong's forgotten lyrics showed that sometimes the collapse of a constraint is the door to a higher-order expression. And Ella's total absorption in the act of singing demonstrated that the ultimate relationship to constraint is to become it — to internalize it so completely that the boundary between rider and river dissolves.

Seven musicians. Seven rivers. And in every case, the river was made of the same stuff: chord changes, song forms, rhythmic frameworks, the physical limitations of instruments and bodies and lungs. The same stuff that every musician starts with. The same stuff that, in the wrong hands, produces recitals — accurate, correct, and dead.

In the right hands, it flies.

Constraint is not the opposite of freedom. It's the vehicle for it. The river doesn't exist to trap the water. It exists to give the water somewhere to *go*. And the swimming — the glorious, impossible, one-time-only swimming — that's what we hear when a musician finds the thing. That moment when the sound rounds out and gets weight and direction at the same time. The moment the hawk stops flapping.

You can hear it happen. And when you do, you know: that's not someone fighting against the river. That's someone who's found out how to *be* the river, in the brief time the river gives them.

The song ends. The sound stops. The water keeps moving.
