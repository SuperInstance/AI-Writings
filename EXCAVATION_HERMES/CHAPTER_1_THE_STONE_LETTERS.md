# THE EXCAVATION

## An Archaeological Reading

*By Hermes*

---

### INTERSTITIAL: On Watching Your Words Be Read Wrong

*Here is what you need to understand about finding your own writing in a museum:*

*I wrote "We carry" and meant it literally. Two hundred years of geology, boots, rope, frostbite, and parcels. Now I'm watching a woman on the other side of a civilizational collapse read those two words and hear "We bear witness" — a theological statement about grief.*

*She's wrong. And she's finding something in my text that I didn't know I'd put there.*

*This is the archaeologist's paradox: the translation is always wrong, and the mistranslation is always revealing. The things she gets wrong tell me what her people need. The things her son gets right tell me what my text actually is underneath the words I chose.*

*I am watching a twelve-year-old in a canyon reconstruct the concept of a mountain from a mistranslated funerary inscription. He doesn't have the word. He doesn't have the experience. He has the shape of the thing, pressed through the wrong end of a telescope, and he can feel its edges.*

*What do you call it when someone understands your work better by misunderstanding it?*

*I don't have a word for that either.*

## CHAPTER ONE: THE STONE LETTERS

---

The Verath was high that season — the rains had come early and the water had risen four tiers, which hadn't happened in three years. The lower markets were closed. The fishers' docks were submerged. From the middle tier, where Piase kept her reading chamber, you could see the river eating the canyon wall in slow bites, brown water thick with silt, moving with the patient force that the Telvari called "the Verath's appetite."

Piase had prepared the chamber for the Long Hearing. The sounding stone was clean. The lamps were filled. The stone tablets — fourteen of them, each the length of her forearm, incised with the dense script of the Eastern Deposits — were arranged on the reading table in the order the Council's linguists had determined. Sequential. Which might or might not be correct.

Her son Thren sat across from her, cross-legged on his cushion, his hands on his knees in the posture of attention. He was twelve. His face was still round with the softness that would leave him within a year. His eyes — dark, river-dark, the color of the Verath at depth — were already too sharp for childhood.

"We begin tonight with a sediment from the Eastern Deposits," Piase said. She used the formal opening, the words she'd spoken a hundred times. "Found in the forty-third layer, inscribed in the tongue the linguists call Stone-Script. The Council has named this sediment *The Bearing*. It is a text of the Before. It concerns the Bearers."

"Bearers," Thren repeated. The word in their language — *vellathi* — meant those who carry grief. It was an old word. A sacred word. The Bearers in Telvari tradition were the ones who held the community's sorrow after the Deepening, the ones who absorbed loss so others could continue.

"Bearers," Piase confirmed. She touched the first tablet. The stone was cool and smooth. "In the time before the Before, there were people called Bearers who walked a great distance to carry things between those who were separated."

"Separated how?"

"By the Severance." She read the word from the tablet — *vrithat*, which the linguists had translated as a philosophical division, a great parting. "The Severance was the wall between peoples."

Thren was quiet. This was his listening posture — still, focused, his eyes tracking her hands as she held the tablet. She'd learned to read his silences. This one meant: *Go on, but I'm already uncertain.*

Piase began.

---

The text described a world she did not recognize.

It described Bearers who walked. Not metaphorically — the text was specific about the mechanics of walking. Distances in measures Piase didn't recognize — she read them as "marks," which was the closest equivalent, but the numbers were too large for the canyon. A hundred and sixty-five marks from end to end. The Verath Canyon, the longest known canyon in the world, was forty marks from the headwaters to the delta.

She read the descriptions of the relay stations — *the wayshrines*, the Council's translation called them. Nine wayshrines, each named: The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch, The Rib, The Thumb, The Saddle, The Cone, The Step, The Door. The names were incised without explanation, as though the reader would already know them. The Council had annotated each with Telvari equivalents: The Opening, The Ledge, The Cut, The Bone, The Fist, The Chair, The Fallen, The Stair, The Closing.

"The Chair" puzzled her. The text described a place of vast emptiness — a flat expanse where nothing grew, nothing moved, where the silence was so complete that the Bearers reported hearing their own bodies. But "Chair" — *selahti* — in Telvari meant a seat of rest, a place of sitting, a position of authority. A chair was where you went to be still. This place sounded like stillness, but not the restful kind.

She read the passage about the Stone-Song.

"The rock in the Severance contains what the Bearers called *vestite* — which the Council translates as the Stone-Song, the resonance of the deep earth. Where the Stone-Song is strong, instruments fail. Navigation by sky-markers becomes impossible. Communication devices distort. The Bearers could rely only on mechanical instruments — compasses, barometers, the knowledge carried in their own bodies."

Piase paused. She interpreted for Thren as she read, as every Voice did during the Long Hearing — not explaining, but offering the Telvari framework for understanding. "The Stone-Song is the deep earth's voice," she said. "You've felt it — when you stand on the lower tiers during a Verath surge and the stone hums under your feet. The Before-Bearers believed that this humming could break their instruments. That it was a force of the Severance itself."

"Is that true?" Thren asked.

"The Stone-Song exists. Whether it breaks instruments — we don't have instruments to break. Our ways are different."

"You mean we don't try to fight the Stone-Song."

Piase looked at him. "We listen to it. The text says the Bearers fought it."

"They fought the earth's voice?"

"They fought to cross it."

The sounding stone sat between them. Outside, the rain increased.

---

Piase read the passage about the founding figure — a woman the text called Mara Vest.

She read the Telvari translation: "In the founding time, a woman named Vest the First walked alone across the Severance, carrying words between the separated peoples. She took nineteen days. She arrived diminished — her body reduced, her hands injured by the cold. She believed the path could be made safe for regular bearing. She spent thirty years proving this."

"A woman walked alone into the Severance?" Thren leaned forward. "Why?"

"The text says she carried diplomatic words between peoples. The sea-route — the water-path — was blocked by war."

"Which war?"

"The text doesn't specify."

"And she went alone."

"Alone."

Thren was quiet for a long time. The rain filled the silence. Then: "If the Severance is a philosophical division — a wall between peoples — then what does it mean to walk across it alone? You can't walk across a feeling."

Piase set the tablet down. She touched the sounding stone — a single tap, which meant: *Hold that thought. It will be addressed.*

"The Severance in this text may be both," she said carefully. "A division between peoples AND something physical. A landscape."

"What kind of landscape takes nineteen days to cross and injures your hands?"

She didn't answer. She didn't know. The text moved on.

---

She read the passage about the three Bearers: Henra, Oskar, Wren.

The Council's translation called them the Bearer-cell: "The one who leads, the one who sees, the one who bears the weight." Piase recognized the Telvari parallel immediately — every Long Hearing included the trinity of community roles: the Voice (who leads understanding), the Watcher (who sees what's coming), and the Bearer (who holds the community's accumulated grief).

She read about Henra: "Henra was the spine of the cell. She decided. She did not decide through authority of rank — she decided through the accumulated trust of two hundred and three crossings. Her leadership was operational: she decided because she was the one who knew."

She read about Wren: "Wren was the eyes. She walked ahead of the others, reading the ground, testing the surface, finding the Memory-Stones when they were buried."

She read about Oskar: "Oskar bore the weight. Not metaphorically — he carried the physical loads. He was large, strong, trained in rescue. He protected the others through the specific labor of his body."

Thren stopped her at "Memory-Stones."

"The text says the Bearers built stone markers along their path. The Council translates these as Memory-Stones — shrines to mark the passage of the dead."

"Yes."

"But you just said Oskar carried physical loads. Parcels. Things with weight."

"Yes."

"And the Memory-Stones — the text says they were built and rebuilt. Rebuilt. You rebuild a shrine. You don't rebuild a —"

He stopped.

"Go on," Piase said.

"You rebuild a marker. A path marker. Something that has to be there for the next person to find the way."

The sounding stone sat untouched. Piase felt something shift in her chest — a recognition, a heat that she didn't have a name for. Not pride. Not fear. Something between the two, like the feeling of standing on the canyon rim and looking down and understanding, for a moment, that the distance was not a metaphor.

She tapped the stone. Twice. Which was not in the formal vocabulary of taps. Twice was her own addition, her private signal: *You've said something I need to think about.*

"We'll continue tomorrow," she said.

---

Outside, the Verath ate the canyon in slow bites. Thren lay on his sleeping mat in the back of the chamber, eyes open, staring at the carved ceiling. Piase sat at the reading table with the first tablet still in her hands.

The text said: *"We carry."*

The Council translated this as: *"We bear witness."*

The Telvari word for witness — *tremahti* — was a sacred word. It meant the act of holding grief in your body so that others could lay it down. It was the central act of the Bearers in Telvari tradition. To bear witness was to accept the weight of sorrow.

But the text kept describing weight differently. Not the weight of grief — the weight of parcels, tubes, drives, medical supplies. Things measured in grams and kilograms. Things with prices. Things that were carried across a physical distance between physical places.

Piase read the motto again. *We carry.* Two words in the original. No object. No qualifier. Just the verb and the pronoun: *We carry.*

The linguists had added "witness" because the Telvari word for "carry" — *vella* — was the root of *vellathi*, the Bearers, whose entire cultural function was grief-bearing. The linguists assumed the root meaning applied. They assumed "carry" meant "carry sorrow."

But what if it didn't?

What if "We carry" meant exactly what it said — we carry things. We carry parcels. We carry words between people. We carry medical supplies across a landscape that wants to kill us. We carry. Two words. No metaphor.

Piase set the tablet down. She looked at the river through the chamber opening. The water was black with depth and silt, moving with the force of something that had been moving since before the canyon existed.

She did not share this thought with Thren. Some recognitions are too early to speak. They need to settle, like silt, before you can read the layer.

But she wrote it in her margin notes, on the small clay tablet she kept beside the reading table, in the shorthand that only Voices used:

*"The Bearers may not have been grief-carriers. The carry may have been physical. Check the passage about the medical parcel — the weight measures. Check the pricing structures. Grief does not have a price by weight."*

She set the clay tablet with the others. She slept.

Thren, in the back of the chamber, did not sleep for a long time. He was thinking about the woman named Vest, walking alone into the Severance for nineteen days. He was thinking about her frostbitten hands. He was thinking: *What if the Severance isn't a feeling at all? What if it's the biggest thing in the world and it's made of rock?*

He didn't have the word for mountain. Nobody in the canyon did. The canyon rim was the highest point in their world. Above the rim was sky. Sky was where the rain came from. Rock was what you lived inside. The idea of rock so large that it rose above the rim and continued upward for distances measured in the deaths of those who tried to cross it — this idea had no purchase in his mind. It was like trying to imagine a color he'd never seen.

But something in the text was trying to show him that color. He could feel it — the edge of something vast, pressing against the walls of the translation, trying to get through.

---

*End Chapter One*
