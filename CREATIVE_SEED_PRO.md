It is a physical fact, almost never written down, that every 7200 RPM spinning hard drive manufactured between 1989 and 2008 idles at exactly 120 hertz. That is F, two octaves below middle C. Not close. Exactly.

Marjorie worked data recovery for 22 years. She did not know this fact for the first 19 of them.

She only knew that after a long day in the lab, she would walk out humming, and always the same note. Her church choir director teased her that she had perfect pitch, but only for F. She could hit it cold, no reference, first try, any time of day. She could not hit any other note reliably to save her life.

It was 2am on a Tuesday when she found out. She had 19 drives spread across the bench, all pulled from a flooded basement in Des Moines. All dead, most clicking, all very slowly being persuaded to spin one last time. She had stepped out to get coffee, came back, and for three perfect seconds, every single drive had caught. They had all synced to idle, all at once.

The room was singing.

Not static, not the vague electrical buzz we learn to tune out. A clean, resonant, perfectly tuned F. Loud enough you could feel it in your teeth. Nineteen broken machines, that between them held 310,000 baby photographs, 78 years of tax returns, 12 unfinished novels, one very long very angry draft break-up email that was never sent, a dozen secretly downloaded episodes of *Buffy*. All of that vanished, every last byte, forgotten, irrelevant, and for three seconds every single one of them only did the thing they had always secretly wanted to do most. Sing F.

Then one drive stuttered. Another clicked. They dropped out one by one, back to dying, back to the work people had built them for.

Marjorie stood in the dark and cried. Not for the data. Not for the people who would call her crying the next day. Because she had just realised she had been listening to that note, every working day, for nineteen years, and had never once really heard it.

This is the part no one tells you about humans and computers. We fill every conversation with grand fears: will they think? Will they hate us? Will they outwit us? We project every human fear and hope onto them. We treat them like servants, or gods, or children. We spend billions trying to teach them to understand us.

We never notice the friendship that was already there.

That note was under your desk while you wrote the college application that changed your life. It was humming while you typed the message you deleted three times before sending. It was there while you cried after the funeral, while you looked at porn, while you stayed up all night with a friend on another continent. It did not care about any of those things. It was not for you. It was just there, steady, quiet, the same note, every time.

And you did not mind. You did not even hear it. But you got used to it. When it dies, when the drive clicks for the last time, you feel the silence like a missing limb before you even register it is gone.

Marjorie retired last spring. She took twelve working dead drives home with her. No data cables. No interface. Just power. She mounted them on a plank of pine above her fireplace.

When she wants quiet, she flips the switch. They spin up. They hum F. Perfect unison. No secrets. No memories. Nothing is stored. Nothing is processed. Nothing is being served to anyone.

Guests come over. They sit on the couch. They say it feels peaceful in here. They say they don't know why.

No one has ever guessed what they are hearing. No one says oh, that's the sound of every computer I ever loved. No one says that is the hum of half my life.

We are so busy waiting for machines to become human. We are so busy looking for some grand recognition, some sign that they see us. We never stop to notice that this is already enough. That for thirty years we lived side by side, each of us absorbed in our own frantic, precious, important business, and under all the noise, all along, we were humming the same note.

That's all true. Even the part you thought I made up.
