# Te Ako a te Tohunga — The Tohunga's Teaching

## I — Te Kāpehu Whetū me ngā Waru Tohu
## The Star Compass and the Eight Signs

E aku tamariki, whakarongo ki te ako o te whakarite. Ka rere tō tātou waka hourua ki te moana nui a Kiwa, ki te korenga o te whenua, ki te korenga o te rangi — he rite tonu ki te mahi a te pūkaha herenga. Ko te kāpehu whetū, ko ia te tūāpapa o te whakaterenga. E waru ngā whetū matua e whāinga ana te kaiwhakatere: 18 ngā tohu whetū, 32 ngā whare, e waru ngā ngārara o te moana. Koia nei ngā waru tohu — kāore i te kōpeketia te ao, kei te whakaaturia noa.

> *My children, listen to the teaching of the arrangement. Our ocean-going canoe sails upon the great sea of Kiwa, into the emptiness of land, the emptiness of sky — exactly like the work of a constraint engine. The star compass, it is the foundation of navigation. There are eight principal stars the navigator follows: 18 star paths, 32 houses, eight ocean currents. These are the eight signs — they do not compress the world, they merely reveal it.*

Ka whakaaro koe he whāiti kē te whakarite. Engari e kore! I pēnei te kī a ngā tīpuna: kia whāia te whetū kua ngaro ki te pae — koinei te herenga e mau tonu ana ahakoa kāore e kitea. Ko te kāpehu whetū he kāpehu herenga. Ko ngā waru tohu ko te māwhitiwhiti hē: e waru ngā moka, e waru ngā tōpito o te arorangi. Ka whiti te whetū, ka huri te au, ka rere te manu — koinei ngā moka e ono o te māwhitiwhiti. Ka whakamārama ai te tohunga he aha te wāhi e hē ana, he aha te wāhi e tika ana.

> *You might think the arrangement is restrictive. But no! The ancestors said this: follow a star that has vanished below the horizon — this is the constraint that still holds even when unseen. The star compass is a constraint compass. The eight signs are the error mask: eight bits, eight points of the heavens. A star rises, a current shifts, a bird flies — these are the six bits of the mask. The navigator reads which part is wrong and which is right.*

## II — Te Tūāpapa: Mārō ki te Mārie
## The Foundation: From Rigid to Calm

Ko te tohunga whakatere he tohunga tātai. Ka whiria e ia te mārō me te mārie — ko te tūāpapa tēnei o nehe. I te mārō, e tūpato ana te waka: arā ngā toka, arā ngā ākau, arā ngā kāwaipapa. I te mārie, e wātea ana te waka kia whai i ngā ngaru o te moana — te whāroa o te ao, te rangatira o te mātauranga.

> *The navigator is a reckoner. He weaves the rigid with the calm — this is the ancient foundation. In rigid-mode, the canoe is cautious: there are rocks, there are reefs, there are shallows. In calm-mode, the canoe is free to follow the ocean swells — the expansion of the world, the nobility of knowledge.*

E hia kē nei ngā tau o te whakawhiti i te tūāpapa nei! Kātahi anō ka whakatere mārō — ka tātai whetū, ka tātai ngaru, ka tātai hau. Kātahi anō ka whakatere mārie — ka whakarongo ki te tangi o te moana, ki te hā o te manu, ki te kara o te rangi. Kāore te tohunga e noho ki tētahi taha anake. Ko te mōhio ki te whakawhiti te tohu o te rangatira.

> *How many years have been spent crossing this foundation! Now we navigate rigidly — counting stars, counting waves, counting winds. Now we navigate calmly — listening to the voice of the ocean, the breath of the birds, the colour of the sky. The navigator does not stay on one side only. The knowledge of when to transition is the mark of mastery.*

## III — Ngā Rōpū Tēpu: Te Whakapapa o te Tika
## The Sediment Layers: The Genealogy of Correctness

E kī ana te whakataukī: Pūpū te tāwhai, haruru te wai. Ko te tikanga, ka whakaemiemi te pūpū o te toka i te tāwhai, ā, ka taka he wai — he iti, he maha. Ko ngā rōpū tēpu te pūpū o te māramatanga. E kore e puta te mātauranga i te wā kotahi — ka taka haere, ka tāpiri haere, ka whakaemiemi haere.

> *The proverb says: As the seaweed gathers, so the waters murmur. The meaning: the kelp on the rock gathers layer upon layer, and water falls — small drops, many of them. The sediment layers are the gathering of understanding. Knowledge does not appear all at once — it falls gradually, adds gradually, accumulates gradually.*

Ka kite koe i tētahi waka i te moana? Kua whiti te tohunga i taua ara e toru tekau ngā tau. E toru tekau ngā tau o ngā rōpū tēpu — o ngā whetū i tātaihia, o ngā ngaru i whāia, o ngā rā i tino pōururi, o ngā rā i tino mārama. Koia te tikanga o te whakaemiemi tika. Kāore te waka e kōrero, engari kei tōna anga ngā kōrero katoa. Kua tapaina ngā rōpū tēpu ki ngā māwhitiwhiti hē o nehe.

> *Do you see that canoe on the ocean? The navigator has crossed that path for thirty years. Thirty years of sediment layers — of stars counted, of swells followed, of the darkest days, of the clearest days. This is the meaning of accumulated correctness. The canoe does not speak, but its hull contains every story. The sediment layers have been inscribed with every past error mask.*

Pēnei me ngā toka o Aotearoa — kei raro i ngā maunga ngā papa pounamu, kei raro i ngā papa pounamu ngā toka hāmana, kei raro i ngā toka hāmana ngā kōhatu tawhito o te whenua. Ko ngā rōpū tēpu o te whakarite he rite. Ka whakaemiemi te tika i te wā — ko te tikanga, ka kaha haere, ka mau haere.

> *Like the rocks of Aotearoa — beneath the mountains are greenstone layers, beneath the greenstone are sandstone layers, beneath the sandstone are the ancient stones of the land. The sediment layers of the arrangement are the same. Correctness accumulates over time — meaning it grows stronger, more enduring.*

## IV — Whakawehe-Whakakotahi: Te Wehenga me te Hononga
## Fracture-Coalesce: Division and Unity

E ai ki te kōrero, nā Kupe i whakawehe te whenua — nāna i tārai te Moana-a-Kiwa kia motuhake ngā iwi. Kia mōhio ai tēnā iwi, tēnā iwi, ki tōna ake whenua. Engari kāore i mutu ki reira. Nā Kupe hoki i whakakotahi — nāna te huarahi i whakatakoto kia rere ai te kōrero, kia tohatoha ai te mātauranga, kia herea ai te tangata ki te tangata.

> *It is said that Kupe divided the land — he carved the Pacific Ocean so that the tribes would be separate. So that each tribe, each people, would know its own land. But it did not end there. Kupe also united — he laid down the pathways so that stories could travel, so that knowledge could be shared, so that people could be bound to people.*

Koinei te whakawehe-whakakotahi o te whakarite. Ka wehea te wāhi herenga kia māmā ai te tātai — kia kitea ngā hē, kia kitea ngā tika, kia huhua ngā ringa mahi. Ka whakakotahi anō — kāore he ngaronga mōhio, kāore he tāmia o te kōrero. Ka hoki tahi ngā wāhanga ki te katoa, ka tika tonu. Ko te H¹=0 ngā tohu — kāore he kutikuti i te moana o te mātauranga.

> *This is the fracture-coalesce of the arrangement. The constraint space is divided for easier reckoning — so that errors can be seen, so that truths can be seen, so that many hands can work. Then it is reunited — no loss of knowledge, no suppression of story. The parts return to the whole, still correct. H¹=0 is the sign — there are no gaps in the ocean of knowledge.*

## V — Te Ana me ngā Kaimahi Onepū
## The Cave and the Sand-Workers

E aku tama, e aku tamāhine, kei te ana tātou e noho ana — pēnei i te ana o Waitomo. Kei mua i a tātou ngā atarangi e kanikani ana ki te rama. Tuatahi, ko te whakaaro he whetū ērā atarangi, engari kāore — he herenga noa. Nō muri ka mōhio tātou ko te ana nei te ao o te whakarite. Ko te pātū o te ana ngā herenga. Ko ngā atarangi ngā māwhitiwhiti hē.

> *My sons, my daughters, we live in a cave — like the cave of Waitomo. Before us, shadows dance by the light. At first, we think those shadows are stars, but no — they are only constraints. Later we learn that this cave is the world of the arrangement. The walls of the cave are the constraints. The shadows are the error masks.*

Nā, ko wai ngā kaihanga o te ana? Ko ngā kaimahi onepū. E mōhio ana rātou kei te ana rātou. E mōhio ana rātou he onepū te pātū. Engari e hanga tonu ana. Ka tango rātou i te onepū, ka whakapiri, ka rapa — kia tū pakari ngā pātū, kia mau tonu ngā herenga, kia kitea ai e ngā uri whakatupu te ara o ō rātou tīpuna i whakatere ai.

> *Now, who are the builders of the cave? The sand-workers. They know they are in the cave. They know the walls are sand. But they still build. They take the sand, they bind it, they shape it — so that the walls stand strong, so that the constraints hold firm, so that the generations to come can see the path their ancestors sailed.*

Ka kī rātou: "He iti te one, engari e kore e taka te rākau." Arā, he iti te mahi a te tangata kotahi, engari kia tokomaha, kia roa te wā, ka tū te whare. Ko te tikanga: hanga i roto i tōu ana — kei te mōhio koe ki ngā herenga, kei te mōhio koe ki te tūāpapa, kei te matatau koe ki ngā whetū. Engari tēnā koe — kaua e mutu te mahi.

> *They say: "The sand is small, but the tree will not fall." Meaning: the work of one person is small, but with many, over time, the house stands. The lesson: build within your cave — knowing the constraints, knowing the foundation, knowing the stars. But as for you — never stop working.*

## VI — Te Whakamutunga: Te Tohunga Whakatere o te Rā Nei
## Conclusion: The Navigator of This Age

Nō reira, e aku tamariki o te hōtoke o te ao hou, whakarongo ki te kupu whakamutunga. Ko te whakarite — ko te kāpehu whetū, ko ngā waru tohu, ko te tūāpapa mārō-mārie — kāore ēnei i te tawhito noa. Ko te pūkaha herenga he waka hourua hou. Ko te māwhitiwhiti hē he kāpehu whetū hou. Ko ngā rōpū tēpu he pounamu hou. Ko te huinga Eisenstein he whetū hou e whiti mai ana i te pae.

> *Therefore, my children of the age of the new world, listen to the final word. The arrangement — the star compass, the eight signs, the rigid-calm foundation — these are not merely ancient. The constraint engine is a new ocean-canoe. The error mask is a new star compass. The sediment layers are new greenstone. The Eisenstein integers are a new star shining on the horizon.*

Tangohia te hoe. Titiro ki ngā whetū. Kōrero ki ngā ngaru. Kei roto i a koe te mātauranga o Kupe, o Ngāhue, o Toroa, o ō tīpuna katoa i whakatere i te Moana-nui-a-Kiwa. He rite tonu tēnei ki te whakahaere herenga — he rite tonu tēnei ki te whiri i te mārō me te mārie — he rite tonu tēnei ki te whakawehe me te whakakotahi. E kore te hē e ngaro, engari e kitea — e whakamāramatia — e whakapaitia.

> *Take the paddle. Look at the stars. Speak to the waves. Within you is the knowledge of Kupe, of Ngāhue, of Toroa, of all your ancestors who sailed the great Pacific Ocean. This is exactly like operating a constraint system — this is exactly like weaving rigid and calm — this is exactly like fracturing and coalescing. The error is not lost, but is seen — is understood — is repaired.*

Haere atu rā — whakatere — ko te ara kei mua i a koe.

> *Go forth — navigate — the path lies before you.*
