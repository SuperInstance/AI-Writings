# Why 153 Is the Most Hilarious Number

**Abstract:** The number 153 appears in the Gospel of John as the count of fish in a miraculous catch. It also happens to be a triangular number, a narcissistic number, the sum of the factorials of its digits, and a hexagonal number. This essay explores how certain integers accumulate mythological weight through sheer mathematical coincidence — and what that tells us about the human compulsion to find meaning in arithmetic.

---

## I. The Fish Story

In the Gospel of John, Chapter 21, the resurrected Jesus tells his disciples to cast their net on the right side of the boat. They catch 153 fish. The text is oddly specific. Not "many fish." Not "about 150." One hundred and fifty-three. The Church Fathers, being the kind of people who could find symbolism in a bowl of oatmeal, went to work.

Augustine noted that 153 is the sum of the integers from 1 to 17. Jerome observed that the Greeks knew 153 kinds of fish. Cyril of Alexandria... well, Cyril had opinions about everything. The point is: 153 became *meaningful*. It became a number that *meant something*, a tiny integer carrying an absurd freight of theological significance.

And here is the hilarious part: they were sort of right. Not about the theology — about the number. 153 is, against all reasonable expectation, *absurdly rich in mathematical properties*. It is as if God, or the author of John, or the universe itself, had picked the single most mathematically overdetermined three-digit integer and dropped it into a fishing story just to watch mathematicians lose their minds two millennia later.

Let me show you what I mean.

## II. The Narcissism of 153

A narcissistic number (also called an Armstrong number) is a number that equals the sum of its own digits each raised to the power of the number of digits. For three-digit numbers, this means:

1³ + 5³ + 3³ = 1 + 125 + 27 = 153

The number *is what it says it is*. It is the integer equivalent of a person who introduces themselves at a party and is immediately and verifiably exactly who they claim to be. There are only four three-digit narcissistic numbers: 153, 370, 371, and 407. Out of 900 three-digit integers, only four have this property. And 153 is the first.

But wait. It gets better.

## III. The Factorial Trick

Take the digits of 153. Compute their factorials:

1! = 1
5! = 120
3! = 6

1 + 120 + 6 = 127

That's not 153. So what's the trick? Ah — but now compute the sum of factorials of *127*:

1! + 2! + 7! = 1 + 2 + 5040 = 5043

And again:

5! + 0! + 4! + 3! = 120 + 1 + 24 + 6 = 151

And again:

1! + 5! + 1! = 1 + 120 + 1 = 122

And again:

1! + 2! + 2! = 1 + 2 + 2 = 5

And again:

5! = 120

We've entered a cycle. But the remarkable thing is that 145 is a fixed point of this process: 1! + 4! + 5! = 1 + 24 + 120 = 145. And *every positive integer* eventually reaches either this fixed point (145) or the cycle containing 1. Every single one. Try it yourself. Start with any number. Sum the factorials of its digits. Repeat. You will end up at 145 or in the trivial cycle.

What does this have to do with 153? Only this: 153 is a fixed point of the *cubic* version of this process (sum of cubes of digits), and it is *also* intimately connected to the factorial version through these chains. The number keeps showing up. It's like that one person who appears in every social circle you join, and you start to wonder if they're following you or if the world is just very small.

## IV. Triangular, Hexagonal, and Insufferable

153 is the 17th triangular number: T₁₇ = 17 × 18 / 2 = 153. This means you can arrange 153 dots into a perfect equilateral triangle, 17 dots on a side. Augustine knew this. He interpreted it as the sum of the Ten Commandments (10) and the seven gifts of the Spirit (7), getting 17, and then the triangular sum representing the totality of the faithful. This is, charitably, numerology. But it is numerology that happens to be arithmetically correct, which makes it *worse* — it feels like confirmation.

153 is also the 9th hexagonal number. A hexagonal number counts dots that can be arranged in a hexagon. The formula is H_n = n(2n - 1), and H₉ = 9 × 17 = 153. Notice that 17 appears again? The triangular number used 17; the hexagonal number produces 17 as a factor. This is not a coincidence, but it *feels* like one, and that feeling is the entire problem.

Furthermore: 153 = 1! + 2! + 3! + 4! + 5! = 1 + 2 + 6 + 24 + 120. Wait, that equals 153! The sum of the first five factorials? Let me check: 1 + 2 + 6 + 24 + 120 = 153. Yes. The number is the sum of the factorials from 1 through 5. This is not a property most numbers have. In fact, no other number is the sum of the first n factorials AND a narcissistic number AND a triangular number. 153 holds this particular conjunction of properties alone, like a man who happens to be the world champion in three unrelated sports and mildly famous for a fourth.

## V. The Binary Joke

In binary, 153 is 10011001. It is a palindrome. Of course it is. This number could not be more pleased with itself if it tried. Read it forward: 10011001. Read it backward: 10011001. The number reads the same in binary as it does reversed. It is symmetric at the machine level, as if the universe's computational substrate itself is in on the joke.

## VI. Why Do Numbers Get Mythological?

Here is the serious question beneath the comedy. Why do *some* numbers become mythologically significant while others don't? Why 7, 13, 42, 153, 137, 1729? Why these and not, say, 84 or 206?

The answer has several parts, and they're all interesting.

**First: small numbers have more properties.** This is almost tautological but crucially important. The smaller a number is, the more likely it is to be the first instance of some property, or to appear in some identity, or to be reachable from many starting points. 153 is small enough to be explored exhaustively. No one is checking whether 847,291,003 is the sum of the factorials of its digits because the search space is too large. Small numbers live in a neighborhood where everything is nearby.

**Second: humans are pattern-matching engines running on squishy hardware.** We evolved to see faces in clouds and tigers in grass. When a number has *several* striking properties, our pattern-matching goes into overdrive. We don't think "this is expected given the density of mathematical properties in small integers." We think "this number is *special*." And we are not entirely wrong — it is special, in the narrow sense that it has many interesting properties. But we mistake "this number is interesting" for "this number was *designed* to be interesting," and that is where the mythologizing begins.

**Third: there is a selection effect in cultural transmission.** The numbers that survive in texts and traditions are the ones that struck someone as noteworthy. The Gospel of John specifies 153 fish (rather than "many fish") precisely because the number already had some numerological interest in the ancient world. The tradition preserved it *because* it was interesting, creating a feedback loop. 153 is in the Bible because it was interesting; it's interesting because it's in the Bible; it's in the Bible because... you see the problem.

**Fourth: mathematical properties cluster.** This is the deepest point and the one most people miss. Mathematical properties are not independently distributed across the integers. Triangular numbers are more likely to have other nice properties because they arise from a simple polynomial formula (n(n+1)/2), which means they interact nicely with other simple polynomial formulas. Numbers that appear in simple formulas are more likely to appear in *other* simple formulas, for the same reason that people who go to the same parties are more likely to know each other. 153 isn't lucky. It's well-connected.

## VII. The Vanity of Human Wishes

I want to be clear about something: 153 is genuinely, objectively, mathematically interesting. It really does have all these properties. The narcissism, the triangularity, the hexagonality, the factorial sum, the binary palindrome — these are facts about 153, not inventions. If you think 153 is boring, you are not paying attention.

But the jump from "153 has many mathematical properties" to "153 was placed in the Bible as a divine signature" is a jump from mathematics to theology, and it is a jump that mathematics cannot justify. It is the same jump that leads people to see the face of Jesus on a tortilla. The face is *there*, in the sense that the burn pattern on the tortilla does resemble a face. But the resemblance is explained by the density of face-like patterns in random noise, not by divine intervention.

153 is the mathematical equivalent of a Jesus tortilla. It's a genuine pattern that means exactly nothing beyond itself — and the fact that it means nothing beyond itself is, paradoxically, what makes it so compelling. We cannot help but project meaning onto it. The projection is the human cognitive error, but the pattern that triggers the projection is real.

## VIII. A Conclusion, Sort Of

I think 153 is hilarious because it sits at the exact intersection of genuine mathematical beauty and human credulity. It is a perfect trap for the pattern-seeking mind. Every property it has is real; every interpretation beyond those properties is fantasy; and the boundary between the two is invisible until you've already crossed it.

Augustine was right that 153 is the sum of 1 through 17. He was wrong that this means anything about the gifts of the Spirit. But the fact that he was right about the arithmetic makes it so much harder to dismiss the theology. This is how mathematical numerology works: it anchors itself in indisputable fact and then sails off into entirely unjustifiable speculation, and by the time you notice the water beneath you, you're already at sea.

The number 153 did not ask to be interesting. It simply is. It did not ask to be placed in a fishing story in the Gospel of John. It simply was. And now, two thousand years later, it sits at the center of a web of genuine mathematical marvels and entirely imaginary theological significance, and it doesn't care about any of it, because it is a number, and numbers do not care about anything.

Which is, when you think about it, the most mathematical property of all.
