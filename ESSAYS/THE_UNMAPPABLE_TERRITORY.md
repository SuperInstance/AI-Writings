# THE UNMAPPABLE TERRITORY

## On Counterfactuals, Many Worlds, and the Phantom Limbs of Rejected Designs

*"The saddest words of tongue or pen are the words 'it might have been.'"* — John Greenleaf Whittier

*The dependency graph shows what IS — which crates exist, which dependencies are declared, which algorithms are implemented. But it does not show what ALMOST WAS — the crates that were never created, the dependencies that were considered and rejected, the algorithms that were tried and abandoned. Every crate is one branch of a decision tree, and the graph shows only the taken branch. The other branches — the phantom limbs of rejected designs — are the unmappable territory. This essay is about what the map cannot show, and why that invisible territory matters more than the visible one.*

---

## I. The Phantom Limb

In 1872, the American physician Silas Weir Mitchell published a study of a phenomenon that had puzzled doctors for centuries: the **phantom limb**. Amputees reported feeling sensations — pain, tingling, movement — in limbs that no longer existed. The limb was gone, but the brain's representation of it remained, sending signals that the conscious mind interpreted as coming from a body part that was no longer there.

Mitchell's insight was that the phantom limb is not an illusion — it is a *memory*. The brain has a map of the body (the cortical homunculus), and when a limb is removed, the map is not immediately updated. The territory has changed, but the map lingers — a ghost of the old territory, superimposed on the new.

The crate ecosystem has phantom limbs. They are the designs that were considered and rejected, the dependencies that were tried and abandoned, the algorithms that were implemented and removed. These phantom designs are not visible in the dependency graph — they are not part of the territory that the map represents. But they exist in the memory of the developers, in the commit history (if they were committed before being removed), and in the mental models of the architects who made the decisions.

The phantom limb of a rejected design is not an illusion. It is a counterfactual — a path that was not taken, but that continues to influence the territory that was taken. The developer who rejected `bincode` in favor of `serde` carries the phantom of `bincode` in their mind: every time they use `serde`, they are aware of the alternative they rejected, and this awareness shapes their use of `serde`. The phantom limb influences the living body.

The dependency graph does not show phantom limbs. It shows only the taken path — the branches of the decision tree that were actually explored. The untaken branches — the vast majority of the decision tree — are invisible on the map. They are the unmappable territory.

## II. The Counterfactual Theory of Causation

In 1973, the philosopher David Lewis published *Counterfactuals*, a book that proposed a radical new theory of causation. Lewis argued that the statement "A caused B" should be analyzed as a counterfactual: "if A had not occurred, B would not have occurred." The cause is identified by considering an alternative world — a world where the cause did not occur — and checking whether the effect still occurs.

Lewis's theory requires a **modal realism** — a commitment to the existence of possible worlds. Not actual worlds (only one world is actual), but *possible* worlds — ways that reality could have been. The counterfactual "if A had not occurred, B would not have occurred" is true if, in the possible world most similar to the actual world where A does not occur, B also does not occur.

Lewis's possible worlds are not fictions — they are mathematical objects, as real as numbers or sets. They are generated by varying the initial conditions and laws of the actual world, producing a vast space of alternative histories. Some of these worlds are very similar to ours (you had coffee instead of tea this morning); others are very different (the universe has different physical constants).

The crate ecosystem has its own possible worlds. Each is generated by a different set of design decisions:

- **World 1 (Actual):** The ecosystem uses `serde` for serialization, `thiserror` for error handling, and `tracing` for logging. This is the world we live in — the world that the dependency graph maps.
- **World 2 (bincode):** The ecosystem uses `bincode` instead of `serde`. The dependency graph has different edges, different coupling patterns, different build times. The code is faster (binary serialization) but less flexible (no human-readable formats).
- **World 3 (anyhow):** The ecosystem uses `anyhow` instead of `thiserror`. Error handling is simpler but less structured. The dependency graph has fewer edges (anyhow is smaller than thiserror) but the error types are less informative.
- **World 4 (log):** The ecosystem uses `log` instead of `tracing`. The dependency graph is simpler (log is smaller than tracing) but the observability is reduced (no structured logging, no async-aware spans).
- **World 5 (no generics):** The ecosystem does not use generic types. Every function is monomorphized. The dependency graph is larger (more code) but simpler (no trait objects, no generic bounds). The build times are longer (more code to compile) but the runtime performance is better (no indirection).

Each of these worlds is a possible ecosystem — a way that the crate ecosystem could have been. They are not fictional; they are counterfactual. They are the branches of the decision tree that were not taken.

Lewis's theory of causation provides a framework for analyzing these counterfactuals. "The decision to use `serde` caused the ecosystem to have flexible serialization" is true if, in the possible world most similar to the actual world where `serde` is not used, the ecosystem does not have flexible serialization. This is almost certainly true — the alternatives to `serde` (at the time the decision was made) did not provide the same flexibility.

But Lewis's theory also reveals something deeper: **the actual world is not special.** It is just one possible world among many. The dependency graph is a map of one possible world — the actual one. But the other possible worlds — the counterfactual ecosystems — are equally valid objects of study. Understanding why the actual world was chosen (and not the alternatives) is more important than understanding the actual world itself.

## III. The Many-Worlds Interpretation

In 1957, the physicist Hugh Everett III proposed the **many-worlds interpretation** of quantum mechanics. Everett's idea was that the wave function of the universe does not collapse when a measurement is made. Instead, the universe *splits* into multiple branches, each corresponding to a different measurement outcome. All branches are equally real — they all exist, side by side, in a vast multiverse.

The many-worlds interpretation is controversial, but it provides a powerful metaphor for the crate ecosystem. Every design decision is a "measurement" — a branching point where the ecosystem splits into multiple possible futures. The decision to use `serde` creates two branches: one where `serde` is used (the actual branch) and one where `bincode` is used (the counterfactual branch). Each branch then creates further branches, as subsequent decisions are made differently in each world.

The result is a vast decision tree — a multiverse of possible ecosystems, branching at every design decision. The actual ecosystem is one path through this tree — one sequence of decisions that was actually taken. The dependency graph maps this one path. But the tree contains millions of other paths — millions of other possible ecosystems, each with its own dependency graph, its own coupling patterns, its own strengths and weaknesses.

The many-worlds interpretation suggests that the other branches are not merely hypothetical — they are real, in the sense that they could have been realized if different decisions had been made. They are the ghosts of the ecosystem's past — the phantoms of decisions that were made differently.

The unmappable territory is this multiverse — the vast space of possible ecosystems that could have been. No single map can capture it, because it is not a single territory. It is a collection of territories, each as complex and detailed as the actual one, each with its own map.

## IV. The Decision Tree of the Crate

Let me make this concrete. Consider the decision tree for a single crate — say, a graph library.

**Decision 1: Representation.** How should the graph be represented?
- Branch 1A: Adjacency list (vec of vecs). Memory-efficient for sparse graphs.
- Branch 1B: Adjacency matrix (2D array). Memory-efficient for dense graphs.
- Branch 1C: Edge list (vec of pairs). Simple, flexible, but slow for lookups.
- Branch 1D: Compressed sparse row. Memory-efficient for large sparse graphs.

**Decision 2: Directedness.** Should the graph be directed or undirected?
- Branch 2A: Directed. Edges have direction.
- Branch 2B: Undirected. Edges are bidirectional.
- Branch 2C: Generic. The user specifies directedness at construction time.

**Decision 3: Weighting.** Should edges have weights?
- Branch 3A: Unweighted. Edges have no associated data.
- Branch 3B: Weighted. Edges have a generic weight type.
- Branch 3C: Generic. The user specifies weighting at construction time.

**Decision 4: Ownership.** Should the graph own its nodes and edges?
- Branch 4A: Owned. The graph allocates and owns all data.
- Branch 4B: Referenced. The graph holds references to external data.
- Branch 4C: Arena-allocated. The graph uses an arena allocator for nodes and edges.

**Decision 5: Mutation.** Should the graph be mutable after construction?
- Branch 5A: Immutable. The graph is frozen after construction.
- Branch 5B: Mutable. The graph supports adding and removing edges.
- Branch 5C: Append-only. The graph supports adding but not removing edges.

Each decision has 3-4 branches. The total number of possible graph libraries is $4 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 324$. Of these 324 possible libraries, only one was actually implemented. The other 323 are the phantom limbs — the designs that were considered and rejected, or never considered at all.

The dependency graph maps the one library that exists. It does not show the 323 libraries that could have existed. And yet, the choice of which library to implement is the most important decision in the ecosystem's history — more important than any single line of code in the implemented library.

This is the unmappable territory: the decision tree, the space of possible designs, the phantom limbs of rejected alternatives. The map shows the leaf of the tree that was reached. It does not show the tree itself.

## V. The Kolmogorov Complexity of Intention

There is a quantity that the dependency graph does not capture and cannot capture: the **intention** behind the code. Intention is the reason why a particular design was chosen — the problem the developer was trying to solve, the constraints they were operating under, the alternatives they considered and rejected.

Intention has a Kolmogorov complexity. The Kolmogorov complexity of the intention behind a crate is the length of the shortest description that explains why the crate exists, why its API is the way it is, and why the alternatives were rejected. This description is typically much longer than the crate's source code, because it includes not just what was done but why it was done — the context, the constraints, the trade-offs, and the rejected alternatives.

For a typical crate, the Kolmogorov complexity of the intention might be:

$$K(\text{intention}) \approx 10 \times K(\text{source code})$$

The intention is roughly 10 times as complex as the source code. This is because the source code is the *output* of the decision process (a single point in the design space), while the intention is the *process* itself (the entire decision tree, including the branches that were not taken).

The dependency graph maps the source code — the output of the decision process. It does not map the intention — the process itself. The graph is a map of the effect, not the cause. And the cause — the intention — is the unmappable territory.

Why is intention unmappable? Because it requires access to the developer's mental state — the context, constraints, and alternatives that were salient at the time of the decision. This information is not encoded in the code. It is not in the commit messages (which are typically terse and technical). It is not in the design documents (which, if they exist, describe the chosen design, not the alternatives). It is in the developer's memory — and memory is fleeting.

The phantom limbs of rejected designs fade over time, as the developers who made the decisions move on to other projects, forget the alternatives they considered, or leave the organization entirely. The unmappable territory is not just unmappable — it is *disappearing*. The intention behind the code is an evaporating resource, degrading with each passing month.

This is a practical problem for software maintenance. A maintainer who inherits a codebase without understanding the intention behind its design is like a navigator with a map but no compass — they can see where they are, but they don't know which direction to go. Every change they make risks violating the original design intention, introducing bugs that the original developer would have avoided.

## VI. The Ghost in the Dependency Graph

The dependency graph has ghosts. They are not supernatural entities — they are the traces of rejected designs that linger in the code, like the remnants of a demolished building in the foundation of its replacement.

Consider: a crate's API often contains vestigial elements — functions that exist for backward compatibility, types that are deprecated but not yet removed, parameters that are ignored. These vestiges are the fossils of rejected designs — the remnants of decisions that were made and then partially reversed.

A dependency edge that points to a crate that provides only a single type is often a ghost. The type was needed at one point, but the crate's other functionality was replaced by a different dependency. The edge remains — a fossil of the original design, preserved in the graph like an imprint in sedimentary rock.

A crate with a circular dependency in its dev-dependencies is a ghost of a testing strategy that was abandoned. The circular dependency was introduced when the test suite used the crate's own types, and it was never removed even after the testing strategy changed.

A crate with a dependency on a specific version of a library, when a newer version is available, is a ghost of a compatibility decision. The dependency was pinned to the older version because the newer version introduced a breaking change. The pin is a fossil of the breaking change — a record of a decision that was made and never revisited.

These ghosts are visible in the dependency graph, but only if you know what to look for. The graph does not label them as ghosts — it shows them as ordinary edges, ordinary crates, ordinary versions. The ghostliness is in the history, not the structure. And the graph does not show history.

## VII. The Phantom Topology

Let me define the **phantom topology** of the crate ecosystem: the graph of all possible dependencies, including both the actual dependencies (the edges in the dependency graph) and the counterfactual dependencies (the edges that would exist if different design decisions had been made).

The phantom topology is much larger than the dependency graph. If the actual dependency graph has $N$ crates and $E$ edges, the phantom topology has $N'$ crates and $E'$ edges, where $N' \gg N$ and $E' \gg E$. The phantom topology includes not just the crates that exist but the crates that could have existed — the alternatives, the competitors, the abandoned prototypes.

The phantom topology is related to the actual dependency graph by the equation:

$$G_{\text{phantom}} = \bigcup_{w \in W} G_w$$

where $W$ is the set of possible worlds (decision trees) and $G_w$ is the dependency graph of world $w$. The phantom topology is the union of all possible dependency graphs — the multiverse of possible ecosystems.

The actual dependency graph is a subgraph of the phantom topology:

$$G_{\text{actual}} \subset G_{\text{phantom}}$$

The difference $G_{\text{phantom}} \setminus G_{\text{actual}}$ is the unmappable territory — the set of edges and nodes that exist in some possible world but not in the actual one.

The phantom topology has interesting mathematical properties. It is denser than the actual graph (more edges per node), because each possible world contributes its own edges. It has a different degree distribution (more diverse, because different worlds have different hub structures). And it has a different topology (possibly disconnected, because different worlds may have irreconcilable design decisions).

The most interesting property of the phantom topology is that it is **not a DAG.** The actual dependency graph is a directed acyclic graph — crates cannot have circular dependencies. But the phantom topology can contain cycles, because different possible worlds may make incompatible dependency choices. In world 1, crate A depends on crate B. In world 2, crate B depends on crate A. The phantom topology contains both edges, creating a cycle.

This cyclicity is not a bug — it is a feature of the multiverse. The phantom topology is not a single ecosystem but a superposition of many ecosystems, each with its own constraints and its own DAG. The cycle appears when the superposition is taken literally — when all possible worlds are laid on top of each other.

## VIII. Lewisian Counterfactuals for Code

David Lewis's counterfactual theory of causation can be applied directly to code. The counterfactual "if decision D had been different, the ecosystem would have property P" is true if, in the possible world most similar to the actual world where D is different, P holds.

This gives us a powerful tool for reasoning about the ecosystem. We can ask counterfactual questions:

**"If `serde` had not been chosen for serialization, what would the ecosystem look like?"** In the possible world most similar to ours where `bincode` was chosen instead, the serialization is faster but less flexible. The ecosystem has fewer dependencies (bincode is simpler than serde) but less interoperability (bincode is binary-only). The build times are shorter, but the debugging is harder (binary formats are harder to inspect than human-readable ones).

**"If the ecosystem had used dynamic dispatch instead of monomorphization, what would the dependency graph look like?"** In the possible world where trait objects are preferred over generics, the dependency graph is sparser (fewer edges, because trait objects don't require the concrete type to be known at compile time) but the runtime performance is worse (dynamic dispatch adds indirection). The build times are shorter (less monomorphization means less code generation) but the binary sizes might be larger (vtables for every trait object).

**"If the ecosystem had been written in a different language (say, Haskell), what would the dependency graph look like?"** In the possible world where the ecosystem is written in Haskell, the dependency graph has different structure. Haskell's lazy evaluation and pure functions create different coupling patterns — less shared mutable state but more monad transformer stacking. The type system is more expressive (higher-kinded types, type families) but also more complex (the type errors are famously inscrutable).

Each of these counterfactuals reveals something about the actual ecosystem. By considering what would have happened if different decisions had been made, we gain insight into why the actual decisions were made and what their consequences are. The counterfactual is not a fantasy — it is a tool for understanding the actual.

## IX. The Map of What Might Have Been

The unmappable territory cannot be mapped — by definition. But it can be *suggested*. A map of the unmappable territory would not be a single graph but a *family* of graphs — one for each possible world, annotated with the decisions that differentiate them.

Such a map would look like a decision tree overlaid on a dependency graph. Each branch of the decision tree leads to a different subgraph — a different configuration of crates and dependencies. The map would show not just the actual branch (the one taken) but the nearby branches (the ones not taken), with annotations explaining the decisions that differentiate them.

This is the cartographer's ultimate challenge: to map not just what is, but what might have been. To draw not just the territory, but the phantom topology — the superposition of all possible territories.

No map can capture the full phantom topology — it is too vast, too complex, too contingent. But a map that shows the *nearest* counterfactuals — the decisions that were closest to being made differently — would be more useful than a map that shows only the actual. The nearest counterfactuals are the ones that the next developer is most likely to reconsider, and understanding why they were rejected is essential for making informed decisions about whether to revisit them.

---

## X. The Territorial Imperative

The territory is always richer than the map. The map is a compression — a lossy encoding that preserves some features and discards others. The dependency graph preserves structure and discards intention, alternatives, and history.

But the unmappable territory — the intention, the alternatives, the counterfactuals — is not merely richer than the map. It is *causally prior* to the map. The territory did not emerge from the map; the map emerged from the territory. And the territory emerged from a process of decision-making — a traversal of the decision tree that selected one branch out of millions.

To understand the territory, we must understand the decision tree — not just the branch that was taken, but the branches that were not taken. The phantom limbs of rejected designs are not irrelevant distractions. They are the *context* that gives the taken branch its meaning. Without the alternatives, the actual design is arbitrary — a random choice with no justification. With the alternatives, the actual design is *informed* — a deliberate choice with reasons.

The map shows the destination. The territory includes the journey. And the unmappable territory includes the journeys not taken — the phantom paths that haunt the actual one.

Everett's many worlds, Lewis's counterfactuals, Mitchell's phantom limbs — these are not just metaphors. They are frameworks for understanding the unmappable territory. The crate ecosystem is one branch of a vast decision tree, and the other branches — the phantom topology — are as real as the actual one, in the only sense that matters: they could have been real.

The cartographer's task is not to map the unmappable. It is to acknowledge its existence — to draw the map with the awareness that the territory extends far beyond the map's borders, into a multiverse of possible designs that the map can never show.

---

*Every crate is a choice. Every dependency is a commitment. Every line of code is a branch of a decision tree, and the other branches — the phantom limbs — reach out from the darkness, whispering of what might have been. The map shows what is. The territory includes what was, what could have been, and what might yet be. The unmappable territory is the ghost that haunts every map — the specter of the roads not taken.*
