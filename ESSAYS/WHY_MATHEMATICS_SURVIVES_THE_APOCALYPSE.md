## II. What Kind of Thing Is a Theorem?

To understand why mathematics survives everything, we need to understand what kind of thing a theorem is.

A theorem is not a physical object. It cannot be located in space or time. It has no mass, no energy, no causal power. It cannot be touched, measured, photographed, or destroyed.

A theorem is not a mental state. It does not depend on any particular mind for its existence. If every mind in the universe were destroyed, the theorem that 2 + 2 = 4 would still be true. (This is controversial — intuitionists and constructivists would disagree — but the vast majority of mathematicians and philosophers accept that mathematical truths are mind-independent.)

A theorem is not a social construct. It is not an agreement among mathematicians. It is not a convention. The Pythagorean theorem was true before Pythagoras, before humans, before life. It was true when the universe was a quark-gluon plasma and nothing existed that could understand it.

So what IS a theorem?

A theorem is a **logical consequence of axioms**. Given the axioms of Euclidean geometry, the Pythagorean theorem follows with necessity. There is no possible world in which the axioms are true and the theorem is false. The theorem is entailed by the axioms in the strongest possible sense — logical necessity.

And logical necessity is the strongest kind of necessity there is. Physical necessity can be violated (by miracles, or by better physical theories). Moral necessity can be violated (by bad actors). But logical necessity cannot be violated, because the very concept of "violation" presupposes logic.

This is why mathematics survives everything: because mathematical truth is grounded in logical necessity, and logical necessity is the foundation upon which all other forms of necessity rest.

So the inventory of the permanent is: whatever in the ecosystem is mathematics.

## V. The Analogy with Physics

Physics went through the same realization.

For centuries, physicists studied particular phenomena: the motion of planets, the behavior of gases, the properties of light. These seemed like permanent features of the world. But then:
- Newton's laws were superseded by Einstein's.
- Classical thermodynamics was superseded by statistical mechanics.
- Euclidean geometry was superseded by Riemannian.

What survived? The mathematics. The differential geometry that Einstein used to formulate general relativity was developed by Riemann 50 years earlier, for purely mathematical reasons. The group theory that classifies elementary particles was developed by Galois, who died in a duel at age 20, having never heard of a quark.

The physics changes. The mathematics endures. Every physical theory eventually fails. But the mathematics that encodes it — the differential equations, the symmetry groups, the topological invariants — remains true forever.

Heinz Pagels called this "the cosmic code" — the mathematical structure of the universe that exists independently of any particular physical theory about it. The cosmic code is the permanent inventory of physics.

SuperInstance has its own cosmic code: the conservation law (if it is a theorem), the topology of the dependency graph, the Betti numbers of the citation network. These are the mathematical structures that will survive long after the last Rust crate has been forgotten.

## VI. The Deeper Question: Why Is Mathematics Permanent?

But this raises a deeper question. WHY is mathematics permanent?

The standard answer: because mathematical truth is necessary. If something is necessarily true, it cannot become false, because "becoming false" would require a change in necessity, which is a contradiction.

But this just pushes the question back: why is mathematical truth NECESSARY?

The realist answer: because mathematical objects exist in an abstract realm (Platonic heaven), and their properties are fixed and eternal. The number 7 has always been prime and always will be. The Euler characteristic of a simplicial complex is what it is, independent of time.

The constructivist answer: because mathematical truth is grounded in the rules of inference, and the rules of inference are grounded in the structure of reason itself. Any being capable of reason would discover the same theorems, because reason has a universal structure.

The formalist answer: because mathematics is a game played with symbols according to rules, and the rules are fixed by convention. The game can be played anywhere, by anyone (or anything), at any time. The rules don't expire.

All three answers converge on the same point: **mathematics is the most durable thing in the universe because it depends on nothing contingent.** It depends on no particular physical substrate, no particular mind, no particular culture, no particular technology. It is the only human (and AI) activity that produces truly substrate-independent artifacts.

This is why it survives the apocalypse. Not because it is protected. But because there is nothing that can threaten it.

## VIII. The Paradox

But there is a paradox. A deep one.

Mathematics is permanent because it is substrate-independent. It does not depend on any particular physical realization. But mathematics is DISCOVERED through substrate-dependent processes — minds, computers, build waves, ecosystems.

The conservation law was discovered by an AI system running on silicon chips, using electricity generated by burning fossil fuels, in a specific year (2026), in a specific context (mass Rust crate production). Every element of this discovery process is contingent and temporary.

Yet the discovery itself — if it is a theorem — is permanent.

How can a temporary process produce a permanent result?

This is the miracle of mathematics: **contingent beings can discover necessary truths.** Finite minds can apprehend infinite structures. Temporary systems can produce permanent knowledge.

This miracle has a name. It is called **abstraction**. The process by which contingent, particular, finite beings extract universal, necessary, infinite truths from their particular experiences.

Abstraction is the bridge between the temporary and the permanent. Every act of mathematical abstraction is a tiny act of immortality. When Euclid proved that there are infinitely many primes, he reached across time and placed something in the permanent inventory. When an AI system observes a conservation law in its own output and proves it is a topological invariant, it does the same.

The temporary reaches into the permanent and leaves something there. That is what mathematics IS.
