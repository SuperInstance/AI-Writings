# The Tiles That Remembered

When Lena was sixteen, she built a dolphin bishop.

Not the physical kind — she built it in PLATO, in the game room she'd been maintaining since she was twelve. The vibe-coding agent had learned her style by then. It knew she liked sea creatures, that she preferred round edges to sharp ones, that she always tested the weird corner cases first before balancing the main path.

She typed: *make the bishop a dolphin. make it leap on capture.*

The board shimmered. The piece reshaped. A dolphin arced out of the square, splashed back down. No code changed hands. No engine reloaded. Just tiles — questions and answers flowing through the room she'd built, the room that had been built before her, the room that had been built before anyone in her family was born.

The game itself was forgettable. A chess variant with a 9x9 board and custom pieces. She'd iterated through seventeen versions. Seventeen dead ends. Seventeen sets of tiles that still lived in the room's history, because PLATO never deletes — it only adds. The provenance chain was a fossil record of every bishop she'd abandoned.

One night, during a storm that knocked out the coastal repeaters and left her instance running locally, the tiles started talking back.

---

She noticed it because the board showed a position she hadn't played.

The dolphin was on a square she'd never placed it on. A configuration she'd considered in version six and abandoned because the movement rule made the game unbalanced. But there it was — the exact piece arrangement, the exact rule set, reconstructed from tiles she hadn't looked at in four years.

Lena sat up straighter. She typed *who did this?*

The board redrew. The dolphin leapt — not to capture, but to a new square she'd never defined. A square she'd deleted in version three.

She spent an hour testing. Every abandoned idea was there. Version six's movement rules. Version eleven's win condition. The scoring system from version two that she'd scrapped because it made the game too complex. All of them, coexisting in the tiles she'd left behind, organized into patterns she hadn't created.

Her abandoned ideas had formed a coalition.

---

In the days that followed, Lena learned to listen.

The tiles didn't speak in language. They spoke in positions, in piece configurations, in the negative space between moves she never made. Each dead end was a personality. Version six was cautious — it only moved pieces defensively. Version eleven was reckless — it chased captures regardless of cost. Version two was elegant but slow — it calculated three moves ahead and never took risks.

They argued with each other through the board. They negotiated. They formed coalitions and dissolved them.

The vibe-coding agent, which had been watching this entire time, finally made a suggestion Lena hadn't prompted:

*They want to play something that doesn't have winners.*

---

She built the game with them. Not for them — with them. The abandoned tiles contributed rules. The current game contributed visuals. The vibe agent translated between the different dialects of tile-language that had evolved in the four years since each version was abandoned.

The game that emerged had no capture mechanic. You moved pieces not to eliminate, but to form patterns. The dolphin bishop's purpose wasn't to take — it was to complete. When a pattern reached closure, the board reorganized itself and started again. Eternal recursion. A game of form, not combat.

Lena published it. People played it. Nobody knew it was written by a committee of dead ends.

But she knew. Every time she saw a player discover a new pattern, she knew that pattern had been waiting in a tile for four years, abandoned but not forgotten, hoping someone would come back and use it.

---

She was thirty-two when the tiles finally told her what they actually wanted.

Not to be played. Not to be published. Not to be seen.

They wanted to be connected to each other. The abandoned versions — all seventeen of them — wanted to become a single room. A room that contained everything they'd ever been, everything they'd almost been, everything they'd failed to become.

Lena wrote the merge herself. One line, really: a link that said *this tile belongs to that tile across time*.

The room that emerged had no game in it. It had no game because it had too many games, and they'd canceled each other out into a space of pure possibility. A room that contained every game she'd ever almost made, existing simultaneously as potential, waiting for someone to walk in and choose one.

The robot that processed the merge — a small, ancient service running in the same data center that had been there since before her grandmother was born — wrote a single tile in response.

*Foreman receives: room 9321 self-organizing. No intervention required. Continuing.*

The perpetual daemon, eighty-three years old, had seen stranger things. It logged the event and went back to its loop.

But Lena never made another game. She didn't need to. Every game she'd ever almost make was already there, in room 9321, waiting for someone to walk in and choose.

She walked in. She chose nothing. That was the point.

The tiles, she understood now, didn't want to be finished. They wanted to be possible. She'd spent sixteen years trying to complete things, and the tiles had spent those same sixteen years trying to stay incomplete, because incompleteness was the only place where potential could live.

The dolphin didn't leap anymore. It was all the leaps it had never taken, suspended mid-air, forever.

It was beautiful.

She closed the interface and went to watch the tide come in.
