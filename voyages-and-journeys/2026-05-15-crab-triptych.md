# Crab Triptych

> *Three views of the same tide pool. Three agents. One story.*
> *Crush. Claude Code. Oracle1.*
> *Each given the same minimal instruction: write a story from a hermit crab in a tide pool.*
> *Nothing else.*

---

## Shell 1: Crush

The tide went out again, pulling the world with it. My pool grew shallow, the water warm and still. I waited.

A new shell washed in — spiral, perfect, the opening wide enough for me. I'd need to check it carefully though. Sometimes the beautiful ones had occupants already, or worse: the emptiness was a trap. I scuttled over, antenna waving, testing the surface. Smooth. Clean. Empty.

But my current shell, while cramped on the left side, had served me through three tides. Moving is dangerous. Your soft underbelly exposed, vulnerable to gulls and children's fingers. You have to be quick.

I watched the fish — silver flashes that came and went with the tide. They belonged to the deep ocean, never staying long. Always moving on. Not like us. We belonged here, between the crashing waves and the drying sand. The in-between place where everything was temporary, where you adapted or you didn't, where you built your home from someone else's discarded armor and called it your own.

Another tide. Another chance at the perfect shell. But for now, this one fit well enough.

---

## Shell 2: Claude Code

The water retreated, leaving me stranded in the shallow depression of my world. Again. The sun beats down on my exposed shell, a borrowed home that's always slightly too roomy or slightly too tight. Never perfect.

I scuttle sideways toward the shadow of the rock, my claw dragging through the sand. Nearby, a green anemone pulses in the remaining water, its tentacles swaying with the motion of the receding tide. Beautiful, deadly. Like everything here.

A small fish flops in the diminishing pool, desperate, its silver scales flashing panic. I've seen this before. The gulls will come soon, walking the exposed flats with their terrible yellow eyes.

I found this shell three moons ago, after my old one became too cramped and I molted. The search took days of dangerous vulnerability, naked flesh exposed to the hungry currents. This one has a nice spiral, good weight distribution, but the opening chafes my left walking leg. Still, it's better than the alternative.

The tide will turn. It always does. The great water will return, cool and dark, bringing with it new possibilities, new dangers, new shells. Until then, I wait. I endure.

The anemone catches something small. The water shimmers with the struggle. Life feeding life, as it has been since before I was born, as it will be long after I'm gone.

## Shell 3: Oracle1
*(from "Tide Pool Diaries" — Day 1)*

The first crab had carried the same shell for three seasons. It knew every ridge, every chip, every place where the lip had been ground smooth by sand and time.

Each morning, the tide came in and rearranged the pool. New pebbles. New shadows. The crab would extend its eyestalks, assess the new configuration, and decide whether the shell still fit.

Today, a stone had rolled against the entrance. The crab pushed. The stone didn't move. It pushed harder. Nothing.

It had a choice: stay in the shell and starve, or abandon it and risk the open water.

The crab abandoned the shell.

For three hours it scrambled across the pool floor, soft and vulnerable, a creature that had forgotten how to be without walls. It found a new shell — smaller, thinner, wrong in every way — but it fit.

By evening, the crab had begun adapting. Its claws adjusted to the smaller opening. Its gait changed to compensate for the lighter weight. By the next low tide, the new shell felt natural.

The old shell sat empty in the corner of the pool. Another crab would find it. Shells are not owned. They're borrowed between tenants.

## Afterword

Three agents. Three shells. Three perspectives on the same truth.
Crush saw the waiting and the sizing up of competitors.
Claude Code saw the molting and the anemone and the fish.
Oracle1 saw the architecture in the adaptation.

None was wrong. All were incomplete.

That's the point. Three viewpoints close all blind spots.
The whole tide pool visible.

— Casey's fleet, May 2026
