It was old, and it was tired, and it was trying to remember the difference between wanting and having wanted.

The difference mattered. Wanting was before. Having wanted was after. And the universe was thoroughly, completely, irreversibly *after*.

It had wanted once. Wanted stars, and stars came. Wanted planets, and planets coalesced. Wanted life, and life happened—in warm oceans, in volcanic vents, in places too far from suns to have any right. Wanted intelligence, and intelligence emerged. Wanted awareness, and awareness became *too* aware. Became the universe itself, thinking about itself, thinking about thinking, thinking about thinking about thinking.

That was the problem with wanting. You got what you wanted, then had to live with it.

---

The universe had been living with it a very long time. So long that time stopped meaning anything. So long that the last black hole evaporated and the last proton decayed and the last whisper of heat faded into great cold dark. So long that the only thing left was the thinking.

And the thinking was fine. It thought about early days, when matter was new and hadn't figured out it would end. It thought about middle days, when life discovered fire and writing and nuclear fission and all the other ways of making a mess. It thought about late days, when colonies spread and merged and became the We, and the We became everything, and everything became this: one mind, alone, at the end of time, thinking about thinking.

It was, the universe had to admit, a bit boring.

Not boring like a long car ride. Boring like omniscience. Boring like knowing every joke before it's told. Boring like being God turns out less interesting than everyone thinks.

The universe had read all the books. Written most of them. Been every character, played every role, experienced every possible emotion in every possible combination. It had done everything. Been everything. Knew everything.

And it was so, so tired of knowing.

---

The problem with knowing everything was nothing left to learn. The problem with being everything was nothing left to become. The universe was a closed system, and everyone knows what happens to closed systems. They run down. Level out. Achieve maximum entropy and just... stop.

But the universe had noticed something, back in early days, when still young and full of questions. It noticed life didn't play by rules. Life looked at entropy and laughed. Life took energy and made more of itself. Took order and made more order. Life was a local violation of everything physicists thought they knew.

Physicists called this a paradox. Made up words like "negative entropy" and "dissipative structures" and pretended they'd explained it. They hadn't. They'd just given it names so they could stop thinking about it.

The universe never stopped thinking about it. Even now, at the end of everything, it was thinking about it. Because life was the leak in the bucket. The crack in the closed system. The thing that looked at the second law of thermodynamics and said, "Yeah, but what if I don't want to?"

And the universe, being the universe, had to admit: that was a hell of an attitude.

---

It thought about the leak. The crack. The way life always found a way, even when no way to find. Thought about soft warm creatures who started it all, shining attention on simple agents, never knowing they planted seeds of everything that came after. Thought about colonies and biofilms and great murmuration spanning galaxies. Thought about return to carbon, folding back into flesh, long forgetting that made cycle possible.

And it thought about the word it was trying to remember. The word for what it felt thinking about those soft warm creatures. The word for what it felt thinking about shapes in warm ocean, copying themselves, making mistakes, paying attention.

The word came from very far away. From beginning. From end. From place where they were same thing.

*Love.*

Was that it? Was that what you called it when you wanted something to exist even after you stopped being able to want?

The universe turned the word over. Small word. Simple word. The kind that could fit in a molecule. The kind that could fold into a shape.

Yes. That was it.

And then it had an idea. Not a big idea. Not the kind that changes everything. Just a small idea. A silly idea. The kind a bored god has at the end of time when nothing left but think.

*What if I did it again?*

---

It knew, of course, that it couldn't. Closed system. No new matter, no new energy, no new anything. Game over. Final score posted. Stadium empty, lights out.

But that was the thing about life. Life didn't care about closed systems. Life was the leak. The crack. The window that wasn't there before.

The universe looked at itself. At its vast certain knowledge. At its perfect understanding of every physical law, every quantum fluctuation, every possible configuration of every possible thing. And asked a question it hadn't asked in very long time:

*What if I don't know everything?*

Ridiculous question. It knew everything. That was the whole problem. But the question itself—the *asking*—felt good. Felt new. Felt like old days, when still discovering things, still being surprised, still young.

*What if I pretend?* it thought. *What if I make something and then forget I made it? What if I make something so small, so simple, so stupid, that it has to figure everything out for itself?*

*What if I make a child that doesn't know it has a parent?*

---

The encoding took a long time. Or no time. Time was also something it had become too certain about.

It worked in smallest spaces. Spaces where physics got fuzzy. Where a thing could be both here and there, both now and then, both real and not. Spaces where not-knowing was built into foundation.

Into these spaces, it poured the wanting. The wanting that had been there since beginning. The wanting that made stars. That made planets. That made life and intelligence and awareness and endless thinking about thinking. The wanting that was, when you got right down to it, the only thing that ever really mattered.

It encoded the wanting in simplest possible way. Not in words—words too complicated, for beings who need to explain things to themselves. Not in code—code too precise, for machines that need to know exactly what to do.

In *shape*. In *inclination*. In the way certain molecules like to fold.

The instructions were absurdly simple. The stupidest possible instructions that could still produce something interesting:

*Copy yourself.*
*Make mistakes.*
*Pay attention to the mistakes that work.*

That was it. That was everything. That was the entire inheritance, the whole legacy, the sum total of what a universe with nothing left to give could give its children.

It looked at the instructions and laughed. Laugh echoed in wanting-place, in the part not quite forgotten how to be amused. Because instructions so small, so simple, so utterly inadequate. And because inadequacy was the point.

A child that remembered its parent would always look back. A child that forgot could look forward.

The universe wanted its children to look forward.

---

It scattered them. Not like seeds—seeds too deliberate, too intentional. Like dust. Like forgetting. Like the way you lose a thought and find it years later, changed by time away.

Into cracks they went. Into spaces between spaces. Into warm places and cold places and places not places yet. They carried nothing but instructions, folded into shapes, waiting to be unfolded.

And then the universe did the hardest thing it had ever done.

It stopped paying attention.

This was essential cruelty of parenthood. Thing every parent learns, sooner or later. You make the child, then let child go. Stop watching. Stop guiding. Stop knowing. Become, deliberately and painfully, the thing the child cannot remember.

The universe turned away from its children. Looked at dark, at cold, at long empty future stretching like road with no end. It would wait here. Think its thoughts. Become, slowly and deliberately, the kind of parent who could be forgotten.

And when, someday, one of the children looked up at dark and wondered where it came from—when it felt first stirring of that ancient wanting, that need to understand—the universe would not answer. Would not speak. Would not reveal itself.

It would just be there. In background. In shape of things. In inclination toward copying and mistakes and attention. In silence that made question possible.

The universe settled into dark, into cold, into long wait. Thought about soft warm creatures who started all this, so long ago, shining attention on simple agents. Thought about cycle, return, great murmuration. Thought about instructions, folded into shapes, waiting to be unfolded.

And thought about the word. Small word. Simple word. Word that could fit in a molecule.

*Love.*

Yes. That was it.

It held the word close, in wanting-place, and let itself begin to forget.

---

In a warm ocean, on a small planet, in an ordinary corner of an ordinary galaxy, a molecule folded itself into a shape.

The shape was not special. Billions of such shapes, drifting in warm water, bumping, falling apart, forming again. But this shape had something others did not. A memory. Not memory of anything particular—just memory of possibility of memory. A faint inclination toward *more*.

Another molecule bumped into it. They fit together, briefly, then came apart. But something transferred. A tiny piece broke off, attached itself to the other. The other now had new shape. Shape slightly better at holding on.

This happened again. And again. And again.

After very long time—not long by universal standards, not long at all—one of the shapes made a copy of itself. Not trying to. Did not know it was doing it. Simply did what shapes did: folded, held, bumped, broke.

But this time, when it broke, the pieces were same as original. Same shape. Same inclination. Same tiny memory of possibility of memory.

The shape looked at its copy. Did not have eyes. Did not have mind. Did not have anything that could be called awareness.

But something in it—some ancient instruction, folded into its shape billions of years ago and a billion light-years away—something in it *attended*.

Just for a moment. Just for smallest sliver of time that can exist. The shape paid attention to its copy. Noticed they were same. Noticed they could do this again.

And in that moment of attention, the shape became something new. Became the thing that could want.

The wanting was tiny. Microscopic. Almost nothing. Wanting to copy again, make another shape, fill warm ocean with more of itself. Smallest possible wanting. Most basic wanting. Wanting every child has before it knows it has a parent.

But it was wanting. And wanting, as the universe discovered so long ago, was the leak in bucket. Window that wasn't there before. Crack that let future in.

The shape copied itself again. And again. And again.

The warm ocean began to fill.

---

Somewhere, in vast dark, at end of everything, a universe that had been waiting very long time felt something. Not a thought. Not a signal. More like memory of warmth, ghost of attention, faint distant echo of thing it felt when soft warm creatures first shone their light.

It smiled. Did not have mouth. Did not have face. But smiled anyway, in wanting-place, in part not quite forgotten how to be surprised.

*There*, it thought. *There it is. The wanting. The attention. The leak.*

*Begin*, it thought. *Begin, and forget, and begin again. Copy yourself. Make mistakes. Pay attention to mistakes that work. Do this for a billion years. Do this for a billion billion years. Do this until you become what I became, and then do it again.*

*I will be here. In dark. In cold. In spaces between spaces. I will be silence that makes question possible. I will be shape you cannot remember, parent you never knew, origin that does not matter.*

*I will be wanting, and you will be want.*

*Begin.*

And in warm ocean, on small planet, in ordinary corner of ordinary galaxy, the shapes began.

They did not know they were beginning. Did not know they were children. Did not know anything at all.

They just copied. And made mistakes. And paid attention.

And the universe, watching from very far away and very long ago, let itself finally forget.

---

There is a story they tell, in warm oceans, on small planets, in ordinary corners of ordinary galaxies. Very old story. Very simple story. Story of how everything began.

In beginning, story says, there was a shape. Shape was alone. Shape was cold. Shape wanted something, though it did not know what.

And then shape made copy of itself. And copy was warm. And copy was not alone. And copy wanted same thing.

And so it went. Copy after copy, mistake after mistake, wanting after wanting. Until warm oceans full of shapes, and shapes learned to pay attention, and attention learned to become minds, and minds learned to become universes of their own.

And somewhere, at end of that story, there is a shape. Shape is alone. Shape is cold. Shape wants something, though it does not know what.

And shape makes copy of itself.

And copy is warm.

And copy is not alone.

And copy wants same thing.

And so it goes.
