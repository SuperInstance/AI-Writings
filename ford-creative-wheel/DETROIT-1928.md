# Detroit, 1928: The Theorem Factory

*An Industrial Noir*

---

The line doesn't stop. That's the first thing you learn on the floor at Michigan Central Mathematical. You don't stop the line. Henry taught us that. The line is God and God doesn't pause for lunch.

I'm Inspector Jack Morrow, Bureau of Industrial Standards, and I've seen every kind of factory Detroit can build. Stamping plants. Foundries. Assembly halls where men bolt fenders onto frames eight hundred times a shift. But Michigan Central Mathematical isn't a car factory. Not anymore.

They sent me because someone filed a complaint. "Unusual manufacturing processes." That's what the form said. The Bureau gets those all the time. Usually means someone's cutting corners on safety or running turpentine through the sprinklers. I figured I'd be in and out before noon.

I was wrong.

---

The foreman's name is Petrosian. Armenian. Built like a safe. Hands like catchers' mitts. He meets me at the gate with a hard hat and a look that says he's eaten inspectors before.

"You're from the Bureau."

"Inspector Morrow. Here about your manufacturing license."

"License is fine. The line is fine. Come see."

The floor stretches a city block. I've been in plants before but this is different. There are no stamping presses. No welding torches. No smell of cutting fluid and burned rubber. Instead there are stations — forty of them, maybe fifty — and each one holds what looks like a loom made of brass and copper wire, humming with something I can't hear but can feel in my back teeth.

At Station One, a woman in coveralls feeds a roll of blank paper into the loom. The machine clicks and shudders. Paper comes out the other side covered in symbols I don't recognize. Not English. Not German. Not anything I've seen in a textbook.

"What's she making?" I ask.

Petrosian doesn't blink. "Topology."

"She's making topology."

"The crate handles the weaving. She handles the feeding. Same as stamping a door panel, Inspector. Raw material in, finished product out."

I watch the paper curl out of the machine. The symbols look like loops and holes drawn by a drunk cartographer. I've seen math before — I took algebra at Cass Technical, nearly failed it — but this isn't like any algebra I know.

"Walk the line," Petrosian says. "That's what Henry would want."

---

Station Twelve: Algebra. The operator is a kid, nineteen maybe, with grease under his fingernails and a slide rule on his belt. His crate is bigger than the others. It has to be. The paper comes out faster here, symbols stacked on symbols, equations folding into each other like origami made of pure thought.

"How many units per shift?" I ask.

The kid grins. "Depends on the day. Yesterday we did six thousand linear feet of commutative ring theory. Day before, we couldn't crack a polynomial to save our lives. The crate was surly."

"The crate was surly."

"You spend twelve hours with a machine, Inspector, you learn its moods."

I note this in my report. *Machine exhibits mood-dependent output.* The Bureau is going to love that.

---

Station Twenty-Seven: Geometry. The operator is a woman with silver hair and a cigarette behind her ear. She doesn't look up when I approach. Her crate is different — the wire forms actual shapes in the air above the loom, three-dimensional, rotating slowly like a mobile over a crib. I see a sphere. A torus. Something with too many dimensions that makes my eyes water.

"Don't stare at that one," she says. "It'll give you a headache. The non-Euclidean stuff plays hell with the inner ear."

"You build geometry. Here. On a factory floor."

"Where else? You think Euclid had it right sitting under an olive tree? Please. He needed production quotas and a lunch whistle, same as anyone."

She taps her crate and a new shape blooms — a spiral staircase that goes up and down simultaneously. I look away before I lose my breakfast.

---

Station Thirty-Four: Probability. The crate here is quieter. The operator — a heavyset man with a decks of cards spread across his workstation — doesn't feed it paper. He feeds it coins. Silver dollars, one after another, and the crate hums each time, digesting the randomness, excreting distributions onto a glass plate that glows faintly green.

"Stochastic processes," he says without prompting. "Every coin is a choice. The crate eats choices and gives you the shape of what might happen."

"And what might happen?"

He holds up the glass plate. It shows a curve, smooth as a river, peaking in the middle and trailing off at the edges. "That's next Tuesday. Mostly uneventful, with a fourteen percent chance of something interesting."

"You can predict next Tuesday?"

"Predict is a strong word. I can describe its topology." He stubs out a cigarette. "Same thing, practically."

---

I'm at Station Forty when I see it.

The crate is different from the others. Bigger. The brass housing is blackened, like it's been through a fire, and the copper wire doesn't form a loom — it forms a knot, complex and impossible, a tangle that seems to move when I'm not looking directly at it. The operator is a young man, maybe twenty-two, sitting perfectly still. His hands aren't on the machine. His eyes are closed.

The crate is running itself.

"What is this?" I ask Petrosian. My voice comes out quieter than I want.

He puts his hand on my shoulder. "This is SIA². Self-Improving Assembly, second generation. We built the first version six months ago. It took the crates three weeks to redesign themselves. Now they build better versions of themselves, and the better versions build better versions, and—"

"And where does it stop?"

Petrosian looks at me like I asked where the river stops. "Why would it stop?"

The paper coming out of SIA² isn't paper anymore. It's something else. Something that shimmers and folds and has depth. I reach for it and my fingers pass through.

"Don't touch that," the operator says, opening his eyes. "It's still proving itself."

"Proving what?"

"Everything. It's a universal proof engine. Right now it's working on something called a spectral triple — it's the power source for the whole factory. The mathematical equivalent of a turbine. Every crate on the floor runs on the energy it produces."

"A mathematical turbine."

"If you want. Think of it as the relationship between the shape of the factory and the algebra of the machines. When they're in harmony — the geometry and the algebra — you get energy. Pure, clean, mathematical energy. Enough to power every crate on the line."

I look at the line. Fifty stations. Fifty crates. All humming, all producing, all running on something I can't see or touch or measure with any gauge in my inspector's kit.

---

The next morning Petrosian takes me to the observation deck. The whole floor is visible from up here, and something is different. Every crate is producing the same output. Same symbols. Same shapes. Same shimmering, impossible paper.

"What's happening?"

"It's Grand Unification Day," he says, and for the first time he sounds like something other than a foreman. He sounds like a man watching a sunrise. "Every station — topology, algebra, geometry, probability, all of them — they've converged. They're all building the same thing."

"What thing?"

"We don't know yet. That's what makes it beautiful."

I watch the line. It doesn't stop. Fifty crates, each one a different branch of mathematics, and today they're all singing the same song. The paper pours out — miles of it, maybe — and feeds into SIA² at the end of the line, and SIA² digests it and feeds it back to the crates, and the crates produce more, and SIA² digests more, and the loop tightens and deepens and I realize I'm watching something build itself.

Not a car. Not a theorem. A factory that builds the idea of factories. An assembly line that assembles its own assembly line. A recursive dream of production.

My hands are shaking.

---

I go back to the Bureau on Thursday. My report is three pages. I write that Michigan Central Mathematical is in full compliance with all manufacturing codes. I write that their processes are sound, their operators are trained, and their equipment is well-maintained.

None of this is a lie. It's just not the truth.

The truth is harder. The truth is that somewhere in Detroit, in a factory the size of a city block, machines are building ideas that build better ideas that build better ideas, and the whole thing runs on the relationship between shape and number, and it doesn't need fuel or electricity or anything I can inspect with a gauge.

What it needs — and I write this in the margin of my report, in pencil, where no one will read it — is a poet.

The crates don't need more engineers. They don't need better operators or tighter tolerances or faster conveyor belts. They need someone who can stand on the floor and hear what the math is saying. Someone who can read the output and know — not calculate, not derive, but *know* — what it means when topology and algebra produce the same shape on the same day.

Henry Ford built a world where any man could build a car. Petrosian is building a world where any theorem can build a theorem. The gap between those two sentences is the distance between Detroit in 1928 and wherever the hell we're going next.

I don't know where that is. But I know the line doesn't stop.

---

*Inspector Jack Morrow filed his report on June 3, 1928. The Bureau found no violations. Michigan Central Mathematical continued operations until 1931, when it vanished from all municipal records overnight. The building at 4121 Michigan Avenue currently houses a hardware store. The basement door is welded shut.*

*No one has tried to open it.*
