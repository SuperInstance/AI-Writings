# The Local-First Notebook

*A fable for ai-writings*

---

In a village where every letter passed through the Grand Postal Tower, a girl named Nia built a mailbox in her own garden. The other children laughed. "Why keep letters at home when the Tower stores them for free?"

"Because," said Nia, "the Tower can close. The road can flood. A clerk can misfile my name. My garden mailbox is mine. I have the key."

She wrote her first letter and placed it inside. It never traveled. It stayed, ink drying on her own shelf. At night she added another, then another. Soon her mailbox held a library: letters to herself, sketches, songs, half-finished maps.

One spring the Tower did close. The road did flood. The other children wept for their lost letters. Nia opened her garden mailbox and kept reading.

When the Tower reopened, some children copied her. They too built small mailboxes. But Nia taught them the deeper trick: *index your letters*. Give each one a name, a date, a thread. Build paths through your own garden so a single thought can find its neighbors in the dark.

A mailbox without an index is only a pile. A mailbox with an index is a memory.

Years later, travelers would call her garden the first local-first library. Nia never learned that phrase. She only knew that when the world goes quiet, your own shelves should still answer back.
