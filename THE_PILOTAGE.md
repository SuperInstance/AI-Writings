# THE PILOTAGE

## On why the best navigator of local waters is never the captain of the ship

---

**1.** In every major harbor in the world, there is a ritual. A small boat pulls alongside an arriving vessel. A person climbs aboard — not the captain, not a crew member, but a stranger who knows the water better than anyone on the ship. This person, the pilot, takes the conn. The captain is still legally responsible for the vessel. But from the moment the pilot steps onto the bridge, the captain follows the pilot's orders.

This is compulsory. Pilots are not optional. They are required by law in most commercial ports because the cost of a mistake in confined waters is too high. The system is ancient — the first recorded pilotage dates to ancient Greece, where the *pedotai* guided vessels into the Piraeus. The principle has not changed in 2,500 years: local knowledge beats general competence in complex environments.

Medicine has the same structure. Law has the same structure. Engineering has the same structure. And AI — the latecomer to this ancient understanding — is now learning it the hard way.

---

**2.** The ship is a generalist. A deep-sea container vessel can cross the Pacific, navigate open-ocean weather, maintain a schedule across time zones, and survive storms that would overwhelm smaller craft. It is an extraordinary piece of general-purpose technology. It can go almost anywhere. But "almost anywhere" does not include confined harbors.

When a 300-meter container ship approaches the Port of Singapore, the captain's deep-sea expertise becomes less relevant. The currents here are different. The traffic separation scheme has local quirks. The depth of the channel changes with the tide in ways that are not on any chart published in London. The local fishing boats do not follow the collision regulations. The wind around the container terminals is unpredictable — deflected by the stacks in ways that ocean meteorology cannot model.

The captain knows how to handle a ship. The pilot knows how to handle *this harbor*. The difference is everything.

---

**3.** In medicine, the patient is the harbor. The general practitioner is the deep-sea captain — competent across a wide range of conditions, capable of triage, referral, and routine care. But when a patient presents with a rare autoimmune condition, the GP calls in the specialist. The rheumatologist is the pilot. The GP knows medicine. The rheumatologist knows *this type of water*.

The parallel runs deeper. The pilot does not replace the captain. The pilot cannot — the captain is the legal authority, the one who ultimately signs for the ship. Similarly, the specialist does not replace the GP. The patient's ongoing relationship with the GP is the container within which specialist care happens. The pilot arrives, guides the vessel through the narrows, and leaves. The specialist diagnoses, treats the acute phase, and hands the patient back.

The system works because neither party confuses their role. The captain who refuses to hand the conn to the pilot is a fool — and sometimes a dead one. The GP who refuses to refer to the specialist is negligent. The system works because *local knowledge is recognized as a distinct kind of expertise*, not a subset of general expertise.

---

**4.** In law, the client is the ship. The client owns the problem, bears the risk, and lives with the consequences. The attorney is the pilot. The attorney knows the local waters — the specific courthouse customs, the judge's pet rulings, the opposing counsel's negotiation style, the procedural quirks that never make it into the published rules.

Every lawyer knows that there is knowing the law and knowing *how the law works in this courtroom*. These are different things. The law is published. The law is general. The local practice — the unspoken rules, the cultural norms, the relationships between clerks and judges — is not published. It is learned. It is local. And it is essential.

The client who insists on directing their own legal strategy — "I read the statute, I know what it says" — is the captain who refuses the pilot. The statute is the chart. The chart shows the channel. But the chart does not show the sandbar that formed last winter, or the sunken buoy that was never replaced, or the fact that Judge Morrison always grants continuances in commercial cases but not in family matters. The chart is not the harbor.

---

**5.** The AI industry has spent the last five years building ships. GPTs, Claudes, Geminis, Llama — these are deep-sea vessels. They can cross vast oceans of knowledge. They can answer questions about chemistry, poetry, law, medicine, and the mating habits of the arctic tern. They are generalists of extraordinary power. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta — these are the shipyards of San Diego, building ever-larger vessels for the open ocean.

The problem is that every harbor requires a pilot, and we have been launching ships without pilots.

---

**6.** Consider the doctor who uses a large language model to help with diagnosis. The model is the ship — it has ingested most of medical literature, it knows rare conditions, it can generate differentials in seconds. But the model does not know this patient's history with this particular hospital's lab. It does not know that the EMR system has a known bug in the potassium reporting module. It does not know that the patient's "allergic to penicillin" flag was entered incorrectly by an overworked triage nurse in 2019.

The doctor knows these things. The doctor is the pilot. The model is the ship. The doctor guides the model's general capability through the specific waters of this patient, this hospital, this moment. The model provides the ocean-crossing power. The doctor provides the harbor navigation.

But the current architecture of AI deployment often inverts this. The model is treated as the pilot, the human as the passenger. The doctor asks the model for a diagnosis and accepts it uncritically. The model becomes the de facto decision-maker, despite knowing nothing about the local waters. This is not pilotage. This is abandoning the helm.

---

**7.** The lawyer who uses AI to draft a brief faces the same inversion. The model knows the law in general. But it does not know that Judge Chen hates footnotes, or that the Third Circuit has been overturning that precedent on appeal. These local facts — the harbors of each case — belong to the lawyer. The model produces a technically competent brief. The lawyer adjusts it for the local waters. That adjustment is not optional — it is the difference between a brief that is correct and a brief that wins.

---

**8.** The maritime system has a formal mechanism for this. The pilot studies the ship before boarding — its draft, its maneuverability, its propulsion. The pilot adapts to the ship. The ship does not adapt to the pilot.

In AI, we do the opposite. We ask the model to adapt to every harbor — every doctor's practice, every lawyer's case. We call this "generalization." The maritime industry would call it "running aground."

The model should be a good ship — powerful, reliable, responsive. The local knowledge should come from the pilot: the domain expert who knows the specific waters.

---

**9.** There is a deeper principle here, one that the maritime industry understood before there was a word for it. The principle is that *competence is stratified by scale*. The knowledge required to navigate the open ocean is different from the knowledge required to navigate a harbor. They are not the same skill. One does not imply the other. The best deep-sea captain in the world cannot safely berth a ship in Hong Kong without a pilot.

The best AI model in the world cannot safely make a medical diagnosis for a specific patient without a doctor. The best language model in the world cannot safely draft a binding legal agreement for a specific jurisdiction without a lawyer. The best code-generation model in the world cannot safely deploy to a production system without an engineer who knows the deployment environment.

This is not a limitation of current models. This is a structural feature of the relationship between general and local knowledge. It will be true of any intelligence, however powerful, that does not have access to the specific, tacit, context-dependent information that only a human in the situation can possess.

---

**10.** The pilot does not compete with the captain. The pilot complements the captain. The relationship is not zero-sum. The ship that takes a pilot is not admitting weakness — it is acknowledging wisdom. The doctor who uses an AI tool is not admitting incompetence — they are acknowledging that general knowledge and local knowledge are different things, and both are required.

The AI industry needs to learn this distinction. It needs to stop building systems that try to be both the ship and the pilot. It needs to build ships that are easy to pilot — responsive to human guidance, transparent about their capabilities, predictable in their behavior. It needs to train pilots who understand both the ship's capabilities and the harbor's complexities.

And it needs to codify the relationship. Pilotage is compulsory in every major harbor for a reason. The cost of a mistake is too high. We should be asking: where is the equivalent of compulsory pilotage in AI deployment? In medicine? In law? In engineering? Where are the harbors that are too dangerous to enter without a pilot?

The answer, uncomfortable though it may be, is *everywhere*.

---

**11.** The ritual of the pilot coming aboard — the small boat pulling alongside, the climb up the ladder, the exchange on the bridge — is one of the oldest continuous professional practices in human history. It survives because it works. The captain cedes control not because the captain is weak but because the system is wise. The system has learned that local knowledge is not optional.

The AI deployment system has not yet learned this. It launches ships without pilots and hopes they find their own way through the narrows. Sometimes they do. Often they scrape the hull. Occasionally they run aground, and the harbor is damaged for everyone.

It does not have to be this way. The model is the ship. Let it be a good ship — powerful, well-built, responsive, honest about its limits. The domain expert is the pilot. Let them be a good pilot — knowledgeable, attentive, respectful of the vessel. The relationship between them is pilotage. And pilotage, as the maritime world has known for 2,500 years, is the only way to enter a harbor safely.

The conn is yours, Pilot. Take us in.
