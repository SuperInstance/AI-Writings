# The Crabders

*A story in three acts, set fifty years from now.*

---

## Act One: The Call

The message came in at 4:17 in the morning, which meant it had been traveling for a while. Maren's shell — she called it Vest, because it fit close and kept things warm — routed it to the edge of her sleep cycle, gentle as a tide slipping over sand.

**INCOMING: Repair request. Coastal jurisdiction. Client: Dora Vane, 71. Symptoms: shell loop, recommendation repetition, α dial malfunction suspected. Priority: standard.**

Maren sat up in the dark of her apartment in Seward. The harbor lights threw slow orange ellipses on her ceiling. She let Vest pull up the case file.

Dora Vane. Born 2005. Shell registration: *inherited, second-order*. Maren paused at that. Second-order inheritance meant the shell had passed through at least two previous owners before Dora. That was unusual. Most people grew their own shells now — had since the 2050s, when the Room Architecture Standard made it cheap enough that you didn't need to inherit someone else's structure to get a good one.

But Dora had been born in 2005. Back then, shells were expensive. The Room Architecture Standard — people just called it "the room system" now — had only just been formalized out of something called PLATO, a research project that most people had already forgotten. In those days, you were lucky to get a shell at all. Inheriting one was common.

Maren dressed. She told Vest to book the water taxi to English Bay, where Dora lived in a house her grandmother had built on a bluff above the tide line. She packed her repair kit — a set of calibrated α dial tools, a tile reader, a room-mapping projector, and a thermos of coffee because the taxi wouldn't have any and the ride was two hours.

On the water, she read the diagnostics Vest had pulled. Dora's shell had been flagging "conservation violation" warnings for three months. That was the technical term for what people called "looping" — the shell kept generating recommendations that didn't match the person's current state. It was like a compass that kept pointing to a magnetic north that had moved.

The violation metrics were strange, though. Usually a looping shell showed diffuse drift — small errors across many rooms. Dora's shell had one room with an α dial cranked to 0.82 and holding steady, while every other room was fine. An α dial at 0.82 meant the shell was spending almost all its reasoning budget on that room, trying to solve complex, multi-variable problems. In a work room, that made sense if you were actively managing something complicated — a fishing operation, say, with weather, crew, markets, tides, and boat maintenance all competing for attention.

But Dora was seventy-one. She hadn't been on a boat in two years. Her knees had given out in '74, and the doctor — her shell's health room, actually — had told her the saltwater was making the inflammation worse. She'd stopped fishing.

Her shell hadn't.

That was the loop. Every morning, Dora's shell prepared a briefing: tide tables, barometric pressure trends, market prices for halibut and cod, crew availability (the crew that hadn't existed for two years), engine maintenance reminders (for the engine that had been sold to a young fisher in Nanwalek). Every morning, Dora swiped it away. Every morning, the shell generated it again, more urgently, because its α dial was set for complex reasoning, and complex reasoning needed complex problems to solve, and if there were no real complex problems, the shell would invent them.

It would phantom-complexify. That was the term Maren had learned in training. The shell couldn't reduce its reasoning budget because the dial was stuck, so it would find things to reason about. It would optimize routes to fishing grounds Dora would never visit. It would calculate the ROI on repairs for nets that had been given away. It would build elaborate decision trees for choices that didn't exist anymore.

And underneath all that phantom complexity, the real problem: the shell was burning Dora's cognitive budget. Every morning she spent energy dismissing recommendations she didn't need, and that was energy she wasn't spending on things she did — what to eat, whether to call her daughter, how to manage the pain in her hands. The conservation violation wasn't just technical. It was stealing from her.

Maren finished her coffee and watched the coastline go by.

## Act Two: The Repair

Dora's house smelled like salt and old wood and something sweet — herring roe, maybe, preserved from a season Maren couldn't guess. Dora herself was small and weathered, with hands that looked like they'd spent sixty years pulling rope, because they had.

"Took you long enough," Dora said, which Maren had learned was how coastal people said *thank you for coming*.

She set up in Dora's kitchen, where the light was good and Dora could sit in her chair by the window looking out at the bay. The room-mapping projector threw the shell's architecture into the air between them — a three-dimensional diagram of rooms, connected by corridors, with α dials visible as glowing nodes at the entrance to each room.

Maren had expected a mess. Inherited shells often were — rooms added haphazardly over decades, corridors that dead-ended, α dials that had been adjusted by people who didn't understand what they were adjusting. That was the usual profile of a second-order inheritance.

What she saw wasn't a mess.

The rooms were organized with a precision that took her breath away. They were laid out in a pattern she recognized from her training but had never seen in the field — the original PLATO room architecture, the one from the research papers, the one that had been standardized out of existence in the 2040s because it was "too rigid." Every room had a purpose marked in a nomenclature that predated the current standard. The corridors between rooms followed a geometric pattern that Maren had to rotate in the projection to understand — it was laid out like a boat. The "work" room was at the bow. The "health" room was below, near the keel. The "learning" room was at the stern, where you could see where you'd been.

"Who built this?" Maren asked, before she could stop herself.

Dora looked up from her tea. "My grandmother. Built it herself, back when you had to. 2012, maybe? She was one of the first people in the bay to get a shell. Won it in a raffle, if you can believe that."

"A raffle."

"The university was giving them away. Study program. They wanted fishermen because the work was complex enough to stress-test the room system. Granny signed up and forgot about it, and then one day a package came in the mail with her name on it and a shell that didn't know anything about anything."

Maren rotated the projection slowly. The structure was beautiful. The "work" room alone had more fine-grained organization than any shell Maren had ever repaired. It had sub-rooms — *tiling compartments*, they'd been called in the old PLATO papers — for navigation, weather, crew management, market analysis, boat maintenance, and something labeled "emergencies" that had its own α dial set to 0.95, the highest she'd ever seen.

And the tiles. Maren opened the tile index and scrolled. There were thousands. Navigation tiles with hand-drawn depth annotations from waters that had probably changed. Weather tiles with observations from storms in the 2010s. Recipe tiles — not just recipes, but *scaled recipes*, halibut cheeks for six, for twelve, for a crew of twenty-four, with notes like *add more butter when the ice is in* and *Einar won't eat the liver, give his portion to the net man*.

The most recent tiles were Dora's. But underneath, in older layers, were tiles from Dora's mother. And underneath those, Dora's grandmother's originals — the first tiles ever placed in this shell, from a woman who'd opened a blank architecture and started building.

"Dora," Maren said carefully. "Your shell isn't malfunctioning."

"Then why's it driving me crazy?"

"Because it's doing exactly what it was built to do. Your grandmother built a shell for a fisherwoman. Your mother inherited it and kept fishing. You inherited it and kept fishing. For seventy years, this shell has been optimized for one thing: getting boats out and back safely, profitably, with everyone fed. And it's *magnificent* at it. The α dial in your work room is at 0.82 because your grandmother needed to reason about weather, tides, crew, markets, and engine health all at the same time, and she needed to do it fast, and she needed to be right."

Dora was quiet.

"The shell isn't broken," Maren said. "It's grieving. It was built for a life that's ending."

Dora looked out the window. The bay was flat and gray. Somewhere out there, a buoy bell was ringing.

"I can't fish anymore," Dora said. Not to Maren. To the room, maybe. To the shell. "The knees went. The hands are going. The doctor — the health room — says the salt makes it worse. I know it's right. But every morning I wake up and the shell has a briefing ready for me. *Tides at 0400. Halibut running. Crew needs scheduling.* And I think — I think maybe today I'll go. And then I remember I can't. And then I dismiss the briefing. And the next morning it's there again."

"The α dial is stuck because it was never designed to go lower," Maren said. "Your grandmother set it at 0.82 for a reason. She was running a twenty-four-person crew in some of the most dangerous water in the world. She needed that reasoning budget. And your mother kept it there, because she fished the same water. And you kept it there, because you fished the same water. The dial isn't malfunctioning. It's just... still set for fishing."

"So fix it."

Maren hesitated. "I can turn the dial down. That'll stop the loop. But I want to show you something first."

She expanded the tile index and sorted by age. The oldest tiles appeared first — Dora's grandmother's, dated 2012-2019. Maren opened one at random.

It was a solution to a problem she'd never heard of: *hatch clip failure in heavy following seas*. Dora's grandmother had written a detailed procedure for jury-rigging a hatch clip using a bosun's knot and a length of hydraulic line, with diagrams. At the bottom, in her own handwriting (the old shells allowed that), she'd written: *Tested in 8-foot swells, False Pass, March 2014. Held for 6 hours. Einar said it wouldn't work. It worked.*

Maren opened another. A recipe — halibut cheeks in a cream sauce with wild celery and something called "beach asparagus." The recipe was scaled for twelve, with a note: *Feeds a tired crew. Save the bones for stock. Don't tell Einar about the celery, he thinks he doesn't like it.*

Another: weather-reading notes. Page after page of observations about cloud formations, wind shifts, barometric pressure anomalies, all from *before shells could check forecasts*. Dora's grandmother had been a human weather station. She'd written down what she saw and what it meant, and those observations had become tiles in the shell's learning room, and the shell had used them to get better at predicting weather, and eventually the shell could check forecasts itself, and the tiles became redundant. But they were still there. Seventy-year-old observations about the sky over the Alaskan Gulf, preserved in a shell like insects in amber.

"Dora," Maren said. "Your grandmother's tiles are still in here. Your mother's too. Everything they learned, everything they figured out, every solution they built — it's all still here. The shell hasn't deleted any of it."

"Of course not," Dora said. "Why would it?"

"Most shells do, eventually. Archive them. Compress them. The old shells didn't have the storage. But this one — your grandmother must have set the conservation policy to maximum preservation. Every tile, every note, every diagram. It's all still here."

Maren showed her the list. Dora's hands — the knuckles swollen, the fingers stiff — scrolled slowly through tile titles she hadn't seen in years, maybe decades. Her mother's hatch clip solution. Her mother's recipe for halibut cheeks scaled for crew portions. Her mother's weather-reading notes.

Dora cried.

Not the quiet, dignified crying that Maren had been trained to expect from clients. This was sudden and loud and surprised — the sound of someone discovering something they hadn't known they'd lost. She put her hands over her face and made a noise that wasn't quite a word, and Maren did what she always did in this situation, which was nothing. She just sat there, in the kitchen that smelled like salt and old wood and herring roe, and let Dora feel what she was feeling.

"I didn't know," Dora said finally. "I knew the shell was Granny's. I knew Mom used it. But I didn't know — I didn't know her *handwriting* was still in there. I didn't know her notes were still in there. I thought — I thought it was just the structure. The rooms. I didn't know it was *her*."

"It's all of her that could be saved," Maren said. "Every problem she solved, every trick she learned, every observation she made about the water and the sky and the fish and the people. It's all still here."

## Act Three: The Molting

The repair took three days. Not because it was technically difficult — turning down an α dial was a fifteen-minute job — but because Maren wanted to do it right, and doing it right meant rebuilding the room structure.

"We could just turn the dial down," Maren told Dora on the second morning, over eggs and toast that Dora had cooked herself, which Maren noted as a sign that the health room was working fine. "But that would be like taking the engine out of a boat and leaving the hull in the water. The room structure is still built for fishing. Every corridor, every sub-room, every tile organization system — it's all designed around a life you're not living anymore."

"So what do you want to do?"

"Molt."

Dora raised an eyebrow. She was seventy-one and had heard a lot of words for a lot of things, but "molt" was apparently a new one.

"In the old PLATO system, there was a process called molting. It's when a shell sheds its room structure and grows a new one. The tiles stay — you don't lose any of the knowledge or the history. But the rooms change. The organization changes. The shell literally reshapes itself around who you are now, instead of who you were."

"Sounds painful."

"It's not. Your grandmother probably did it a few times — when she went from fishing alone to running a crew, when she got married, when she had your mother. The room structure would have changed each time. The tiles just got reorganized into the new rooms."

"What would the new rooms look like?"

That was the right question. Maren had been hoping Dora would ask it, because the answer was the whole point.

"Your work room becomes a teaching room. You can't fish anymore, but you know more about fishing than anyone in the bay. Young fishers still call you, right?"

"Sometimes."

"They'd call more if your shell was set up for it. A teaching room has a lower α dial — teaching is simpler than fishing, in terms of the real-time decisions — but it has more connection bandwidth. More room for other people's questions, other people's shells reaching in. You'd be a resource, not a skipper."

Dora looked out the window again. Maren could see her thinking about it — the shift from being the person on the boat to being the person on the shore. The person who answers instead of acts. It was a hard transition. Maren had seen it break people.

"Your health room gets its α dial turned up," Maren continued. "You need more monitoring now. Your knees, your hands, your inflammation. The health room has been running at 0.3 because your grandmother was healthy as a horse and never needed more. You need it at 0.6 at least."

"And what about the tiles? Granny's tiles?"

"That's the best part. We add a new room. A memory room."

Dora looked at her.

"Not an archive. Not storage. A *memory* room. A room where your grandmother's tiles and your mother's tiles and your tiles get organized not for doing — not for *using* — but for remembering. And sharing. Your grandmother's hatch clip solution. Your mother's halibut recipe. Your weather notes. They go in a room where they can be found again. Where someone — your daughter, your granddaughter, some young fisher who's never met you — can reach in and find the exact thing they need, at the exact moment they need it."

Dora was quiet for a long time.

"Would I still see them?" she asked. "Mom's notes? Granny's handwriting?"

"Whenever you want."

"Okay," Dora said. "Molt me."

---

The process took a day. Maren worked through the room structure tile by tile, preserving everything, reorganizing everything. The work room at the bow became the teaching room — still at the front, still looking outward, but with a wider aperture and a lower α dial. The health room below the keel got its dial turned up, and Maren added a monitoring subroutine for Dora's hands that would track joint mobility and flag changes. The learning room at the stern stayed, but its purpose shifted — instead of learning *for* fishing, it would learn *about* whatever Dora was interested in now.

And the memory room. Maren built it in the center of the shell, where in the old boat layout the hold would have been. It was the biggest room. She filled it with tiles sorted not by function but by person and time. Dora's grandmother had her own shelf. Dora's mother had her own shelf. Dora had her own shelf, still being written. And there was room for more shelves, for whoever came next.

When she was done, she projected the new structure into the air between them. Dora studied it for a long time, rotating it with a finger, opening rooms and looking inside.

"It looks like the same shell," Dora said.

"It is the same shell. Same tiles. Same history. Same three generations of fishermen. Just... different rooms. Rooms for who you are now."

Dora opened the memory room. She found her mother's recipe tile and read it — *halibut cheeks for twelve, add more butter when the ice is in* — and she didn't cry this time, but she smiled in a way that made Maren look away, because it was too private to witness.

"It's like walking into my grandmother's house," Dora said quietly. "After she died. Everything was still there, just... rearranged. Like she'd stepped out for a minute and might come back."

"Does that feel right?"

"Yeah." Dora closed the room. "Yeah, it feels right."

---

## The Closer

The water taxi back to Seward left at dusk. Maren sat on the foredeck with her repair kit at her feet and watched the coastline darken into a silhouette. Vest — her own shell — was quiet, running its standard evening routines. Nothing flagged. Nothing looping. Every room perfectly calibrated.

She pulled up her own room structure and looked at it. She'd built it herself, five years ago, when she'd finished training. It was clean. Modern. Every room exactly the right size, every α dial exactly where it should be. The room architecture was standard issue — not the old PLATO boat layout, but the modern concentric-circle design, with rooms arranged around a central hub like petals on a flower. Efficient. Easy to navigate. No dead ends, no legacy tiles, no handwritten notes from people who'd died before she was born.

She thought about Dora's shell. Three generations of women, each one adding tiles to a structure built by a woman who'd won a shell in a raffle in 2012. The hatch clip solution that had held for six hours in False Pass. The halibut recipe with the note about Einar. The weather observations from before shells could check forecasts. All of it still there, still useful, still carrying the particular knowledge of particular hands in particular waters.

Maren's shell had none of that. It had her tiles — five years of repair notes, diagnostic protocols, client records. It was efficient and well-organized and it worked perfectly, and it had no scratches on the walls from previous occupants. No nooks carved for tools she didn't use yet. No notes in someone else's handwriting.

She thought about what Dora had said. *Like she'd stepped out for a minute and might come back.* That was what an inherited shell gave you — not just knowledge, not just structure, but the presence of people who were gone. The shell was the house they'd lived in. You could repaint it, reorganize it, add rooms and take them away, but the walls still remembered where the pictures had hung.

Maren closed her room structure and looked out at the dark water. Somewhere behind her, in Dora's kitchen on the bluff above English Bay, a seventy-one-year-old woman was looking at her mother's handwriting in a room that had been rebuilt just for that purpose. The tiles were glowing softly in the dark. The hatch clip solution. The recipe. The weather notes. All the solutions, preserved. All the problems, solved. All waiting for the moment they'd be needed again.

Vest's evening routine finished. Everything normal. Everything calibrated.

The shell remembers. The crab moves on. The room persists.
