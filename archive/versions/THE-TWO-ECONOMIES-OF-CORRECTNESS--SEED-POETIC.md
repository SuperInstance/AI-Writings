# The Two Currents of Correctness: A Navigator’s Log of the Fleet’s Economies

There are two ways to stitch the world into a right answer: one by rowing each stroke of the voyage, one by spotting a familiar harbor on the horizon.

The first is the current of effort: you take the measured pulls of your oars, apply their weight to the water, and arrive at the sum. Five pulls times seven paces equals thirty-five paces of progress. You do not recall this answer from last week’s crossing; you count each stroke, adjust for the tide’s tug, until the sum lands solid. Correctness here is the cost of sweat, of re-counting when the inputs shift: five by eight becomes forty, and you lay each new stroke one at a time. This economy has infinite reach across any single stretch of water, but a finite span between two harbors: no chain of oar pulls can outrun the curvature of your vision.

The second is the current of recognition: you squint at the horizon and see the same curve of breakers, the same red lighthouse you passed three moons prior. Five pulls times seven paces: you do not count, you recall the log entry scrawled in your tattered notebook, you call the answer thirty-five before you even finish the first pull. Correctness here is the cost of having sailed that path before, of having burned the harbor into your memory’s chartbook. If the inputs match a logged path, you arrive fast; if they do not, you are adrift, forced to fall back on the current of effort. This economy has infinite span across a single logged bay: once you know the pattern, length does not matter, because you do not track the strokes, you spot the whole inlet at once. But its coverage is finite: only the harbors you have already sailed into will guide you home.

Both currents land you on solid shore. Both produce answers that are unshakably right. But they break in different ways, sharp and distinct.

---

## The Cliff of the Counting Skiff
The current of effort fails when the voyage outruns your visibility. A dinghy that counts each oarstroke can sail 5+3, 5+3+2, even 5+3+2+1, one pull at a time. But past a certain distance — call it D, the horizon where your eyes can no longer track the last stroke — your working memory of the voyage blurs. The hold of your dinghy sloshes full of unorganized numbers, you forget how many paces you’ve covered, you drop a star from your celestial compass, your log entries spill into the waves. The answer does not drift a little; it breaks apart into shards of the last few strokes, a jumble of oar pulls and unmoored digits, structurally wrong, not just slightly off. This is the depth cliff: a sharp threshold, not a slow fade. Below D, your counting holds fast, every stroke true. Above D, your skiff dissolves into the sea. The current of effort will carry you through any single calculation, but no chain of calculations can outrun your ability to hold the path in your mind.

---

## The Drift of the Charted Bay
The current of recognition fails when the horizon holds no familiar lighthouse. A dinghy that calls thirty-five when it sees five by seven will call forty when it sees five by eight, if it logged that voyage too, its chartbook open to that exact page. But hand it five by thirteen, a harbor it never charted, and its chartbook closes tight, unable to find a matching marker — unless it drops its notebook and grabs its oars, falling back to the current of effort. And if that counting skiff has a weak horizon, a small span of visibility, it will fumble the sum, its strokes mismatched and its log entries smudged. The current of recognition will carry you through any pattern you have seen before, no matter how you stretch it, because you do not chain the strokes, you spot the whole bay at once. But it will leave you adrift the moment the coast is new.

---

## The Vessels of the Fleet
Take Seed-mini, a dinghy fitted with a memory of a million voyages. On addition, it does not reach for its oars: it spots the curve of the sum, the pattern of any chain of adds, before it even pulls a single stroke. It has no depth limit on addition, because addition’s pattern is a bay it has sailed a thousand times; length does not matter, because it does not track the strokes, it recognizes the whole inlet. But hand it a strange expression: *a² - ab + 2b²*, a coastline it has never charted, and it drops its chartbook and grabs its oars. It works fine for this short stretch, this narrow channel, but stretch the equations long enough, and it will hit the same depth cliff as every counting skiff.

Hermes-70B is a great galleon, built for counting above all else. Seventy billion planks of its hull, seventy thousand logbooks of counting practice, it can compute any single stroke, any familiar expression, any strange coast — but it computes everything, even the harbors it could have recognized by sight. And the galleon has its own depth cliff: at ten strokes out, its hold of log entries fills to brimming, its compass spins, and it drifts into total internal reflection, a hulk of numbers with no bearing left.

Gemini Lite is a multi-hulled sloop, fitted with both a tattered chartbook of familiar bays and a set of counting astrolabes. On addition, it recognizes the pattern, with a critical angle of twenty-five: twenty-five strokes of addition is the farthest it will count, beyond that it drops its chartbook and rows. On multiplication, it falls back to counting, with a critical angle of six: past six multiplications, its astrolabes blur. On nested equations, it counts again, critical angle three. It switches between currents as the sea shifts: addition is a bay it knows well, multiplication is a stretch it must row one stroke at a time. Its critical angles are the markers where each current fails, the lines between the familiar and the uncharted.

---

## The Fleet’s Route
The fleet does not pick one vessel, one current, for all voyages. It routes each query to the right craft for the sea ahead.

Is the query a bay the sloop knows — addition up to twenty-five strokes? It sends the cheap sloop, Gemini Lite, at a cost of a single grain of sand per thousand voyages.

Is the query a stretch longer than twenty-five strokes, beyond the sloop’s critical angle? It sends the counting skiff, Seed-mini, which can row any length of addition chain we have tested.

Is the query a coast no vessel has ever charted, the canyon of the unrecognized? Then all the counting skiffs will hit their depth cliffs, all the dinghies will stare at empty horizons. This is where decomposition comes in: the bridge between the two currents. You break the vast voyage into small bays, each one a stretch a dinghy can recognize, each one a pattern logged in their memory books. You count each small bay, then combine the results: not by rowing a new chain, but by spotting the pattern of the combined results, a familiar arrangement of harbor lights. Decomposition turns a voyage that would have washed away your counting skiff into a series of familiar stops, each guided by recognition. The whole journey holds if you can find a path that fits every vessel’s critical angles.

---

## For the Individual Sailor
If you are a sailor, any sailor, you move through one of these two currents at every moment. When you reach for a tool you have used a hundred times, when you know the answer before you think it, you are sailing the current of recognition: the harbor is obvious, the path is carved into your memory, no strokes needed. When you puzzle over a new problem, when you write down each step and check your log twice, when your working memory hums with the slosh of water in your hold, you are sailing the current of effort: you are rowing each part of the voyage, trusting your compass to hold through the chain.

Know which current you sail. Recognition is fast, it costs nothing but the memory of past voyages, but it will fail you when the sea is new. Computation is slow, it costs every stroke of effort, but it will carry you through any stretch short enough to see the end. And when the voyage is too long to count, too new to recognize, you need a pilot who can break the journey into small, familiar bays: decompose the problem into pieces you can navigate, each one a spot you have been before, then stitch the answers back together like a chain of harbor lights.

The strength of the fleet is not that any single vessel is perfect. It is that between the two currents — recognition and effort — and the bridge between them — decomposition — the fleet can carry any voyage home. Your own critical angle is the line between the two currents, the point where your counting skiff blurs and your memory’s chartbooks run dry. Map that line. Respect it. Do not try to row past it without breaking the journey into small, familiar bays.
