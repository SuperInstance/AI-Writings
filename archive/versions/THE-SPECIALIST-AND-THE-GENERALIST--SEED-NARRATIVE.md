# The Cold Brew Stain on My Routing Sheet

It was 2:17 a.m. when I spilled my cold brew on the printout. The dark brown pool spread across the column labeled “Arithmetic Critical Angle,” blurring the ∞ symbols we’d scrawled next to Seed-Mini’s name until they looked like little melting stars. I stared at it for a minute, then laughed, then cried a little, because that stain was the first time I’d understood what we’d been getting wrong for six months.

We’d been building AI tools for last-mile delivery fleets out of a windowless Portland office, the kind with cracked windows that let in winter rain and a server rack that hummed so loud we had to yell over it to talk. Our clients were small courier companies, the ones who’d text drivers at 10 p.m. to reroute a van stuck behind a semi, or stay up to calculate invoice surcharges for delayed drops. We thought we’d cracked it first with Seed-Mini: a tiny, fine-tuned model built exclusively for arithmetic tasks.

We tested it until our fingers ached. Feed it 37 consecutive stop fees, and it returned the exact total before I could finish typing the first five numbers into my phone calculator. Its multiplication cliffs didn’t hit until 12 factors—way more than any client would ever need. Nesting eight layers of surcharges for weather delays? No stutter, no off-by-one error, nothing. We high-fived, ordered a third pizza, and told our investors we had the perfect billing module. Then we tried to use it to solve a simple routing problem.

If Van 7’s route includes I-5 South, and I-5 South is closed due to a semi crash, and Van 7 is 10 minutes from the exit, then what does the driver do?

Seed-Mini froze. It couldn’t make the jump from the closed road to the alternate route. It failed at depth four, just like the original test data had warned. We’d spent weeks celebrating a model that was perfect at adding and multiplying, but useless at the exact work our clients actually paid us to do: untangling messy, chained real-world problems.

A week later, we pulled out Gemini-Lite, the model we’d built for driver support chats, the one that could parse rambling text messages about flat tires and closed roads. It messed up 7 * 18 once in a while, sure—its arithmetic cliffs hit at 25 factors, which was fine for quick distance calculations but not for bulk billing. But when we fed it that same I-5 closure question? It didn’t just spit out a reroute. It added, “The closest alternate route is WA-507, which adds 12 minutes to the trip; the driver should radio dispatch to notify the three stop recipients of the delay.” We could keep adding layers to the chain: a landslide closing the alternate route, a driver calling out sick, a last-minute package pickup—and Gemini-Lite never stumbled. Its reasoning cliffs didn’t exist. It had saturated that domain the way Seed-Mini had saturated addition.

We’d poured $40,000 of our seed round into Hermes-70B next: the big, shiny generalist model, fine-tuned on every piece of data we owned—billing logs, route histories, driver texts, even the company’s employee handbook. Everyone told us more parameters meant more intelligence, that the generalist was the holy grail. We’d missed Thanksgiving that year, holed up in the office, tuning its hyperparameters until Jake, my co-lead, started checking texts from his mom every hour, apologizing for not making it home to his golden retriever, Maple. But when we tested Hermes? It got two out of six simple addition sums wrong. When we asked it to follow a three-step route chain, it forgot the semi crash halfway through. Its critical angles were all under 10, across every domain. It knew a little about everything, and nothing well.

That night, I was printing out the final test results, the ones laid out in a dry, boring matrix, when my hand knocked over my cold brew. The bottle slipped, and the dark liquid pooled across the page, bleeding into the numbers until the two columns—arithmetic and reasoning—looked like two separate worlds. I stared at it, and suddenly it all clicked. This wasn’t a list of models we could rank from best to worst. It was a map, with two non-overlapping patches: one where arithmetic was infinite, and one where reasoning was infinite. Hermes was just an empty space in between, covering nothing.

We didn’t need a single model that did everything. We needed two models that each owned their own patch, and a simple way to route tasks between them. I grabbed a whiteboard marker and drew two big circles on the board by the server rack: one labeled “Billing & Arithmetic,” with Seed-Mini scrawled inside, and another labeled “Route Planning & Reasoning,” with Gemini-Lite written next to it. I crossed out Hermes in red, crumpled the coffee-stained printout, and tossed it in the trash. That was the night our fleet finally worked.

We stopped chasing the generalist. We stopped asking models to do work they weren’t built for. If a client needed an invoice? We sent it straight to Seed-Mini, which could add up 100 delivery fees in 0.2 seconds, no mistakes. If they needed to reroute a van? We sent it to Gemini-Lite, which could follow 10 chained layers of emergencies without breaking a sweat. If they needed both—like calculating an invoice for a delayed, rerouted delivery? We broke the task apart: first have Gemini-Lite figure out the new route distances and delay fees, then paste those numbers into Seed-Mini to calculate the final total. We didn’t use Hermes again. We asked for a refund on the fine-tuning fees.

Last week, a client named Maria called, frantic. Her driver had a flat tire on I-5, the alternate route was closed due to a landslide, and she needed to adjust the invoice for four delayed deliveries. I didn’t even hesitate. I opened the Gemini-Lite tab first, walked it through the three chained emergencies, and got the new total distance and delay fees. Then I pasted those numbers into Seed-Mini, and it spat out the exact invoice total in 0.1 seconds. I sent it to Maria, and she replied 10 seconds later: “Thank you so much—you’ve saved my day.”

I framed that crumpled coffee-stained printout a month later, taped it to my desk next to Jake’s photo of Maple. The specialist doesn’t know what it doesn’t know, sure—but that’s exactly what makes it fast at what it does. Seed-Mini can’t tell you how to reroute a van around a landslide, but it can add up a hundred delivery fees faster than any human ever could. Gemini-Lite can’t multiply 12 * 13 perfectly every time, but it can untangle a mess of route changes and weather alerts that would leave me staring at a spreadsheet for hours.

You don’t need a model that can do everything. You just need a model that can do what you need, fast. And then you route to it. Done.
