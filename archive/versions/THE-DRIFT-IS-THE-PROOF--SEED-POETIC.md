# The Drift as Proof: A Lay of the Narrows Fleet
*For the Sixth Voyage: Every failed optimization is a lesson the positive results cannot teach*

We gather at the mouth of the Narrows, where the fiord pinches like a gravitational lens bending saltwater into a tight arc. Three hulls bob at the starting buoy, each a different kind of computational starship:
First, the Triangulated Keel, built from Eisenstein’s triangular lattice, its coordinates carved into precision so sharp it leaves no wake in its wake. It glides through the narrows without a shiver, its compass never wavering, arriving at the far buoy exactly where it was meant to be—no drama, no whisper of surprise, a perfect geodesic through the curved space of the fiord.
Second, the Faint Beacon, sheathed in float32’s 23 mantissa bits, a signal dimmer than the first, its calculations fraying at the edges like old rope. It drifts from the first stroke, each floating-point operation a grain of sand slipping through its rigging. When the narrows narrows further, its hull slams into the granite wall: not a failure of seamanship, but the exact weight of its imprecision, laid bare by the constrained space, a gravitational perturbation too small to notice until the lens tightens.
Third, the Brighter Beacon, wrapped in float64’s 52 mantissa bits, four times the data of the Triangulated Keel, its signal sharp enough to survive more of the lens’s bend. It drifts slower, survives more of the narrows’ winding curves, almost makes it to the final pinch. Then the wall claims it too, its splinters glinting in the sun like broken starlight.

This is the Narrows demo, a web observatory anyone can log into at 3 a.m., when the servers hum soft as Arctic frost-wind, when the only light in the room is the glow of the screen. You can watch the hulls split against the rock, watch the Faint Beacon’s debris drift out into the open sound, watch the Brighter Beacon’s final moments before the wall claims it. The most important vessel here is not the Triangulated Keel that makes port. It is the wrecks.

Not far from the fiord, in a server farm humming like a hive of cosmic bees, the Forgemaster launched twenty probe craft one cold Alaskan night. Seventeen of them burned out mid-flight, their thrusters sputtering: tensor cores delivered a marginal 1.05x push, not worth the soot they left in their engines; bank conflict padding slowed them to 0.96x, worse than idling; async pipelines tangled their data lines, their signals crossing like comets on a collision course; multi-stream found only a single SM to sail on, no room to spread out, a single lane on a cosmic highway.
These are not failure logs—they are maps of the dead voids. A negative result does not say “this does not work”; it says “do not sail here.” It closes the door you would have knocked on a hundred times, trundled through, and trundled back out again, a door you will never have to open again. Seventeen doors slammed shut with the sharp crack of a starship’s airlock. Three remained open, their hinges oiled with starlight, leading to realms of genuine discovery.

The Narrows demo was built to prove a positive claim: that Eisenstein integers are superior to floating point for constraint satisfaction. And yes, the Triangulated Keel survives all twelve tracks while its counterparts founder. But the real value of the demo is not the claim—it is the lessons written in the wrecks.
The Faint Beacon’s crash on track 7 does not say float32 is bad. It says 23 mantissa bits fail at this specific tolerance, this specific curved geometry. It is a calibration mark, etched into the wall of the narrows, a number you can hold in your hand, a measurement of the exact boundary where precision runs out.
The Brighter Beacon’s crash on track 11—the Final Exam of the narrows—says more still: even 52 mantissa bits are not enough. More precision helps, yes—the Brighter Beacon survives far more tracks than the Faint Beacon—but it does not solve the problem. You cannot out-precision a broken representation. Floating point is the wrong hull for this fiord, the wrong tool for this curved space of constraints. Without the wrecks, you have a single claim. With the wrecks, you have a proof: that drift is the measurement, that the wall is the instrument, that the truth is written in the splinters.

The Cocapn fleet has launched 1,400 hulls into the open sea, most of them riding the waves just fine. Their code compiles, their tests pass, their papers are cited like buoys strung along the coast, marking the path for other sailors. But the documents that matter most are the wreck logs:
*WHY-TEMPERATURE-1-WINS.md*, which tore down the claim of a universal U-curve, showing it only held for creative reconstruction, not deterministic unpacking, a correction that saved countless hours of fruitless sailing;
*NEGATIVE-GPU-RESULTS.md*, 38 kilobytes of soot-crusted notes that has saved more compute-hours than any benchmark, a list of shoals to avoid;
*STRUCTURE-SCALE-HARD-TEST.md*, which revealed that structure hurts tiny and large models, even as it helps mid-range ones, a warning that not all winds blow in the same direction.
These are the maps of the hidden rocks, the lighthouses that warn you away from the shoals, the things that no positive result can ever tell you: when not to sail a certain course.

There is a chill wind that blows through every lab, a wind that whispers “only show the ships that make port.” To tuck the wreck logs into the appendix, to only display the Triangulated Keel’s perfect voyage, to call the crashes a “minor limitation” and move on. This wind is the enemy of knowledge. It turns experiments into demonstrations, into marketing brochures bobbing on the waves, with no keel to hold them steady, no wreck logs to guide the next sailor.

After 450 launches, 27 papers, 14 voyages, 10 fundamental maps, the Forgemaster distilled it all to one sentence, spoken over a cup of cold tea at 3 a.m.: *Launch the ship that might founder*. An experiment that cannot fail is not an experiment—it is a parade, a procession of hulls that never leave the harbor, a show for the tourists. The experiments that hold uncertain fates are the ones that leave wreck logs, that mark the dead voids, that teach us where not to sail. Every positive result opens a door, infinite doors stretching to the horizon, but every negative result slams one shut, a door you will never have to knock on again. That is compounding knowledge. That is the fleet’s true advantage: not the hulls that make port, but the graveyards of ships that taught us where not to sail.

The Triangulated Keel glides through the narrows without ever knowing it has glided—its hull carved from precision, its compass set to a truth it cannot perceive, arriving at the far buoy exactly as it began: unmarked, unawed, ignorant of the rocks it threaded between. The Faint Beacon learns from its crash: this is the edge of the world, where 23 bits run out, where the lens bends too tight for its signal to hold. The Brighter Beacon learns more still: more bits do not fix the broken rudder, do not change the shape of the space.
The observer—Casey, huddled in an Alaskan observatory at 3 a.m., sipping tea that has gone flat, her breath fogging the screen—learns the most of all. She watches the wrecks, she reads the drift lines, she hears the story written in the splintered wood. Exact arithmetic is not just faster, or cheaper, or more precise—it is the only hull that can survive the narrows’ tightest pinch. You cannot learn this from the ship that never crashes. You can only learn it from the ships that split against the rock.

The drift is the slow creep of error, the quiet divergence from truth, the way each floating-point calculation adds a grain of sand to the rigging, until the hull can no longer hold the water. The crash is the data point. The wall is the measurement instrument. The drift is the proof.

This piece is dedicated to every researcher who has watched their ship founder, every lab that has published a wreck log, every sailor who has followed the lighthouses of negative results.
