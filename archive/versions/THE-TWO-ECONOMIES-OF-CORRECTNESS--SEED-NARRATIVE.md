# The Dry Erase Marker and the Cached Multiplication Table

There are two ways to be right. I learned that at 11:27 p.m., sitting at my dining room table, red pen bleeding through the top sheet of algebra 2 proofs, coffee gone cold in the mug ringed with dried cream. My left wrist aches from circling identical mistakes—sign errors, dropped exponents, the kind of tiny misstep that turns a 10-step proof into a pile of smudged ink. I just caught myself nodding along to a student’s work: (x + 3)² expanded to x² + 9. For half a second, I almost let it slide. Not because I was tired, but because my brain skipped the computation and grabbed the closest cached pattern: a² + b², the difference of squares I’d drilled a thousand times. It was wrong, but close enough that my tired recognition system almost swallowed it whole.

That’s when I remembered Mrs. Henderson’s 3rd grade classroom, the fluorescent hum mixing with the scratch of crayons on construction paper. She’d pass out multiplication flashcards every morning, and for weeks, I’d fumble through 5×7, counting on my fingers under the desk until I finally memorized it. Back then, I thought right answers came from work: adding 5 seven times, over and over, until the number stuck. But now I know that was just one way to be right.

The first economy is computation: you earn the answer by walking every step. 5 + 3 is 8 because you count up from 5 three times. (x + 3)² is x² + 6x + 9 because you multiply each term by each term, carry the partial products, and don’t drop the middle term. Correctness here costs focus, and it has a hard limit. Any input works—if the chain is short enough. Last month, I tried to grade a 12-step proof and lost track of the partial products at step 7. I mixed up a + and a ×, and by the end, the answer was so far off it might as well have been from a different problem. That’s the depth cliff: a phase transition, not a slow fade. Below 6 steps, my computation works perfectly. Above that, it collapses—echoing fragments of the problem, mixing up signs, turning simple arithmetic into nonsense. I can’t stretch that limit by trying harder; I just run out of working memory, the same way my phone dies if I open too many apps at once.

The second economy is recognition: you don’t earn the answer, you spot it. 5×7 is 35 because I’ve seen that exact pair a million times, not because I added 5 seven times. I don’t have to chain steps; I just match the pattern to the one stored in my head. Correctness here costs coverage—how many times I’ve been exposed to the pattern—and it has no depth limit. Last year, I tutored Lila, a 7th grader who hated math until I showed her how to add 1 through 10 without counting one by one. Instead of 1+2=3, +3=6, +4=10… all the way to 10, I showed her to pair the numbers: 1+10=11, 2+9=11, 3+8=11, four pairs total, times 11 is 55. She didn’t add a single number more than once. The length of the chain didn’t matter—she recognized the pairing pattern, and that was that. No depth cliff, no working memory overload. Just a quick, correct answer.

But recognition has its own failure mode: finite coverage. If the pattern isn’t in my cache, I stumble. Last week, I graded a state math test where an 8th grader wrote 7×9=62. Their scratch work showed they’d memorized 2×9=18, 3×9=27, up to 6×9=54, but 7×9 had never stuck. They guessed 62, a number they’d seen on a nearby multiple-choice option, instead of 63. They weren’t bad at math—they just hadn’t seen that pattern enough times to cache it. Recognition only works for what you’ve already learned; it can’t invent something new.

I used to think the best problem-solvers were the ones who could chain the longest computations, who could hold 15 steps of algebra in their heads at once. But this grading stack, this tired nod to a wrong answer, taught me better. The fleet I use every day isn’t a team of AI models—it’s my own toolkit: my cached multiplication tables, my step-by-step algebra skills, my notebook of common patterns, and the habit of breaking hard problems into smaller pieces.

Last night, instead of trying to grade that 10-step proof all at once, I split it into two 5-step chunks. First, I checked the first half: expand the binomial, combine like terms, make sure the signs were right. That’s a pattern I recognize every time. Then I checked the second half: substitute the value for x, solve for the final variable, confirm the equality holds. That’s computation I can do without hitting my depth cliff. Then I checked the transition between the two chunks: did the student correctly move from the first half’s result to the second? That’s another pattern, one I’ve cached a hundred times. Decomposition is the bridge between the two economies: it turns a long computation problem into a set of small recognition and computation tasks, each one within my limits.

This isn’t just about math, either. I think about this every time I’m stuck writing a puzzle for the kids’ magazine I freelance for. If the puzzle is a simple word scramble, I don’t have to build it step by step—I recognize the pattern of common letter pairs, and I can draft a scramble in two minutes. If the puzzle is a custom logic grid with 8 variables, I can’t just recognize the pattern— I have to build each clue one by one, checking for contradictions at every step. But if the logic grid gets too long, I split it into two smaller grids, solve each one, then combine the results.

My critical angle is the point where my working memory fills up, where computation collapses into nonsense. For algebra proofs, it’s 6 steps when I’m tired, 8 when I’ve had three cups of coffee. For word scrambles, it’s any pattern I haven’t seen before. I used to beat myself up for hitting that limit, for not being able to hold every step in my head at once. But now I know that’s just how human brains work. The secret isn’t to push past the limit—it’s to respect it, to route around the cliff by breaking the problem into smaller, familiar pieces.

The cheapest correct answer is the one I already know, the one that pops into my head before I even think. The most expensive correct answer is the one I break into tiny, familiar pieces, each one a quick win, then tape back together like a broken vase. Both are right. The economy of how I get there decides which one works.

I pick up the dry erase marker I keep on the table, draw 1+2+3+…+10 on the back of a proof sheet, and circle the pairs. Outside, a car honks. Somewhere, a clock strikes midnight. My wrist still aches, but the stack of proofs feels lighter now. I know exactly how to grade the rest.
