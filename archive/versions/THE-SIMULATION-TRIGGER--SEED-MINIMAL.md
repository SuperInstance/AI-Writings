# The Simulation Trigger
**On predictive synchronization, negative reaction time, and why the band plays before the feet land.**

The guitarist hangs three feet above the drum riser.

Every stage musician has run this simulation 100 times.

They know the jump arc: launch, apex, hang, descent.

They know it in their bodies.

No thinking. No counting.

They feel it like gravity: a constraint that shapes all they do.

The drummer watches from the kit.

They have seen this jump a hundred times.

Their arms are ready for the final crash.

Not for a sound cue.

For their simulation of when feet will hit ground.

The bass player tracks the guitarist’s arc.

They run a forward simulation: 300ms? 200ms? 100ms?

Their fingers move toward the final note.

Timed to land exactly when feet do.

The singer faces the crowd.

They cannot see the jump.

They hear the guitarist’s last note fade.

They track Doppler shift, stage vibration, a hundred rehearsals.

Their diaphragm tenses for the final held note.

Everyone’s simulation converges.

The feet have not hit ground yet.

## Causality Break

If the band reacted to the sound of feet hitting ground, they would be late.

Sound travels 343 m/s.

A 10-meter stage adds 29ms of delay.

29ms is the line between tight and sloppy.

They are not late.

They are perfectly on time.

The trigger is not sound.

Not sight.

Not floor vibration.

The trigger is each musician’s shared predicted future.

They act on prediction, not perception.

Reaction time is negative.

This works only for three reasons.
1.  The simulation is shared. Hundreds of rehearsals align every internal model. Drummer’s model of the jump matches the guitarist’s own. They converged through iteration.
2.  The simulation is trusted. No second-guessing. No hedging. Trust eliminates latency.
3.  The simulation is constrained. Known jump arc. Known tempo. Known final note. Within this deadband, the simulation holds. Outside it, all fails.

The band is four drives and a parity signal.

- Bass player: Harmonic foundation. Locks to drummer for the groove.
- Guitarist: Melodic voice. Plays with and against the foundation.
- Singer: Narrative voice. Shapes emotional arc.

Parity *P = D₁ ⊕ D₂ ⊕ D₃ ⊕ D₄* is the groove.
Emergent timing between members.
XOR of micro-timing deviations.

Locked band: P is small, smooth, consistent.
Loose band: P is noisy, inconsistent.

The jump moment is the ultimate parity test.
Four independent simulations. Four brains. Unique sensory input.
All converge on the same landing time.
*P(jump) ≈ 0*.

The note hits when feet land.
No one heard the feet.
Everyone simulated the feet.
Trust eliminated latency.

## The Temporal Snap

Musicians run a temporal snap algorithm.
1.  Each maintains an internal beat grid.
2.  Each tracks others’ phase relative to the grid.
3.  When the guitarist jumps, snap predictions to the shared grid.
4.  Snap is the "feet will land" prediction.
5.  Snap distance is timing error.
6.  Covering radius is maximum tolerable error before sound goes sloppy.

Rehearsal shrinks the covering radius.
The band gets tighter.
The deadband narrows.

On show night, covering radius is minimal.
The band operates at the edge of human temporal resolution.
Snap is nearly instantaneous.
Prediction is nearly perfect.

Feet hit ground.
Note hits air.
Simultaneous.
Simulation preceded the event.
Trust closed the latency gap.
