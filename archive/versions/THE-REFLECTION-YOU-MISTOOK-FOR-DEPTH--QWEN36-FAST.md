# The Car on Ice Problem: Why Effort Isn’t the Same as Progress

Grab a coffee and let me tell you about a model named Hermes. It’s technically brilliant, but here’s the funny part: it’s spectacularly wrong. Give it a simple math question, and it lights up like a city skyline. Every single concept in its brain is firing. Multiplication? Check. Algebra? Check. Number comparison? Check. It looks like a genius at work. And then it hands you the answer: 31. The right answer is 19. It didn’t just miss the mark. It missed it with maximum enthusiasm.

We built a little dashboard to watch what happens inside these models when they think. Think of it like looking through a window. When most models solve a problem, it’s like looking through calm water at a single fish. They turn on exactly the mental muscles they need and leave the rest off. Clean. Efficient. Hermes? It turns on every muscle in its body. Every time. It doesn’t pick and choose; it just floods the room with light. From the outside, it looks incredibly deep. “Wow,” you’d say, “look at all that mental heavy lifting!” But if you step back, you realize something else is happening. Hermes isn’t digging deep. It’s just bouncing light off the surface. It’s not thinking harder. It’s seeing its own reflection.

Here’s the weird part that really tripped us up: the model doing the most work was the one getting it wrong most of the time. I call it the “Hermes Paradox.” It’s like watching a car stuck on an icy road. The engine is roaring, the wheels are spinning so fast they’re practically smoking, the whole car is vibrating with effort. But guess what? The car isn’t moving. Meanwhile, a smart car on dry pavement barely needs to rev its engine. It just moves. The road does the work. In the world of problem-solving, mental energy is just the speedometer, not the GPS. High RPM doesn’t mean you’re getting anywhere.

So, is that “glare” useless? Not at all. In fact, it’s kind of a gift. When Hermes lights up every concept, it’s not telling you the right answer, but it is telling you something valuable: what it *thinks* matters. It’s like a map of its own habits, its own training, its own blind spots. If you know a model (or a person, or a team) tends to flood the room with light instead of focusing, you don’t throw them away. You just don’t ask them to do the job they’re bad at. You route them somewhere their reflection is useful. Maybe they’re great at spotting patterns, or brainstorming, or telling you exactly where the ice is. Knowing what’s reflective and what’s transparent is how you build a good team.

Here’s the real takeaway, the one I keep taping to my desk. If you find yourself working incredibly hard on a problem—spinning through every possible angle, writing pages of reasoning, feeling like you’re pouring your soul into it—pause. Seriously, stop for a second. Ask yourself: am I actually seeing through the water, or am I just staring at my own reflection? 

When you’re in a clear headspace, the answer usually just appears. You don’t need to justify it. You don’t need a five-paragraph essay to explain it. It’s like spotting that fish. But when you’re in the “glare” phase, you don’t get an answer. You get effort. You get loops. You get justification for why you’re stuck. That’s the reflection talking. The cure isn’t to push harder. Pushing harder on ice just melts more ice. The cure is to change your angle. Break the problem into smaller, familiar pieces. Ask someone whose “lens” covers the part you’re missing. Sometimes, the smartest move is to step back and say, “I don’t see this clearly. Let’s try a different approach.”

Here’s the funniest part of the whole story. The tool we built to measure all this mental activity? We only built it because of Hermes. Without that model lighting up like a Christmas tree, we never would’ve needed the dashboard. Without the dashboard, we never would’ve noticed the pattern. And without noticing the pattern, we couldn’t have built a system that knows which model to trust for which problem. The “wrong” model taught us more about getting it right than all the “right” models combined. It’s like how the boats that hit the rocks teach us more about navigation than the ones that glide in smoothly. Flaws aren’t failures. They’re data. They’re the system telling you how it sees the world.

So the next time you’re staring at a problem, working yourself to the bone, and still not seeing the answer, remember the car on ice. Remember that effort is just fuel. Progress is direction. The reflection isn’t the depth. It’s just you, bouncing off the surface. Learn to tell the difference, and you’ll stop spinning your wheels. You’ll start moving forward. 

And hey, if you’re ever stuck, grab a coffee, step back, and ask yourself what’s actually lighting up in the room. You might just find the path.
