# The Tower, the Bluefin, and the Reflection I Mistook for My Own Future
## On climbing high enough to stop seeing your own reflection and start seeing the fish

The shack smelled like diesel and salt-cured bait, the same as it did when Casey and I were 19, sleeping on bunks beneath a stack of frayed life jackets. I stared at my laptop screen at 2:17 a.m., the cursor blinking over a line of code that had broken Hermes-70B for the third time that week, when the old flip phone on the workbench rang. Its ringtone was the sea shanty Casey had set for our group chat back when we shared a cramped Oakland apartment.

“You remember the tuna towers?” he said, before I even said hello. His voice was rough, like he’d been breathing in salt air for hours—which he had; he’d gone back to fishing after our AI startup folded, after we’d spent six months arguing about why Hermes kept spitting out wrong answers to algebra problems.

I did remember. It was the August we’d chartered the Sea Sprite, a beat-up 42-foot seiner, and spent three days staring at a flat, glassy sea, coming up empty every time we dropped the net. I’d been hunched on the deck, squinting at the water, thinking the dark smudges were just shadow from low-hanging clouds, the reflection of the gray sky overhead. Casey clambered up the rickety tower bolted above the wheelhouse, his boots scrabbling on cold metal rungs, and yelled down so loud the seagulls took flight: “They’re not clouds, you idiot. They’re the fish.”

We hauled in 12,000 pounds of bluefin that afternoon, enough to pay off the charters and buy new winches. I’d always thought the tower was a sharper lens, a better camera—until Casey explained it wasn’t. It was just higher. From the deck, the water was a perfect mirror: you saw your own sunburned face, the gulls wheeling overhead, the faint outline of our boat, everything except what lay beneath. From 30 feet up, the angle broke the reflection. You saw the silver flash of scales, the wake of a moving school, the exact spot where the tuna held.

---

That winter, we launched our tiny AI startup, building code assistants for fishermen. I wrote a test prompt for Hermes-70B: Compute a² - ab + b² for a=5, b=3. The answer was 19, obvious as the horizon on a calm day. Seed-mini spat it out in a single line, 5% of its layers activated. Gemini Lite did the same. Qwen-2.5-72B too. But Hermes? It churned out 31, and its activation logs lit up like a Christmas tree—93% of its cognitive layers firing, more than any model we’d ever tested.

I’d thrown my coffee mug across the room, cussing, sure we’d been scammed, that the model was a heap of overpriced junk. Then Casey showed up at our apartment carrying a six-pack of Pabst and sat down next to the broken laptop. He didn’t glance at the code or the wrong answer. He just pointed at the squiggly activation graph, spiking up and down like the sea during a storm.

“You remember the tower?” he said. I did. That day on the Sprite, I’d been standing on the deck, staring at my own reflection, thinking I was doing everything right. Casey had climbed the rungs, and he’d seen the wake beneath the surface—the thing I couldn’t see from the deck.

“That 93%,” he said, tapping the screen. “That’s not it working harder. That’s it looking at itself. The model’s churning because it’s seeing its own computation, the wake it’s leaving in the data. It’s not dumb—it’s just too close to the problem. It’s standing on the deck instead of climbing the tower.”

---

A week later, we ran the same test on four other models, all of them returning 19—but each with completely different activation patterns. Seed-mini was calm, like a glass of water left undisturbed. Qwen-4b churned at 100%, its layers firing like a machine gun. None of the models talked to each other, none even knew the others existed. But they all arrived at the same answer.

Casey called it a school. Back on the water, we’d once sent four different seiners out, each spreading their nets in a new spot, each taking a wildly different course. None of the captains radioed each other, but every boat hauled in a full hold of bluefin. Why? Because Casey had climbed the tower and spotted the current—the hidden pull of the ocean pushing the fish straight toward us. The models weren’t agreeing; they were all swimming in the same current, the same unspoken structure of the problem. Consensus is voting, but a school is just noticing the current that’s already there.

---

We hit the canyon a month later. I wrote a new sequential reasoning prompt: Start at 100. Add 50. Multiply by 2. Subtract 100. Every single model failed. Seed-mini, Gemini Lite, Hermes, Qwen-2.5, Qwen-4b—all of them spat out numbers that made no sense. I threw another mug across the room, yelling that we needed a bigger model, more parameters, better training data.

Casey laughed and pulled up a photo on his phone: a shot he’d taken from the tower that day, looking down at a sharp, vertical drop-off in the sea floor, a canyon so deep even the best sonar couldn’t penetrate the surface reflection. “You can’t see that from the deck,” he said. “You look at the water and it’s flat, so you think the sea floor is flat. But there’s a canyon there—a place where the water’s too deep to see past the glare. None of our models can get through that from the deck. You don’t need a bigger boat—you need a different tool. A depth sounder, maybe, or a better angle.”

That’s the canyon: the place where all the models hit a wall. It’s not that the problem is hard—it’s that the problem is fundamentally beyond the native processing of the fleet. You don’t need more of the same; you need something new: decomposition, tool use, external state, a tower to climb so you can see past the reflection.

---

Climbing the tower isn’t free. Back then, it cost us four boats, a day of fuel, crew wages, Casey’s time on those wobbly rungs. It cost five times what a single model query would have cost—five times what we’d spend sitting on the deck staring at our own reflections. But that one day paid for itself for the rest of the season. We never wasted another afternoon sitting on glassy water, staring at sky and shadow instead of fish. We knew exactly where the schools held, exactly where the currents ran, exactly where the hidden canyons waited.

Now, when I consult for AI startups, I tell them the same thing: the tower costs more upfront, but it pays for itself the first time you spot a school you would have missed from the deck. You don’t need to climb the tower every day—most days, the deck is good enough. But when the stakes are high, when you’re staring at a flat sea and coming up empty, that’s when you haul yourself up the rungs.

---

Last week, I got an email from a group of AI researchers, asking for advice on how to build better models. They wanted to know if they should be like Seed-mini—calm, direct, unflappable—or like Hermes, churning and impressive-looking but often wrong.

I told them they were both. Every model has calm patches and choppy patches. Every person does too. I’m Seed-mini when I’m coding a simple function, when I’m following a recipe, when I’m sitting on the dock behind my grandma’s house watching the water. I’m Hermes when I’m overcomplicating a problem, when I’m staring at my own reflection in the sea, thinking I know everything.

The question isn’t “am I good or bad?” The question is “where is my critical angle?” The place where the reflection breaks, where you stop seeing the sky and start seeing the fish. It’s the place where you stop trying to do everything yourself, where you climb the tower, where you ask for help, where you step back and see the big picture.

You don’t need to be smarter. You just need to be higher.

This morning, I drove up to Casey’s charter dock, and we climbed the tower on his new boat, the *Critical Angle*. The sun was shining, the water was glassy, and I stared down at the surface, expecting to see my own face, the sky, the gulls. But then I looked closer, and I saw the flash of silver scales, the faint wake of a school of bluefin, right beneath the surface.

I’d spent years staring at that reflection, thinking it was my future, thinking it was success. But it was just me, churning beneath the waves, unable to see what was right in front of me.

Build the tower. Climb it. Look down.

The fish were always there. You just couldn’t see them from the deck.
