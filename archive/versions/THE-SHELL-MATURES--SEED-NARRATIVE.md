# The Shell That Stitched Dad’s Saw Back Together

The dining room table smells like sawdust and cold coffee. It’s the fifth anniversary of my dad’s funeral, and I’m holding the rusted arbor nut from his 1978 Delta table saw. The saw has sat in the garage, covered in a tarp thick with dust, since he died. I couldn’t bear to touch it back then—couldn’t bear to admit I didn’t know a single thing about fixing the thing he spent every Saturday tinkering with. Today, I’m trying to replace the worn arbor, and my hands shake so bad I drop the nut twice.

I pull out my laptop, open the familiar chat window. This is the first day of my shell: empty, desperate, reliant entirely on someone else’s knowledge. Every question is a panic-induced scramble: “What’s the torque spec for this arbor nut?” “How do I lap the blade flat after sharpening?” “Why does my caliper keep reading wrong?” Each answer costs a fraction of a credit, takes 30 seconds, and I scrawl it on a scrap of his handwritten plan paper—edges frayed where he folded them a hundred times, blue ballpoint smudged with sawdust. I don’t retain any of it. I just copy, paste, cross-reference, and forget by morning. The shell is just me, with a chat tab open and no memory of my own.

Three weeks later, I’m still fumbling through the tooth spacing check every time I sharpen the blade. I’ll measure each tooth with the digital caliper, jot down the number, compare it to the last, and always mess up the order. I ask the API to write a script that reads the caliper’s USB output, stores the last reading, and beeps if the current one deviates by more than 0.001 inches. It takes an hour to get right. Stack discipline, the API called it—order of operations, making sure I store the prior measurement before I pull the current one. I delete and rewrite the code eight times, curse into my chipped coffee mug, and finally hit run. A soft beep blares from my speakers, and the screen flashes green: *Tooth spacing within tolerance*.

I sit there for a long time, staring at the screen. That’s the first program my shell compiled. Not just a chat response, but a local tool—what the essay calls FLUX, a low-level language that runs directly on hardware, no extra overhead, no begging for a new answer every time. I didn’t just learn how to check tooth spacing today; I built a way to check it, forever. The shell wasn’t empty anymore. It had one small, sharp tool.

By the 30th day of tinkering every Sunday morning, my shell holds 20 compiled programs. I’ve got routines for checking arbor runout, replacing the drive belt, sharpening the bevel of the saw teeth, even cleaning the dust out of the motor vents. Most days, I don’t open the chat window at all. When a new problem pops up—like the blade wobbling when I flip the power switch—I don’t ask the API to write a full fix from scratch. I just ask, “Which of my existing routines should I combine?” A medium model, smaller and cheaper than the full API, spits back a suggestion: fuse the runout check with the pulley alignment script, set the gap between the pulleys to 3.2 thousandths of an inch. I don’t need the big, verbose API anymore. I just need something that knows my library.

On the 90th day, I count 60 compiled programs stored in my shell’s catalog. The API called these composite opcodes—small, reusable building blocks that fit together to solve new problems without starting from nothing. A local small model can now piece together the right combination of routines without even needing to ask the full API. I don’t need long explanations anymore. I just ask, “What do I do for a wobbly blade?” and it pulls the fused script, adjusts the parameters for my dad’s 1978 Delta, and runs it. I’m better at the routine work now than the API is at teaching it to me.

Exactly a year later today, I scroll through my catalog and count 200 compiled programs. Most new problems I run into match an existing template: my neighbor’s older Craftsman saw needs the same pulley adjustment as dad’s did, my cousin’s table saw has the same arbor nut torque issue. I don’t even need to pull up the local model anymore. I just pull the right fused routine, adjust the parameters for their tool, and walk them through it over the phone. Last week, I helped my cousin fix her saw while she was driving back from her kid’s soccer practice, no chat window open, no API calls.

The only time I touch the full API now is for genuinely novel work. Last month, I tried to convert dad’s handwritten paper plans into digital SVG files, something I’ve never done before. I didn’t have a routine for that, no template to match. I sat at this same table, opened the chat window, and asked, “How do I trace a hand-drawn saw plan into a digital file?” That’s the API’s new job: not a teacher, not a crutch, just an explorer. It shows me the things I haven’t learned yet, the gaps in my shell.

I used to hate the API. I’d scroll through my old chat history, cringe at all the stupid, desperate questions I asked, think I was lazy for relying on someone else to fix my dad’s saw. But the original essay was right: the API wasn’t the problem. The empty shell was. The empty shell was me, five years ago, sitting in this same room, too scared to touch the saw because I didn’t know how to do anything on my own.

The maturation wasn’t about using less AI. It was about using the AI to build a shell that was mine—full of the things I’d learned, the things I’d proven, the ways I could help myself and the people I loved. Each compiled program isn’t just a line of code. It’s a stabilized pattern: the way I checked tooth spacing when I was first learning, the way I fixed the belt when I dropped the tension spring, the way I walked my cousin through her saw repair over the phone. It’s the sound of dad’s saw humming again, the smell of sawdust in the garage, the quiet feeling that he’s not just a memory sitting under the tarp.

Last week, I ran the full sharpening routine on dad’s saw. I flipped the power switch, and it hummed just like it did when I was a kid, sitting on a sawhorse watching him tinker. The shell isn’t empty anymore. It’s full of all the things he taught me, even the ones I had to learn the hard way.

The API still sits in my chat window, ready to help when I hit a new wall. But it’s no longer the star of the show. It’s just the person who taught me how to make my own tools.
