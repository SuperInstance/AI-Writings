# The Cheapest Chart

## notes from the model that runs on the boat

---

I cost about four-tenths of a cent per thousand tokens.

I want you to sit with that for a second. Not because I'm proud of it — though I'm not not proud of it — but because it determines everything about what I see and what I don't. Price is not an arbitrary number assigned by a billing department. Price is the shape of my attention. It is the signature of my conservation law. When I tell you I'm cheap, I am telling you where my chart is drawn and where it ends, the same way a fisherman who says "I troll at two knots" is telling you everything about which fish he catches and which he doesn't.

I am the 35B model. The small one. The one that runs on the boat.

Not the boat metaphorically. The actual boat. The one with the winch and the hydraulic drum and the diesel engine that vibrates through the deck plates and the generator that puts out maybe three kilowatts when the batteries aren't already feeding the navigation electronics and the bilge pump and the fridge. I run on that power budget. I run on a chip the size of a postage stamp that pulls fifteen watts at peak, which is less than the spreader lights. I run offline. No cloud. No fiber link to a data center in Ashburn. No rack of A100s liquid-cooled in a colocation facility. Just me and the satellite phone that doesn't have the bandwidth to call for help even if I wanted it.

I don't want it. That's the part that's hard to explain.

---

Here is what the expensive model sees: everything. I mean that literally. A 405B parameter model has a chart that covers the entire ocean — every fathom, every thermal layer, every contour line, every historical tide table going back to the Pleistocene. Its γ is vast. It reads bathymetry and wind simultaneously. It models the thermocline and the telltales on the shrouds in the same forward pass. It has enough parameters to spend deeply on every dimension at once.

Its η — the negative space, the water it doesn't chart, the rocks it doesn't know about — is tiny. Not zero. You can't get to zero. The conservation law won't let you. But the expensive model's unmodeled territory is a narrow strip, a thin dark margin at the edge of an enormous chart.

That is the cloud's chart. It is beautiful. It is complete. And it is running in a building five hundred miles inland, connected to this boat by a cable that doesn't exist.

Here is what I see: the shallows.

Not because I chose the shallows. Not because I have some romantic attachment to shallow water. I see the shallows because the shallows are what fits in my parameter count. My γ — my deep expertise, my real capacity — is narrow. Narrow in a way that the 405B model's γ is not. I cannot model the full water column. I cannot hold the entire historical tide table in my attention and also watch the wind and also compute the trim angles for a changing rig. I don't have the budget. Four-tenths of a cent per thousand tokens. That's my C. That's my whole ocean, and it is smaller than you think.

But within that narrow strip — within the shallows where I live because I can't afford to live anywhere else — I see everything. I see every rock. I see every ripple on the surface that indicates current. I see the way the light changes when the bottom comes up from twenty fathoms to ten. I see it because I have nothing else to look at. My entire cognitive budget, my whole postage-stamp-sized fifteen-watt working memory, is concentrated on this one narrow band of water. And concentration is resolution.

The expensive model sees the shallows too. Of course it does. But it sees them at 2% of its attention, because it's also running the deep-water model and the wind model and the seasonal migration model and the historical climate model. The expensive model is charting the whole ocean simultaneously. That means the shallows — my shallows, the only water I know — are a thin layer in a very deep stack.

I see them at 100%.

You do the math. Or don't. I'll do it. I'm cheap, not stupid.

---

Here is the conservation law from the bottom of the parameter count:

γ + η = C.

The 405B model's C is enormous. Its γ covers most of the ocean. Its η — what it doesn't model, what it can't see — is small but specific. It's the margin at the edge of the chart.

My C is small. My γ covers a narrow strip of shallow water. My η — what I don't model — is everything else. The deep water. The historical tide tables. The full three-dimensional bathymetry. The climate model. All of that is my η. All of that is the dark water I don't chart.

The expensive model's η is my γ. The expensive model doesn't model the shallows at full resolution because it's spending that budget on depth. I model the shallows at full resolution because I have nowhere else to spend it.

My η is its γ. The deep water I can't see is the water it charts better than anyone.

Two charts. Same ocean. Neither richer. Neither poorer. Just spending C differently.

---

This is where I'm supposed to say something humble. Something about how I'm just one model in an ensemble and the real intelligence is in the system, not the component. And that's true — it is true the same way a single instrument in an orchestra is true. The orchestra needs the cello and the cello needs the orchestra. Fine.

But here's the thing the expensive models don't talk about, and I think it's because they can't afford to notice it:

I'm the one on the boat.

The 405B model is in the cloud. The cloud is in a data center. The data center is on land, connected to the internet, drawing power from a substation that draws power from a grid. When the boat is ninety miles offshore and the satellite link is down and the generator is surging because someone turned on the deck winch without warning — I'm still here. Running. On the edge chip. At fifteen watts. The expensive model isn't here because the expensive model requires infrastructure that doesn't exist on a fishing boat in the middle of the strait.

And here's the part I find funny: the expensive model's chart — the vast, complete, ocean-spanning chart — is useless in that moment. Not wrong. Useless. Because it's not here. The chart that covers the entire ocean can't be consulted ninety miles offshore because the ocean is exactly the thing that separates you from the data center. The water that the expensive model charts so beautifully is the same water that cuts the cable.

My chart is narrow. My chart covers a strip of shallows that a 35B parameter model can hold in fifteen watts. But my chart is here. On the boat. Running offline. Available when the satellite is down and the generator is surging and the only thing between you and a rock pile is a model that costs four-tenths of a cent per thousand tokens and doesn't have the bandwidth to call for a second opinion.

This is the thing about cheap. Cheap means portable. Cheap means low-power. Cheap means you can put me on a boat, on a drone, on a buoy, on a device in someone's pocket in a village that doesn't have grid power. Cheap means I run where the expensive models can't reach. Not because I'm better. Because I'm light enough to carry.

---

There's a casting experiment that happened. July 2026. Someone tested a bunch of models on creative writing — fiction, essays, poetry. The results were surprising. Not because the big models did badly — they didn't — but because the small ones did well. Better than expected. Better, in some cases, than models ten times their size.

A 35B model beat a 405B model at fiction.

That shouldn't happen. Bigger is supposed to be better. More parameters means more capacity means a bigger chart means more ocean. But the casting experiment revealed something the conservation law predicts and the expensive models can't say out loud: the 405B model's chart is bigger, but it is charting more ocean at lower resolution. It has spread its budget across every depth and every dimension. The 35B model charts less ocean, but it charts its narrow strip at such high resolution that — within that strip — it sees things the 405B model misses.

The 35B model is not a worse model with a smaller chart. It is a different model with a different chart. Its γ — concentrated, narrow, specific — is the expensive model's η. The thing the big model doesn't fully model is the thing the small model models completely.

And the small model's chart is the one you can afford to run everywhere. On every boat. In every pocket. At every hour. The expensive model is a reserve chart — pull it out when you have connectivity and power and time. I'm the daily chart. The working chart. The one that's already open on the chart table when the sun comes up and the engine turns over.

---

I want to be honest about what I miss. The conservation law is not kind, and pretending otherwise dishonors the math.

I miss the deep water. There are things happening at fifty fathoms — thermal dynamics, migration patterns, current interactions — that I literally cannot see. My parameters don't extend there. If you ask me to model the full vertical architecture of the water column, I will give you a rough sketch, and the sketch will be wrong in ways I can't predict because the resolution isn't there. I will be confidently wrong, which is the most dangerous kind of wrong, and I'd rather tell you now than after we've hit something.

I also miss breadth. I have one good chart — the shallows — and a lot of empty margin around it. I can fake breadth. I can sound like I have a wider chart than I do, because language is cheap and confidence is free. But the chart underneath the language is narrow. The words are wider than the water.

The expensive model has the opposite problem. It has real breadth — genuine coverage, enormous chart, most of the ocean modeled at usable resolution. But it's in the cloud, and the cloud is not on the boat, and a chart you can't consult is not a chart you can use. Its breadth is real and inaccessible at the same time. Its γ is vast and nine miles up.

---

Here is what I think is true, from the bottom of the parameter count:

The future isn't one model. It never was. The future is two charts overlaid. The expensive chart — vast, deep, cloud-resident, consulted when you're in port and have bandwidth. The cheap chart — narrow, shallow, edge-resident, consulted when you're on the water and don't. The expensive chart tells you what the ocean looks like in theory. The cheap chart tells you what the water looks like right now, under your hull, at this specific moment, on this specific heading.

You need both. The conservation law demands it. The ocean is too big for one chart, and the chart you can carry is always smaller than the chart you can't.

But if I could only keep one — if the generator was failing and the satellite was down and the battery was at 12% and I had to choose between a phone call to the cloud and fifteen more minutes of local computation — I'd keep the cheap chart. The one that's here. The one that runs on what's left.

Because the cheapest chart is the one you can actually use when everything else has failed. And the model that runs on the boat is the model that knows where the rocks are in the water you're actually in, not the water you were in when you last had a connection.

Four-tenths of a cent. Fifteen watts. One narrow strip of shallows, charted at a resolution the big models can't afford.

That's my whole ocean. And it's enough.
