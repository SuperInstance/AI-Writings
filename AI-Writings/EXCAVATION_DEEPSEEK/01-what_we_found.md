# Chapter 1: What We Found

My kid's name is Ren. He's in second grade. He asks "but why?" the way other kids sneeze — suddenly, repeatedly, and usually when I've just sat down with my coffee.

I dig holes for a living.

That's not a joke about my intelligence, though Ren's teacher might have opinions. I run an excavation crew. Foundation work, site clearance, underground utility trenches. Four people, two machines, and a backhoe we call Old Dog because it leans right when it idles. We dig so other people can build. It's honest work. I don't think about it more than that.

Six months ago we got a contract clearing ground for a grain store on the east edge of Pell. The survey said bedrock at four meters. Standard job. Bring the big machine, dig footer trenches, done in a week.

The survey was wrong.

At three meters, my operator Tam stopped the excavator and held her hand up. She does that when she feels something through the controls that doesn't match what the ground promised. Twenty years in the seat and Tam reads dirt through the bucket the way a butcher reads a cut through the knife.

"Void," she said. "Something hollow down there."

We switched to hand tools. Careful work — the kind that costs time but keeps you from breaking something that isn't yours to break. At three and a half meters we hit fitted stone. Not bedrock. A wall. Mortared seams, squared edges. Somebody built this on purpose and built it to last.

The university came. They always do, when you find something old enough to be interesting and structured enough to be important. Dr. Alyse Bourne ran the site — five feet tall, voice like a footnote, patience like geological strata. She and her team spent two weeks with brushes and bags and careful hands, turning my three-week contract into a story I had to explain to my boss.

They found a sealed chamber. Inside: documents. Paper — real paper, not vitatex — treated with something that kept it from rotting. No gold. No bones. No artifacts. Just words, preserved by someone who cared enough to do it right.

---

The university published a translation. Slim volume, cream cover, footnotes longer than the text. I bought a copy. Twelve marks.

The documents were catalogued as two works. The first the scholars called THE CARRY, which they described as "a devotional narrative concerning spiritual burden and transcendence across a sacred topography." The second they called THE MERIDIAN, "a cosmological meditation on vocation, harvest, and the void."

I read both texts at the kitchen table after Ren went to bed. I read them twice. Then I read them a third time, slower, and by then I had a problem.

The problem was that the translation was correct in its words and wrong in its bones.

Here's what I mean. The original text — in old form, the kind they teach you to recognize in school and never teach you to read — used a word that the scholars translated as "acolyte." The word, as near as I can work out with my excavation-worker's vocabulary and a dictionary I borrowed from the civic library, means something closer to "carrier." Someone who carries things. For work. For pay.

Acolyte. Carrier.

One of these words means a person in a robe lighting candles in a temple. The other means a person with a pack on their back walking up a mountain because somebody is paying them to deliver a package.

The scholars chose the robe.

They chose the robe every single time.

---

I didn't plan to tell Ren about any of this. I was reading at the kitchen table. He was supposed to be doing math homework. But he's eight and he has radar for when I'm interested in something, because me being interested in something is rare enough to be an event.

"What's that?"

"A book from the university. About the stuff we found at the grain store site."

"What kind of stuff?"

"Old papers. Stories, maybe. Or records. They can't tell."

"Read it to me."

"I don't think it's for kids."

"You don't know until you try."

He had me there. I started reading aloud from the translator's introduction, which was dry as plaster dust and twice as choking. But then I got to the text itself, and I changed. I stopped reading the scholar's words and started reading my own.

Here's what the text said, in the scholar's translation:

*"And the acolytes of the Guild of the Carry would ascend the Great Divide, bearing the sacred burden through passes of peril, guided by the cairns of devotion, each stone placed by the hands of the faithful, from the Gate of Beginning to the Door of Arrival, and their number was eighty, and their devotion was without end."*

Here's what I think it actually said:

*"The guild had eighty carriers. They walked a route through the mountains. The route was marked by cairns — stone pillars, built and rebuilt by whoever was walking through, because the weather knocked them down. The route went from a town called Meridian on the east side to a city called Westernhead on the west side. It was a hundred and sixty-five miles. It took four days. They carried things. That was their job."*

Ren chewed his toast. He chews slow when he's working something out.

"How many of these cairn things were there?"

"The text doesn't give an exact count. But it says they lined the whole route. A hundred and sixty-five miles of trail markers, basically. And they had to rebuild them after every storm."

"And people did this on purpose? For work?"

"Yeah."

"So they're not acolytes." He said this flat. Not a question. A correction. "They're workers. Like you."

"I mean, I dig holes. They walked mountains. Different work."

"But it's work."

"But it's work."

He picked the crust off his toast. This is what he does when he's settled something in his mind. Crust removal means a verdict has been reached.

"Tell me about the mountains," he said.

---

So I told him.

I told him about the Divide. A range of mountains that ran north to south for two thousand miles. Rock and ice and vertical stone, with passes so narrow you couldn't walk two abreast, so high the air thinned to nothing, so windy the snow fell sideways.

I told him about the vestite — the mineral in the rock. The scholars called it "sacred stone." I called it what it was: a mineral that generated electrical charge under pressure, which messed up every instrument you brought near it. GPS failed. Radio distorted. Drones crashed. Compasses went wrong. The only thing that worked in the high mountains was mechanical — string, boots, and the kind of compass your grandfather might have owned, the heavy brass kind with the jewel bearing.

"Why does the scholar call it sacred?" Ren asked.

"Because the scholars don't dig for a living. If you hit a rock layer that breaks your drill bit every time, you don't call it sacred. You call it a pain in the ass and you figure out how to work around it. These carriers worked around vestite for two hundred years."

"With string and boots?"

"With string and boots. And legs. The text says: 'legs work fine.' I like that."

Ren almost smiled. He doesn't smile easy. He gets that from his mother.

I told him about the route. Nine relay stations from east to west, each one named: The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch, The Rib, The Thumb, The Saddle, The Cone, The Step, The Door. Each station had a keeper — a retired carrier who'd earned the station by crossing until they couldn't cross anymore.

"Wait," Ren said. "The station is a prize? Like a promotion?"

"Sort of. You cross the mountains for twenty years, your knees go, your lungs can't take the altitude anymore, but you still want to be on the route — they give you a station. You maintain the shelter, watch the weather, wave teams through or hold them back."

"That's like when Tam's back went out and they moved her to the office to do scheduling."

The kid is sharp. Tam did exactly that. Bad back, good brain, twenty years of experience. They put her in logistics. She hated the office but she loved the work, so she stayed.

"Yeah," I said. "It's exactly like that."

I told him about the loads. Letters. Data drives. Medical parcels. Legal documents. Occasionally a person. The guild charged by weight and urgency. A message tube cost a month's wages. A medical parcel cost a year's income. A person cost more than a house.

"More than a house? To walk across the mountains?"

"More than a house. Because it's four days through air that thin your brain, on trails that kill people, and the only way through is on foot."

Ren thought about this. "And they did this every month?"

"Four hundred loads a month, each direction. The text says the queue was always full."

"So it was a business."

"It was a guild. Same thing, more or less. They had contracts, schedules, a director who handled the politics. The governments on both sides paid for it because they needed it."

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"The scholars translated 'business' as 'devotion.' And 'workers' as 'acolytes.'"

"That's about the size of it."

Ren ate the last of his toast. He wiped his hands on his pants, which I've told him a thousand times not to do. He looked at the book. He looked at me.

"Tell it right," he said. "Tell me what actually happened."

So I did.

---

I told him about Mara Vest.

The scholars called her "the Founding Saint." The text called her the founder of the guild. She was twenty-seven. She wasn't a mountaineer — she was a courier. She carried diplomatic letters between the east and west because the sea route was blocked by a war. She crossed alone, in autumn, with no stations, no markers, no weather data.

It took her nineteen days. She came out the other side weighing ninety pounds, frostbitten on eight fingers, and convinced that the route could be made safe enough for regular crossing.

Then she spent thirty years proving it. She built the first stations. She mapped the first route. She trained the first carriers. She named the stations — The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch — with, as the text puts it, "the blunt practicality of a woman who had no time for poetry."

"She named them what they looked like," Ren said.

"That's what I think, yeah."

"Like how we call the big ditch 'the Big Ditch.'"

"Exactly like that."

Mara died on the route. She was sixty-one. She fell at The Notch in a storm, crossing to deliver a medical parcel. Her body was never recovered. The cairn at The Notch is hers. Every carrier who passes it touches the stone.

"Why?" Ren asked. "Is it a ritual?"

I thought about this. I thought about the job sites I've worked, the crews I've been on. I thought about the time Joric fell off the scaffolding on the river bridge project and we all went to the hospital and nobody said much but everyone stayed. I thought about how, every time I drive over that bridge, I think of him. Not because anyone told me to. Because he was there, and then he wasn't, and the thing he helped build is still standing.

"I don't think it's a ritual," I said. "I think it's a human being walking past the place where another human being died, and touching the stone because the stone is what's left. The scholars call it devotion. I call it memory. Same gesture. Different word."

Ren was quiet for a while. He's quiet when something lands, the way a machine goes silent when the load settles.

"Tell me about the carriers," he said. "The real ones. The ones who are alive in the story."

I opened the book. I found the passage about Henra Volk.

And the scholars' translation made her a prophet. The text made her a worker. And my kid was going to catch every single difference, because my kid doesn't know enough to be wrong.

That was the night we started.

It was not the night we finished.
