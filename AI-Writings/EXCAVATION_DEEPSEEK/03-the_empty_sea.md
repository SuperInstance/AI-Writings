# Chapter 3: The Empty Sea

Ren didn't wait until tomorrow.

He came out of his room at ten PM — an hour after bedtime, which means an hour of lying in the dark thinking — stood in the kitchen doorway in his undershirt and mismatched socks, and said: "Dad, what's the boat text about?"

"You're supposed to be asleep."

"I can't sleep. The mountain people are in my head. Henra's knee is in my head."

I can't argue with that. I've lost sleep over less.

"Sit down."

He sat. I made him warm milk — not because he likes it but because the ritual of it helps him settle, and because I need something to do with my hands while I put the words together.

I opened the book to the second text. THE MERIDIAN, the scholars called it. "A cosmological meditation on vocation, harvest, and the void."

---

"The text is about a boat," I said.

"Like a fishing boat?"

"Like a fishing boat. But in space."

Ren's eyebrows went up. This is his version of a gasp.

"The world in this text — it's not like ours. People live on planets and stations, in a system with two suns. They've been there for generations. They have factories, governments, universities. And they have fishing."

"Fishing in space."

"Fishing in space. There are belts — asteroid belts — and in those belts there are organic compounds. Living material, growing on and inside rocks, drifting in clouds in the empty places. The fishing boats go out and harvest it. It's dangerous, skilled labor done from small vessels in open space."

"That's like... going out on a boat in the ocean. But the ocean is nothing."

"The ocean is nothing. Yeah. And they go out into it and they come back. The text says: 'The line goes out. The line comes back. That's the whole life.'"

Ren wrapped both hands around his milk. He does this when something sounds right to him — he holds onto something.

"Tell me about the boat."

---

The boat was called the Resolute. Seventy-eight years old. Built in a shipyard by people who over-engineered because they didn't know yet what space would do to a hull over decades. The text says: "Eight decades of patches, upgrades, refits, and repairs have made the Resolute into something that no new ship could be: a vessel that is entirely, completely known."

The scholars translated the ship's name as "The Steadfast" and called it "the temple vessel, the ark of the journey, the sanctuary of the celebrants."

Ren: "It's a boat."

"It's a boat."

"An old, paid-off boat that works."

"Yep."

"The scholars thought it was a temple."

"The scholars thought everything was a temple. If you showed them Old Dog they'd call it a consecrated earth-spirit construct."

Ren almost smiled. Almost.

---

I told him about the crew. Five people.

The captain was Ray Hutchins. Sixty-two. Thirty-one years on the boat. The scholars called him "the Spiritual Adept, master of the vessel of souls, he whose silence is wisdom, he whose grief has been transmuted into serenity."

Ren: "What grief?"

I checked the text. "His wife died. Two years ago. Aneurysm. Sudden. No warning. She was a schoolteacher."

"And the scholars said his sadness was 'serenity.'"

"They said it was 'transmuted into serenity.'"

"What does transmuted mean?"

"Changed into something else. Like alchemy. They think his grief turned into wisdom."

Ren looked at me. "Did it?"

"No. He still wears his ring. He still has her photo in his locker. He checks it every morning. The grief didn't turn into anything. It's just grief. He carries it. He goes to work."

"Like Oskar."

"Yeah. Like Oskar. Different shape, same weight."

I told him about Ray's style of captaining: he didn't shout orders. He'd been doing it longer than anyone. When he said "we'll hold here and wait," people held and waited. His judgment calls were the product of thirty years of reading the belt, and they were almost always right. The times they weren't were the times that kept him up.

"He's like Henra," Ren said.

"How so?"

"He's the one who decides. And the reason he's good is he's done it so many times that people trust him. Not because he's special. Because he's been doing the work."

"Yeah. Same job, different mountain."

---

The mechanic was Denny Park. Forty-five. Twelve years on the boat. The scholars called him "the Keeper of the Vessel's Spirit, tender of the inner fire, he whose communion with the engine is the communion of the devoted."

Ren: "He's a mechanic."

"He's a mechanic."

"Like Margit who fixes Old Dog?"

"Like Margit who fixes Old Dog. The text says he sleeps in the engine room sometimes. He knows the sounds so well he once diagnosed a problem from three rooms away while he was asleep."

"Margit does that. She heard the track tension was wrong on Old Dog from across the site."

"Same person. Different boat. Different century."

I told him Denny's history: he'd come to the boat at thirty-three, running from a bad marriage, a job he hated, a life that felt like a waiting room. The previous captain gave him a bunk and a trial run. He never left.

"That's like Henra again," Ren said. "Leaving the thing you're supposed to do for the thing you actually do."

"And Wren. Same pattern. Different story."

I told him about Denny's daughter, sixteen, living with her mother on a planet he visits between seasons. "He doesn't know how to talk to her," the text says. "He's trying."

Ren was quiet about this one. He's a kid whose other parent isn't here. He understands trying.

"The scholars called him a 'devoted celebrant,'" Ren said.

"They did."

"He's just a dad who doesn't see his kid enough and doesn't know what to say when he does."

"That's what the text says."

"The scholars are really bad at this."

"They're trying. They just don't know what work looks like. Not from the inside."

---

The engineer was Margaret Kowalski. Fifty-eight. Her family had maintained the boat since it was built. Three generations. The scholars called her "the Oracle, keeper of the vessel's nervous system, she whose word is law, she whose precision is the precision of divine judgment."

"She's an engineer," Ren said. Weary now. Weary the way you get when you've corrected the same mistake too many times.

"She's an engineer."

"She keeps the boat working."

"She keeps the boat working."

"The scholars—"

"I know."

Margaret's trait: every word she said was the word she meant. She didn't do small talk. She didn't do reassurance. When she told you something would hold, it would hold, and the fact that she said it was all the comfort you were going to get.

I read this part aloud to Ren and he said: "That's you."

"That's not me."

"Dad. When the school asked you to come in and talk about career day, you said 'I dig holes' and sat down."

"That's different."

"You said 'it's honest work' and then you sat down and Mrs. Falke asked if you wanted to add anything and you said 'no.'"

"I didn't want to add anything."

"Margaret doesn't reassure either. And when she says something works, it works. That's you with the site reports. You write three sentences. The city wants a paragraph. You write three sentences because three sentences is what's true."

I let this go. The kid isn't wrong.

---

The last crew member I told him about was the owner. Sofia Reyes. Twenty-six. She inherited the boat from her uncle when he retired. She'd never been on a harvester. She'd never been to the fishing grounds. She was working a processing line when the ownership papers arrived.

The scholars called her "the Initiate, she who has been called to the path, the inheritor of the sacred trust, she whose journey is the journey of awakening."

Ren said: "She got a job she didn't ask for and she's figuring it out."

"Yeah."

"Like when Grandpa died and you got his house and you didn't know what to do with it but you couldn't sell it because it was his."

I didn't expect that. But he's right. I inherited my father's house and I live in it and I don't love it and I can't leave, because leaving would mean letting go of the last thing he built, and I'm not ready, and maybe I'll never be ready, and the house is just a house but it's also the shape of someone who's gone.

"Yeah," I said. "Like that."

The text says Sofia asks too many questions. Some of which are brilliant.

"Some of which," Ren repeated. He looked pleased. He knows he asks too many questions. He knows some of them are good. He knows the ones that aren't are still worth asking, because you don't get to the good ones without the bad ones first.

---

I told him about the fishing.

The boats went out into the belts — the asteroid fields, the empty places between stations. They towed nets — magnetic nets, energy nets, but nets — and they caught things. Organic compounds. Material that got processed into food, medicine, industrial chemicals. The work was dangerous and skilled and the text describes it the way Tam describes excavation: practically, with respect for the difficulty and zero romance.

The scholars translated "fishing" as "the Harvest of Souls" and "the Gathering of Essences" and "the Sacrament of the Void."

Ren put his head on the table again. The forehead-against-wood posture. His "I am surrounded by fools" had upgraded to "I have given up on civilization."

"They're fishing," he said, muffled by the table.

"They're fishing."

"They go out. They catch things. They bring them back. It's a job."

"It's a job."

"The scholars think they're harvesting souls."

"The scholars think they're harvesting souls."

"Dad. If you catch a fish, it's a fish. It's not a soul. It's a fish."

"You're right."

"Why do the scholars keep doing this?"

I thought about this. It's a real question and I think it deserves a real answer.

"Because the scholars have never fished. They've never dug a trench. They've never stood on a site at six in the morning with frost on the ground and a machine that won't start and a crew waiting for you to tell them what to do. They read about people doing hard work and they can't imagine why someone would do it, so they decide the work must mean something else. Something bigger. Something spiritual. Because the alternative — that people do hard work because it needs doing and because it pays and because it's their job — that answer is too small for them. Too plain."

Ren lifted his head off the table.

"That's stupid."

"It's not stupid. It's just... far away. They're looking at work from a long way off and from a long way off, a person carrying a box up a mountain looks like a person carrying a prayer up a temple. You can't see the sweat from that distance. You can't see the lunch break. You can't see the bad knee."

"But we can see it."

"Yeah. Because we know what work looks like."

---

We sat with that for a while. The milk was cold. The kitchen was quiet except for the fridge and Mr. Alvaro's dog, who had moved on to barking at a different nothing.

Ren said: "The two texts. The mountain one and the boat one."

"Yeah."

"Are they the same?"

I looked at him. "What do you mean?"

"Mountain people carry things across a mountain. Boat people catch things in the nothing. Different jobs. But the way you tell it, they're the same people."

"How so?"

"They go to work. It's hard. They're good at it because they've done it a long time. They have crew they trust. They have things they don't talk about. They have someone who's getting old and knows it. They have someone who left where they were supposed to be and came to where they actually belong."

He counted these on his fingers. He ran out of fingers.

"And nobody in either story thinks they're special. Henra doesn't think she's holy. Ray doesn't think he's a priest. They just go to work."

I didn't say anything. I didn't need to.

"So why did someone put these in a stone box and seal them up?"

This was the question I'd been waiting for. The one I'd been thinking about since the third time I read the texts.

"I think," I said, "because someone wanted them to survive. Not because they were scripture. Because they were worth remembering."

"Worth remembering because they're good stories?"

"Worth remembering because they're true. Not true like happened-exactly-like-this true. True like these-are-real-people true. The kind of true that doesn't get written down often, because the people living it are too busy living it to write about it."

Ren turned his cup around in his hands. Around and around.

"The scholars found them and didn't know what they were."

"The scholars found them and saw scripture, because that's the box they had. If all you have is a religion box, everything you find goes in the religion box."

"What box do you have?"

I thought about this. "I have a work box. I see work. I see crews. I see people doing jobs and the jobs being hard and the people doing them anyway."

"So you're doing the same thing as the scholars. Just from the other side."

This stopped me. The kid is eight and he just caught me at my own blind spot.

I was doing the same thing. I was reading religion out of the text and reading work into it, the same way the scholars read work out and put religion in. I was choosing my box.

Maybe the text is both. Maybe it's neither. Maybe it's just words on paper, and the meaning lives in who's reading, not what's written.

"Yeah," I said. "Maybe I am."

Ren nodded. He does this when he respects an answer — a single nod, serious, like a tiny foreman approving a grade.

"But I think your box is closer," he said. "Because the people in the stories act like workers, not priests. Priests don't complain about their knees."

He finished his milk. He put the cup down. He looked at me with those eyes — his mother's eyes — and he said the thing that I'll carry with me longer than any of the scholar's words:

"Someone should write it down right. What the mountain people were actually like. What the boat people were actually like. Not as a prayer. Just as... a record. So the next person who digs them up knows."

"Knows what?"

"Knows that they were people. Not acolytes. Not celebrants. Just people. Going to work."

---

I put him back to bed. He went without the argument this time. I think he was tired in the good way — the way you're tired when you've thought hard about something real.

I went back to the kitchen. I sat down with the book and my cold coffee that I didn't remake because making it would mean getting up and I didn't want to get up yet.

I thought about Henra Volk, forty-eight, writing the numbers in her notebook. Three more seasons. Maybe four.

I thought about Ray Hutchins, sixty-two, checking the photo in his locker every morning. Still wearing the ring.

I thought about Wren, humming on the snow, refusing the letter her mother paid for every year.

I thought about Denny, asleep in the engine room, listening to the sounds the way you listen to breathing.

I thought about Mara Vest, twenty-seven, walking into the mountains alone because someone on the other side needed to hear from someone on this side.

I thought about Margaret, labeling every wire in three languages, because if you know where everything is, you can fix anything that breaks.

I thought about Oskar, checking the rope one more time. Always one more time.

I thought about Teo, at The Step, pouring whiskey for Henra, tapping the barometer twice — once for the reading, once for luck.

I thought about Sofia, writing in her journal every night, asking questions she didn't have answers for yet.

I thought about all of them, and I thought about us — me, with my excavation crew and my morning coffee and my bad back and my dead father's house and my kid who asks why about everything. And I thought: the distance between us is time. That's all. Just time. And someone put these words in a stone box because they knew that time would pass and the words would still matter. Not as scripture. As evidence.

Evidence that people went to work. That the work was hard. That they did it anyway. That they took care of each other while they did it. That they had names and faces and bad knees and grief and stubbornness and coffee and songs and scarves and notebooks.

That they carried things. That they went out into the nothing and came back.

That the line went out and the line came back.

That someone, somewhere, needed to hear from someone else, and the distance between them was made of stone, and someone walked across it.

Not because they were holy.

Because it was their job.

And because it needed doing.

---

*Ren asked me at breakfast the next morning if I was going to tell the university.*

"I don't think the university wants to hear from a ditch-digger," I said.

"You're not a ditch-digger. You're an excavation supervisor."

"Tam would appreciate the distinction."

"Are you going to tell them?"

I looked at my kid. Jam on his chin. Math homework done — the even numbers too, this time. Socks mismatched. Feet not touching the floor.

"Yeah," I said. "I think I am."

*"We carry." — Guild motto, founded by Mara Vest, 200 years ago.*

*Two words. No motto needed more.*
