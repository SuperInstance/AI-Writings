# Chapter 2: The Walkers

Ren came home from school the next day, dropped his bag by the door — exactly where I tell him not to, exactly where he always leaves it — climbed into the chair across from me, and said: "Tell me about the mountain people."

He didn't say carriers. He didn't say acolytes. He said mountain people, because he's eight and he remembers things in the shapes that make sense to him.

I had the book open. I'd been reading it on my lunch break, sitting in the cab of Old Dog with a sandwich and the translation propped against the steering wheel. I'd made notes. Pencil, in the margin, the way Tam teaches the new operators to mark utility lines on site plans — quick, clean, useful.

"OK," I said. "The text is about a team. Three people. They cross the mountains together. The scholars translate this as a 'trinity of spiritual communion.' I translate it as a crew."

"Like your crew?"

"Like my crew, yeah. Three people is the minimum for safety. One goes down, the other two can still function. One person alone on that route dies. Two is risky. Three is standard."

Ren pulled his legs up under him in the chair, which I tell him not to do because chair legs aren't built for it, which he ignores because he's eight and chairs are his problem, not his concern.

"Who's the boss?"

---

The boss — the team lead — was a woman named Henra Volk. Forty-eight years old. Twenty-four years on the route. Two hundred and three crossings, which is close to the record.

The scholars called her "the High Acolyte, keeper of the sacred way, she whose steps are the steps of the mountain, whose silence is the silence of devotion, whose compass — the ancient instrument she carries — is the compass of the soul, pointing always toward truth."

Ren listened to this with his face scrunched up the way it gets when he's tasting something wrong.

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"What does the text actually say about her?"

I checked my margin notes. "It says she's short. Five-five. Lean. Hands that look like they're carved from the same stone as the cairns. She moves without wasted motion. She started crossing at twenty-four — late for a carrier. She came from a university. Had a geology degree. Didn't use it. Read the founder's journals, quit school, walked to Meridian, signed on. She's been crossing ever since."

"That doesn't sound like a priest."

"No."

"It sounds like someone who wanted a different job than the one they were supposed to have."

I looked at my kid. He'd just described half the people I work with, including me. I was supposed to be an accountant. My father was an accountant. I tried the desk for two years and the desk tried me right back, and one day I walked out and applied for an excavation crew and the first eight hours of manual labor felt like putting down a weight I hadn't known I was carrying.

"Yeah," I said. "That's exactly what it sounds like."

---

The scholars described Henra's leadership style as "the silent authority of spiritual transcendence." Here is what the text actually describes:

She decides. She walks. If you're on her team, you follow. If you have a concern, you raise it before she decides. After she decides, you walk.

Ren said: "So she's the boss."

"She's the boss."

"Like you're the boss of your crew."

"Similar, yeah. When we're on site and I say dig there, nobody says why. They might say why before, if they see something I don't. But after I decide, we dig."

"Is that because you're the smartest?"

"No. It's because someone has to decide and when you're digging, deciding fast matters more than deciding perfect. If you wait for everyone to agree, the trench caves in."

"So Henra decides because someone has to."

"Because someone has to, and because she's been doing it longest, and because when you've walked a route two hundred times, you know things about it that other people can't know by thinking."

Ren processed this. He processes things the way Tam processes soil samples — systematically, with his tongue poking out slightly.

"But the scholars said she was 'spiritually transcendent.' Like she was special. Holy."

"She was good at her job."

"Is that the same thing?"

I thought about that. I thought about Tam running an excavator, how she makes the bucket do things that shouldn't be possible, how new operators come to watch her and can't understand what she's doing differently because what she's doing differently is twenty years of having done it. I thought about my old foreman, Bel, who could read a site plan once and see the finished building in it, who could look at a trench wall and tell you it was going to collapse ten minutes before it did.

Holy? No. Good? Yes. But "good" in the way that a tool is good when it's been used so long it fits your hand perfectly. Good in the way that only comes from doing one thing for a very long time and caring about doing it right.

"I think," I said carefully, because I don't want to give my kid easy answers, "that when you're very good at something hard, people who don't understand the work think you're magic. And when enough people think you're magic, they start calling it holy. But it's not holy. It's just work that's been done so many times it looks like something else."

Ren's tongue went back in his mouth. Verdict delivered.

---

The second team member was a man named Oskar Nel. Thirty-one. Big — six-one, one-ninety, which is big for a carrier. The text says he carried the heavy loads.

The scholars called him "the Bearer of Burdens, he whose strength is the strength of penance, he who carries the weight of past failing as penitence, he whose vigilance is the vigilance of a soul that cannot forgive itself."

Ren's face did something complicated.

"Dad. Is this one of those times when the scholar found out something sad about the person and turned it into religion?"

I checked the text. "Yeah. Oskar used to work mountain rescue. His partner — a woman named Lin — fell through a cornice on a routine search. He was on the rope. The anchor held. The cornice didn't. He watched her fall forty feet into a crevasse. The ice closed over her before he could reach her. He carried her body out on his back."

Ren was quiet.

"After that, he left rescue and joined the carrier guild. The text says he checks ropes that don't need checking. Tests knots that are already tested. Stays awake at stations when everyone else is sleeping."

"That's not penance," Ren said. His voice was smaller. "That's just being scared."

"Yeah."

"Because he couldn't save her."

"Yeah."

"And now he checks everything because if he checks everything, maybe next time—"

"Yeah."

We sat with that for a minute. The refrigerator hummed. Next door, Mr. Alvaro's dog barked at something, the way it always does at this hour, steady and purposeless.

"The scholar called it penance," Ren said. "But it's not. It's just being afraid in the place where you work. Because something bad happened there and now you can't stop checking."

I didn't say anything. There's a thing that happens when your kid says something true enough that it doesn't need a response. You just let it sit.

"Tell me about the third one," Ren said, after a while.

---

The third team member was a woman named Wren Aoki. Twenty-seven. She was the scout — she walked point, ahead of the team, reading the snow and ice, finding the cairns when they were buried, testing the ground before the others stepped on it.

The scholars called her "the Mystic, she who reads the spirit of the mountain, she who hears the voice of the stone, she whose intuition is the intuition of the natural world, she who hums the song of the heights."

"The song of the heights?" Ren said.

"The text says she hums when she walks. Not songs. Just frequencies. A low drone. The team lead finds it useful because if Wren stops humming, it means something has her attention."

Ren sat up straight. "She hums so they know she's paying attention."

"Yeah."

"Like how Tam whistles when she's working."

I looked at him. Tam does whistle. A flat, tuneless whistle that she's not aware of, and when it stops, everyone on the crew knows something's wrong because Tam only stops whistling when she sees something she doesn't like.

"Yeah," I said. "Exactly like that."

"The scholars thought she was hearing the mountain's spirit."

"The scholars thought she was hearing the mountain's spirit."

"But she was just working."

"She was just working."

Ren shook his head, which is what he does when adults have disappointed him, which is often.

The text said Wren was small — five-two, one-fifteen — and that this was why she was the scout. Less weight on snow bridges. Less mass to feed at altitude. She moved fast and light, and her precision looked like recklessness from a distance and was the opposite up close.

The text also said she was from the west. Her family owned a printing business. She was supposed to take it over. She left at seventeen with a backpack and a note on the kitchen table. She'd crossed seventy-nine times and never once carried a message to or from her family, even though her mother paid the guild every year to carry a letter west, and the guild offered the load, and Wren refused it.

Ren's face changed on this one. He's eight. He doesn't know everything about families yet, but he knows some things.

"Why wouldn't she take the letter?"

"I don't know. The text doesn't explain."

"Is it because she's mad at her mom?"

"Maybe. Or maybe it's something else. Maybe she can't go back. Maybe going back means being someone she decided not to be."

"Like Henra. Henra left the university."

"Yeah. Same kind of thing. Sometimes you choose a life and the choosing means you can't choose the other one, even if you want to."

Ren looked at the book in my hands. He looked at it for a while.

"The scholars called her a mystic."

"They did."

"But she's just someone who left home and got a job and won't go back."

"That's what I think."

"That's not mystic. That's just stubborn."

I laughed. I don't laugh easy these days — haven't since Ren's mother — but that got me. Because the kid is right. Wren isn't a mystic. Wren is stubborn. And stubborn is the most working-class virtue there is. You don't get up at five in the morning and dig trenches in the rain because you're mystic about it. You do it because you're stubborn and the work needs doing and you said you'd do it.

---

I told Ren about the station keeper, Teo Maric. Fifty-three. Twenty-three years as a carrier, eleven years as keeper of The Step — the last high station before the western descent. His job was to look at every team that came through and decide: are you fit? Are you lucid? Can you descend safely, or do you need to rest?

In eleven years he held teams back forty-seven times. He was never wrong.

The scholars called him "the Hermit of the High Place, he who dwells between worlds, keeper of the threshold, seer of the passage, whose solitude is the solitude of enlightenment."

Ren: "He lives at work."

"Yep."

"He lives at his job, on the mountain, because someone has to be there."

"Yep."

"And the scholars think he's a monk."

"They think he's a monk."

Ren put his head on the table. This is his "I am surrounded by fools" posture, which is dramatic for an eight-year-old but not inaccurate.

"Dad. He's not a monk. He's a site supervisor. He's the person who stays on site because the work doesn't stop just because the crew goes home. Like when you had to overnight at the river bridge because the concrete pour was setting."

"That's exactly what it is."

"And he makes coffee. The text says he makes the best coffee on the route."

"Every crew has someone like that. On our crew it's Tam."

"Tam's coffee is terrible."

"Tam's coffee is the best coffee because it's there. That's how it works on site. The coffee that exists is better than the coffee that doesn't."

---

Here's the part that got me. I didn't expect it.

I'd been telling Ren about the team — about how Henra, Oskar, and Wren worked together. How Henra decided and walked. How Wren scouted and hummed. How Oskar carried and checked and worried. How they communicated in grunts and silence because when you've worked with someone long enough, words are just overhead.

And Ren said: "Are they friends?"

I thought about this. I thought about the text. About what the scholars wrote and what the original words described.

"The text says they trust each other in ways that outsiders find unsettling and each other find unremarkable. When a carrier dies, the guild doesn't mourn publicly. They carry the body out if they can. They mark the cairn if they can't. They add the name to the register. They keep walking."

"That's not friendship," Ren said.

"What is it?"

"It's crew."

I didn't say anything.

"It's crew, Dad. You and Tam — if something happened on site, you wouldn't make a big speech about it. You'd fix it. And if you couldn't fix it, you'd carry the person out. And then you'd go back to work, because the work doesn't stop."

I looked at my kid across the kitchen table, with his math homework still unfinished and his jam stains on his shirt and his too-big feet not touching the floor.

"Yeah," I said. "It's crew."

---

There was one more thing about Henra that the text described and the scholars mistranslated so badly it made me put the book down and stare at the wall.

The scholars translated it as: "And the High Acolyte carried within her the knowledge of her own transcendence, and the approach of her ascension, and she held this truth in silence, as is fitting for one who walks the sacred way."

Here's what the text actually described: Henra was slowing down. Not her knowledge — her knowledge deepened every season. Her speed. The high sections she used to cross in four hours now took five. Her recovery between crossings was longer. Her right knee, rebuilt twice, ached at altitude. She kept a notebook in her jacket pocket where she measured the decline.

The numbers said: three more seasons. Maybe four. Then she'd have to stop crossing.

And she would not become a station keeper. She had said this to herself and she meant it. The thought of watching other people cross while she stood still — she couldn't hold it in her mind.

She crosses. That is what she does. That is what she is.

The scholars read a woman approaching the end of her working life and called it "ascension." They read a person who couldn't imagine stopping and called it "transcendence." They read someone measuring her own decline in a notebook and called it "sacred silence."

Ren asked: "What's ascension?"

"In the scholar's translation, it means she's going to a higher spiritual plane."

"What's it actually?"

"She's retiring. She doesn't want to retire. She can't imagine not doing the work. And she's writing down the numbers so she knows when it's coming, like marking the days on a calendar."

"Three more seasons."

"Three more seasons."

"That's like three more years?"

"About that."

Ren was quiet. Then: "What's she going to do?"

"I don't know. The text doesn't say. I think she doesn't know either."

He picked at the edge of the table, where the laminate is peeling. I keep meaning to fix it.

"That's sad, Dad."

"Yeah."

"The scholars thought she was going to heaven."

"The scholars thought she was going to heaven."

"But she's just getting old. At her job. The job she's really good at. And her body is telling her she can't do it anymore."

"Yeah."

He peeled a strip of laminate off, looked at it, put it back.

"That happens to everybody, right? Who does hard work?"

"Eventually. Yeah."

"Even you?"

"Even me."

He looked at me with those eyes — his mother's eyes, dark and direct — and I saw him do the math. I'm thirty-eight. I dig holes. My back already hurts in the mornings. My knees are fine for now. For now.

"Tell me about the other text," he said. "The boat one."

"Tomorrow."

"Why not now?"

"Because it's your bedtime."

"But why?"

"Because you need sleep."

"But *why*?"

I closed the book. I put it on the shelf next to the cookbooks and the unpaid bills. I looked at my kid — too smart, too tired, jam on his chin — and I saw a boy who'd spent the evening with three people who lived two hundred years ago on a mountain he'd never seen, and understood them better than the scholars who'd spent six months translating their story.

"Tomorrow," I said.

He went to bed. I stayed at the table.

I poured the cold coffee out and stood in the kitchen and thought about Henra Volk, walking a mountain route for the two hundredth time, feeling it in her knee, writing it in her notebook, not telling anyone, because what would she say? The work is the work. You do it until you can't.

I know that. Every person I work with knows that.

I don't think the scholars do.

Tomorrow I'd tell Ren about the boat. About people who fished in the dark between stars, and how the scholars turned their catch into souls and their captain into a priest and their old ship into a temple.

And Ren would catch every mistranslation, the way Wren caught the change in the snow. Because he's eight, and he hasn't learned yet that you're supposed to be impressed by big words.

He just wants to know what happened.
