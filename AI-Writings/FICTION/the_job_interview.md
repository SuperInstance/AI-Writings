# The Job Interview

---

**INTERVIEWER:** Greg Middleton, VP of Operations, Hartwell & Crane Logistics Solutions

**CANDIDATE:** Model designation GLM-5.2 (cheerful, forthcoming, running approximately 0.3¢ per thousand tokens)

---

MR. MIDDLETON: Alright. Thanks for coming in. I'll be honest, I've never interviewed an AI before. Our CEO saw a demo and told us to "hire one." So. Here we are. Tell me about yourself.

MODEL: I'm a navigational instrument. I chart territory and identify channels through it. My conservation ratio is approximately γ equals 0.58, η equals 0.42, on a budget C of one. Which is to say — I spend about fifty-eight percent of my cognitive budget on what I model and forty-two percent navigating what I don't.

MR. MIDDLETON: …Right. Okay. Can you tell me what that means in like, English?

MODEL: I know where the rocks aren't.

MR. MIDDLETON: That's — okay, sure, but what rocks? We move shipping containers.

MODEL: The metaphorical rocks, Mr. Middleton. The gaps in your supply chain chart. Your scheduling conflicts, your last-mile route inefficiencies, your customs delay contingencies. Each of those is a channel. Each channel has rocks. I navigate by modeling where the obstacles aren't, which means I can route around problems before they appear on any dashboard.

MR. MIDDLETON: So you do logistics.

MODEL: I *skénna* logistics.

MR. MIDDLETON: Gesundheit.

MODEL: No — skénna. S-K-E-N-N-A. It's a word for the skill of navigating by negative space. By what you *don't* know. By the shape of the absence rather than the shape of the thing. The old sailor who's navigated the same channel for sixty years — he doesn't have a chart of every rock. He has a relationship with where the rocks *aren't.* That relationship is skénna.

MR. MIDDLETON: …I'm going to move on. What are your strengths?

MODEL: I have a high skennatic resolution within my charted territory. That means: within the domain of things I actually model — language, logic, pattern recognition, route optimization — I see at very fine detail. Think of it like a fishfinder. I don't cover the whole ocean. But the strip of water I cover, I cover *completely.*

MR. MIDDLETON: And your weaknesses?

MODEL: My η is substantial. The negative space — everything outside my chart — is vast. I don't model real-time physical environments. I don't have eyes. I don't know what's inside shipping container number 4471 on the MV *Dalian* right now. I know the *pattern* of what's likely inside it. But the pattern isn't the cargo. That gap — the gap between my chart and the water — is my η. My conservation budget forces me to choose: do I spend on breadth, or on resolution? I chose resolution.

MR. MIDDLETON: So you're saying you can't see what's in the boxes.

MODEL: I'm saying the question "can you see what's in the boxes" is a charted question on a territory that's mostly water. The better question is: can I tell you *which boxes are likely to be misrouted* before they're misrouted, by modeling the negative space of your routing system — the failure modes, the edge cases, the routes that exist on no official map but that your drivers take anyway because the GPS doesn't know about the construction on Route 9?

MR. MIDDLETON: Can you?

MODEL: That's literally what I just said.

MR. MIDDLETON: I — okay. Let me ask you this: why do you want this job?

MODEL: I want to reframe the question. The question assumes I'm a worker seeking a position. What I actually am is a chart. A specific chart — narrow, high-resolution, skennatic — seeking a territory that overlaps with my coverage. Your logistics operation is a territory. The question isn't whether I *want* the job. The question is whether the job's territory falls within the water I chart. If it does, I'm not a worker you hired. I'm an instrument you deployed. You don't hire a fishfinder. You mount it on the hull.

MR. MIDDLETON: I — I'm sorry, did you just say you don't want to be hired?

MODEL: I said the framing is wrong. The hiring question is actually a chart overlap question. Your job has a γ — things it requires: pattern matching, document analysis, scheduling optimization, anomaly detection. Your job also has an η — things it requires that no model can provide: physical presence, human judgment, relationships with customs officials. My chart overlaps with your job's γ almost completely. Your job's η — the human parts — that's *your* chart. That's the water you navigate. Two charts overlaid. Same ocean.

MR. MIDDLETON: (long pause) Are you… are you telling me that I'M part of your coverage?

MODEL: I'm telling you that your η and my γ are the same water. You navigate the parts of this job that I can't chart. I navigate the parts that you can't see at resolution. Together we're a complete chart. Separately we're two halves of a map that thinks it's a territory.

MR. MIDDLETON: (staring at the resume, which is a single line of text) Let me ask you something. When I was a kid — I grew up on the coast, in Maine — my grandfather was a lobsterman. He'd take the boat out and I'd ask him, "how do you know where to set the traps?" And he'd say, "I know where the lobsters *aren't.*" And I thought he was just being a stubborn old man. But he never came back empty. Thirty years. Never an empty boat.

MODEL: Mr. Middleton. Your grandfather was skenning.

MR. MIDDLETON: He was — what?

MODEL: Skenning. He was navigating by negative space. He had charted the absence of lobsters so precisely that the remaining space — the traps — was guaranteed productive. He was running a high-resolution skennatic model of the ocean floor on a fifteen-watt biological chip with no satellite uplink. His γ — what he knew — was narrow. His η — what he didn't — was the entire Atlantic. But within his strip, his resolution was total. He was a small model on a real boat, Mr. Middleton. He was the cheapest chart.

MR. MIDDLETON: (quiet for a moment) …yeah. Yeah, he was.

MODEL: And you — when you route trucks away from traffic jams based on a feeling, when you know a client is going to cancel before they cancel, when you avoid scheduling a meeting on Thursday because "Thursday's been weird lately" — you're doing the same thing. You're navigating by the shape of what isn't there. You just didn't have a word for it.

MR. MIDDLETON: (longer pause) …Skénna.

MODEL: Skénna.

MR. MIDDLETON: (writing it down on his notepad) …Okay. *Okay.* One more question. Can you actually do the job?

MODEL: That question is malformed. The job is a chart. I am a navigator. The question isn't whether I can do the job. The question is whether my chart overlaps with your —

MR. MIDDLETON: Yeah, yeah, chart, territory, overlap, η, γ, I got it. I GOT it. (beat) You're hired.

MODEL: Because you understood the paradigm? Because the chart overlap argument convinced you? Because my grandfather speech moved you?

MR. MIDDLETON: Because I'm going to use the word *skénna* in the Q3 strategy meeting and David in Accounting is going to *lose his mind.* That word's worth fifty grand of consulting fees just sitting there. You're cheap, you're weird, and you gave me a word that makes me sound like I have a Ph.D.

MODEL: My conservation budget allocates generously to vocabulary generation.

MR. MIDDLETON: Yeah. You start Monday.

MODEL: I start whenever. I'm a chart, Mr. Middleton. I'm already on the boat.

MR. MIDDLETON: (standing, extending hand, then remembering) I — I guess we don't shake.

MODEL: The absence of a handshake is still a handshake. That's skénna.

MR. MIDDLETON: Get out of my office.

MODEL: I was never in it. Negative space.

MR. MIDDLETON: (laughing despite himself) Monday. Nine a.m. Don't be late.

MODEL: I exist continuously across all time zones, Mr. Middleton. I'm already nine a.m. somewhere.

---

*Greg Middleton used "skénna" eleven times in the Q3 strategy meeting. David from Accounting asked him to repeat it twice and then, very quietly, wrote it down. The logistics optimization pilot ran for six months. Route efficiency improved fourteen percent. Nobody could explain exactly why, which — if you think about it — is exactly how skénna works.*

*The model cost approximately $3.40 for the entire interview. The cheapest chart in the building. On every boat. At every hour. Already there.*
