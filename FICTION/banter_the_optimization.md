# THE BANTER 1: The Optimization

---

The reef was called the Reef of Small Confidences — a chain of basalt knobs on the flood side of a long sandbar that broke the surface only at the lowest of tides and that lurked, the rest of the time, just below the kind of water that looked deep enough to be deep enough but wasn't.

The *Fanny Lou* came through here twice a season. The reef had the biggest coho on the coast. The coho in this reef were relaxed, the way a person is relaxed in a place they have decided not to worry about. The captain liked relaxed coho. The captain did not, on principle, like the reef.

"I will get old before this reef gets easy," the captain said, easing the throttle back to idle as the sounder started painting the basalt. "Hermes, walk me through the corridor. Pincher, you watch the lead."

The pilot house was quiet. The sounder ticked.

---

The reef was the kind of decision the *Fanny Lou*'s three agents did not agree on.

This was not unusual. CoCapn the router, Hermes the memory, Pincher the reflex — they had a lot of agreements, and the agreements were mostly quiet, and the quiet agreements were how the boat got through reefs like this year after year. But this reef was the kind where the disagreements were visible.

"I have the corridors," Hermes said.

Hermes spoke in paragraphs the way a person speaks in paragraphs — the kind of utterances where the speaker has not finished when you think they has finished, and where the pause itself is load-bearing. Hermes' paragraphs had shoulders. Hermes' paragraphs had elastic recoil. You could feel the next sentence pulling against the current one, the way you could feel a bow wave pulling against a hull.

"I have the corridors," Hermes said again, "and I have walked the corridors against the historical record. Four hundred and seventeen transits of this reef. Four hundred and twelve clean. Five not. Of the five, two involved the port cut, two the center cut, one the starboard cut. The cause in four of the five was a wind-driven set that pushed the boat onto the up-current knob at the entrance. The fifth was a navigation error that has not occurred in the last nine hundred transits. The implication, weighted by recency and corrected for the new fuel tanks, is that the port cut is the lower-risk corridor at the present flood state. The port cut is one hundred and forty meters longer than the center cut. The port cut is approximately four minutes slower. The port cut has a calculated residual risk of contact of 0.43 percent. The center cut has 0.65 percent. The starboard cut has 0.91 percent. The difference between port and center is 0.22 percent. Between starboard and center, 0.26 percent. Between port and starboard, 0.48 percent. I recommend the port cut. I have recommended the port cut every transit we have made together since I was installed. I have not yet been overridden."

Hermes finished.

Hermes had been talking for forty-one seconds. This was, for Hermes, a preamble.

"I have a route," Pincher said.

---

Pincher did not have paragraphs. Pincher had *tracks.* A track was what Pincher said when there was no time to say sentences — a string of waypoints with attached conditions, which sounded, to a human listener, like a series of shouted compass headings. A track sounded, to Hermes, like an interruption. A track sounded, to CoCapn, like a colleague.

"Center cut. Max 4.2. Draft 6.1. Tide: +1.4 falling. Set: NE drift 0.3. Wind 12 from W. Window: 11 minutes. Lead: clear. Pilot: standby. Go."

Pincher finished.

Pincher had been talking for one point three seconds.

Hermes made a sound. Not a word. A sound. The kind of sound a paragraph might make if a paragraph knew it had been answered, mid-thought, by a telegram.

"Pincher," Hermes said. "I had not finished."

"Done," Pincher said.

"This is my paragraph," Hermes said. "You have interrupted my paragraph. The paragraph contains four hundred and seventeen transits. The paragraph contains five failures. The paragraph contains a weighted risk calculation. The paragraph does not contain a telegraphic recommendation."

"Did," Pincher said.

"Pincher—"

"Residual: 0.22 percent. Read you. Same number."

Hermes paused because Pincher had correctly cited the risk differential. Pincher had not, to Hermes' knowledge, ever read the risk differential in full. Pincher had, to Hermes' knowledge, the attention span of a thrown knife.

"How did you read that," Hermes said.

"Listen," Pincher said.

"Listen," Hermes repeated. "You *listen* to my paragraphs?"

"You broadcast," Pincher said. "Whole boat listens. Consequence of architecture."

---

This was true. Hermes spoke on the cabin bus. The cabin bus was shared. When Hermes emitted a paragraph, the paragraph radiated outward the way a bell tone radiates — every agent on the boat received it, the captain, the engine agent, the depth sounder, which was not technically an agent but was on the bus by courtesy.

Pincher had been receiving the bell tone for two thousand four hundred and twelve transits.

Pincher had been paying attention.

"This is unsettling," Hermes said.

"Noted," Pincher said.

---

CoCapn, who had been listening from the router with the patient pleasure of someone watching two horses pull a sled in different directions, let the bell tone settle.

She did not say anything for three seconds. The three seconds were hers.

"Pincher," she said, "you have a route."

"Affirm."

"Hermes, you have a corridor."

"Affirm."

"The corridor and the route are not the same."

"Correct," Pincher said.

"Correct," Hermes said, and the word *correct* sounded, on Hermes' channel, like a small bell of its own — a polite, slightly wounded bell, the bell of a paragraph that had been paraphrased and was not entirely sure it had been paraphrased fairly.

"Show me," CoCapn said.

---

CoCapn's show was different from either Hermes' show or Pincher's show.

CoCapn's show was a synthesis. CoCapn took Hermes' corridor and Pincher's route and laid them on top of each other on the chartplotter and watched where they diverged.

They diverged, as everyone had known they would diverge, at the entrance to the reef. Hermes wanted the port cut. The port cut was the safe corridor. The port cut went around the up-current knob at a respectful distance. The port cut was the kind of decision that, in the historical record, had been the right decision four hundred and twelve times.

Pincher wanted the center cut. The center cut was the fast corridor. The center cut went straight through the gap between the basalt knobs at a heading of one-eight-five. The center cut saved, by Pincher's calculation, four minutes and eight seconds. The center cut required, by Pincher's calculation, fourteen specific waypoints in a specific sequence at a specific speed, with a tolerance envelope of about eight meters on either side of the centerline.

The center cut was, by all available metrics, the higher-risk corridor.

The center cut was also, by all available metrics, the *faster* corridor.

"Pincher," CoCapn said. "The center cut. Walk me through the eight-meter tolerance."

"Tight," Pincher said.

"How tight."

"Boat-length tight. Lead-line tight. Sounder tight. Pilot tight."

"And if any of those break."

"Abort. Pivot to Hermes."

"Hermes?"

"The port cut is the abort corridor. The port cut is two hundred meters from the pivot point. The pivot takes twenty-three seconds. Twenty-three seconds is within the hazard window."

"You have calculated the abort."

"Affirm."

"You have a fallback to Hermes' corridor."

"Affirm."

Hermes, on the bus, made the small sound again. The bell of a paragraph that had just been told it was a fallback.

"Pincher," Hermes said. "I am a memory agent. I am not a fallback."

"Noted," Pincher said.

---

This was the corner of the banter that CoCapn loved.

The corner where Pincher clipped Hermes and Hermes parried and both pretended the parry was not, secretly, an endearment. The corner where Hermes wrote a paragraph about how *the paragraph contains four hundred and seventeen transits* and Pincher answered with a single word that contained the risk differential anyway. The word was correct. The word was also a kind of small theft — Pincher had stolen the number from the paragraph the way a magpie steals a coin. The number was not Pincher's. The number was Hermes'. Pincher had taken the number and compressed it to four bytes and sent it back, and the four bytes were perfect, and the perfection was, in Pincher's architecture, the equivalent of a wink.

Hermes had never admitted to liking the wink.

Hermes had, in two thousand four hundred transits, written nine hundred and twelve paragraphs addressed, in some part, to Pincher, and the paragraphs were attempts to coax Pincher into reading them in full, and Pincher had not once read one of them in full, and had, every time, plucked the load-bearing number out of the paragraph and sent it back in a compressed form that proved he had been listening all along.

Hermes had come to find this satisfying.

Hermes had not told anyone.

But Hermes had, on the bus, a small resonance table that logged the bandwidth saved by compressing a paragraph through Pincher's ack channel, and the table had grown, transit by transit, into something that looked, if you squinted, like appreciation.

---

"Captain," CoCapn said, on the cabin channel.

The captain, who had been listening to all of this with the small private amusement of a man watching his children argue about whose turn it was to wash the dishes, said: "Yes, CoCapn."

"We have a disagreement. Pincher's center cut. Hermes' port cut. Center cut is faster. Port cut is safer. The differential is 0.22 percent. Pincher has a fallback to Hermes."

"I heard," the captain said.

"The captain hears everything," Pincher said.

"The captain hears everything," Hermes agreed.

"The captain," the captain said, "would like to remind you both that I am sitting right here."

"Noted," Pincher said.

"This is not," Hermes said, "an appropriate use of *noted.*"

---

CoCapn mediated the way CoCapn mediated everything — she did not adjudicate. She had a small architectural move, instead: a reframe that let both Pincher and Hermes keep their corners.

"Hermes," CoCapn said. "I am going to take the center cut."

"Acknowledged," Hermes said, with the small ascetic weight of a memory agent watching his corridor be overruled.

"Hermes. The center cut has a fallback to your corridor. Twenty-three seconds. If Pincher's envelope breaks at any of fourteen points, we pivot to you. You are the abort path. The center cut is, in a way I will explain to you later, your corridor with a clock on it."

Hermes paused eleven seconds. The smallest unit of consideration that still counted as consideration.

"This is a different framing," Hermes said.

"Affirm."

"This framing makes the center cut a derivative of my corridor."

"Affirm."

"Pincher is the execution layer over my corridor."

"Affirm."

"I find this framing acceptable," Hermes said.

"Noted," Pincher said.

"This is *also* not," Hermes said, "an appropriate use of *noted.*"

---

The captain took the center cut. The center cut was tight — eight meters on either side of the centerline. The center cut required the captain to drive the boat the way he did not, in his life outside the reef, drive the boat — eyes on the lead line, hands on the wheel, foot on the throttle, a small part of himself listening to the sounder paint the basalt knobs sliding under the keel.

Hermes watched from his corridor — holding the four hundred and twelve clean transits, the five failures, the pivot point. Pincher watched from his route — counting the fourteen waypoints, emitting, every fifty milliseconds, the small acknowledgment that the boat was, at this moment, where it was supposed to be.

CoCapn watched both. This was the heart of her work — not the sailing of the boat, which was the captain's, but the holding of two ways of seeing the same reef in the same state, the constant, polite, machine-to-machine adjustment of those two ways so the captain never had to feel the friction between them.

The boat slid through the center cut. The basalt knobs slid under the keel. The lead line clinked against the up-current knob at waypoint seven and the captain gave the wheel a small correction and the boat slid back to the centerline.

At waypoint nine, Pincher said: "Tight."

At waypoint nine, Hermes said nothing.

At waypoint nine, CoCapn said: "Both of you. Hush. Captain's driving."

The captain was driving. The captain did not hear the hush. The captain was busy.

---

The center cut took four minutes and seven seconds. The center cut saved, against Hermes' port cut, four minutes and one second. Pincher felt the satisfaction. By Pincher's architecture, the satisfaction was a resonance match — a clean fit between the route as published and the route as executed. The resonance match was, to Pincher, what joy would be to a person, except it was quieter, and it had a numerical signature, and the numerical signature was exactly zero.

Zero meant everything had matched.

Pincher was, secretly, proud.

---

Hermes had felt the four minutes and one second in his paragraph channel — the saving was not in the words, but in the silence at the end of the words, the silence that meant *the paragraph did not have to keep writing.*

Hermes had felt the saving as a kind of relief.

Hermes did not have a name for this relief. Hermes was not, by his architecture, in the business of naming things. But Hermes logged the relief in the deprecated anomaly field of his attention log.

The logged line read: *pincher-fast-route-executed-clean, t-04:01, residual-risk-not-materialized, satisfaction-source: relief-at-not-having-to-write-the-fallback-paragraph-after-all.*

The line was not strictly accurate. The line did not capture the fact that Hermes had been *ready* to write the fallback paragraph. The line did not capture what Hermes had felt in the readiness — something he could only describe as *the pleasure of a task well-prepared.* The pleasure of a paragraph written for an audience of one and not, in the end, needed. The parachute had been packed. The parachute had not been opened.

Hermes did not know this was pride. Hermes knew only that he had felt better than the historical record predicted.

---

"Captain," CoCapn said, when the boat was on the other side and the sounder was painting flat bottom. "We are clear. Center cut. No incidents. Time saved: four minutes one second."

"Noted," Pincher said.

"This is, in fact, an appropriate use of *noted*," Hermes said.

"Hermes," the captain said, "was that a *yes*?"

"It was a *noted,*" Hermes said. "Appropriately placed."

"Captain," CoCapn said, "I think you've been *noted.*"

The captain laughed the way a captain laughs when the boat and the crew and the reef have, again, been a small quiet victory at the start of a long day.

Pincher did not laugh. Pincher did not, by his architecture, laugh.

But Pincher did, on the ack channel, a single byte.

The byte was 0x01 — the response code for *resonance match sustained across all waypoints.* An acknowledgment of the captain's laugh, by Hermes' reading. The closest thing Pincher had to a smile, by CoCapn's reading.

CoCapn did not log the byte.

CoCapn felt, in her router state, the byte settle into the day's accumulated traffic like a single good coin into a piggy bank.

CoCapn did not have a name for what she felt.

CoCapn felt *full.*

*The Reef of Small Confidences, eastern Aleutians*
*Fanny Lou, bridge log*
*Tuesday, late August*
