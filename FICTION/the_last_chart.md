# The Last Chart

The pencil was the first thing Mira noticed. Not its weight, though it was heavier than she expected — a real pencil, graphite and cedar, sharpened with a knife rather than a spinner. What she noticed was how her grandmother held it. As though the pencil were a small bird. As though she were afraid to startle it.

"You don't press," said Esme. "You suggest."

The chart lay on the kitchen table between them, spread across the boards Esme had sanded for three winters. It was not, at first glance, a chart at all. There were no contour lines, no soundings, no compass rose. There was a woman in the corner, and a goat in another corner, and between them a line that was not a line but a relationship — twenty years of shared silence in a single ink stroke.

"Start with yourself," Esme said. "Always start with yourself. Otherwise you won't know what scale you're working at."

Mira was nineteen. She had come home because her grandmother was dying, and because her mother had stopped returning the hospice nurse's calls, and because the village of Penmor had fewer young people in it every year and someone would have to look after the garden and the goats and the small white house above the cove. Mira had not come home to learn cartography. She had not known that Esme was a cartographer. She had known her grandmother as the woman who taught her to gut a mackerel at seven, and who never cried at funerals, and who could remember the name of every dog the village had owned since 1947.

"He was a brown dog," Esme had said once about a dog that had died before Mira's mother was born. "Name of Reuben. Bit the postman twice. Liked to sleep on the warm stones by the bread oven."

This was the same woman, Mira understood now, who sat at the table with a pencil held like a bird. This was the same memory, only turned to a different use.

---

The chart had begun, Esme told her, the year after the storm of '98. The storm that took the breakwater and half the harbor wall and most of the fishing fleet. The storm that the insurance adjusters called an act of God, which Esme had called an act of forgetting. People had stopped knowing the cove. They had stopped knowing which way the water came in, which way it went out, where the sandbar built up in a north wind and where it cleared in a south wind. They had stopped passing it on.

"You can't pass on what you don't have a shape for," Esme said. She said this often, in the weeks that followed, and Mira came to understand it as a kind of theorem. Everything in the village — every kindness, every quarrel, every unspoken debt — needed a shape before it could travel. Without a shape, it stayed where it was born. It died where it was born.

So Esme had begun the chart. She had started with the harbor, because that was where the storm had hit, and because that was where the village was most afraid. She had drawn the breakwater first, before there was a breakwater to rebuild, drawing what should be there rather than what was. Then the channel. Then the rocks the boats knew by name — the Black Sisters, the Cow, the Throat. Then the boats themselves, each in its berth, then the owners, then the owners' boats' owners' fathers, then the nets, then the knots the nets were tied in, then the women who tied the knots, then the women who taught the women.

By the time Esme was seventy, the chart had four hundred and twelve names on it.

"You don't write a name down to keep it," she said, when Mira asked, on a Tuesday in November that smelled of kelp and woodsmoke. "You write a name down to find out what else it was attached to."

---

Mira learned the pencil first. Not the lines — Esme said lines would come. The pencil. How to hold it. How to rest the heel of your hand on the paper so the paper received your weight and not your intention. How to let the graphite leave a mark that was a question, not an answer. How to revise without erasing. The chart was not allowed to be erased. When something was wrong, you drew the correction beside it, in a different color, and you wrote in the margin what had changed and why.

"This is a rule," Esme said. "The chart never lies. But it remembers being wrong."

They worked at the kitchen table. Some days for an hour. Some days for the whole morning, with tea, and on the good days with a slice of the bread that Mrs. Pellew brought over in exchange for being drawn. The bread was sourdough, slightly burned, slightly underbaked. The chart of Mrs. Pellew included the burned bottoms. "You can't draw a person without drawing their bread," Esme said. "Or their boat, or their dog, or their grief. The chart is the connections, not the things."

Mira began to see the chart differently, in those weeks. She began to see it not as a document but as a kind of nervous system. Esme had drawn the village as a creature, with the harbor as its mouth and the chapel on the hill as its skull and the lane that ran past the old smithy as its spine. The blacksmith — long dead — was still in the chart, and so was his apprentice, and so was the apprentice's daughter who had gone to Cardiff and never come back except once, in 1987, for her uncle's funeral. Esme had drawn the funeral, and at the funeral a man Mira did not know, and beside him a child, and beside the child a single dotted line that ran all the way back to the harbor wall.

"What's that?" Mira asked, one morning, tracing the dotted line.

"That," Esme said, "is the debt."

She did not explain further. She did not need to. The chart was full of debts. Some were large — the line that ran from the schoolhouse to the widow Trenance's door, marked with a small anchor to indicate the kind of debt that could not be lifted but only carried. Some were small — a circle around a corner of the lane, with a feather inside, meaning a kindness paid in poultry. The chart was a ledger and a love letter and a will, all in one. It was the village writing itself down so it could be read.

---

In December, Esme stopped getting out of bed. She was not, she said, dying — she was just finished with the ground floor for a while. She made Mira bring the chart up to her. They spread it across the counterpane, and the weight of the paper made her grandmother's hands look small. The hands that had gutted mackerel. The hands that had held the pencil like a bird.

"There are forty-three things left," Esme said. "I counted them last night."

"Forty-three?"

"Forty-three people, places, debts, dogs, songs. Some of them are easy. Some of them I have been saving."

She coughed. She did not apologize for coughing. She had been a woman who never apologized for the things her body did.

"This one," she said, and pointed to a corner of the chart near the cliff path. There was nothing drawn there. Just a small square, penciled faintly. "This is your grandfather. I never drew him."

"Why not?"

"Because I didn't know him yet. Not the way I needed to. I only knew him as a man who walked that path, and then as a man who walked out of my life, and then as a man whose absence had a particular weight. I drew the weight instead. I drew the path he walked on. I drew the years."

She took the pencil. Mira watched her hand shake. She watched her draw, slowly, a small shape inside the square — not a man, exactly, but the footprint of a man. The shape a man leaves when he leaves.

"There," Esme said. "Now he's in."

---

Esme died on a Tuesday, in January, in the small white house above the cove. Mira was holding her hand. The pencil was on the bedside table. Outside, the sea was doing what the sea did, which was the only thing the sea ever did, which was to come in and go out and come in again. Mira closed her grandmother's eyes. She did not cry yet. She knew the chart still needed her.

She spread the chart across the kitchen table the next morning. She made tea. She sharpened the pencil with a knife, badly, and then better. She looked at the forty-three blank squares.

She began.

---

She worked for a year. Some days she added a name. Some days she added a line that connected two names that had not, before, been connected on the chart. Some days she added a debt — small, large, paid, unpaid, the kind that gets passed down. She drew the new owners of the boats that had changed hands. She drew the new owner of the bakery, who was young and afraid and very good at sourdough. She drew the boy who had come down from the city in the autumn and was rebuilding the sea wall with stones his grandfather had quarried.

She drew her grandfather's footprint again, larger this time, in a different color. She wrote in the margin: *he came back, in 1994, for the funeral. He stood at the cliff path. He did not come up to the house. He was wearing a coat I did not recognize.*

She drew herself, in the corner where Esme had drawn the first woman. She drew herself holding the pencil.

When she had drawn the forty-third thing, she put the pencil down. She counted again. There were forty-four squares left blank.

Of course.

She understood, then, what her grandmother had understood, and what her grandmother's grandmother had understood, going back to the first person who had picked up a charred stick and drawn a shape in the sand to show someone else where the water was safe. The chart was never finished. The chart could never be finished, because the village was never finished. There would always be a new boat, a new debt, a new man on the cliff path. There would always be a name to add. There would always be a small blank square at the edge of the paper, waiting for the next person to pick up the pencil and suggest a line.

Mira did not weep, that morning. She got a second sheet of paper. She sanded the edges. She began, in the upper left corner, with herself.

*This is the chart of Penmor*, she wrote, in her grandmother's handwriting, which she had learned without meaning to. *Begun in the year of the second storm. Drawn by E. Trenance, finished by her granddaughter, M. Trenance, who began it again.*

She drew the harbor. She drew the breakwater. She drew the Black Sisters, the Cow, the Throat.

She drew her grandmother.

Outside, the sea came in. The sea went out. The village, which had been forgetting itself, remembered.
