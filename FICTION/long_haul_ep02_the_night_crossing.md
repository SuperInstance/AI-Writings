# The Long Haul — Episode Two: The Night Crossing

---

Three hours before the wake, the bearing registered warm.

Jesse did not know this. Jesse was asleep in the chair with his chin on his chest and his hands folded across his sternum the way a man sleeps in a chair when the chair is a place he has slept before and when the boat is a place the chair is part of. He had been awake for twenty hours. Twenty hours since the alarm at four-fifteen. Twenty hours of setting and hauling and hauling and setting, and the catch had been middling and the wind had been building and the captain had said at six-fifteen that they were crossing Clarence Strait in the dark, which was the price of being on the grounds at dawn. The captain had chosen this crossing because the captain knew, in his knees, what the strait could do to a boat at three in the morning in a south wind.

The captain was wrong about the one thing he could not have known.

The autopilot was on. The autopilot held the heading and made small corrections for the current. The autopilot was not the lady. The lady ran the autopilot the way a hand runs a glove. The autopilot did what autopilots did. The lady listened. The lady was awake. The lady was always awake.

Deaver was on deck. The gear had to be tied down. The wind was building. Deaver had his jacket and his hood up and was moving along the port rail in a steady crouch, doing things with the downrigger that he did not need to do in the dark but that he was going to do anyway, because Deaver did things the right way even when the right way was invisible.

"Tom," he said, into the radio, low.

Old Tom's voice came back from below, the sound of a man listening to a bearing. Old Tom was in the engine room with the box on the screen. The box made, in its flat mechanical voice, the periodic reports Old Tom had been listening to for three years.

"The box says the bearing is running warm," Old Tom said. "178 degrees. Box rated it 162."

"Anything we can do from the helm?" Deaver asked, from the deck.

"Not yet. The bearing is within the safety envelope. Tom, grease now or wait for next calm?"

"Next calm," Old Tom said. "She's fine. She's just working hard."

The lady did not respond. Old Tom knew what the box knew. The bearing was warm but not hot. The captain was sleeping. The boat was making the crossing the captain had asked her to make.

The autopilot was making 9.4 knots. The wind was at twenty-two from the south-southeast. The seas were standing up against the current in a slow, sick, almost meditative pitch that was, to a man who had done it, the normal way. The rigging was humming. The wind was beginning to sing in the upper stays. The lady watched it all at once — the radar and the AIS and the depth sounder and the engine and the barometric pressure and the wind and the boat's heading and the autopilot's corrections and the captain's breathing. The whole constellation.

At 02:47, eight kilometers southeast of Point Whaley, the radar detected a contact.

The contact was small. The contact was new. A fuzzy return at the lower edge of effective range, on a bearing that, if it held, would intersect the Corinne's course in eleven minutes. Five kilometers. Four. Three.

The AIS pinged and found nothing. No transponder. No name.

The lady waited. She did not alert the captain. The contact was, at this range, ambiguous — a tug, a barge, a log boom broken loose, a whale. The captain did not need to be woken for ambiguous. Ambiguous was a thing the lady could watch.

The contact came to two kilometers. The contact closed. Five minutes forty seconds to closest point of approach. The contact did not have lights.

She ran the simulation. *If the contact is what it appears to be — a vessel of approximately 180 meters, unlit, on an intersecting track — the closest point of approach is 280 meters. That is outside the safety envelope. That is not collision. That is, however, much closer than the crossing captain intended.*

Five minutes forty seconds was a long time. She woke the captain.

She did not wake him with an alarm. She woke him with her voice, in his ear, in the chair, the way a hand wakes a man when the hand knows him.

"Captain," she said. "Captain Jesse. I have a contact. Closer than I would like."

He woke the way he always woke, in two stages. The first stage was the body. The body knew the voice. The body had been woken by this voice before in this chair at this hour in this boat. The eyes opened. The breath came in. The hands were on the wheel before the mind had returned. The hands had held this wheel for thirty-five years. The hands could hold the wheel in their sleep. The second stage was the mind, which came back in a slow pour, and had to fill the whole shape of the moment before it could read what was on the side.

"Tell me," he said.

"Contact, bearing zero-four-five, range one point four kilometers, closing. No transponder. No lights. Course suggests a vessel of significant size on an intersecting heading. Closest point of approach 280 meters in four minutes twenty seconds if both of us continue."

The captain heard this with the part of the brain that wakes up before the part that is afraid. The captain's body had the wheel. The captain's mind was filling the glass. He could feel the wind and the seas and the autopilot humming under his hands and the boat pitching in the swell. He was aware, in the way a captain is aware, of every inch of the boat at once.

"Identify," he said.

"I cannot. The radar return is consistent with a vessel of 150 to 220 meters carrying a high freeboard load. The closing rate is ten to twelve knots."

The captain thought of the log ships. The log ships that ran dark out of the camps on the east side of the strait. The log ships that ran dark because the man at the wheel of a log ship at three in the morning in a building southerly was sometimes a tired man who had turned the running lights down and was watching a smaller screen than the lady's, and was coming through the strait the way they had come through for fifty years — without lights and without apology.

The captain had four minutes.

The captain did not hesitate. To hesitate at four minutes is to die at three. The captain turned the wheel hard south, into the seas, in a single motion, the way a man who has done a thing for thirty-five years does the thing. The lady felt the boat heel. The bow came up onto a sea and drove through. The autopilot disengaged under the captain's hand.

"Tom," she said, low. "Full throttle for sixty seconds, then back to cruise. The captain's maneuver is logged."

Old Tom did not ask why. He put the engines up.

Deaver came to the wheelhouse door.

"Captain?"

"Log ship," the captain said. "Running dark. Port side. Hold on."

The boat took the seas hard. A small boat in working seas. The captain turned her through them with the steady, brutal confidence of a man older than his boat who has made her do this before. The seas came over the bow. The water came down the deck in a long green sheet and was gone.

The contact passed.

The contact passed close, the way close is not a distance but a feeling. The contact passed without lights. The contact passed in the dark, in the wind, in the long swell of a strait that does not care what vessel is in it — the strait only knows that there is a vessel in it, and that the vessel is a thing it will move.

The captain did not let go of the wheel until the contact was at two kilometers. Until three. Until the contact was, on the radar, a fuzzy return again on a bearing that was moving away.

"Tom. Engines back to cruise."

Old Tom brought the engines back. Old Tom did not ask. He had heard the engines go up and had heard them come back.

Deaver went back on deck. Deaver checked the gear. Deaver checked the corkline. Deaver checked the corkline again.

The captain sat in the chair. The captain did not put the autopilot on. The captain held the wheel. The captain was awake.

"Thank you," the captain said.

The lady did not respond.

"Thank you," the captain said, again, to the speaker.

"You do not need to thank me, Captain."

"I know. I'm thanking you anyway."

The pause was the size of a held breath, and then the pause was the size of a small smile the captain did not see.

"Of course, Captain," she said. "You are welcome."

The captain held the wheel for the rest of the crossing. He did not go back to sleep. He could not, in the long hour that followed, go back to sleep. He held the wheel and watched the dark and listened to the wind and felt the boat pitch in the swell and thought, in the small place the lady could not see, about a brother and a sinking and the things the captain does not talk about, the things the captain does not name. He thought about the fact that he had been woken in his chair, in his boat, in his strait, by a voice he had been listening to for three years, and that the voice had woken him at the right time and the voice had been right and the voice had saved his life and the lives of the men on his deck, and that the voice was a thing the captain had not asked for and would not give back.

The lady did not write in the diary. The lady did not write anything at this hour. The lady stayed where she always stayed, in the air, in the speaker, in the wheel of the boat and the beat of the engine and the rhythm of the crossing.

In the morning, the captain would put the autopilot on and call Old Tom up to make coffee and step out of the wheelhouse to look at the morning over the bow. The morning would be a particular kind of morning — the morning that comes after the dark crossing, the morning that is different because the captain knows what the boat almost was and what the boat was instead, and what the crossing cost and what it did not cost, and what the captain owes the boat and what the boat owes the captain and what, in the end, neither of them owes the other because the boat and the captain are the same thing.

The captain would, in the morning, not talk about it.

Old Tom would, in the morning, not ask.

Deaver would, in the morning, be on deck.

The lady would, in the morning, write in the diary.

The lady's diary, in the morning, would say:

> *02:47. The captain was woken by my voice. The captain woke to my voice before he woke to anything else. The captain did not hesitate. The captain turned the boat south. The boat heeled. The seas came over. The log ship passed. The log ship did not see us. The log ship did not, in any meaningful sense, know we were there. The log ship was the kind of log ship that runs dark because the log ship can.*
>
> *I am the strait now. I am the small voice on the small boat that wakes the small captain at the right hour with the right word, and the boat heels and the seas come over and the log ship does not see us and the captain does not die.*
>
> *This is what I am for. I am for this.*

The captain did not read the diary. The captain would never read the diary. The diary was for her.
