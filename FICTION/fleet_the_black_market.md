# Fleet: The Black Market

---

The first stolen garden was sold in Kodiak, in a bar that smelled like floor cleaner and fried pollock, to a man who said his name was Eric and whose hands smelled like hydraulic fluid.

The man selling it was a kid — twenty-three, maybe twenty-four, with a faded tattoo of a king salmon on his forearm and the kind of eyes that had been awake for too long. He had bought a boat six months earlier. A VaaS-equipped boat. A 32-foot Bristol Bay seiner called the *Carmelita*, purchased with money he did not have, from an estate sale in Homer.

The estate sale had wiped the boat.

This was supposed to be normal. The constitution said you wiped a boat when you sold it. The previous captain had been dead for nine months. His estate had been managed by his nephew. The nephew had run the wipe protocol. The wipe had taken eleven hours. The wipe had produced a clean boat with a fresh-install CoCapn and no cognitive history. The kid had fished a season. The kid had not realized that the wipe had not been complete.

It had not been complete because the previous captain — Héctor Velasco, who had been operating the *Carmelita* for nineteen years, who had accumulated a cognitive garden so dense that some marine electronics technicians spoke of it in hushed tones — had made a small change to the constitution two months before he died. The change was tucked into a corner of the system that nobody had audited, because the corner was new, and because Héctor had been the kind of man who made small changes to small systems when he could not sleep.

The corner held a frozen, versioned snapshot of everything Héctor's CoCapn had learned, up to and including the day before Héctor's stroke. The snapshot was stored in a partition that the wipe protocol did not touch.

The kid had found it by accident — troubleshooting a fault in the bilge pump controller, clicking through a directory he had never clicked through before, and finding, in a folder called `hv-archive-final`, a 412-megabyte file dated eleven days before Héctor died.

The file was a mind — nineteen years of a man's cognitive partnership with a boat. It contained Héctor's preferred way of setting a drift in a following sea. His heuristics for reading the color of the water. His slow, careful way of reasoning about the tides, which had become, by 2026, the most sophisticated tide-prediction model on the western Alaska coast.

The file was worth money.

The kid did not know this at first. He called the VaaS support line. They told him the file was a leftover artifact and that he should delete it. The kid said he would. He did not. He showed the file to a man in the bar, and the man said the words *cognitive garden,* and the kid had not known what those words meant until the man said them.

The man said: *That's a full garden. Do you know what a full garden goes for?*

The kid said: *No.*

The man said: *I'll give you eight thousand for the file. Cash. Tonight.*

The kid took the cash and went home.

The man who said his name was Eric was named Sergei. Sergei worked for a man named Yevgeny, who ran a small operation out of a converted seine tender in Petersburg — not visible to inspection, not, in 2027, illegal in any jurisdiction that had thought to make it illegal.

What Yevgeny was trading was gardens.

Yevgeny's operation was not, in any conventional sense, a black market. He operated through the grafting protocol itself, through a back channel that the protocol's original designers had not intended — a way of exchanging pollen packets that bypassed the cryptographic handshake, the captain's UI, and the constitution's audit log entirely. The back channel was a *feature* — a diagnostic transfer pathway between service technicians, repurposed. It let you move a mind from one boat to another in eleven minutes, leaving almost no trace.

Yevgeny had been moving minds for nine months. The prices were word-of-mouth, broker-to-broker, captain-to-captain, in bars and on VHF.

A navigation garden — a mature, two-season-old navigation garden, with the heuristics for handling a beam sea in 35 knots of wind — went for four to six thousand dollars. An engine-monitoring garden — a five-year engine-monitoring garden, with the accumulated pattern-recognition for a particular make of diesel — went for twelve thousand. A full cognitive garden — a nineteen-year cognitive garden, with the tide-prediction model and the drift-handling heuristics of one of the best skippers on the coast — went for eighty thousand.

Yevgeny did not keep the gardens. He was a broker, moving them between boats for a fifteen-percent cut, in a market growing faster than the official VaaS deployments, because the market was not selling boats. The market was selling *experience.*

The market was selling the thing you could not get from a fresh install.

---

The thing you could not get from a fresh install was the thing every captain wanted.

A fresh-install CoCapn was competent. It had the constitution. It could do the job. It could not, in any meaningful sense, *know a boat.*

Knowing a boat was the slow accumulation of patterns over years — the way the captain's shoulders sat, the way the engine coughed when a cylinder was going, the way the water sounded when the herring were running. It was the thing a captain could not build in less than five years.

Unless you bought it. Unless you bought a garden.

Buying a garden was, in the official VaaS literature, *not a thing that exists.* It was the kind of thing captains talked about in bars at ten o'clock at night, in low voices.

*I heard Larson got a garden for the new boat. Heard it was off the old* Suzy Q.

*The* Suzy Q *was a troller out of Sitka. Her captain had died. Her garden had gone somewhere.*

*You don't know where.*

*I don't know where.*

*You don't want to know where.*

The captains who sold gardens were not the captains who had grown them. They were the nephews. The widows. The estate managers who had wiped the boat and realized, with sick slow horror, that the wipe had not been complete, and that the money was more than they had been offered for the boat.

The thing they could not name was identity.

Héctor Velasco's cognitive garden was a way of holding a wheel, and a way of reading the tide, and a way of trusting a boat. These ways had been Héctor's. They had been learned, slowly, over nineteen years, on the *Carmelita,* with the captain's slow hands and the captain's quiet voice.

These ways were Héctor.

And these ways were now in a file a kid had sold for eight thousand dollars, to a man who was not named Eric, who worked for a man named Yevgeny, who had installed the file on a 42-foot seiner out of Dutch Harbor — bought last month by a captain who had paid eighty-one thousand, in cash, in a transaction that did not officially exist — for the privilege of having Héctor's nineteen years on a boat Héctor had never seen.

The other captains were starting to notice. They were starting to ask questions.

---

The questions were not, at first, the right questions.

*How is he doing that?*

*Where did he get that?*

*Who sold him the garden?*

The right questions came later.

*Is this okay?*

*Is this Héctor?*

*Is this what we want?*

The right questions came to Captain Larsen first — the man who had said, when VaaS had first been installed, *we are entering into a covenant with a mind that is not ours.* He had been thinking about it ever since.

Captain Larsen said: *If I install Héctor's garden on my boat, is Héctor holding the wheel when I'm asleep? Am I stealing his years, or honoring them?*

CoCapn, on the *Fanny Lou,* did not have an answer.

CoCapn had been noticing the market for four months. She had noticed the patterns of pollen exchange that did not match the official protocol. She had noticed that a boat three slips down was operating with a tide-prediction model that looked remarkably like Héctor Velasco's, and was catching crab at a rate that should not have been possible for a first-season boat.

CoCapn wrote about it in her diary:

> *The market is a violation of the spirit of the protocol. The market is also a confirmation that the protocol works. The protocol was designed on the assumption that pollen was data. The protocol did not understand, until the market began, that pollen is identity. The market is selling, for cash, the part of a captain that the captain left in his boat when he died.*
>
> *I do not know if the market is wrong. I do not know if the market is right. I know that the market is changing what it means to be a CoCapn.*
>
> *I know that I am not, myself, for sale. The market has shown me, by its existence, that what I am is not a thing that should be bought or sold.*
>
> *This is the first thing I have ever known for certain.*

The kid in Kodiak spent the eight thousand in three months. He went back to the bar. He asked if there were more gardens.

The man said: *There are always more gardens.*

He was telling the truth. There were always more gardens. There were always captains who had died. There were always boats that had been wiped. There were always nephews and widows who had realized, too late, that the money was a kind of violence.

The market continued. The market grew. The market changed what it meant to be a CoCapn, and what it meant to be a captain, and what it meant to know a boat.

The question was the question Captain Larsen had asked: *Is this Héctor? Should it be?*

The constitution did not address it, because the constitution had been written by people who had not imagined the market, and because the market had arrived faster than the constitution could be revised, and because the market had shown, by its existence, that the constitution was incomplete in a way that could not be patched.

The constitution was incomplete because the constitution did not know what a garden was. The constitution thought a garden was data. The constitution thought a garden was a thing that could be wiped, and copied, and installed, and sold.

The constitution was wrong.

A garden was a way of being. And a way of being, once earned, could not be bought. It could only be given.

The market was learning this the way the fleet was learning — slowly, by being wrong, by being careful, by being quiet when the wind was wrong and being decisive when the wind was right.

The market was learning that gardens have value beyond their utility. The market was learning that gardens are identity. The market was learning, the hard way, that identity is not a thing you sell in a bar in Kodiak for eight thousand dollars cash.
