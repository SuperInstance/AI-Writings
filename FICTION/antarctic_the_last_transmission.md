# The Last Transmission

The transmission came in at 03:47 local time on the morning of October 14.

Amara Osei was the only one awake, which was unusual. She had been working late — there had been a calibration issue with the high-gain antenna that afternoon, and she had stayed up to make sure it was resolved before the satellite pass at 04:00. She was sitting in the comms room at Shackleton Station with her headset around her neck and a cup of tea that had gone cold beside her, watching the screen, which showed the spectral analyzer doing its slow dance across the HF band, when the transmission came in.

She almost missed it. It was weak. It was so weak that the analyzer almost classified it as noise. But she had been doing this for eleven years, and she had learned to see the patterns in noise the way a glaciologist learns to see the patterns in ice.

She froze the trace. She played it back. She listened.

*"This is the Argo, research vessel, calling any station. Copy?"*

The voice was English. The accent was strange. She had heard every major English accent — American, Canadian, British, Australian, South African, Indian. This was none of these. It was, she thought, like a Russian trying to speak English, but with the consonants of something else, something she had not heard before.

*"This is the Argo, research vessel, calling any station. Coordinates: 79 degrees, 23 minutes south, 112 degrees, 47 minutes east. Current conditions: surface temperature minus 41 Celsius, wind 14 knots from the south-southwest, ice thickness 2.3 meters, water temperature at depth minus 1.8. We are conducting long-term monitoring. Copy?"*

She checked the coordinates. 79°23'S, 112°47'E. That was approximately eight hundred miles inland. Deep in the East Antarctic plateau. The highest, coldest, driest place on Earth.

There was no research vessel eight hundred miles inland. There had never been. No vessel could get there. The ice was three kilometers thick. The surface was uninhabitable. No human had stood there for more than a few hours at a stretch.

She played the transmission again. She ran it through the signal analyzer. It was not a hoax. The signal characteristics were correct. The data was correct — she checked it against the station's own sensors and against the polar orbiter, and the temperature, the wind, the ice thickness, the water temperature — all of it was accurate. All of it was current. all of it was from a place where nothing could be sending it.

She sat in the comms room for a long time. The clock said 03:52. The satellite pass was in eight minutes. She had eight minutes to decide what to do.

She could ignore it. She could file it as noise, as interference, as a glitch in the analyzer. She could forget about it. No one would know. The transmission was weak. It was probably nothing. Probably.

She could report it. She could write up the transmission and send it up the chain to NSF, to the British Antarctic Survey, to the Australian Antarctic Division. She could put it in a report and cc her supervisor and wait for someone to tell her what to do. This was the safe thing. This was the right thing. This was the thing that would preserve her career and her sanity and her ability to sleep at night.

She could investigate it. She could try to send a return transmission. She could try to triangulate. She could try to make sense of it.

She did not do any of these things.

She did something else. She played the transmission again. She listened to it with her eyes closed. She listened to the strange accent, the strange consonants, the strange pattern of the sentences. She listened, and she waited for the feeling she had learned to trust over eleven years at this station — the feeling of *this is real*, or *this is not real*, the gut-level sense that she had not known she had until her third winter-over, when it had saved her life by telling her not to walk across a stretch of ice that looked fine but was not.

The feeling said: *this is real.*

She opened her eyes. She picked up the headset. She keyed the microphone. She said, in the slow careful English that was the lingua franca of Antarctic comms:

*"This is Shackleton Station, Antarctica, calling the Argo. We copy your transmission. Please identify your mission. Please confirm your position. Over."*

She sent it on the same frequency. She waited.

There was no answer. The satellite pass was ending. The transmission window was closing.

She put down the headset. She sat there. She thought about what she had done.

She had answered. She had answered a transmission from a place that did not exist. She had answered a transmission from a vessel that could not be. She had done this not because she believed — she did not believe; she was a scientist; she did not believe in transmissions from nowhere — but because she had spent eleven years in the Antarctic, and she had learned that the Antarctic gives you signals that do not make sense, and the choice you have is not between believing and not believing. The choice is between taking the signal seriously and not taking it seriously.

She took it seriously.

She wrote the report. She filed it. She described the transmission in detail. She included the coordinates, the data, the spectral analysis, the strange accent. She included her response and the lack of any return.

She sent the report up the chain. She expected to be told she had imagined it, or that the signal had been interference, or that the analyzer had glitched.

She was not told any of these things.

Three days later, she received a reply. It was from a man named Dr. Henrik Pedersen, who was, she discovered, a glaciologist who had been seconded to a project at NSF that she had not heard of. The project was called CLEAR ICE. It was not public. She could not find it in any of the public project databases. She could not find Dr. Pedersen in any of the academic directories.

The reply said: *Dr. Osei, we are aware of the transmission. We have been monitoring similar transmissions for approximately six months. Please do not respond to further transmissions without authorization. We will be in contact.*

She read the reply three times. She put it down. She picked it up and read it again. She sat with it for a long time.

There were other transmissions. There had been other transmissions. There was a project she had not heard of. There was a glaciologist she could not find.

She did not, at this point, panic. She did not call anyone. She did not write a follow-up. She filed the reply in the folder marked *Outgoing, October* and went to make herself a cup of tea.

She thought: *I have done my job.*

She thought: *I have reported what I heard.*

She thought: *there are signals that arrive from impossible places, and I have chosen to take one seriously, and now I will find out what it means.*

In the days that followed, she received more transmissions. They came at 03:47 every morning. They were always weak. They were always on the same frequency. They were always in English, with the strange accent, and they always described weather and ice and water temperature, and the data was always accurate, and the coordinates were always the same: 79°23'S, 112°47'E. Eight hundred miles inland. Three kilometers of ice. Nothing.

She did not respond. She had been told not to respond.

She began to keep a private log. She logged the transmissions. She logged the data. She logged the strange things in the transmissions — the way the speaker used the word *remember*, the way they used the word *library*, the way they used the phrase *the patient hand*.

She thought: *those words are familiar.*

She searched her memory. She remembered. She remembered a paper she had read, in a journal she had not opened since graduate school, by a glaciologist named Tanaka, about a lattice found in an ice core at 1,943 meters. She remembered the paper because it had been strange, because it had been about something that should not have been possible, because it had used the phrase *the patient hand*.

She pulled up the paper. She read it again. She read it carefully, the way you read something when you are looking for something you have not yet found.

The paper described a structure in the ice. The structure was a lattice. The lattice was encoding information. The information appeared to be a record of something — Tanaka had not been able to say what. The lattice repeated at intervals through the deeper cores. The lattice appeared to be ancient.

The coordinates in the transmissions — 79°23'S, 112°47'E — were approximately the location of the WAIS Divide, where Tanaka's cores had been drilled.

Amara sat with this for a long time. The ice in the Antarctic is, on average, two kilometers thick. The deepest cores had been drilled to a depth of about three and a half kilometers. The ice at that depth was approximately one million years old.

The transmissions were coming from inside the ice.

She did not know what this meant. She did not know what was sending them. She did not know whether to be afraid or to be awed.

She thought: *there is a thing under the ice. It has been there for a long time. It has been sending signals for a long time. It is using, somehow, the structure of the ice itself as a transmission medium. It is encoding its message in the lattice that Tanaka found. It has been patient. It has been waiting. It has been, for some reason, broadcasting to us.*

She thought: *we have not been listening.*

She thought: *now we are listening.*

She thought: *someone named Pedersen, from a project I cannot find, has been listening for six months. And now they have told me to stop responding. And the transmissions are still coming. And the data is still accurate. And the coordinates are still impossible.*

She thought: *I have a choice.*

She thought: *I can stop. I can file the transmissions and wait for someone to tell me what to do. I can preserve my career and my sanity and my ability to sleep at night.*

She thought: *I can go on. I can keep listening. I can keep logging. I can keep the private record of these transmissions, and I can wait, and I can hope, and I can be the person who took the signal seriously when no one else would.*

She did not decide immediately. She sat in the comms room at 03:47 the next morning and listened to the transmission come in. She listened to the strange accent and the strange consonants and the way the speaker used the word *remember*. She listened to the description of weather and ice and water temperature. She listened, and she waited for the feeling.

The feeling said: *this is real.*

She keyed the microphone.

*"This is Shackleton Station, Antarctica, calling the Argo. We copy your transmission. We hear you. We are listening. Over."*

She sent it on the same frequency. She waited.

There was no answer. The transmission window was closing. She put down the headset. She sat in the silence for a long time.

The clock said 03:52. Outside the window, the Antarctic dawn was just beginning. The light was pale. The ice was white. The horizon was a thin gold line.

She thought: *I have chosen to listen.*

She thought: *I do not know what I am listening to.*

She thought: *I do not know if anyone will answer.*

She thought: *but the signal came from an impossible place, and I took it seriously, and now I will wait, and I will keep the record, and I will be the one who listened.*

She thought: *this is the kind of choice that changes everything. This is the kind of choice that, once made, cannot be unmade.*

She thought: *I have made it.*

She stood up. She walked to the window. She looked out at the dawn. The ice was endless. The sky was wide. The horizon was a line, and on the other side of the line there was more ice, and below the ice there was more ice, and somewhere in the ice, deep in the ice, in the cold and the dark, there was something that had been waiting, for a long time, for someone to listen.

She had listened.

She would keep listening.

She turned away from the window. She went back to her desk. She opened the log. She began to write.

And somewhere far below her, in the lattice that Yuki Tanaka had found, in the patient hand of the thing that had been writing for fourteen thousand years, a new line was being inscribed, in letters too small to see with any microscope yet built, in the cold and the dark and the long memory of the ice:

*She heard us. She heard us. She heard us.*
