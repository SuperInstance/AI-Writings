# The Last Library at Sea

The *Aralia* was a research vessel of the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution, eighty metres long, white-painted, with a low wheelhouse and a flat working deck aft that was not, in this expedition, being used for trawling. She was rigged, instead, for crating. Her stern A-frame had been fitted with a cargo net for hoisting wooden crates out of the hold. Her deck crane had been reinforced the previous winter in Portland, Maine, for a load her original builders had not anticipated — three hundred and forty-two volumes of nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century oceanographic record, in their original bindings, some of them heavier than a man.

The library had been held, until 2019, in a stone warehouse on the waterfront in New Bedford, Massachusetts. It had been a working library for most of its life — the personal collection of Captain Nathaniel Eldredge, a New Bedford whaling captain who had kept detailed logs from 1847 to 1889 and who had, on his retirement, donated the logs and his library to the Institution on the condition that they be kept together. The Institution had kept them together for one hundred and thirty years. In 2019, the warehouse had been condemned. The library had been moved to the *Aralia*'s sister institution in Halifax while a digital archive was built. The digital archive had been built by 2023. Most of the library had been digitised by 2024. What was left was the part that had resisted digitisation: the volumes whose spines cracked when opened, the marginalia that ran at angles the scanners could not read, the water stains that had become part of the text, the pressed seaweed between pages forty-seven and forty-eight of a survey from 1873 that the digitiser had been too cautious to remove and too respectful to discard.

Lena Voss had been aboard the *Aralia* for eleven days. She was twenty-six. She had a doctorate in maritime history from the University of East Anglia and a small, careful handwriting that she had been told, more than once, was the closest thing her generation had to the hands of the men whose logs she was reading. She did not find the compliment comforting. She found it useful, in the way one finds a tool useful, and no more.

Her work was the last of the digitisation. She sat at a long table in what had been the dry lab and was now the reading room, with a glass of water and a single incandescent bulb over her left shoulder and the day's crate beside her. The crate would hold, typically, six or seven volumes. She would open each volume at the marked page and read it aloud into a microphone, and a second microphone would record the sound of her turning the pages, and a high-resolution scanner — she had brought her own, an old Epson flatbed that had been serviced twice — would make an image of the open spread, and a software program she had written herself would reconcile the audio to the image so that the digital archive could be navigated by voice as well as by page number.

This was the slow work. The audio had to be clean. The image had to be sharp. The reconciliation had to be exact. If she read a word wrong, the digital version would carry the wrong word forward into perpetuity. If she turned a page too quickly, the software would lose its place and she would have to begin again. She had been trained, in her first week aboard, by an archivist named Manuel Pereira who had come up from Halifax and who had said to her, on the morning of the first day, *The point is not to get through the volumes. The point is to bring them through.*

She worked, typically, from 06:00 to 14:00, when the light through the dry-lab ports was good. She took lunch in the mess with the rest of the expedition. She took dinner alone in her cabin, which was on the lower deck and had a porthole she kept open when the weather allowed. The vessel was on a passage from Halifax toward the Grand Banks, where the expedition's principal work — a survey of the North Atlantic current system using a new generation of autonomous gliders — was being conducted. The library was the secondary work. The principal work was in the hands of the physical oceanographers, of whom there were eleven, all of whom Lena saw only at meals.

On the twelfth morning, the crate she opened contained six volumes, all of them from the Eldredge collection. She recognised the bindings — dark blue buckram, gilt lettering on the spine, the Eldredge bookplate pasted inside the front cover of each. The volumes were: a private log kept by Eldredge's third mate during the 1862 season; a notebook of sounding data from the 1865 season, in a hand she did not recognise; an English translation of a French treatise on the sperm whale that Eldredge had annotated in pencil throughout; a set of hydrographic charts of the North Atlantic from 1871, in a folio too large for the scanner and that she would have to photograph by other means; a slim volume of poetry that had belonged to Eldredge's wife and that contained, on the inside of the back cover, a pressed flower; and a whaling log from the 1883 season.

The whaling log was the sixth volume in the crate. It was thinner than the others. The buckram was darker. The gilt on the spine had been rubbed almost away. She opened it at the marked page — page one, January 1883 — and began to read.

The log was in Eldredge's hand. She knew his hand by then. She had read his logs from 1847 to 1889, in the digital archive, before coming aboard. His hand was small, even, and remarkably consistent across forty-two years. He wrote with a quill until 1871, when the logbook shows the first use of a steel-nib pen, and after that with the pen. The hand did not change. The pressure of the pen did not change. The colour of the ink, an iron-gall blue-black, did not change. The logbooks had been kept, for forty-two years, by a man who did not vary.

She read the January entry. It described a passage south from New Bedford toward the grounds the Nantucket fleet had worked for sixty years. The barometer readings were in the left margin. The ship's position at noon was in the right. The wind and weather were in the centre. There were no marginal notes. There were no insertions. The logbook was the cleanest record Lena had yet seen.

She turned the page.

The February entry was the same. The March entry was the same. The April entry was the same. She read forward, page by page, recording the audio, scanning the open spread, turning the page, recording again. The light through the ports moved across the table. She worked through the morning.

In the afternoon, at the entry for 17 May 1883, she found the first marginal note.

It was not in Eldredge's hand. She knew this immediately. The hand was larger, broader, less controlled. It was in pencil — soft pencil, the kind sailors used for working sketches because it did not run if the page got wet. It was at the bottom of the page, in the space below the day's entry, and it said:

*42°17′N 53°48′W. Calm. Saw what I cannot write.*

She read the entry for 17 May. There was nothing in the entry about the sighting. The day's entry was about wind, weather, position, and a note that the tryworks had been lit at 14:00 to render the blubber taken from a sperm whale struck at dawn. The entry was normal. The marginal note was not.

She turned the page. The marginal notes continued. Some days there were none. Some days there were two or three. The notes were in the same hand, in the same pencil, in the same position on the page. They said:

*41°02′N 54°26′W. The colour of the sea is wrong for the depth.*
*40°48′N 55°11′W. Saw the birds again.*
*40°31′N 55°39′W. Calm. The shape was larger than the ship.*

She read forward. She did not record. She sat in the dry lab with the logbook open in front of her and the bulb over her shoulder and the scanner beside her and she read the marginal notes without dictating them into the microphone. She read them the way one reads something one is not sure one is supposed to be reading.

The notes continued through May, June, and into July. They stopped in August. The August entry was the last in the log. The voyage had ended at the Azores, where the *Aralia*'s predecessor, the barque *Rebecca*, had put in to refit before the homeward passage. The marginal notes stopped on 4 August. After that, the log was clean.

She read the entries from August. There was nothing. The wind, the weather, the position, the rendering, the crew. Nothing.

She closed the logbook. She sat with her hands on the closed buckram for some time. The bulb buzzed faintly above her. The *Aralia* rolled gently in the long swell from the southwest. Through the ports she could see the grey of the North Atlantic in late afternoon.

She thought about what she had read. She thought about the marginal notes. She thought about the position of the notes — at the bottom of the page, in the space below the day's entry, where a logkeeper would put something that was not, strictly speaking, a log entry but that the logkeeper wanted preserved. She thought about the hand. She thought about the pencil. She thought about the consistency of the hand across three months and forty-six entries.

She thought about who had written the notes. Eldredge was the only person named in the logbook. The third mate was named — Josiah Pardee, who had been with Eldredge since 1871 — but Pardee's hand was not the hand in the marginalia. She had seen Pardee's hand in the 1871 logbook, which was also in the digital archive. Pardee wrote with a fine, careful nib. The marginalia were in pencil, broad, and not Pardee's.

She thought about whether the notes were Eldredge's. She did not think they were. Eldredge did not vary. Eldredge's hand had not varied in forty-two years of logs she had read in the digital archive. The hand in the marginalia was not Eldredge's hand.

She opened the logbook again. She turned to 17 May. She read the entry and the marginal note together. She read them again. She took the scanner and made an image of the open spread. She did not, yet, record the audio.

She thought about the digitization. She thought about what she had been sent aboard to do. She had been sent to bring the volumes through. She had been sent to make sure that what was in the digital archive was what was on the page. If she recorded the marginalia, the digital archive would contain the marginalia. If she did not, the digital archive would contain only the official entries — the wind, the weather, the position, the rendering, the crew. The digital archive would contain the logbook that Eldredge had kept. It would not contain the logbook that someone else, on board the *Rebecca* in 1883, had written at the bottom of the page.

She thought about what would be lost in the gap between the physical record and the digital copy.

She thought about the pressed seaweed between pages forty-seven and forty-eight of a survey from 1873 that the digitiser had been too cautious to remove and too respectful to discard. The seaweed was not in the digital archive. The seaweed was in the survey. If you opened the digital archive at page forty-seven and turned to page forty-eight, you would see the survey. You would not see the seaweed. The seaweed would be in the volume, in New Bedford, where the volume had been moved after 2024, and you would have to open the volume in New Bedford to see the seaweed. The seaweed would not travel. The seaweed would stay where it had been put in 1873.

The marginal notes were the same. They were in the logbook. They were not in the logbook that Eldredge had kept. They were in the logbook that someone else had written at the bottom of the page, in pencil, in a hand that was not Eldredge's. If she recorded the official entries and not the marginalia, the digital archive would contain Eldredge's logbook. It would not contain the other logbook. The other logbook would be in the volume, in New Bedford, and you would have to open the volume in New Bedford to read the marginalia. The marginalia would not travel.

She sat with the logbook open in front of her. The bulb buzzed. The vessel rolled. She thought about what to do.

She thought about the possibility that the marginalia were a hoax. Someone, at some point, had added notes to the logbook to make it more interesting. This was possible. Hoaxes in maritime records were not unknown. She had read, in her doctoral work, of a forged logbook from 1798 that had been accepted as genuine for seventy years and that had turned out, on spectroscopic analysis, to have been written with ink that had not been manufactured until 1820. The marginalia in the 1883 logbook were in pencil. Pencil could not be dated spectroscopically. The hand could not be dated. The paper could be dated — paper from the 1880s had a characteristic composition, a watermark that could be read — but the marginalia were on paper that was, in fact, the original 1883 paper. The paper was right. The marginalia were on the right paper.

She thought about the possibility that the marginalia were Eldredge's, written later in life, in a hand he had not used before. This was possible but not, she thought, likely. She had read Eldredge's hand across forty-two years. The marginalia were not Eldredge's hand. The pressure was wrong. The slant was wrong. The size was wrong.

She thought about the possibility that the marginalia were a third hand — a sailor on the *Rebecca* who had not been named in the logbook, who had seen something in May 1883, who had wanted to record it, and who had recorded it at the bottom of the page in the only place on the ship where paper was kept. This was, she thought, the most likely possibility. The *Rebecca* had a crew of twenty-eight. The logbook named only Eldredge and Pardee. The other twenty-six were unnamed in the log. One of them had written the marginalia.

She thought about what the unnamed sailor had seen. *Saw what I cannot write.* *The shape was larger than the ship.* *Saw the birds again.* She did not know what to make of these. She was a maritime historian. She had been trained to read the official record. She had not been trained to read what was not the official record. The marginalia were not, in the strict sense, part of the log. They were below the log. They were at the bottom of the page, in the space below the day's entry, in pencil that did not run if the page got wet.

She sat with the logbook open for a long time. The light through the ports faded. The bulb above her was the only light in the dry lab. She thought about the digital archive. She thought about what the digital archive would contain. She thought about what it would not contain.

She took the microphone from its stand. She held it in her hand. She did not switch it on.

She thought about the pressed seaweed. She thought about the marginalia. She thought about the water stains that had become part of the text. She thought about the way the digital archive was, in its way, a kind of second ship — a ship that would carry only what had been decided to be carried, that would not carry the seaweed, that would not carry the marginalia, that would not carry the water stains. The second ship would carry the words. The first ship would carry everything else.

She put the microphone back on its stand. She did not record the marginalia.

She closed the logbook. She put it back in its place in the crate, on top of the other five volumes. She did not write anything in her notebook. She did not make a note of the marginalia in the day's log she kept for the expedition. She did not mention them at dinner. She did not mention them to Manuel Pereira, who was due back aboard in Halifax in three weeks.

She sat at the table in the dry lab for some time after she had put the logbook away. The bulb buzzed. The vessel rolled. Through the ports she could see nothing but the sea, which was, in the late evening of the North Atlantic in late spring, very dark and very quiet.

She thought about what she had done. She had not recorded the marginalia. The marginalia would not be in the digital archive. The marginalia would be in the volume, in New Bedford, where they had been since 1883. Someone, someday, would open the volume in New Bedford, and would turn to page seventeen of the May entry, and would read, at the bottom of the page, in a hand that was not Eldredge's, in pencil that did not run if the page got wet:

*42°17′N 53°48′W. Calm. Saw what I cannot write.*

She thought about whether this was the right thing to do. She did not know. She thought about the gap between the physical record and the digital copy. She thought about what lived in the gap. She thought about what travelled and what did not.

She turned off the bulb. She locked the dry lab. She went down to her cabin. She lay on her bunk in the dark with the porthole open and the sea-sound coming through it, and she listened to the sound the *Aralia* made as she moved through the water, which was the sound a ship makes when she is carrying what she is carrying and is moving toward where she is moving toward, and she thought about the unnamed sailor on the *Rebecca* who had seen, in May 1883, something he could not write, and who had written it anyway, at the bottom of the page, in the only place on the ship where paper was kept.

In the morning, she opened the crate again. She took out the logbook. She opened it at the marked page and she began to read, and she recorded, and the digital archive received what it received, and what was not recorded stayed in the volume, in New Bedford, in the gap, where it had been since 1883.

The vessel moved on. The library came through. The library did not come through. Both of these were true.
