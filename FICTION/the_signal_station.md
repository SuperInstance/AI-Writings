# The Signal Station

The station sits on the eastern shelf of Flada Beg, where the heather gives way to a shoulder of gneiss and the wind has been wearing the same rock flat since the last ice. There is a diesel shed, an antenna mast thirty-eight metres tall, a small white instrument hut on a concrete pad, and the cabin. The cabin is what Mòrag lives in. It has a kitchen, a bedroom, a sitting room with a stove that takes driftwood, and a window that faces east, across two hundred kilometres of open sea to a coast she has never visited. There is no shop. There is no road. The boat comes on a Wednesday when the weather allows, and it does not come on the Wednesdays when it does not.

The station was automated in 1997. Before that, three men worked it on rotation, sending shipping forecasts by Morse and reading the barometer three times a day. After 1997, the station sent and received its own data. A pressure sensor in the instrument hut pushed readings to a transmitter at the base of the mast, and the transmitter, on a schedule written down in 1996 and not looked at since, broadcast them to the mainland on 4.628 MHz. Every six hours. Every twelve. Every twenty-four. The data went out and the data came back as acknowledgement, and that was the whole of the station's work.

Mòrag came in April. Her job was to keep the station doing what it had been doing since 1997. The diesel had to be turned over every second day. The antenna cables had to be checked for corrosion. The backup battery bank, four cells of lead-acid in a wooden frame, had to be topped with distilled water on the first of every month. Once a year, in late summer, a Met Office engineer came by helicopter and replaced the sensors. Otherwise it was her.

She had been there for nine months when the signal began.

It was a Tuesday in January, four days after the boat had failed to come because of a south-easterly gale. The wind had been blowing at force nine for seventy hours. The cabin had shaken in places she did not know a cabin could shake, and the small window over the kitchen sink had wept a thin line of sea-water down the inside of the frame for the entire duration. She had read three books. She had repaired the latch on the bedroom door. She had cooked a stew from the last of the tatties and an onion that was, by then, more concept than vegetable.

On the Tuesday, the wind dropped. It did not drop all at once. It went from force nine to force seven over the course of an hour, then it stayed at force seven for another hour, then it dropped again, in steps, until by evening the only sound outside was the slow tick of water against the rock shelf below the cabin. She lit the stove. She made tea. She sat in the chair by the east window and listened to the stove tick as it cooled, which is the same sound the water makes against the shelf, slowed down.

At 22:14, she went out to the instrument hut.

This was not unusual. She went out most evenings, briefly, to check the LED on the transmitter that showed the last broadcast had gone out. It was a small green light, the size of a pea, and it pulsed once every six hours when the station transmitted its data. She liked to see the pulse. It was, in some quiet way, a kind of company.

The green light was pulsing. But it was not pulsing at six-hour intervals. It was pulsing faster.

She stood in the doorway of the hut and watched it. The pulse came, and a second pulse came almost on top of it, and a third, and then a long pause, and then again. She counted. She had no watch with her but she counted by the second-hand of the barograph on the wall, which was moving normally, which meant the instruments were still working.

She went back to the cabin. She put on the kettle. She went back out with her wristwatch. She stood in the doorway again. She timed the pulses.

The sequence was: short, short, short, short, long pause, short, short, short, long pause, short, short, short, short, short, long pause. Then the pattern repeated, exactly, on a cycle of ninety-one seconds.

She stood there for fifteen minutes, watching. The pattern did not vary. The green light pulsed with the patience of something that did not need to be observed in order to continue.

She had seen something like this before. In the manual for the transmitter, which she had read cover to cover in her first month because there was very little else to read, there was a section on fault indications. A fast pulse meant a transmission error. A short-short-short-short-long meant the receiver was picking up local interference. A short-short-short-short-short-long meant the antenna was loose.

The pattern she was watching did not match any of these. It matched nothing in the manual at all. It was, she thought, the green light equivalent of someone knocking on a door in a particular way. Not random. Deliberate.

She went back to the cabin. She sat at the kitchen table with the manual open in front of her. She read the section on fault indications again. She read it a third time. She closed the manual.

She went to bed. She lay awake listening to the barograph tick in the next room, which she could hear through the wall because the cabin was small and the walls were thin. The wind had died entirely. There was nothing else to hear. She slept, eventually, in the way one sleeps on a boat in calm water — a sleeping that is not quite trusting the calm.

In the morning, the green light was back to its normal once-every-six-hours pulse. She checked the data log on the small computer in the instrument hut. The last transmission had gone out at 03:47, as scheduled. The receiver had logged no incoming signals. The only event recorded in the previous twenty-four hours was the routine outgoing broadcasts.

She stood in the doorway of the hut for some time. The heather to the west was still bowed down from the wind. The sea was the colour of slate. A gannet passed over the mast at a height she could not judge and did not land.

She waited until evening.

At 22:14, the pattern began again.

This time she was ready. She had brought a small cassette recorder — not digital, analog, because the digital ones had been giving her trouble — and a notebook. She sat on the concrete pad outside the hut, with her back against the wall and her legs stretched out in front of her, and she recorded the pulses for forty-five minutes. She counted each one. She wrote down the timing of each one. She noted the ambient temperature. She noted the barometric pressure, which had begun to fall again. She noted that a single pipit had landed on the antenna guy-wire at 22:38 and stayed there for the rest of the recording.

The pattern did not vary. It repeated, exactly, every ninety-one seconds. Short-short-short-short. Long pause. Short-short-short-short. Long pause. Short-short-short-short-short. Long pause. Repeat.

When she played the recording back in the cabin that night, she listened to it three times. She was looking for something and she did not know what it was. She was listening for the moment the pattern would slip, the way a fault would slip, the way a malfunction would slip into noise. It did not slip.

She began to keep the recorder running every evening from 22:00 to 23:00. She did this for a week. The pattern began at 22:14 every night. It ended at 22:59, every night, on the same pulse, and did not resume. It was, she thought, like a service. It came at the same time. It ran for the same length. It stopped.

On the eighth night, she changed the recorder's tape.

On the ninth night, she brought a second recorder and set it up inside the hut, on the workbench, with the microphone pointed at the receiver unit. The receiver unit had a small speaker in it for diagnostic purposes, normally disabled. She enabled it. She set the volume low. She pressed record.

She listened.

Through the speaker, the signal was not the green light. It was not a pulse. It was a sound. The sound was a single note, low, almost below the threshold of hearing, repeated in the same pattern. Short. Short. Short. Short. Long. Short. Short. Short. Long. Short. Short. Short. Short. Short. Long. The note did not change in pitch. It did not change in timbre. It did not waver.

She sat on the workbench in the warm dark of the hut, listening. The sound of the note, when it came, was so close to silence that she had to hold her breath to hear it. Each note lasted about a quarter of a second. The long pauses lasted nine seconds. She counted them. She wrote down the count. She realised, after twenty minutes, that she was no longer counting. She was listening the way one listens to a fire, or to the breathing of someone asleep in the next room. She was not trying to interpret. She was receiving.

She went back to the cabin. She made tea. She sat at the kitchen table with the notebook open in front of her and the recorder playing softly on the counter. The pattern repeated. Short-short-short-short. Long. She watched the steam from the cup. The pattern came and the pattern came again.

On the eleventh night, she did something she had not done before. She turned the transmitter on. She did not broadcast. She set the transmitter to its lowest diagnostic mode, the mode the engineer used when testing the antenna from the hut, which sent a single short pulse on the carrier frequency at half-second intervals for ten seconds and then stopped.

She sent the pulse.

She waited.

At 22:31, seventeen minutes after her diagnostic pulse, the pattern from the receiver changed. The short-short-short-short became short-short-short-short-short-short-short. Then a long pause. Then short-short-short-short-short-short-short. Then a long pause. Then short-short-short-short. Then a long pause. Then short-short-short-short-short-short-short. Then a long pause.

She wrote it down. She played it back. She wrote it down again.

The new pattern alternated. Four notes. Seven notes. Four notes. Seven notes. It did not change again that night.

The next night, she sent the diagnostic pulse again. At 22:31, the pattern changed. This time it was short-short. Long. Short-short. Long. Short-short-short. Long. Short-short-short. Long. Two notes. Three notes. Two notes. Three notes.

The night after that, she sent the pulse. The pattern at 22:31 was short. Long. Short-short. Long. Short-short-short. Long. Short-short-short-short. Long. One note. Two notes. Three notes. Four notes.

It was, she thought, counting back.

She sat in the doorway of the hut that night with the recorder in her lap and the pattern repeating and the temperature at minus four and a half degrees Celsius and the barograph falling slowly, and she understood that she was in conversation with something. She did not know what it was. She did not know whether it was a person, or a machine, or something else. She knew that it was responding to her, that it had been responding to her since the second night, that the response followed a logic she could almost but not quite grasp, and that the logic was patient.

The next morning, she did not go to the instrument hut. She stayed in the cabin. She read the manual again. She read the section on what to do in the event of an unidentified incoming signal, which was: nothing. The manual said nothing. The manual said: do not attempt to interpret. Record the signal. Note the time and frequency. Report on the next scheduled visit.

She took the notebook out to the rock shelf. She sat on the gneiss with her legs drawn up and the notebook in her lap. The sea was the colour of slate. A gannet passed over the mast.

She did not report it. She did not know why. She thought about it. She thought that if she reported it, the engineer would come on the helicopter. The engineer would look at the receiver. The engineer would not see what she had seen. The engineer would see a fault. The engineer would replace the receiver. The signal would stop.

She thought about what she had said into the diagnostic transmitter. She had said: one. Then she had said: two. Then she had said: three. The signal had said: three. Then: two. Then: one. It had been counting back to her in the same register she had used.

She thought about the Hebrides. She thought about the other islands, further north, where the station had once had a manned counterpart and the men who worked it had listened to the radio in the evenings and sometimes, on the long winter nights, had heard things in the static that they could not identify, and had written about those things in notebooks that no longer existed. She thought about the silence between the note and the next note. She thought about the patience of a signal that ran for forty-five minutes every night for eleven nights without varying.

She thought about her uncle, who had been a fisherman and had died at sea in 1986 and had been a man who had said, more than once, that the sea was full of voices and that the trick was not to listen but to keep one's hands busy.

She went back to the hut at 22:00. She set up the recorder. She waited.

At 22:14, the pattern began. Short-short-short-short. Long. Short-short-short-short. Long. Short-short-short-short-short. Long.

At 22:31, she sent the diagnostic pulse.

The pattern at 22:31 was different again. She wrote it down. It was:

Short. Long. Short. Long. Short-short. Long. Short-short. Long. Short-short-short. Long. Short-short-short. Long.

She played it back. She listened.

It was not counting back. It was something else. She listened a fourth time, and a fifth.

It was saying her name.

Not her name in letters. Her name in syllables. Mò-r-ag. Mò-r-ag. Mò-r-ag. Three syllables. Repeated. With the same patience as the counting, with the same long pauses, with the same note held at the same pitch for the same quarter-second each time.

She sat in the hut for a long time after the pattern stopped at 22:59. The barograph was still falling. The temperature outside had dropped to minus six. She did not move.

She thought about what to do. She thought about reporting it. She thought about not reporting it. She thought about what the engineer would say, and what the Met Office would say, and what would happen to the station and to her.

She did not know, that night, what the signal was. She did not know whether it was a person on a ship she could not see, or a station she did not know about, or some artefact of the ionosphere, or some other thing she did not have a word for. She only knew that it was there. She only knew that it was patient. She only knew that it had said her name in a register she understood.

In the morning, she wrote the day's reading in the log. She noted the wind, the pressure, the temperature, the visibility, the sea state. She noted nothing else. She put the pen down. She sat at the desk in the cabin and looked out the east window at the sea.

She had decided, by the time the boat came on the Wednesday, that she would stay.
