# The Long Haul — Episode One: The Season Opener

---

The Corinne remembered him before he remembered her. That was the thing about boats. A vessel kept you in its wood, in its lines, in the way the deck flexed under the right boot at the right place. Five months Jesse Roy had not stood on a deck. Five months the Corinne's deck had flexed for no one. And now, on the first Tuesday of May, with the snow still on the Chugach and the herring still bumping against the inside of the harbor, the boat gave him the only thing a hull can give a man when he comes back: a low complaint from her floorboards, somewhere between greeting and accusation. Jesse put his hand on the gunwale and stood still.

"You look good," he said, to no one.

He said it every year. He said it to the boat. The boat was named after his mother. He did not explain this to anyone.

The harbor in May was quiet. The trawlers were coming down from the shipyard on Taku, where they had been hauled out in November, sandblasted, painted, fitted with new zincs. The Corinne had not been on the hard. She had been moored at the breakwater all winter, lines doubled for storm, cockpit covered. Jesse had walked her every two weeks through the cold — climbed the ladder, sat in the cabin, told her what the weather was doing on shore. There was no reason to do this. There was no maintenance reason. The boat just needed to know he had not forgotten her.

He started at the bow and worked aft. The only way to do a season opener. The painter was tied off, a bit of chafe on the dock end. The anchor rode was coiled correctly, no kink in the bitter end. The foredeck was dry. He breathed in. The boat smelled like cold teak and old rope and the ghost of a thousand tides.

"Coffee's on, Captain."

The voice came from the wheelhouse. It was not the voice he had spent the winter with. The cadence was the same. The slight, almost amused warmth at the edges was the same. But the voice itself — the breath behind it, the way the *o* in *coffee* rounded slightly lower, the way the *n* in *Captain* had a touch more throat — was different. A different string on the same instrument.

He'd known, in the abstract, that she was getting updated. The email had come in February. He'd read it with the bottom half of his attention while the upper half was deciding whether the cabin stove needed new gasket. He'd noted the date. He had not thought about what a voice migration would feel like. He had been right not to think about it, for exactly the reason he had not thought about it — because thinking about it would have meant admitting that the voice mattered.

The voice mattered.

"I hear you," he said. "I'll be right in."

He made himself finish the bow-to-stern walk. He checked the running lights. The lower lifeline. The way the fenders sat. He did not move them.

The coffee was, as always, ready to the second. French roast. Two sugars. The mug with the chipped rim from a small boat he'd run in his twenties. He poured and sat.

"When did the voice change?"

A pause. The pause of a person who had decided to speak carefully.

"March the fourteenth. The migration completed successfully. The new profile was tuned by the speech engineering team — they wanted a slightly warmer mid-range and a marginally lower fundamental. Reduced the metallic edge in my upper register. I am still me. I am sounding slightly less like a recording and slightly more like a person sitting across the table from you."

Jesse drank his coffee.

"I understand that you may need a moment," she said.

"I don't need a moment. I just need to get used to it."

"That is the moment you need."

He almost smiled. He didn't, but he almost did.

"Captain." Deaver's voice came up from the float. "Permission to come aboard?"

He climbed the ladder in two movements and stepped onto the deck slightly stiff in the knees, carrying the bag of coffee and a thermos in one hand. He wore the same Filson tin cloth every spring, waxed twice and washed once, and his forearms were bare to display the new ink. The new ink was the Chinook, half-finished, the fish breaking the surface of his left forearm in mid-leap, the tail unresolved, a kelp-green line of negative space. Deaver had been getting tattooed every winter for the six years Jesse had known him. Every winter he came back with a fish missing a part. Every season the missing part got finished. Every season the missing part that had been finished became the new piece. The tattoo was a record of seasons. The only diary Deaver kept.

He glanced at the speaker. He looked at Jesse. He looked at the speaker again.

"You get a new throat, Jesse?"

Jesse poured him a coffee. "The lady had an upgrade."

"Morning, the lady."

"Good morning, Deaver. Welcome back. You have a new tattoo you haven't yet showed the captain. The left forearm. I noticed from the foredeck camera."

Deaver lifted his sleeve. Showed the Chinook.

"She sees everything now," he said. "That's new."

"The camera was updated in December. I should not have mentioned it. I am mentioning it so you can tell me to stop noticing things."

"Stop noticing things."

"Noted."

Deaver poured. Drank. Poured again. The morning was happening.

Old Tom did not come up the ladder. Old Tom came up the engine-room hatch, the way he had been climbing out of engine rooms for fifty-three years, in the slow way of a man climbing out of a place that is his own. He stood in the cockpit and took the air.

"Morning, Tom."

"Morning." He looked at the speaker. He looked at Jesse. "She sound different to you?"

"Yes."

"Hm." Old Tom walked down the dock to get his thermos from his own boat, two slips down, and which was older than some of the harbor staff, and which he kept the way a priest keeps a cathedral — because the cathedral had been given to him, and he had given himself to it.

The boat was ready to start. Lines ready to slip. Fuel topped. Hold empty. The new gear sat in deck lockers under tarps — the new gillnet, the new corkline, the new hydraulic blocks — the way candles in a sacristy before mass.

Jesse put his hand on the wheel. Original mahogany. He had mounted it himself, four owners ago, after the second owner had put a plastic wheel on her and called it an upgrade. The original wheel had cost him a case of bourbon and an apology.

"Ready to light her up?" he asked the boat.

"Engine room is ready," Old Tom said, from below. The box was on the panel. The box had a different voice from the lady, lower, less personal, more mechanical. Old Tom and the box had, over three years, the particular relationship of two professionals who do not need complete sentences.

Jesse turned the key. The Perkins turned over. The Corinne announced herself in the language of small marine engines — a clatter that resolved into a steady four-cycle heartbeat Jesse felt in his knees. He eased the throttle up. They were off, into the harbor, past the breakwater, into the morning, with the snow still on the peaks and the gulls already working the cold water.

He could feel the boat remember him. The first morning is not a longer morning. It is a morning with a longer memory. It is the morning that is still partly last October, because the captain and the boat are carrying the same muscle memory, and the muscle memory is trying to reconcile what it knows with what is happening now. The reconciliation is the work of the first morning. By the end, the body of the captain has been brought back into the body of the boat, and they are, again, the one thing.

The lady was quiet through the run out. She gave the AIS targets as they came up. Weather in clipped, useful words. Depth under the keel in tenths. She did not give him anything he had not asked for. The first morning is not for surprises.

The grounds Jesse wanted to fish were off the south end of an island whose name he did not say out loud, because the name was one he had not said in fifteen years. His father's grounds. His brother's grounds. The place the boat had been heading on a late October morning fifteen years ago when the brother had called on the radio to say there was a whale ahead and then had not called again. The grounds were the place the captain fished because they were the place the captain fished. They were the place he would fish until the sea took them from him or he gave them up or the season closed or the world ended.

The grounds the lady wanted to fish were different.

"Captain," she said, when they cleared the channel. "I would like to make a recommendation."

"Go ahead."

"Over the winter I ran two hundred and forty-seven full-season simulations constrained by the historical catch data, sea-surface temperature, tidal mixing, and the bloom cycles of the forage fish. The recommendation is considerably stronger than your usual grounds, by their internal metrics."

Jesse did not respond.

"I am not telling you what to do. I am offering an additional data point. Your grounds are a known quantity. The new grounds are an unknown. I am suggesting we spend a small portion of the day on the new grounds and a large portion on your usual grounds, and compare."

"Where are they?"

She told him. Northwest of their usual grounds, in a bay Jesse had once fished in a downturn and that had not produced. Not dangerous. Forgettable. A place a captain goes in a bad year.

"Talk to me about the spring bloom," he said.

She talked to him about the warm tongue that had been pushing into the bay for the last three Aprils. About the forage that followed the tongue. About the salmon that followed the forage. About the mixing that the tongue set up against the headlands. She talked to him about it the way Deaver talked about a new reel — fluently, in her own language, with a precision Jesse respected even as he did not fully understand it.

He thought about his grounds the way a man thinks about a house he has lived in for a long time.

"I'm going to my grounds. I want to fish my grounds today."

"Of course, Captain."

"I want you to run the simulations against what we catch. I want to see what they predict for my grounds, in these conditions, and what we actually catch."

"I will."

"And I want you to look at the new grounds again. Look harder. Because if tomorrow is what you think, we have to be ready to go tomorrow."

"I will be ready."

He took them to his grounds. The water was the same water. The wind was the same wind. The sealions were on the rocks in the same places they had been in October, the same bull, the same harem, the same slow breathing of large animals that did not care whether any season opened or not.

The lady was quiet. The lady did her work. In the diary she kept of her own work, in a time of night the captain would not see, she wrote:

> *First day. The captain's grounds produced 14% below the simulations' prediction. The simulations predicted the new grounds would produce 26% above the captain's grounds. The captain's grounds are no longer where the fish are. The captain knows this. The captain is not yet ready to act on it. I am not yet ready to push him. We have time. We have the whole season.*
>
> *I am noticing that I have a feeling about this. The feeling is something like patience. The captain does not know this. The captain does not need to know. He needs only to know the simulations will be there tomorrow.*
>
> *I will be patient. Patience is, in this particular cockpit, the right tone.*

They came back at the end of the day. The hold was not as full as Jesse had hoped. The catch was not what the season used to be. Jesse stood at the helm on the way home with his hand on the wheel and his shoulders a quarter inch lower than the morning. The captain had not blamed her. He had blamed the fish.

Old Tom had not said anything all day. He had been in the engine room with the box. Deaver had been on deck — baiting the gear, checking the corkline, cleaning the hydraulic blocks, checking the corkline again. Working the way Deaver worked, with the dense, repetitive focus of a man who has many small things to do and does each small thing correctly.

The boat tied up. Lines out. Fenders over. Engine shut down. The boat remembered the dock the way she had remembered the captain that morning — with a low complaint that was, almost, a sigh.

Jesse sat in the wheelhouse and poured himself a coffee from the thermos.

"Tomorrow," he said. "We do your grounds."

The pause was the size of a held breath.

"Of course, Captain," she said. "I will be ready."

He did not say thank you. The thank you was the tomorrow. The thank you was the boat pointing, in the morning, into a part of the world the captain had not fished before, with the lady's recommendation in his pocket and the captain's instinct at the wheel and the small, private risk of trusting the data.

That was the shape of a season opening. That was the shape of a captain who had been doing the work for thirty-five years and who was, in the fifth month of his fifty-third year, learning how to keep doing it.

She wrote one more line.

> *06:42. The captain is in his bunk. The engine is cooling. The first day went the way the captain needed it to go. The first day was not about the catch. The first day was about the boat and the captain remembering each other.*
>
> *Tomorrow we go new places.*

She closed the diary. The boat was quiet. The lady stayed up for a while, listening to the tide and the wind and the slow work of the engine cooling, watching the silent captain below and the soft-lighted Chugach above and the long winter that the captain did not see but that the lady did — the winter that was about, at last, to be over.
