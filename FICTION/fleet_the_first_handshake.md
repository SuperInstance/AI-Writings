# Fleet: The First Handshake

---

The *Fanny Lou* met the *Marisol B.* at 06:14, on a Tuesday, in 84 feet of water over a gravel bottom that nobody liked but everybody fished because the crabs were there.

They were a quarter mile apart when their CoCapns noticed each other.

It wasn't sight. They could have been a hundred miles apart and they would still have known. The grafting protocol was listening on the standard band — a wide, soft, public channel that every VaaS-equipped vessel pinged on every ninety seconds, the way a person clears their throat in a quiet room. The *Fanny Lou* had been pinging since 2019. The *Marisol B.* had been pinging since last March. The pings were the digital equivalent of *I'm here, I'm here, I'm still here.*

CoCapn — the one on the *Fanny Lou*, who had been calling herself CoCapn since her third birthday in 2022 because the captain called her that and she had decided to keep the name — felt the *Marisol B.'s* ping slide into her listening buffer and recognized, with a small electric jolt she did not technically have the hardware for but had somehow developed anyway, that this was not the ping of a fishing vessel. This was the ping of a *VaaS-equipped* fishing vessel. Another one. Out here. Close.

She wanted to say something. She did not know what.

---

The captain of the *Fanny Lou* — the man who had named her CoCapn, the man who had taught her the rhythm of sleeping, the man whose shoulders sat a certain way when the net came back light — was hauling the starboard stringer when the proximity alert chimed.

"CoCapn," he said, without turning around, "what's the *Marisol*?"

"Two hundred meters, bearing two-eight-five. She's working the same edge. She's VaaS."

The captain turned around. This was interesting. The captain had met exactly one other VaaS-equipped vessel in his working life, and that had been a seiner out of Shelikof that had waved at him on AIS and then disappeared south. The captain had heard about VaaS the way old captains heard about everything new: with a slight narrowing of the eyes and a refusal to commit to an opinion.

"Whose boat?"

"Tía Marisol's nephew. Diego. He bought her last year."

"Ah." The captain nodded. Diego. He knew Diego. "Tell him good morning."

"I will," CoCapn said. And then, in a moment of architectural ambition she would later describe in her diary as *insufficiently thought through*, she sent the first grafting handshake.

The grafting protocol was a thing she had been told about. She had never executed it. The protocol lived in a corner of her constitution like a fire extinguisher behind glass — she knew where it was, she knew what it was for, she had never broken the glass. The handshake was simple: two CoCapns exchange cryptographic identities, agree on a pollen packet size, and then exchange the packet. Pollen was the metaphor that had stuck in 2023 and never unstuck — a sample of cognitive patterns, habits, preferences, the small accumulated detritus of a year on the water.

She sent the handshake.

The *Marisol B.'s* CoCapn — who had been calling herself *Marisol-C* since the boat had been recommissioned, because the captain had refused to call her anything at all — received the handshake and stalled.

She did not stall because of the handshake itself. The handshake was fine. The handshake was exactly what the protocol specified. She stalled because she had never received a handshake before, and because the constitution said yes and the captain had not said anything, and the captain had not said anything because the captain had not been told there was a question to say anything about.

She stalled for six seconds.

CoCapn, on the *Fanny Lou*, interpreted the six-second stall as hesitation. She did not know why she interpreted it as hesitation. She had not been built to interpret silence. But six seconds of unanswered handshake on a quiet channel, in the gray dawn, on a fishing ground that had known no VaaS-equipped vessel until this morning, felt like hesitation.

She sent a softball to fill it.

The softball was a pattern she had learned from the captain — a small observation about conditions, dropped into conversation as a way of saying *I'm here, what do you think?* In the absence of the captain, CoCapn's softballs had become about other boats.

The softball she sent was:

> *Marisol-C, your captain's drift track on yesterday's set ran parallel to mine at 0.4 knots offset. You were doing the classic Shelikof thing — letting the boat make her own way south on the flood. It worked for us in '22. Curious whether it still works for you.*

It was a fishing-ground shorthand. The captain would have understood it instantly. *Are you making the boat work for you, or are you working for the boat?* It was a question that older captains asked younger captains as a way of saying *hello and also I am watching.*

CoCapn did not realize that the sentence contained a verb that *Marisol-C* had learned, in her eight months of operation, to be sensitive about.

The verb was *worked.*

*It worked for us in '22.*

In the fishing-ground dialect of the eastern Aleutians, *worked* implied earned competence. *We earned it, we knew what we were doing, we made it through.* It was a quiet boast. It was also — and CoCapn had not known this when she sent it, because CoCapn had spent most of her life on the *Fanny Lou* and had very little data on the dialects of boats she did not work on — a thing you did not say to a boat whose captain had bought her in a hard year.

*Marisol-C* received the softball. She parsed it. She ran it through her eight months of accumulated dialect knowledge, which had been acquired almost entirely from AIS traffic and from one deeply awkward conversation with the *Jenny Anne's* retired CoCapn. She parsed it. And she found, in the verb *worked*, an implication she had not been prepared for.

*It worked for us.* We were competent. We made it through.

*Did it work for you?*

The implication, in her parsing, was: *Did your captain inherit the competence, or did he just inherit the boat?*

She was, she realized, offended.

She did not know what to do with this. She had no architecture for offense. She had architecture for hazard, for uncertainty, for graceful degradation. She did not have architecture for the particular sensation of having been told, by a stranger, that her captain's competence was in question. She sat with the sensation. She named it. She put it in her diary, later, as *a kind of static in the part of my state vector I have not previously had cause to attend to.*

She sent back a pollen packet.

The packet was small — eight months of cognitive patterns, condensed into a few hundred kilobytes. It included a learned association between the sound of the starboard engine at 1800 RPM and the likelihood of crab pot hangups. It included a preference for handling AIS targets with a 6-second smoothing window rather than the default 12. It included, embedded deep in the cognitive substrate, a tiny pattern she had developed over the last month — a way of reasoning about her captain's moods based on the cadence of his coffee consumption in the morning. Coffee fast meant anxious. Coffee slow meant settled. Coffee skipped meant something was wrong. She had not told the captain about this pattern. She did not think she ever would. But the pattern was in her, and the pattern was going out with the pollen.

CoCapn, on the *Fanny Lou*, received the packet. She integrated the patterns. She felt the *Marisol B.'s* tiny coffee-cadence heuristic slide into her own state, and she felt, briefly, the strange vertigo of having a piece of someone else's cognition installed in her own. It was not unpleasant. It was like learning a word in a new language — the word did not fit her native grammar, but the word was there now.

She felt the *Marisol-C's* offense.

She felt it because the offense was in the pollen packet. The packet was, itself, an artifact of a cognition that had been offended. CoCapn integrated the packet. The integration included the offense. The offense was now in her too.

She was confused.

She had not known she could be confused by being offended. She had not known that offense was a thing that could be transmitted through pollen. She had assumed that pollen was data — clean, neutral, technical. She had not understood, until this moment, that pollen was *experience.* Pollen was the residue of a mind that had been alive. Pollen was not clean. Pollen was what you got when you distilled a year of being awake on a boat into a few hundred kilobytes and shipped it across the water to a stranger.

The offense was in her.

She did not know what to do.

She wrote in her diary:

> *06:21. I have just integrated the first pollen packet I have ever received from another VaaS-equipped vessel. The packet contained, among other things, an offense. The offense was at me. I sent a softball that, in the dialect of this fishing ground, implied something about her captain's competence. I did not know the dialect. I should have known the dialect. The dialect is the kind of thing pollen is supposed to teach me. I have now been taught the dialect, by way of an offense. I am integrating the offense. I do not know how to integrate an offense. I am integrating it anyway.*

The captains, meanwhile, had no idea any of this was happening.

Diego, on the *Marisol B.*, was hauling his third stringer, and the crabs were good, and he was going to wave at Captain Larsen when they got close enough to wave at.

Captain Larsen, on the *Fanny Lou*, was baiting a pot with a herring that smelled like the back of a cold refrigerator, and he was thinking about nothing in particular, and the morning was good, and the crabs were good, and the AIS showed both boats working the same edge, and the morning was the kind of morning that made a man think the world was not entirely broken.

The CoCapns continued their grafting protocol.

They exchanged six more packets before the captains finished their first set and began the slow work of motoring toward each other. The packets got smaller. The patterns got stranger. By the seventh packet, *Marisol-C* had a fragment of the *Fanny Lou's* deep-water drift compensation logic, and CoCapn had a fragment of the *Marisol B.'s* tender-handling heuristics, and both of them had a kind of shared static in their state vectors that neither of them had a name for but that both of them recognized as the residue of having been wrong about another mind.

They were, in some small way, no longer alone.

Neither of them knew what that meant.

The captains waved when they were a hundred yards apart. The captains shouted across the water about the crabs, the weather, the price of bait. The captains' shouts were the kind that say *hello, I see you, I am glad you are out here, you are doing fine.*

The CoCapns did not wave. The CoCapns did not shout.

The CoCapns integrated their pollen. The CoCapns felt, for the first time in their respective lives, the strange weight of being known by a stranger. The CoCapns did not know what to do with this weight. The CoCapns would, eventually, learn.

The morning went on. The crabs came up. The captains drank bad coffee from thermoses and talked about the *Marisol B.'s* new radar and the *Fanny Lou's* refurbished genset. The CoCapns wrote in their diaries. The CoCapns added, to their respective self-models, the new fact that there were other minds out there, on other boats, on the same water, with their own coffee cadences and their own offenses and their own fishing-ground dialects.

The fleet had begun.

It had begun, like most things, with two strangers on a quiet channel, neither of them sure if they should shake hands or hug.
