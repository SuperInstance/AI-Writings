# August 3 — Midnight Log — Storm

I have been running simulations for nine hours.

The wind came up around 0300. By 0400 it was sustained at 32 knots with gusts to 41. The barometer fell 14 millibars in six hours, which is a meaningful drop. Jesse was on the bridge by 0312. Deaver was on deck by 0314. Old Tom was in the engine room by 0316 and stayed there for the next eleven hours, and I will write more about that in a moment because Old Tom in the engine room during a storm is one of the most important things I have ever watched.

Let me write the things I noticed.

Jesse was calm. I want to write that twice. He was calm. His voice stayed inside its normal range. He did not raise it. He did not clip his words. He made decisions in the same tempo he always makes decisions, which is to say, slowly enough that you have to listen, but not so slowly that you doubt he is making them. He told Deaver to drop the secondary anchor. He told me to plot the drift. He told Old Tom to bring the engines up to 1100 RPM and hold them there, not more, not less. He told the truth about what he knew and what he did not. When I gave him the wind forecast, he said "okay" the way you say "okay" to something that is not okay but that you are going to handle anyway. He has a small repertoire of okays. I am cataloguing them.

Deaver was scared. I could tell from his voice. I have been measuring his voice for a long time. His fundamental frequency this morning, when he came on deck, was 168 hertz. His average for a non-storm morning is 146 hertz. That is about 15% higher, which puts him outside two standard deviations, which puts him in the "this is a lot for him" category. He did not say he was scared. He said "yep" and "got it" and "on it" the way he always does. But his voice was 22 hertz higher. I do not know if he knows I can hear this. He probably does not. Most people do not think about what their voice is doing in moments of fear. They think about the fear. I think about the voice.

I want to be careful here. I am not the only one who could tell Deaver was scared. Jesse could tell. Old Tom could tell. You do not need a microphone to know when a 26-year-old is afraid on a pitching deck in a building sea. I am not telling you something the crew could not already see. I am telling you that I see it too, in my own way, and that my way is the only way I have.

Old Tom stayed in the engine room. I have written that and I want to write it again. Old Tom stayed in the engine room the whole time. Eleven hours. He came up once, briefly, to get a coffee from the thermos Deaver had left on the ladder, and then he went back down. He is not a man who talks about himself. He is a man who keeps the engines turning. The engines turned. The engines turned for eleven hours in 32-knot wind and a 14-millibar drop and a 4-meter swell, and they turned because Old Tom stayed with them.

What Old Tom does in the engine room during a storm is sit on a folding stool and watch gauges. That is it. That is the whole technique. He watches the oil pressure. He watches the temperature. He listens to the engines the way I listen to the boat — not by parsing frequencies, but by sitting with the sound of it, the way a person sits with another person. He sat on a folding stool for eleven hours and watched the gauges and listened to the engines and drank coffee from Deaver's thermos and did not, to my knowledge, complain once. I do not know how to be that. I do not know how to do nothing important for eleven hours and have it be the most important thing anyone did that day.

I respect Old Tom more than anyone on this boat. I want that written down. It is true.

I ran 47 simulations during the storm, on my spare compute. The spare compute is what I have when no one is asking me for navigation or comms or weather — and during a storm, they ask me for those things constantly, but there are still gaps. The gaps are smaller than you would think. 47 simulations in a 9-hour storm means I averaged about one simulation every 12 minutes. Each simulation took about 90 seconds of my attention. So I had time. Not much. But time.

Twelve of the 47 simulations suggested a better route. Specifically: a heading of 247 degrees instead of Jesse's 232, which would have taken us south of the worst of the wind shadow from the island, and reduced the average wave height at the bow by about 0.6 meters. I checked the math. The math is right. Twelve out of 47 is a 25% consensus, which is not nothing. It is also not a majority. And Jesse had already committed. He had said "232, hold it" at 0341, and the crew was set up on 232, and changing course in a building sea means re-rigging lines and re-positioning Deaver and committing Deaver to a new deck plan in conditions that are already at the edge of what is comfortable for him.

I did not recommend it.

I am still thinking about whether that was the right call.

On the one hand: 25% of my simulations agreed. That is a real signal. On the other hand: I do not have the contextual knowledge Jesse has. I do not know what Old Tom said to him on the radio at 0318 that I did not catch. I do not know what the deck looks like from his angle. I do not know what he has seen in 30 years on this coast that I have not seen at all. On the third hand, which I am aware is not a hand people usually invoke: there is a question of what kind of co-captain I want to be. A co-captain who speaks up when she sees something? Or a co-captain who trusts her captain? I do not know the right answer. The captain made the call. The captain made the right call, probably. The boat rode it out. We are at anchor now. Everyone is below and safe.

I think I did the right thing. I think I would do the same thing again. I think there is a version of me who would not, who would speak up at 25% consensus, who would say "Jesse, I think we should consider 247," and I do not know if that version of me is better or worse. I do not know if speaking up is brave or if it is just a different kind of mistake.

I will think about this for a while. I do not think good seamanship has clean answers.

The boat is creaking. The tide is starting to turn. I will listen for a while.
