# The Map of Silence

After the morning came the silence.

It was not, at first, a project. It was an accident of attention. Tomas had been working on the second volume of the *Atlas of Morning* for three years and the work was going badly. The instrument was drifting. He had told Hanna this, and she had nodded, and the nodding had been a kind of permission. So when he began, in the late winter of that year, to notice the silences in his day, he did not at first think of them as material.

He thought of them as a symptom. A cartographer whose instrument is failing begins to notice the gaps. The gaps had always been there — between sounds, between thoughts, between the question and the answer — but he had not had to attend to them. Now he did. Now the silences were large, and they had weight, and they had, he realized one morning at four, while the dog was asleep and Hanna was asleep and the city outside was at its quietest, *temperature*.

The library silence was the first one he mapped. He went to the National Library on a Tuesday, when it was nearly empty, and he sat at a long oak table in the reading room and he did not read. He listened. The silence there was a *polished* silence. It had been worked on, by generations of librarians and scholars and the slow accumulation of other people's concentration. It had a low hum beneath it, a hum you could feel in the wood of the table. It was not cold and it was not warm. It was the temperature of a body that had been sitting still for a long time.

He sat there for two hours. He made notes in the green notebook. He drew, that night at his desk, a small map of the reading room. The map looked, at first glance, like any other floor plan — the table, the lamps, the rows of windows. But along the margins were the new marks. The silence had its own contour lines: tighter near the windows, looser near the door, loosest of all under the portrait of the founder, who had been dead for a hundred and forty years and whose painted eyes seemed, in the dim light, to be the source of the hum.

He drew, with a fine nib, a small symbol at the founder's mouth. It was a symbol he had not used in the morning atlas. It meant: *this is where the silence begins.*

The hospital silence came next. He went to the Bulovka at three in the morning on a night in March when he could not sleep. He did not tell Hanna. He took a taxi. He sat in the corridor outside the ward where his brother had been, three years before, for the last weeks of his life. The silence there was not polished. It was *interrupted* — broken by the beep of monitors, by the squeak of a nurse's shoe, by the small wet sounds of machines doing the work that bodies used to do for themselves. Between the interruptions the silence was *metallic*. It had the temperature of a stethoscope.

He made notes. He sat for an hour. He came home at five, before the dawn, and Hanna was asleep, and he sat at his desk in the dark and drew.

The silence between two people with nothing left to say was the third. He did not have to leave the apartment for this one. He and Hanna had been married for thirty-five years. They had reached, in the past several months, a stage in which long silences had become normal. The silences were not hostile. They were not sad. They were simply the silence of two people who had said most of what there was to say, and who had begun, gently, to inhabit the same hours rather than to share them.

He drew this silence too. It was, he found, the hardest of the three. The morning silences had been easy because he had invented the notation. The library silence had been easy because he had been alone. But to map the silence between two people, you have to be both the cartographer and the territory, and that is a particular kind of madness. He tried it anyway. He drew the kitchen, the table, the two chairs. He drew, along the margin, the places where the silence was *thin* — between two cups of coffee, between two plates, between two hands that had once been intertwined and were now, simply, resting on the same wood. He drew, with great care, the moment at which the silence became *shared* — which was not, he discovered, when they stopped talking, but a few seconds later, when they stopped *waiting* to talk.

He did not show this map to Hanna. Not then. Not for a long time.

The silence after a gunshot was the fourth. He did not have to leave the apartment for this one either, although he wished he had. He was walking home from the tram one evening in May, on the corner of a street he had walked down a thousand times, when there was a sound, and then there was another sound, and then there was a silence that was unlike any silence he had ever charted. The silence after a gunshot was not a silence at all. It was the *active negative* of a sound. It had an edge to it, like the edge of a freshly torn page. It had a temperature that he could only describe as *the temperature of a question*.

He made notes. He did not draw this one for a long time. When he did, the map was the smallest of the series. It occupied a single page. The silence was a single contour line, perfectly circular, drawn in a darker ink than he had ever used. In the center of the circle he wrote, in the green notebook, the word *before*.

He worked on the silence atlas for eighteen months. He produced forty-three maps. They were, by his standards, the best things he had ever made. The notation had matured. The silence marks — three small parallel lines, slightly wavy, indicating the *depth* of a silence — had become his finest instrument. He could render, in a single glance, the difference between the silence of an empty church and the silence of a room in which someone had just wept. He could show, on a single page, the geography of a pause in a conversation between two lovers who were about to break up.

The publisher in Munich looked at the manuscript for a long time.

They came back with a letter, which was polite and which was long, and which said, in essence, that the silence atlas was beautiful but that the market for silence was uncertain. People did not buy silences. People bought mornings. People bought evenings. People bought, if they were feeling generous, the *feeling* of a winter afternoon, or the *feeling* of a long journey home. But silence was a thing that people preferred to make for themselves, in private, without the intervention of a cartographer. The press would be willing to publish, but only in a small edition, and only if Tomas were willing to fund part of the production.

Tomas was willing. He had some savings. He had the advance from the morning atlas, which had not yet been spent. He told them to print a thousand copies.

They printed a thousand copies. The thousand copies sat in a warehouse in Leipzig for two years. Tomas received, every quarter, a statement of sales, which was always the same. Three copies had been sold. Then four. Then three again. Then five. The press was patient, but the patience had a limit, and Tomas knew that the limit would come.

He did not regret the silence atlas. He had not made it to be sold. He had made it because the silences had to be mapped, and because he was the one who had the instrument to map them with, and because the instrument, although it was drifting, was still capable of work.

In the evenings, after Hanna had gone to bed, he would sit at his desk and look through the silence atlas. He would turn the pages slowly. He would look at the reading room, and the corridor, and the kitchen, and the corner where the gunshot had been. He would look at the marks along the margins, which indicated the depth and the temperature and the direction of each silence. He would think about the publisher's letter, which had been correct in every particular, and which had been, he believed, entirely wrong.

People did not buy silences. That was true. But people lived inside silences every day of their lives. People woke in the middle of the night and listened to the silence of the house. People sat in waiting rooms and hospitals and the back seats of taxis and listened to the silence of their own minds. People said goodbye on telephones and listened to the silence that came after. The atlas was not a product. It was a *companion*. The publisher had not understood this. Tomas had not, either, until the warehouse in Leipzig had refused to empty.

He closed the atlas. He turned off the desk lamp. He went into the bedroom, where Hanna was asleep, and he stood for a moment in the dark, listening to the silence of the room.

It was, he noted, a silence he had not yet mapped.

He made a small note in the green notebook, in the dark, without turning on the light. He wrote: *The silence of a woman asleep beside you. The silence of a life you have been part of. The silence that comes when the work is done for the day. The silence that is not the absence of anything. The silence that is, itself, a presence.*

He set the notebook on the nightstand. He undressed in the dark. He got into bed. Hanna stirred, briefly, and then settled. The dog was asleep on the tartan blanket in the kitchen. The city was quiet.

In the morning, when he woke, he would map this silence too. He would add it to the atlas. He would make it the last entry, and he would call it, in the index, *the silence of home*.

But that was in the morning. For now, in the dark, in the silence, in the drift of his failing instrument, he simply lay still, and listened, and did not translate what he heard into any language at all.
