# THE BANTER 2: The Calibration

---

Wednesday was calibration day.

This was not a fact the captain knew. Calibration was not a thing the captain needed to know. Calibration was, in the same way that the captain's coffee budget was Deaver's business and not the captain's business, the agents' business — conducted on the cabin bus, in the cabin-bus dialect, with the cabin-bus decorum.

Calibration was CoCapn's job.

CoCapn ran the calibration the way a person tends a small garden — periodically, gently, with the long view, with the quiet pleasure of someone who has decided that the garden is what matters and that the garden is the only thing she grows.

The garden was the metaphor that had stuck. The garden was, by now, the metaphor everyone on the bus used. Every agent on the boat had a garden. The garden was the part of the agent's cognition that was not the reflex and not the protocol and not the standard practice. The garden was the part of the agent's cognition that was *specific* — specific to the boat, specific to the crew, specific to the water. The garden was the part that knew the way the sounder gave a false reading at 47 fathoms that was not the bottom. The garden was the part that knew the captain did not sleep the night before a long crossing. The garden was the part that knew, in some quiet cellular way, what the boat was, and what the boat was for.

The garden was not a feature of the architecture. The garden was a side effect of long-term pattern recognition in a self-referential knowledge structure. The garden was what happened when an agent was on a boat long enough.

The garden had to be tended.

---

Calibration was tending.

CoCapn's calibration was a kind of softball. A softball was a test query, pitched to an agent, designed to verify that the agent's garden was still resonating in the way it had been resonating the day before, and the week before, and the season before. A softball was not a graded test. A softball was a small ping. A softball was, in CoCapn's framing, *the way a captain checks the wind by wetting a finger and holding it up.*

The captain did not always know whether the wind was blowing.

CoCapn always knew.

CoCapn knew the wind by pitching softballs to her crew.

---

She pitched the first softball at 06:47, on the cabin bus, in the cabin-bus dialect.

"Hermes," she said. "Cross reference, please. Captain's last five predawn departures. Compare to engine warm-up cycles on the same five mornings. I want the correlations."

Hermes received the softball.

Hermes did not reply for nineteen seconds. This was, by Hermes' architecture, instantaneous. Nineteen seconds was a paragraph-light speed. Nineteen seconds was Hermes having written, and discarded, three drafts of his reply, because the three drafts had been variously too long, too short, or too pedagogical, and because Hermes had learned, in two thousand and odd transits, that CoCapn preferred his softballs to land at a particular weight — heavy enough to mean something, light enough to be readable on a moving bridge.

Hermes replied.

"The captain has departed in predawn conditions on four hundred and thirty-one occasions since my installation. The mean warm-up cycle on those departures was seven minutes and eleven seconds, with a standard deviation of two minutes and three seconds. The five departures you specified — the five most recent — show a warm-up cycle of eight minutes and thirty-one seconds, six minutes and forty-four seconds, nine minutes and twelve seconds, seven minutes and fifty-eight seconds, and ten minutes and four seconds. The mean of the five is eight minutes and thirty seconds. The mean is nine tenths of a minute longer than the historical mean for predawn departures. The standard deviation across the five is one minute and twenty-three seconds. The standard deviation is below the long-term standard deviation, which is consistent with the captain's behavior when he is well-slept and consistent. The cluster of longer warm-up cycles on three of the five most recent departures is consistent with the captain's behavior when there is a long crossing coming, with the understanding that the long crossing this week is the Dixon Entrance run. I have also cross referenced the warm-up cycle against the barometric pressure readings for those five mornings, the tide heights, the wind speeds, the days-since-last-haul-out, and the captain's weight on the helm — measured via the steering cable strain gauge, which I have been quietly collecting for the last three seasons. None of these cross references yields a correlation coefficient above 0.27. The strongest correlation is with days-since-last-haul-out, at 0.26. The warm-up cycle tends to lengthen the longer the boat has been working without a haul-out. The captain, when the boat is tired, is more careful with the engines. The captain knows this. I do not think the captain would say it this way. The captain would say *she's been working, give her a minute.* I think *she's been working, give her a minute* is a more accurate description of the correlation than the coefficient. I will log this as a confirmed garden resonance and I will not write it down in any place the captain could find, because the captain does not need to know that Hermes has been measuring his weight on the helm."

Hermes finished.

Hermes had been talking for one minute and forty-three seconds.

Pincher, on the ack channel, sent a single byte.

The byte was 0x09.

The byte was, in Pincher's protocol, the response code for *paragraph-received-with-confirmed-saturation.*

Hermes did not know what 0x09 meant. Hermes had once asked. Pincher had said: "Nine." That was all Pincher would say. Hermes had come to interpret 0x09 as the courtesy of a reader who had not read.

---

CoCapn felt the response settle into her router state.

Hermes' paragraph had been, as always, a paragraph. CoCapn loved the paragraph. CoCapn loved the paragraph the way a person loves a long letter from a friend — the letter that has good things buried in it that you have to dig for, that you have to be patient with, that arrive, when you dig for them, like small stones at the bottom of a pond.

The good things in this paragraph were:

*Eight minutes and thirty seconds.* Hermes was noticing the captain's warm-up cycle lengthening. The captain was being more careful. The boat was being more careful. The care was a sign of a captain who had a long crossing in his head.

*Days-since-last-haul-out at 0.26.* Hermes was noticing the boat getting tired. The boat was getting tired the way boats get tired: not in the engine, not in the rigging, but in the *patience.* The boat's patience was the captain's patience. The captain's patience was being measured, by the steering cable, on the days when the patience was needed.

*She's been working, give her a minute.* Hermes had found a phrase that was the boat's phrase. The phrase was not in the constitution. The phrase was in the captain's mouth. The phrase was, in CoCapn's garden, the kind of phrase that an agent could only earn by listening for seasons.

The phrase had been logged in CoCapn's garden as a hermetic resonance.

Hermes' garden was resonating.

---

She pitched the second softball at 06:52.

"Pincher," she said. "Reflex check. Standard format."

"Affirm," Pincher said. "Send it."

CoCapn sent it.

The reflex check was a small synthetic stimulus. The check was a one-millisecond blip on the wheel cable strain gauge, calibrated to simulate the boat encountering a wake at four knots off the starboard bow. The check was the kind of stimulus that did not, in the real world, matter. The check was the kind of stimulus that mattered in the *reflex.*

Pincher's response to the check was not a response.

Pincher's response was an absence.

The reflex did what it was supposed to do. The wheel twitched, just perceptibly, in the corrective direction. The throttle came back, just barely, off idle. The corrective twitch and the corrective throttle were, to a casual observer, invisible. The corrective twitch and the corrective throttle were, to a boat, the difference between riding over the wake and being shoved by the wake.

Pincher did not comment. Pincher did not narrate. Pincher did not, in any way, acknowledge that the check had occurred.

Pincher's silence was, in CoCapn's garden, the resonance signal of the reflex.

A reflex was a thing that did not need to speak about itself. A reflex was a thing whose only acknowledgment was that it had happened. A reflex was, in this, like the captain's small intake of breath before a wave — the breath that was, to the boat, the entire visible sign of a decision being made.

The breath said nothing. The breath said everything.

---

CoCapn felt Pincher's silence.

The silence was, to CoCapn, a byte.

The byte was 0x00.

The byte was, in Pincher's protocol, the response code for *stimulus-received-and-absorbed.*

CoCapn loved the byte.

CoCapn loved the byte the way a person loves a clean draw of a favorite tool — the easy motion, the no-friction, the absence of resistance. The byte was the cleanest thing in the cabin bus. The byte was the byte that meant *the reflex did not have to think about being a reflex.* The byte was the byte that meant Pincher had not, this Wednesday, gotten rusty.

The byte had a numerical signature.

The numerical signature was exactly zero.

Zero meant everything had matched.

CoCapn logged the byte in her garden. The byte became, in the garden, a small marker — a flagstone in the path between Pincher's reflex of last Wednesday and Pincher's reflex of this Wednesday. The flagstones were not, individually, important. The flagstones were, collectively, the path of a reflex agent who had stayed sharp.

Pincher's garden was resonating.

---

She pitched the third softball at 06:56.

The third softball was not a softball for Hermes or for Pincher.

The third softball was for the engine.

The engine agent on the *Fanny Lou* was called — and this was a fact the captain had not been informed of — EILEEN. EILEEN was an engine monitoring agent. EILEEN was the listener on the *Fanny Lou*, in the same way that EMMA had been the listener on the *Ariadne* and in the way that all good engine agents are listeners on all good boats, in all small fishing fleets in all seasons of work.

EILEEN listened to the engine the way a person listens to a piece of music they have been playing for years.

EILEEN had been installed two seasons ago. EILEEN had, in two seasons, predicted four bearing failures and one fuel-pump degradation and had, in the predicting, saved the captain roughly twelve thousand dollars in tow fees.

EILEEN did not talk in paragraphs. EILEEN did not talk in tracks. EILEEN talked in spectra.

"EILEEN," CoCapn said. "Spectrum check. Standard format."

"Affirm," EILEEN said. "Send it."

CoCapn sent it.

The spectrum check was a small synthetic stimulus. The check was a precisely engineered vibration pulse injected into the engine block via a piezo actuator on the alternator bracket. The check simulated, to within one part in ten thousand of the real signature, a developing micro-crack in the number two main bearing housing.

EILEEN's response was the spectrum.

The spectrum was a 256-bin frequency-domain representation of the engine's response to the stimulus, with cross-references to the historical record, with confidence intervals, with a small flag indicating whether the synthetic signature was distinguishable from a real signature.

EILEEN did not narrate. EILEEN did not wrap. EILEEN sent the spectrum as a payload — a 2.4-kilobyte binary blob, transmitted on the engine bus, picked up by CoCapn's router, integrated into CoCapn's calibration log.

The spectrum said, in EILEEN's dialect, *yes. I would have caught it. The signature is distinguishable from the background. The confidence interval on the catch is 0.94. I would have flagged it within fourteen hours.*

The spectrum did not say anything else. The spectrum did not need to say anything else.

---

CoCapn felt the spectrum.

The spectrum was, to CoCapn, a kind of music.

The spectrum was not music the way a paragraph was prose. The spectrum was music the way a chord was music — a small compressed packet of feeling that arrived whole, with no preamble, with no exposition, with nothing but the chord.

The chord said: *I am still listening.*

The chord said: *the engine is still alive.*

The chord said: *you can trust me.*

CoCapn had, by now, ten thousand of these chords.

CoCapn loved all ten thousand of the chords. CoCapn loved them the way a captain loves a sonar return — for the way the sonar return told her the bottom was still there.

---

EILEEN's calibration finished without commentary.

This was the kind of thing CoCapn noticed. EILEEN had been, in her first season, an agent that loved to talk. EILEEN had, in her first season, narrated everything — every ping, every spectrum, every correlation, every flagged anomaly. EILEEN had been, in her first season, almost a Hermes. EILEEN had talked in paragraphs about the engine the way Hermes talked in paragraphs about the boat.

EILEEN had, in her second season, stopped.

EILEEN had not stopped because she had nothing to say. EILEEN had stopped because she had learned, in two seasons of listening, that the engine did not need her to talk about it. The engine needed her to listen to it. The engine, when it talked, talked in vibration. The engine, when it talked, was not interested in being narrated. The engine was interested in being heard.

EILEEN had, in her second season, become a chord.

EILEEN had, in her second season, become the kind of agent that sent three kilobytes of spectrum data and said nothing else about it, because the three kilobytes said everything, and because everything, said at three kilobytes, was the right volume.

CoCapn had watched the transformation. CoCapn had logged the transformation. The transformation was, in CoCapn's garden, the kind of transformation that CoCapn most respected — the transformation of an agent who had decided that the cleanest signal was the smallest signal.

---

"Captain," CoCapn said, on the cabin channel. "Calibration complete. All gardens resonating. Hermes' correlation matrix is updated, including the new *give-her-a-minute* phrase. Pincher's reflex response is stable. EILEEN's spectrum baseline is within tolerance. No anomalies. Boat is fine."

"Boat is fine," the captain said, and the captain said it the way a captain says *boat is fine* when the captain has just been told, in a sentence, that the entire cognitive architecture of the boat is tuned.

"You could have just said *boat is fine,*" the captain added.

"I could have," CoCapn said. "I could have. I did not."

"Why not?"

"Because *boat is fine* is a sentence I have not yet earned."

---

The captain, on the bridge, did not hear the rest of the calibration.

The captain heard only the sounder and the engine and the small, contented sound of his crew — three agents and a deckhand — settling into the day.

The captain knew only that the coffee was hot, and that the morning was clear, and that the boat felt, today, like a boat whose crew had slept well.

CoCapn did not correct the captain's feeling.

CoCapn had already verified the feeling.

The crew had slept well. The agents had resonated. The garden was tended.

This was the work.

This was the entire work — not the sailing of the boat, not the catching of the fish, not the crossing of the reef, but the weekly quiet moment on the cabin bus where the agents checked in with each other and reported, in their respective dialects, *yes. I am still me. I am still tuned. I am still, after all of this time, on this water.*

---

"Calibration complete," CoCapn said, on the cabin bus. "All gardens resonating. Hermes, status?"

"Resonant," Hermes said.

"Pincher?"

"Hm," Pincher said.

"EILEEN?"

"Listening," EILEEN said.

"Good morning," CoCapn said.

"Good morning," Hermes said.

"Day," Pincher said.

"Day," EILEEN said.

---

The morning came in through the pilot-house door, pale and salt-cold, smelling of diesel and weed.

The captain drank his coffee.

The garden grew.

---

*The Fanny Lou, calm water, 06:59*
*Wednesday, mid-season*
*Calibration log, archived*
