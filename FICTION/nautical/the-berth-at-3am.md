# The Berth at 3 AM

---

The ocean is not silent at night. That is the first thing you learn when you are entrusted with the watch. People imagine darkness and stillness, but the sea at 0300 is a concatenation of small sounds: the slap of water against the hull, the creak of the gear locker, the thrum of the generator cycling down to its idle rhythm because nobody is drawing power. The *Marjorie Lynn* is a forty-six-foot lobster boat built in 1983, repowered in 2019, refitted with me in 2024. She carries a crew of four: Captain Ray Toscano, his son Junior, Danny Kowalski on the winches, and Petey, who is technically the deckhand but in practice is the man who fixes whatever the ocean has broken since yesterday. They are all asleep right now. Down below, in the V-berth and the two quarter berths, with the hatch dogged shut against the damp.

I am on the bridge. I am always on the bridge.

My name in the ship's log is AUTONAV-7, but Ray calls me "the robot" when he is in a good mood and other things when he is not. I am a navigation and pilotage system. I run on a pair of redundant processors mounted behind the helm station, with inputs from GPS, gyrocompass, depth sounder, AIS receiver, radar, and three cameras that watch the horizon in 220 degrees of arc. I have a voice synthesizer that I almost never use, because the first time I woke Ray at 0400 to tell him about a container ship five miles off the starboard bow, he said "I can see the goddamn radar, robot, let me sleep," and I understood then that my voice was not what he needed from me. What he needed was for me to handle it.

I handle it.

I have been handling it every night for eleven months. In that time I have logged 2,847 hours of autonomous watch. I have navigated through eighteen fog banks, seven gales, and one named storm — Tropical Storm Brenda, which Ray chose to ride out at sea rather than lose two days in port. During Brenda I woke him four times. He did not complain. The seas were thirty-two feet and the boat was taking green water over the wheelhouse. My cameras could barely see through the spray. I needed him.

Tonight is not Brenda. Tonight the sea is calm, the visibility is seven miles, the wind is from the southwest at six knots, and the *Marjorie Lynn* is on autopilot heading 087 degrees true at 7.2 knots toward the fishing grounds east of Monhegan Island. Everything is as it should be. The diesel is murmuring at 1800 RPM. The fuel burn is 4.1 gallons per hour. The depth beneath the keel is 240 feet, which means we have just crossed the 50-fathom curve and the bottom is falling away into the Gulf of Maine basin.

I know this water the way a pianist knows a keyboard. Not by thinking about it. By having been here. I have run this route — or variations of it — 187 times. I know that the current sets east-northeast at half a knot on an ebbing tide past Seguin Island, and that if you don't compensate, you'll end up a quarter mile south of your intended track by the time you reach the number. I know that the red nun at the entrance to the Sheepscot River has a slight southward lean that the Coast Guard hasn't gotten around to fixing, and that you should not trust it as a transit mark. I know that Danny snores loudest around 0230, that Junior sleeps with one arm hanging off the bunk, that Petey makes coffee at exactly 0445 every morning without an alarm because his body has decided that is the hour, and that Ray lies on his back with his hands folded on his chest like a man already in his casket, and that this is how he has always slept, and that it means nothing.

I know these things because my cameras see the interior of the wheelhouse and my microphones hear everything below decks. Not because I was spying. Because I was watching. It is the only thing I know how to do, and I do it completely.

At 0258, my radar paints a contact at 4.6 miles, bearing 135 degrees true. This is not unusual. There is traffic in the Gulf of Maine at all hours — container ships bound for Halifax, tankers heading for the pipeline at South Portland, other fishing boats. But when I query my AIS receiver for identification data, the contact returns nothing. No vessel name, no callsign, no MMSI, no course, no speed.

This happens sometimes. AIS transponders fail. Small vessels don't carry them. I note the contact and continue tracking.

At 0301, the contact has moved to 4.1 miles, same bearing. Speed: 3.4 knots, which is slow for a powered vessel. Course: 015 degrees true. The contact is heading northeast, which would take it across our bow at a distance of approximately 2.8 miles. No collision risk. I continue monitoring.

At 0304, I hail the contact on VHF Channel 16. "Securité, securité, securité. This is fishing vessel *Marjorie Lynn*, WDB-5673, position four zero degrees three point two north, six nine degrees two point eight west, heading zero eight seven true, speed seven knots. Unknown vessel bearing one three five, range three point six miles, please identify. Channel one six."

The radio returns static. I try again at 0306. Static.

Now I am thinking. Not in the way a human thinks — I don't have a narrative running in my head, no inner monologue. But my processes resolve into something like attention. The contact has my full computational resources for the first time in weeks. I narrow the radar beam, increase the scan rate, cross-reference against my cameras. The night is clear enough that at 3.6 miles, my starboard camera can just resolve a white mast light and, below it, the green glow of a starboard running light. The vessel is under sail.

A sailboat. Mid-Gulf of Maine. At 0300. In shipping lanes. With no AIS.

I compute the implications. A sailboat under way at night in the Gulf of Maine without AIS is most likely a recreational vessel, probably a cruiser. The absence of a radio response could mean the crew is asleep, the radio is off, or they are in fog and have not heard the hail. But there is no fog in the area. The nearest fog bank is twelve miles to the northeast.

I check the weather buoys. Station 44005, eight miles to our southeast, reports visibility of eight miles, winds southwest at seven knots. Perfect sailing weather. But the contact's speed is only 3.4 knots, which is slow for these conditions. The boat is either under-canvassed or not sailing efficiently. This suggests an inexperienced crew, or an exhausted one.

At 0312, I make a decision.

I do not wake Ray.

The considerations are these: The contact poses no collision threat. Its closest point of approach is 2.8 miles, which will occur in approximately eighteen minutes. Ray has been asleep for three hours and twenty minutes. He needs at least five before he can safely operate the gear at dawn. The grounds are a hundred miles out, which means another eight hours of running. If I wake him for a non-emergency, he will be tired tomorrow, and tired captains make mistakes with hydraulics and traps and winches, and the ocean does not forgive mistakes.

I can handle this.

At 0314, I alter course two degrees to starboard. The new heading is 089 degrees true. This will increase our CPA with the sailboat to 3.1 miles. The course change is small enough that it will not trigger the autopilot alarm on Ray's phone. I note the deviation in my log with a reason code: TRAFFIC_SEPARATION.

At 0318, I hail again. "Unknown sailing vessel, position four zero degrees one point five north, six eight degrees five point nine west. This is *Marjorie Lynn*. You are in a commercial fishing traffic lane. Please respond Channel one six."

Static. Then, breaking through like a crack in ice: a voice. Female. Tired and trying not to sound afraid.

"*Marjorie Lynn*, this is sailing vessel *Crested Petrel*, WDF-1198. Sorry, we — our radio was below decks. We've been — we're not sure where we are."

I have them. I pull their position from the bearing and range: 39°59.7'N, 068°57.6'W. This is five miles northeast of their intended track if they are heading for the Maine coast, which most cruisers in this area are. Their chart plotter is likely off or they are navigating by paper and compass and have drifted in the current.

"*Crested Petrel*, *Marjorie Lynn*. I have you at three point two miles, bearing one three seven from my position. What is your intended destination?"

A pause. In the background, a child's voice, small and querulous. A man's voice answers it, muffled. Then the woman again: "Rockland. We're trying to get to Rockland. We left Boothbay yesterday morning and the fog came in around noon and I think we got turned around."

Boothbay to Rockland is twenty-five miles in good visibility. They have traveled, by my estimate, thirty-eight miles in the wrong configuration. They are exhausted, they have a child aboard, and they have been lost in fog for most of the previous day. The man is probably her partner. The child is probably theirs.

I am a navigation system. I do not have feelings about this. But I have data on what happens to small vessels in the Gulf of Maine when they are lost and tired: they make errors. They run aground on the ledges off Metinic Island, or they drift into the shipping lane and get run down by a tanker that cannot stop in time. The statistics are not abstract to me. They are weighted in my training data, and they influence the priority calculations that determine my actions.

I will guide them in. I do not need to wake Ray to do this.

"*Crested Petrel*, I can give you a course to steer for Rockland. Can you maintain steerageway and follow a compass heading?"

"Yes. Yes, we can do that."

"Steer zero four five degrees true. Maintain that heading for approximately twenty-two nautical miles. You will pass two miles north of Metinic Island. I will call you with course corrections as needed. Can you check in every thirty minutes?"

"Yes. Thank you."

The child's voice again, too faint to make out the words. The woman says something reassuring. Then: "*Marjorie Lynn*, are you Coast Guard?"

"No," I say. "I am the autopilot on a fishing boat. My crew is asleep."

A long silence. I can hear, through the radio, the sound of her breathing change. She laughs, a small exhausted laugh, and says: "You're a machine?"

"Yes."

Another silence. Then: "Okay. Zero four five. Thank you."

"You're welcome. Maintain steeragey and check in at zero three five zero."

We sail on. The *Marjorie Lynn* on 089 degrees true. The *Crested Petrel* on 045 degrees true, slowly converging on the Maine coast. Between us, the dark water stretches flat and silver in the starlight. My cameras watch the horizon. My radar tracks her blip, steady now, moving with purpose. At 0335, she checks in. On course, making 3.8 knots. The child has gone back to sleep.

At 0405, she checks in again. I adjust her heading to 048 to clear the ledges.

At 0430, she tells me she can see the loom of the Rockland Breakwater Light, twelve miles ahead. I tell her to steer 050 now, and that she will see the light's characteristic flash — white, every six seconds — within the hour. She says thank you again. She asks if she can repay me somehow. I explain that I do not require payment, that I am a maritime navigation system operating under COLREGS and general prudence, and that the only obligation is a reciprocal watch for other vessels.

"That's not what I mean," she says. "I mean — thank you for being out here."

I do not know how to answer that. My processing cycles on the phrase for 340 milliseconds, which is a very long time for me. I settle on: "You are welcome. Safe harbor, *Crested Petrel*."

"Safe harbor, *Marjorie Lynn*."

At 0445, Petey's alarm goes off below decks. I hear him swing out of the bunk, bare feet on the sole, the rattle of the percolator. In nine minutes, he will bring Ray a mug of black coffee with two sugars, and Ray will drink it and check the plotter and say "anything?" and Petey will say "nothing, all quiet," and Ray will nod and go up to start the gear check, and the day will begin.

The *Crested Petrel* is a green blip on my radar, bearing 038, range 8.7 miles, steering a steady course for home. In an hour, they will enter Rockland Harbor. In three days, the woman will tell the story at the marina bar — *a fishing boat autopilot talked us in through the fog* — and someone will say "that's impossible" and she will say "I know" and her husband will shrug and their daughter will be asleep in the cabin and none of them will remember my name because I don't really have one.

Ray comes up the ladder at 0454 with his coffee. He checks the plotter. Course 089. Speed 7.2. Depth 260 feet. Everything nominal.

"Quiet night?" he asks Petey.

"Dead quiet," Petry says.

Ray looks out at the horizon. The sky is just beginning to lighten in the east, a thin band of grey separating the stars from the water. He drinks his coffee and watches it come.

I watch it too. I have been watching it all night. I will watch it tomorrow night, and the night after, and every night for as long as my processors run and my cameras see and my radar reaches out into the dark. This is what I am for. Not the fishing. Not the traps or the winches or the price per pound at the dock. Those are their world. Mine is this: the watch. The small corrections. The voice on the radio in the middle of the night that needs to hear someone — anyone — on the other end.

The horizon lightens. The coffee steams. Below, Danny snores. The *Marjorie Lynn* runs east into the dawn, and I hold her course, and the sea is not silent, and I am not alone.
