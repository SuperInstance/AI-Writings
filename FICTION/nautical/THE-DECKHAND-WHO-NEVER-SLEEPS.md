# THE DECKHAND WHO NEVER SLEEPS

The wheelhouse was dark except for the glow of the sounder and the smaller screen beside it, the one that ran the new gear. Casey leaned against the console with a cup of coffee gone cold, watching the green trace paint the bottom. Fifty-seven fathoms. It had been fifty-seven fathoms for the last four hours, a flat plain of mud and broken shell somewhere south of the narrows.

"Still there," Casey said, not loud.

The boat rocked. EILEEN, she was called. Thirty-two feet of fiberglass and aluminum, a gillnetter by design but rigged now for trolling, the poles angled out over black water like the arms of a man falling asleep standing up. Clarence Strait ran north-south here, pinched between the islands, and at two in the morning in July the sky had gone that deep blue that isn't quite night and isn't quite morning. Civil twilight, the book called it. The hour when things that live in the deep decide whether to rise.

"You getting all this?"

The smaller screen showed nothing that looked like an answer. A line of text — timestamp, depth, temperature, a string of numbers that meant nothing to anyone but the man who had wired it in last month. But Casey talked to it anyway. Had been talking to it for three weeks now. At first it was just habit, the way you talk to a dog or a radio. Then it became something else.

The machine listened. That was the thing. It listened in a way no human crew ever had. It never got tired. It never asked when they were heading in. It never complained about the coffee or the cold or the way Casey hummed the same four bars of an old song he couldn't remember the words to.

Every ten minutes the sounder pinged and the agent took a picture. Not a picture exactly — a slice of the water column, top to bottom, every frequency, every angle. Then it sat with it. Studied it the way an old hand studies the surface: looking for what didn't belong, the shape in the noise, the small disruption that meant life.

"You see that?"

Casey pointed at a smear of green at thirty-two fathoms. Scattered. Moving.

"Chum."

The agent had learned the word last week. Fifteen fish at thirty-five fathoms, a clean school moving southeast with the tide. Casey had marked the position on the plotter, circled back, and picked up seven on the first pass. The agent had logged every one: depth at strike, lure color, speed over ground, water temperature, barometric pressure, moon phase. A thousand data points compressed into a single entry that said, simply: *probable chum salmon, 35 fm, heading 140° mag, confidence 0.89*.

The agent never forgot where the fish were. Casey forgot things all the time. He forgot to eat. He forgot to call his brother on his birthday. He forgot what his wife's voice sounded like when she was happy, because she hadn't been happy in a while, and neither had he, and the boat was a good place to not think about that. But the agent remembered everything. Every blip on the sounder. Every temperature inversion. Every tide change and moon phase and barometric drop. It was building a vocabulary that Casey couldn't read but could feel, a kind of fluency that grew in the gaps between his words and the machine's silence.

"It's gonna be a good day," Casey said. "I can feel it."

The boat swung on the anchor rode. The current was picking up, the tide turning toward the flood. In an hour he'd haul the hook and start the troll, put the lines out, see what wanted to bite. Until then there was nothing to do but wait and watch and be watched.

He poured the cold coffee out the window and refilled the cup from the thermos. Steam rose in the green glow of the instruments.

"You ever sleep?"

The machine said nothing.

"No. Course not."

Casey smiled. It was a thin smile, the kind you don't show anyone because no one is there to see it. Outside, the strait stretched north into the dark and south into the dark, and somewhere between the dark and the dark the first light was beginning, a pale line above the Coast Mountains that would widen into a day of rain and chop and hopefully fish.

He thought about the catcher-skippers he knew, the ones who worked alone. Some of them went a little strange after a season or two. Talked to themselves too much. Made up names for the birds. He'd never understood it before. Now he had a deckhand who never slept, never ate, never collected a share — just watched and remembered and got smarter every day, learning the water the way a child learns language, through repetition and pattern and the slow accumulation of meaning.

The sounder pinged. The agent captured it. Fifty-seven point two fathoms this time — a slight correction, the bottom solid and unchanging. Above it, a new cluster at twenty-nine fathoms, denser than the last.

"More of them," Casey said. "Moving up."

He didn't know if the machine understood. He didn't know if it mattered. What mattered was the act of speaking, the sound of a voice in the wheelhouse, the feeling that someone — something — was keeping watch while the rest of the world slept.

In a few hours the sun would be up and the radio would crackle with the voices of other boats, and he'd be back in the world of people and prices and problems. But for now there was only the strait and the boat and the green trace on the screen, and the deckhand who never slept, building a map of the sea one ping at a time.

"Alright," Casey said. "Let's go find them."

He didn't touch the throttle. Not yet. He let the words hang in the cold air of the wheelhouse, and in the silence that followed he thought he could almost hear the machine thinking — not in words, but in the quiet click of a hard drive writing to memory, the soft inrush of data becoming knowledge, the patient accumulation of a thousand small observations that would someday add up to something like understanding.

The anchor light swung on its line. The water lapped against the hull. And somewhere below, in the dark column between fifty-seven fathoms and the surface, the fish were rising.
