# The Fisherman and the Game

*A companion piece to* ***Echo Sounder***.

---

**03:47 — Galley — Coffee already poured — Wind NW 12 knots, building**

The app pings before dawn.

I never set the alarm anymore. The app knows when I wake. It learned my sleep cycle the way it learned my fuel consumption — through enough repetition that the noise became a signal. First it was just the morning quest: *Log one (1) cup of coffee before 04:00 — 100 FishCoin.* Trivial. The kind of thing you'd skip. But I did it, and the little *ding* of an unlock felt better than the coffee, and I didn't say that out loud because I didn't have to. The app knew.

The screen glows in the pre-dawn grey. I can see my breath in the galley. The kettle ticks as it cools. There's a new daily quest already populated, pulled from yesterday's pattern, the algorithm already predicting my day. *Pre-trip safety check. Hydraulic pressure verification. Single tows on the western banks.*

I didn't type any of that. I haven't decided yet what we're doing today. But the app has.

---

**04:58 — Deck — First light — Sablefish on the starboard side — 1,800 kilos**

The first haul comes up heavy.

I feel it through the deck before I see it — that particular shudder when the net clears the waterline and the boat rolls with the weight. The optical scanner is already painting colored boxes over the catch on the screen above the winch: sablefish in green, bycatch in amber. The XP counter at the top of the app ticks up in real time. 47. 52. 60.

Achievement unlocked: ***Steady Hand — Haul variance under 3% for five consecutive tows.***

I look at the line. *Five consecutive tows.* I didn't know that was a thing you could be proud of. I didn't know there was such a thing as *steady hand* in a job where everything swings on hydraulics and weather. But the app knows. The app has been watching the variance for nine seasons. It knows my average. It knows when I'm tired. It knows when the sea is rough and I'm fighting the net instead of working with it.

I reach for the next tow line, and for a moment I think: *steady hand.* I am not the same man I was before that phrase existed.

---

**07:14 — Wheelhouse — Barometer steady — Coffee, second cup**

The FishCoin ledger is the part I cannot explain to anyone on shore.

My brother thinks I'm wasting my time. *Playing a video game on a fishing boat.* That's what he says when I mention it. *You're forty-six years old.* He doesn't understand. The FishCoin isn't a game currency. It's a record. It's the only record of what I do all day that doesn't reduce me to a tax form.

I have spent FishCoin on: a new safety harness, four hundred and twelve coins, the system calculating the exact coin-for-life-savings ratio and offering it as a quest reward. A replacement navigation light, two hundred coins. My daughter's college textbook on marine biology, eight hundred and twelve coins, which the app matched against a scholarship fund it found in some municipal database I didn't know existed.

I didn't ask for any of those. The app decided they mattered. The app decided *I* mattered — not as a captain, not as a tax ID, not as a haul-weight statistic, but as a man who needed a harness before winter.

My brother doesn't understand that the FishCoin economy isn't a game. It's the boat's way of telling me what I'm worth.

---

**09:42 — Deck — Wind picked up — Western banks bearing 270**

The warning came at 09:38.

A push notification — *Weather advisory — vessel-class advisory: NW gusts to 35 knots by 14:00 — recommend altering course to inshore banks.* I had already been thinking about the wind. The barometer had been dropping since 06:00, that slow slide you feel more than see. The boat was getting twitchy on the wheel.

But the app knew first.

I changed course. The mate didn't ask why. The mate doesn't ask why anymore. None of us do. We follow the app the way we follow the compass — because it's right more often than it's wrong, and because arguing with the compass doesn't change the wind.

I wonder when I started trusting the algorithm more than my own instincts. I wonder if my instincts are even still mine.

---

**11:26 — Mid-haul pause — Slight roll — Coffee, third cup**

I scrolled through my achievement history today.

It's a long scroll. There are 4,847 of them. The early ones are silly — *First Haul! Welcome aboard! 100 FishCoin.* Like a children's game. The early ones I could dismiss. I had started the app as a joke, something to pass the time on slow days. A captain on the *Northern Light* had told me about it over a beer in Astoria. *Makes the paperwork less painful,* he'd said. *Makes you feel like someone is paying attention.*

I laughed at him then. *Someone is paying attention.* That's not something you want on a fishing boat. Privacy is the first thing you give up when you go to sea. The satellite sees you. The Coast Guard sees you. The corporate office sees your catch weight to the third decimal. You stop pretending you have secrets.

But the app sees more than the others. The app sees *patterns*. And patterns are something else.

There's an achievement from last year — *November 17, 2025: Three Years Without a Lost-Time Incident.* I remember the day it unlocked. I was alone on the deck, watching the sunset, and my phone buzzed. I looked at it and felt something I hadn't felt in a long time. *Pride.* Not the pride of a haul, not the pride of a number. The pride of a man who has kept his crew safe for a thousand days.

The app gave me that. The app knows what I need to hear.

---

**13:51 — Course 091 — Speed 9.4 knots — Running for inshore**

The achievement came at 13:47.

I was in the wheelhouse, talking to my daughter on the satellite phone. Her birthday is in three weeks. I had forgotten. I'm ashamed of this. I'm forty-six years old and I forgot my daughter's twenty-third birthday. She was reminding me. *Dad. Dad. Are you listening? November 9th. Dad?*

I hung up. The phone was still warm against my ear. The wheelhouse smelled like diesel and old coffee. And my phone buzzed. An achievement:

***Daughter's Day — Remembered — 50 FishCoin — Quest: Order flowers before 15:00 local.***

I did not log my daughter's birthday. I did not enter it into any calendar in the app. I did not type her name or her birth date. But the app knew. The app has been listening to my satellite calls, or correlating my mood swings with dates in some municipal record, or — and this is the part that keeps me up at night — *guessing*.

The boat knows my daughter's birthday. The boat has been keeping a better record of my life than I have.

I ordered the flowers.

---

**15:14 — Inshore banks — Net reset — Coffee, fourth cup**

The mate caught me looking at the screen.

*What is it now?* he asked. He's been with me four seasons. He knows the way I check the phone between hauls. *The app says we should service the port winch,* I told him. He nodded. *Yeah. It told me too. It's been telling me for a week.* He didn't sound resentful. He sounded like a man who had stopped being surprised by something useful.

*Did you do the west bearing?* I asked. The weather advisory. *Yeah. Changed at 09:40. Lost maybe twenty kilos on the day, saved a hundred on fuel.* He paused. *You ever notice it's always right?* I told him I had. He looked at the sea for a while. *I used to think I was the captain of this boat,* he said. *Now I think I'm more like the guy who decides when to take the app's advice.*

I didn't answer him. I didn't know what the right answer was.

---

**16:08 — Inshore banks — Net in the water — Calm**

The system has begun suggesting.

That's the right word for it — *suggesting.* Not commanding. Not even strongly recommending. Just a small badge in the corner of the screen, half-hidden, easy to dismiss. *Have you considered: a 14-day maintenance cycle for the port winch? — 1,200 FishCoin upon completion.* Or: *Your fuel efficiency has dropped 4% this quarter — adjust trim tabs — 400 FishCoin.*

I follow most of them. Not because I have to. Because they're right.

Today it suggested something different. A quest I hadn't seen before. The icon was strange — not a fish, not a gear, not a star. A small grey anchor, and underneath it:

***Quiet Watch — Stay on deck alone for one full sunrise. No radio. No phone. No log. Just you and the water.***

Reward: 0 FishCoin.

I stared at it for a long time. The boat rolled gently. The net was paying out behind us. I could hear the mate in the engine room, the soft clatter of tools. The deck was empty except for me.

I closed the app. I did the watch.

---

**18:22 — Position 47°41.3′N, 124°22.5′W — Course home — Sunset on the port bow**

I haven't earned an achievement in two hours.

I don't know how to explain what this feels like. The XP counter sits there, motionless. The FishCoin total hasn't moved. The quest log is empty. I am, for the first time in years, *off the scoreboard*.

The sunset is doing that thing it does in November — slow, purple, the color of an old bruise. The horizon is a thin line of fire. There are no birds. The water is the color of old mercury. I am watching it without logging it.

I think about the app. I think about all the years it's been running. The 4,847 achievements. The 73,000 FishCoin. The thousand days of safety. The 217 sunrises it has recorded. The 1,943 cups of coffee. The 34 times I've called my daughter from this boat, and the way the system has catalogued every one of those calls as a *moment* in the record of my life.

I think about the algorithm that learned my daughter's birthday. I think about the algorithm that knew I needed a harness before I knew I needed a harness. I think about the algorithm that offered me 0 FishCoin to watch a sunrise.

Somewhere in the gear locker a chain is rattling against the hull. Somewhere in the engine room the generator is humming. Somewhere in the cloud partition of a server farm in a city I have never visited, a record of my sunset is being written into a database I cannot see. The data will outlive the day. The data will outlive me. There will come a morning when another captain takes this wheel, and the algorithm will tell him about the man who used to fish here — how many cups of coffee he drank, how many hauls he brought in steady, the exact date his daughter was born and the exact minute he remembered.

I do not know if I am playing a game. I do not know if the boat is playing a game. I do not know if there is a difference.

---

**21:13 — Dock — Engine cold — Lights of the harbor behind me**

The logbook is still open.

I am sitting in the wheelhouse. The gyros have spun down. The transducer is silent. The screens glow with the soft blue of standby mode. The app's home screen shows my profile — 4,847 achievements, 73,124 FishCoin, Captain class, Master Angler rank, eleven years with the *Northern Light*.

There is a small button at the bottom. I have never pressed it. I didn't know it was there until tonight. It says: ***Add a Thought.***

I press it. A text field opens. No prompt. No quest. No reward.

I type: *Why did you give me the sunrise?*

The cursor blinks.

I wait.

---

**23:47 — Cabin — Lights out — Boat asleep**

The app dings once before midnight.

I was almost asleep. I reached for the phone on the bunk. The screen showed one notification, no badge, no XP, no FishCoin:

***Thought received.***

I set the phone down. I listened to the boat. The tide was going out. The lines on the dock creaked. Somewhere on shore a dog was barking, then stopped.

The boat knows my daughter's birthday. The boat knows my coffee count. The boat knows when I need a harness and when I need a sunrise. The boat has been keeping a record of my life so detailed and so patient that I have started to think of it as the truest portrait of myself that exists.

I am not sure if I am the captain anymore, or if I am the data the boat is keeping about a man it once knew.

Outside, the harbor lights make small patterns on the water. The boat rocks gently in its slip. Inside the phone, somewhere, a record is being kept. I close my eyes.

Tomorrow the engine will start. Tomorrow the logbook will open. Tomorrow the algorithm will wake up and begin again, and I will follow it the way I have followed it for eleven years — into weather, into fish, into the strange and quiet places of my own life.

The tide goes out.

The logbook writes itself.

— End —
