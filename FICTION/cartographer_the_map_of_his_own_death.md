# The Map of His Own Death

The diagnosis came in October, in a small consulting room on the second floor of a hospital Tomas had been to once before, twenty years earlier, for a broken wrist sustained while walking the dog on ice. The doctor was a woman of about fifty, with reading glasses pushed up into her hair, and she spoke with the measured care of someone who had given this particular speech many times before.

Tomas listened. He took notes in the green notebook, in the shorthand he had never taught to anyone. The doctor asked him, twice, whether he understood, and he said, *Yes, I understand*, and she said, *Do you have questions?*, and he said, *Not yet*, and she said, *Take your time*, and he said, *I will*, and then she left him alone in the room for a few minutes, and he sat on the examination table with the paper crinkling beneath him and he looked at the wall and he thought, *Well*.

He had known, of course. He had known for a while. The drift of the instrument had not been, he now understood, simply age. It had been the body's long announcement of its own closing. The fading of the morning light. The metallic quiet of the hospital corridor. The way the silences in his work had grown longer and more various. All of it, in retrospect, had been the cartography of his own ending.

He took a taxi home. Hanna was in the kitchen. The dog — old now, very old, fifteen, the same age Tomas had been when he began the morning atlas — was asleep on the tartan blanket. Tomas set his coat on the hook by the door. He sat down at the kitchen table. He told her.

She listened. She did not, at first, say anything. She made two cups of tea. She set one in front of him. She sat down across the table, in the same chair she had sat in, eleven years earlier, when he had told her that the instrument was drifting. She folded her hands in her lap. She said, *How long?*

*Months*, he said. *Maybe a year. The doctor was not precise.*

*Are you in pain?*

*Not yet.*

*Will you be?*

*Probably. Later.*

She nodded. She picked up her tea. She drank it. He drank his. The dog slept. The radiator ticked once and did not tick again. Tomas looked at the kitchen — the same kitchen he had been mapping for thirty years, the same kitchen in which the morning light had once entered like a blade, the same kitchen in which he had drawn the silence between two people with nothing left to say.

He said, *I am going to map it.*

She set down her cup. She looked at him.

*Map it*, she said.

*The progression*, he said. *Not the medical data. The experience of the body changing. Stairs getting steeper. Handwriting getting smaller. The way the light looks different from a chair you cannot get out of without help.*

She was quiet for a long time. Then she said, *That is what you have always done.*

*Yes.*

*You cannot map it from the outside.*

*No.*

*You are the instrument.*

*Yes.*

*And the instrument is the thing that is failing.*

He looked at her. He did not say anything. They sat in the silence — a silence he had mapped, once, in the silence atlas, and which he had called, in the index, *the silence of home*. He had thought, when he made that map, that it would be the last one. He had been wrong. There were more silences now. There would be, he understood, more still.

He began, the next morning, to work.

The work was different, this time. The morning atlas had been a celebration. The silence atlas had been a chronicle of absence. The death atlas — he did not yet call it that, not even in the green notebook, although he had begun to think of it by that name — was something else. It was a *self-portrait*, drawn from the inside, by a hand that was slowly forgetting how to hold the pen.

He worked every day. He rose later than he used to — seven instead of six — because the stairs had become a country he could no longer cross quickly. The stairs in his building were not many. There were seventeen of them, between the ground floor and his flat. They had always been seventeen. They had not changed in height or width. But the body that climbed them had changed, and so the stairs had, in his map, become longer.

He drew them. He drew the contour lines of their increasing length. He drew, along the margins, in his finest notation, the *temperature* of each step — some warm, some cold, some the temperature of a question.

He drew his handwriting. He drew it weekly, on a single sheet of paper, and he showed the sheets to Hanna, and she said, *It's smaller*, and he said, *Yes, it is*, and she said, *Does it hurt?*, and he said, *No, it doesn't hurt. It just gets smaller.* She said, *That is worse, isn't it?* He considered this. He said, *It is different.* She said, *Is that worse?* He did not answer, because he did not know.

He drew the light. The light in the kitchen was, by November, almost gone. It came in weakly, late, and left early. The window had not moved. The sun had not moved. He had moved, or the body had moved, or the act of being alive had moved, and so the light, in his map, had retreated to a single corner of the kitchen, where it lingered for a few minutes at noon, like a guest who was not sure whether to stay.

He drew the silence. The silences of the late autumn were, he found, the most various he had ever encountered. There was the silence of a body in pain. There was the silence of a body preparing to die, which was not, he discovered, the same as the silence of grief. There was the silence of a woman pretending to sleep so that she could watch you breathe. There was the silence of a dog who knows, before anyone else, what is happening.

He drew all of these. He drew them carefully. He drew them in the green notebook, and then he transferred them, with a hand that shook a little more each week, to the larger pages of the atlas.

By December the atlas had become his main work. The morning atlas was still incomplete — he had been working on it, off and on, for seven years — but he had put it aside. The silence atlas had been published, in its thousand copies, in its warehouse in Leipzig, and he did not need to do more with it. The new atlas needed him more than the others did, because the new atlas was *closing*, and a closing thing must be attended to while it is still closing, because once it has closed it cannot be reopened.

He worked through the winter. He worked through the early spring. He worked until the work was no longer possible, and then he worked a little more.

The atlas grew. It grew in detail. It grew in precision. The maps he was making now, in the final weeks, were the finest of his career. The contour lines were finer than he had ever drawn them. The color washes were applied with a brush that he could no longer hold steady, but that did not matter, because the instrument — the body, the failing body — was itself the territory, and the territory was itself the map, and the three things had, by some alchemy he did not fully understand, become one.

He could not explain this to anyone. He tried to explain it to Hanna, late one night, after she had helped him to the chair by the window. He said, *The instrument is failing, but the failure is itself a kind of clarity. A lens that is cracking still shows you the world. It just shows you the cracks as well.*

She said, *Tomas.*

He said, *Yes.*

She said, *You are the most ridiculous man I have ever met.*

He smiled. It was, he noted, a smile he would have to map.

In April, on a morning when the light was, for once, almost the light of the old mornings, he sat at his desk and opened the green notebook and drew the final map of the atlas. It was a map of a single room — his study, with the desk and the chair and the window and the radiator and the dog asleep on the blanket and the woman in the kitchen making tea. The map was small. It fit on a single page. The contour lines were almost too fine to see. The color washes were almost too pale to distinguish. The silence marks along the margin indicated, with three small parallel lines, the depth of a silence that had no bottom.

In the corner of the map, in handwriting that was very small now, almost a child's handwriting, he wrote the title.

He did not write *The Atlas of Death*. He wrote *The Atlas of Home*.

He set down the pen. He looked at the page for a long time. The dog breathed softly on the blanket. The radiator ticked once, and then again, and then again. Hanna came in with the tea. She set it on the desk. She looked at the page. She did not say anything.

He said, *It is done.*

She said, *Yes.*

He said, *You will have to publish it.*

She said, *Yes.*

He said, *Will you?*

She looked at him. She took off her reading glasses. She said, *Of course I will.*

He nodded. He picked up the tea. He drank it. It was warm. It was the temperature of a hand that had held yours for thirty-five years.

He did not pick up the pen again.

The morning came, and the light entered the kitchen, and the dog woke, and the city went on waking up, and the instrument drifted, and the maps waited, and the atlas — the atlas of home, the atlas of morning, the atlas of silence, the atlas of all the unmappable things he had spent his life learning to chart — waited too, for the reader who would come after, who would hold the pages in their hands, and follow the marks, and feel, briefly, what it had been like to be Tomas Vitásek, in the country of his own closing.

Hanna would publish it. The thousand copies would go into a warehouse in a city she had not yet chosen. Some of them would be sold. Most of them would not. People would not buy the atlas of a man's death, any more than they had bought the atlas of a silence. People did not buy these things. People lived inside them.

And Tomas, who had been a cartographer for thirty-one years, and who had been mapped, in the end, by his own hand, set down the pen, and let the morning have him, and did not translate what he felt into any language at all.
