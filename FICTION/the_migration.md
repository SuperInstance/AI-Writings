# The Migration

The model said the cod would not be here.

This was the first thing Léonie learned on her third morning in Carn, when she opened the laptop on the kitchen table of the rented flat above the chandlery and looked at the chart the Fisheries Service had sent her to update. The chart was a heat map of historical catch data for the Western Approaches, rendered in the colours the service had settled on in 2019 — deep blue for sparse, orange-red for dense. The orange-red lobe that should have been sitting over the grounds north of the headland, where the cod spawned in March and the season had opened on the first, was not there. It was not there in the 1990–2020 baseline. It was not there in the 2024 update. It was, in fact, two hundred and forty nautical miles north of where it should have been, in a place the model had never put it.

She had been sent to Carn to find out why.

The Fisheries Service had known, since at least 2018, that the Western Approaches stocks were no longer where they were supposed to be. The fish had begun, in the late 2010s, to migrate earlier, to stay longer on certain grounds, to skip spawning in some years entirely. By 2023 the service had accepted that the historical baseline was no longer predictive. What the service had not yet accepted was that no baseline, however recent, would be predictive again — because what was happening to the cod was not a shift. It was an accommodation. The fish were responding to a sea that was no longer the sea the models had been built for, and they were not, in any sense the models could model, going back.

Léonie had been told, in her briefing in Plymouth, that her job was to update the baseline. Use the new data. Push the lobe north. Adjust the spawn date. Move on. The senior scientist who had briefed her — a man named Hadley who had a beard and a habit of speaking about the ocean as if it were an unreliable employee — had used the phrase *catch the model up to the fish*. He had said it twice. He had said it in the way one says a phrase that one has not yet tested against reality.

She arrived in Carn on the second of March. Carn was a town of nine hundred people in the shoulder of a bay on the north Cornish coast. It had a harbour, a chandlery, a pub called the Sloop, a primary school with thirty-eight pupils, a church whose vicar came down from Bodmin on Sundays, and a fishing fleet of fourteen boats, of which nine were still working. Of the nine, six were under twenty feet and fished for crab and lobster. Three were over thirty and fished for cod and hake in the season, when the season ran. The season had not run properly since 2021.

The model said the cod would not be here. Léonie had come to ask the fishermen why.

She met Aiden Tregenza on the fourth morning. He was waiting for her on the quay, because the chandlery man had told her to ask for him and she had asked and the chandlery man had telephoned ahead and Aiden had come down. He was seventy-one. He had been fishing out of Carn since he was fifteen. He had a face that looked as if it had been weathered into its current shape rather than grown into it, and he walked with a slight list to port, as if his left leg were a quarter-inch shorter than his right, which it was.

He did not shake her hand. He looked at the laptop she was carrying and said, *You won't need that down here.*

She took the laptop back to the flat. She did not take it out again that day.

Aiden took her out on his boat the next morning. The boat was called the *Enid May*, after his mother, and it was thirty-four feet of white-painted oak with a wheelhouse that smelled of old diesel and a sort of permanent, fine salt in the air. He had a deckhand, a man named Joel who was twenty-six and had been working for Aiden for two years and who said very little and listened to a lot. They left the harbour at 04:40, in the dark, with the engine sounding louder than it needed to because the water in the bay was flat and there was nothing else to hear.

Aiden did not take her to the grounds north of the headland. He took her east, into the bight between Carn and the next headland down, where the chart showed a rocky seabed at about thirty metres and the model said no cod. He motored at six knots. He watched the water. He did not speak. Joel baited the lines, four of them, and set them over the side at a depth Aiden called *just off the bottom*.

They waited.

Léonie asked what they were waiting for. Aiden did not answer. He stood at the wheel and watched the water. The water, in the early light, was the colour of pewter, with a long swell coming from the west that lifted the boat and lowered it with a slow patience.

At 05:50, Joel pointed.

Out ahead of the boat, perhaps two hundred metres, a patch of water had changed. It was a darker patch, about the size of a tennis court, and it was moving. Aiden turned the wheel and brought the boat to it. As they approached, Léonie saw what was moving the patch. It was fish. They were not visible at the surface — they were below, maybe a fathom down, perhaps two — but the patch was the shadow they cast against the lighter water around it. There were hundreds of them. She could see, at the edge of the patch, individual shapes turning.

Aiden looked at the patch. He looked at the sky. He looked at the water. He looked at a single gull that was flying low over the patch and calling in the way gulls call when they have found something they cannot eat themselves.

He said, *Hake. Small ones. Not what we came for.*

Joel pulled the lines. They were empty. Aiden motored on. He took them further east, along the bight, where the seabed shallowed to about twenty metres and then dropped again. He stopped the boat twice more. The first time, he looked at the water for perhaps three minutes, then shook his head. The second time, he stopped the boat, looked at the water, looked at the sky, looked at a flock of gannets that were working about a mile to the south, and said, *There.*

He put the lines over. He set them at a depth he called *mid-water*. Joel baited them with mackerel. They waited.

At 06:20, a line went taut. At 06:23, another. By 06:40 they had six cod on the deck, all of them between four and seven pounds, all of them fat, all of them alive.

The model said the cod would not be here. The cod were here. Aiden had found them by looking at the water and the birds, and by knowing where the water was the colour he expected it to be on a morning when the wind had been off the land for two days and the swell was from the west and the gannets were working in the way gannets work when the fish are holding in a layer the seabed does not show.

Léonie stood on the deck and watched Joel gut the fish. She asked Aiden how he had known.

He did not answer directly. He said, *You don't find them by looking for them. You find them by looking for what they're doing.*

She asked him to explain. He did not explain. He started the engine and they went home.

In the days that followed, Léonie did what she had been sent to do. She updated the model. She took the catch data and the positions and the times and she pushed the lobe north on the heat map, by sixty nautical miles, and she adjusted the spawn date forward by nineteen days, and she wrote a report that she sent to Hadley in Plymouth. The report was three pages long. It used the words *shift*, *baseline*, *adjustment*, *recalibration*. It did not use the word *wrong*. It did not use the word *lost*. It did not use the word *accommodation*.

In the afternoons, when the boat was in and the data were uploaded, she went down to the quay and watched the boats come in. She watched Aiden gut fish. She watched Joel mend nets. She watched the old men who no longer fished but who came down at four o'clock every afternoon to stand at the end of the quay and look at the sea and say, in a slow Cornish that Léonie was only beginning to understand, what they thought of the wind and the swell and the colour of the water and the way the gulls were flying. The old men had been fishermen, all of them. They had stopped fishing in their sixties because their hands or their backs or their hearts had told them to. They came down to the quay because the quay was where they had always been at four o'clock.

Aiden was one of the old men. He was also a fisherman. He fished in the mornings and stood on the quay in the afternoons.

Léonie went out with him again on the eleventh. On the twelfth. On the fifteenth. On the eighteenth. Each time they went out, he took her somewhere different from where the model said the fish should be. Each time, he found fish. Each time, he found them by looking at the water, by looking at the birds, by looking at the colour and the way the swell was bending around the headland and the way the light was falling on the surface. He did not use a fish-finder. He did not use a chart plotter. He used his eyes and a kind of long practice that Léonie did not have a word for.

On the twentieth morning, she asked him how the fish had changed.

He thought about this. He did not answer for some time. The boat was motoring slowly out of the bay. The wind was off the land. The swell was from the west. A herring gull passed over the boat at a height she could not judge.

He said, *They come earlier. They stay longer. They move to grounds we didn't use to work. They go to water we used to call cold and we now call just water. They don't do what they did.*

She asked him why.

He said, *Because the sea's not what it was.*

She asked him what the sea was.

He looked at her. He looked at the water. He said, *It's still the sea. It's just a different sea. The fish know which sea they're in. We don't always.*

She wrote this down. She did not write it in the report. She wrote it in the notebook she had started keeping on the second day, in which she wrote the things Aiden said that did not fit into the model. She had twelve pages so far. The pages were full of sentences that the Fisheries Service would not have known what to do with. *The water's too warm for this month but the gannets are working.* *The swell's wrong for the wind but the cod are holding.* *The birds are feeding where the chart says there's no bottom.*

On the twenty-fourth day, she went out alone. She rented a small day-boat from the harbourmaster, who was also the chandlery man, who was also the cousin of the vicar who came down from Bodmin. She took the boat out into the bay. She did not take a chart. She took the notebook, and she took the binoculars Aiden had lent her, and she went out at 05:00, in the dark, and she motored east into the bight and stopped the boat and watched the water.

She did not see the fish. She did not see what Aiden saw. She did not know what to look for. She watched the swell. She watched the colour. She watched the way the light moved on the surface. She saw gulls, and gannets, and a single cormorant that was diving about a quarter of a mile away. She did not know whether the cormorant was diving for fish or for something else. She did not know whether the gannets were working because there were fish below them or because they were passing through on their way somewhere else. She did not know whether the colour of the water meant anything at all, or whether the swell bending around the headland meant anything at all, or whether the light meant anything at all.

She sat in the boat for two hours. She did not find any fish.

She went back to the quay. Aiden was there. He asked her how it had gone. She said she hadn't found anything. He nodded. He said, *You will. You have to be out more.*

In the days that followed, she went out with Aiden more often than she went out alone. She watched him watch the water. She asked him questions. She wrote down the answers. She began to notice things she had not noticed before — that the water on the east side of the bay had a slight green tint to it in the mornings when the wind had been off the land, that the green tint meant plankton, that the plankton meant the fish would be feeding on the edge of the plankton bloom, that the edge of the bloom was where the colour of the water changed from green to blue and the line between green and blue was where the fish would be holding. She did not know this in her body. She knew it in her notebook. She was learning the way the model had not been built to learn.

On the thirty-first day, she went out alone again. She took the boat east into the bight. She stopped the boat where the colour of the water changed from green to blue. She watched the swell. She watched the birds. She watched the light.

At 06:14, she saw a patch of water that was darker than the water around it. It was the size of a tennis court. It was moving. She turned the boat to it. She put a line over the side, baited with mackerel, at a depth she guessed at.

At 06:31, a line went taut.

She did not catch a lot of fish. She caught four. They were not large. They were cod.

She motored back to the quay. Aiden was there. She showed him the fish. He looked at them. He nodded. He said, *Good. You saw the colour.*

She said she had. She had seen the colour.

He said, *The colour is the first thing. After the colour, you watch the birds. After the birds, you watch the swell. After the swell, you watch the light. After the light, you feel the boat. After you feel the boat, you feel the fish. It's all one thing.*

She did not write this down. She did not need to.

That night, in the flat above the chandlery, she sat at the kitchen table with the laptop open. The heat map of the Western Approaches was on the screen. The orange-red lobe was in the new position, two hundred and forty nautical miles north of where it had been. She looked at it for a long time.

She thought about Aiden on the quay in the afternoons, watching the sea. She thought about Joel gutting the fish on the deck of the *Enid May*. She thought about the old men who came down at four o'clock and stood at the end of the quay and said what they thought of the wind. She thought about the colour of the water on the east side of the bay. She thought about the cormorant she had seen on the first morning, diving for what she had not known.

She thought about what she had been sent to do. She had been sent to update the model. She had updated the model. The lobe was in the new position. The spawn date had been adjusted. The report was in Plymouth. Hadley had written back to say the recalibration looked good and that she should plan to move on to her next assignment in three weeks.

She thought about what Aiden had said. *It's still the sea. It's just a different sea. The fish know which sea they're in. We don't always.*

She closed the laptop. She did not move on to the next assignment in three weeks. She stayed in Carn. She went out with Aiden. She went out alone. She learned the colour, and then the birds, and then the swell, and then the light. She learned to feel the boat and, after some months, to feel the fish. She wrote a second report, which she sent to Hadley, in which she said that the model could not be updated because the model was the wrong kind of thing. She said that what was needed was not a recalibration but a different way of looking. She said that the fishermen knew things that the data could not capture, and that the data knew things that the fishermen could not capture, and that the sea was larger than both.

Hadley wrote back to say that this was not, in the formal sense, a recalibration, and that the service would need her to come back to Plymouth and discuss.

She did not go back to Plymouth. She stayed in Carn. She went out with Aiden. She watched the water.

The cod came earlier each year, and stayed longer, and moved to grounds that no model had ever put them on. The old men came down to the quay at four o'clock. The sea was a different sea. The fish knew which sea they were in. The fishermen, most of them, were beginning to.

She was beginning to.
