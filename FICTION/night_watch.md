# Night Watch

The autopilot holds 187° magnetic. South-southeast. Dutch Harbor to Kodiak, eighteen hours of open water. The chartplotter draws the route as a green line across nothing.

Tomás Reyes sits in the portside helm chair with his boots on the console and a thermos of bad coffee between his thighs. He is twenty-three. Second season on the F/V Resolute. And for the first time, he is on watch alone.

Behind him, through the watertight door and down three steps, the captain sleeps.

---

He has earned this the way you earn anything on a fishing boat — slowly, through accumulated proof. First season he was useful only for his back and his hands. Hauling gear, sorting catch, pressure-washing the deck until his fingers went white and numb in the hydraulic mist. He stood watch in the wheelhouse but only as a body, a pair of eyes the captain didn't trust to interpret anything. Every contact on the radar, every flicker of the AIS, every shift in the wind — the captain handled it himself, or made Tomás wake him.

*You watch,* Captain Beatty had told him that first summer. Meaning: you observe. I decide.

This season is different. Tomás knows the boat. He knows the sound the main engine makes when the fuel filters need changing — a faint asterisk of vibration in the throttle lever. He knows how the Resolute carries following seas, her stern lifting, the propeller racing for a half-second before the blade bites again. He knows the autopilot's habits, the way it overcorrects in a beam sea.

And the captain knows Tomás. That is the part that matters.

*You're on tonight,* Beatty had said at 1900, as they cleared the breakwater at Dutch. He'd said it the way he said everything — flat, declarative, already past the point of discussion. He'd plugged in the route, checked the weather one final time, and gone below.

He hadn't said *call me if you need me.* He hadn't said *wake me for traffic.* He'd just gone below.

That is the trust. Not words. The absence of them.

---

Here is what no one tells you about standing watch alone at sea: the loneliness is not the hard part. The hard part is that the loneliness has a shape. It fills the wheelhouse like fluid, settles into every corner, presses against your eardrums. At twenty-three, Tomás does not yet have language for this. He would say it's *quiet* if anyone asked, but quiet isn't it. Quiet is absence. This is presence — the sea at night, full of its own dark intention, and you a small lit room in the middle of it.

The wheelhouse is forty-five degrees. The heater broke in the way boat heaters break — it either freezes you or roasts you, nothing between — and Tomás has left it off. He wears Grundéns over a fleece over a thermal. The fatigue sits behind his eyes like a low-pressure system, not blinding but present, tinting everything gray. He has been awake since five. Hauling the last strings. The coffee helps. The cold helps more.

The Furuno radar sweeps its circle every two seconds, painting range rings on the dark. The AIS transceiver broadcasts their position to every vessel within VHF range. The chartplotter shows the Resolute as a tiny boat icon crawling along the green line. And on the laptop mounted to the starboard bulkhead, the edge AI runs its constant, quiet analysis — a neural network trained on millions of hours of maritime traffic data, ingesting the radar and AIS feeds, outputting probabilities. Not commands. Not recommendations. Probabilities.

Right now the traffic area is clear.

Tomás watches the radar paint its empty circle. He thinks about the hold below him, full from the last strings of the season. He thinks about Kodiak, fourteen hours ahead. He thinks about the girl in Dutch Harbor who kissed him on the dock Tuesday, the taste of her lip balm, cherry and something chemical beneath it, and how he'd thought about it for exactly six hours before the Resolute cast off and he stopped thinking about it because the ocean does not care about your mouth.

He thinks about his mother, who is probably asleep in a house with too many rooms in a town where nothing happens, and who worries about him in the vague, persistent way that mothers worry — not about specific dangers but about the general condition of being far away.

He thinks about these things because the watch is long and the sea is empty and thinking is what the mind does when there is nothing to react to. It manufactures. It reaches for what it knows. It tries to build a world out of the dark.

---

At 0203 the contact appears.

Seven miles to the northwest, right on the edge of the twelve-mile range ring. A faint smudge of green on the radar — not AIS-correlated, which means it isn't broadcasting. Tomás watches it. One sweep. Two. Three. The smudge resolves into a discrete blip, a point of light crawling across the range rings.

He sits up. Puts his boots on the deck.

No AIS. He feels the small kick of adrenaline at the base of his spine, the body's animal response that has nothing to do with the mind above it. No AIS means one of three things: military, broken transponder, or someone who doesn't want to be seen.

He leans forward. Touches nothing. Watches.

The bearing is not changing. This is the math that lives in every mariner's bones — if the bearing to another vessel holds steady while the range decreases, you are on a collision course. First thing they teach you. The thing you check in your sleep.

Range: 6.8. Bearing: 337° relative.

He counts sweeps. The blip moves.

Range: 6.5. Bearing: 337°.

Steady bearing. Decreasing range.

Tomás's hand moves to the VHF radio. Then stops. He pulls it back. He is thinking about Beatty, asleep below. About what it would mean to open that watertight door, to climb down the three steps, to say *Captain, I've got a contact and I don't know what it is.* Beatty would come up. Beatty would look at the radar. Beatty would make a decision. And Tomás would be the deckhand again, not the watch-stander. The trust would reset to zero.

He does not touch the radio.

The laptop updates at 0207. The neural net has classified the contact: 85% probability USCG cutter — patrol profile, no AIS, speed consistent. Twelve percent fishing vessel, AIS inoperative. Three percent other.

He reads the numbers. Eighty-five percent. The system is fairly sure but not sure, the way a weather forecast is fairly sure about rain. And fifteen times out of a hundred, it is wrong.

He watches the blip. He watches the bearing.

Range: 6.1. Bearing: 337°.

If he alters course — a jog to starboard, opening the distance — it costs twenty minutes and fuel they've budgeted tightly. And if it turns out to be Coast Guard, it means he flinched at nothing. Beatty will see the GPS track in the morning, the little wiggle in the green line, and he will know Tomás was scared.

If he holds course and it isn't Coast Guard —

He does not finish that thought.

At 0211 the contact slows. Tomás can see it in the radar track — the blip's rate of closure decreases, the angle shifting. Eight knots. Patrol speed. Fourteen knots had been transit speed, getting somewhere. Eight knots is a cutter working a sector, combing a grid. The laptop recalculates: 92% probability. CPA four-tenths of a mile off the port beam, fourteen minutes out.

Tomás exhales. He has not realized he was holding his breath. He looks at the radar. He looks at the laptop. He looks at the dark window, where there is nothing to see but the absence of everything.

Ninety-two percent.

He reaches for the coffee thermos, and that is when the laptop screen flickers and goes black.

---

The crash is not dramatic. No spark, no pop. The screen simply goes dark, the fan winding down like a sigh. Tomás's hand is on the thermos. He looks at the laptop. He looks at the radar.

The blip is still there.

Range: 5.2. Bearing: 337°.

His stomach drops. Not because the laptop has failed — the laptop is a tool, tools fail, this is the ocean and everything out here fails eventually. His stomach drops because the ninety-two percent is gone. The number he was building toward, the number that was going to make this all right, has vanished into a dead screen.

Five miles. Steady bearing. Closing.

He tries the power button. Nothing. He checks the cable — seated, the LED on the power strip green. He holds the button for five seconds. The screen stays black.

Five minutes ago he had a probability and a fourteen-minute window. Now he has a radar blip and nothing else.

The wheelhouse is very quiet. The engine vibrates through the deck plates, through the chair, through his boots. The radar sweeps. The blip moves.

Range: 4.9. Bearing: 337°.

He thinks: *It is a Coast Guard cutter. The system was at ninety-two percent. Patrol speed. Eight knots. That is real data.*

He thinks: *Fifteen times out of a hundred.*

He thinks: *I should wake Beatty.*

The watertight door is six feet behind him. He could be there in four seconds. The handwheel takes five turns — he knows because he has opened it a thousand times bringing the captain coffee. Beatty is a light sleeper for a fisherman. He would be awake by the second turn.

Tomás looks at the radar. He looks at the door. He looks at the radar.

Range: 4.6. Bearing: 337°.

He makes his decision at 0217, except it is not really a decision. It is the absence of one. He waits. He stands in the wheelhouse with his hand three inches from the radio and his back to the watertight door and he waits, because part of him — the part that is twenty-three and stubborn, the part that needs this to be his watch and not Beatty's — believes the system was right, believes that a steady bearing at patrol speed in the Gulf of Alaska at two in the morning is Coast Guard, believes it deeply enough to bet the boat on it.

And part of him — the part that is counting the range — does not believe it at all.

Range: 4.2. Bearing: 336°.

One degree. The bearing has changed. It could be the contact's movement. It could be his own movement — the Resolute is making nine and a half knots, the geometry shifting with every second. One degree is noise. One degree is nothing. One degree could save him or kill him and there is no way to know.

Range: 3.8. Bearing: 335°.

Two degrees. The bearing is opening. Or wobbling. Or his radar is less precise than he thought and the bearing was never as steady as it looked. The laptop would have told him. The laptop is dead.

Range: 3.4. Bearing: 334°.

The range is still closing but slower. The angle of the blip across the range rings has shifted — it is crossing now, not approaching. CPA will pass aft of the beam. Maybe. He is doing trigonometry in his head that the machine did in milliseconds, and he is not sure of his numbers. He was never good at trigonometry. Beatty wasn't either, but Beatty had thirty years of watching blips, thirty years of the specific knowledge that lives in the body and not the brain.

Tomás has two seasons. He has a dead laptop and a radar blip and a bearing that might be opening and might be noise.

Range: 3.0. Bearing: 332°.

Everything in him says turn. The conservative instinct, the training, the cold voice in the back of his skull: *you don't know what this is and you are responsible for a sixty-two-ton boat and a sleeping captain.* Put the wheel over. Open the distance. Accept the cost.

He does not turn.

He does not turn because he has decided — below language, below reason, in the place where decisions actually live — that this is Coast Guard. That the system was right. That he knows this the way Beatty knows it, the way every fisherman in the Bering Sea knows the silhouette of a cutter. He does not turn because turning means he was scared, and being scared means he is not ready, and not being ready means he goes back to hauling gear.

He stands at the helm and watches the blip and does not turn.

Range: 2.6. Bearing: 330°.

Range: 2.2. Bearing: 328°.

Range: 1.9. Bearing: 326°.

The bearing is opening. The range is slowing. The contact is crossing — left to right, passing ahead of the Resolute at something less than half a mile. Close. Close enough that Tomás can feel the wrongness of it in his teeth, the proximity of sixty-two tons of steel to whatever is out there in the dark.

Then, through the window: a shape. Black on black, visible only because the three-quarter moon is behind it and it blocks the moonlight differently than the sea. A hull. Long, narrow, military. White slashes of foam at the bow. A hundred and fifty feet, maybe more, cutting through the water at eight knots. Running lights on, AIS dark.

Coast Guard.

Tomás's knees go weak. Not metaphorically — the ligaments actually loosen and he sits down hard in the helm chair and his hands are shaking and he cannot stop them. He presses his palms flat against his thighs and holds them there.

It was Coast Guard. He was right.

He was right, and the winning feels exactly like losing, because the outcome had nothing to do with the quality of his decision. He held course out of pride. Not out of judgment — pride. The need to be the person who doesn't wake the captain. And the contact happened to be what the system said it was, and the bearing happened to open, and he is alive and the boat is fine and none of it is because he made the right call. He made the wrong call and the ocean was merciful.

No — that is not fair either. The contact was at patrol speed. The system was at ninety-two percent. He had data, even if the data died. He made a judgment call based on incomplete data and the judgment was correct.

But if the data had been the only thing standing between him and a turn, then the data was everything, and the data was gone, and what was left was a twenty-three-year-old who didn't want to go back to hauling gear.

The cutter slides astern. He watches it disappear. His hands have stopped shaking. The coffee thermos is still wedged between his thighs, somehow. He pulls it out and drinks. The coffee is cold.

---

He does not tell Beatty.

Not in the way of concealment — not the way you hide a mistake. He doesn't tell him the way you don't mention every wave you cross, every hour of empty water. It was his watch. He handled it. Whether he handled it well is a question he cannot answer and so he sets it down the way you set down a heavy thing, carefully, in a place where you will not trip over it in the dark.

The watch ends at 0600. Beatty comes up the companionway at 0545, rubbing his face, smelling like the bunk. Tomás says "Quiet night" and the words feel like something he has swallowed.

Quiet. Relative on a boat. It means nothing tried to kill us.

Beatty grunts. Pours coffee from his own thermos — he keeps a separate one below, always. Checks position. Checks fuel. Checks weather. Then he sits at the laptop and presses the power button.

The screen stays black.

He presses it again. Holds it. Lets go. Checks the cable. Taps the screen with one calloused finger.

"What happened."

Not a question. A prompt.

"Crashed around 0215," Tomás says. "Power was fine — cable seated, strip LED green. Just went dark."

Beatty looks at him. "Contact during that time?"

Tomás tells him. He tells him clean — the way you report, no justification leaking in around the edges. Radar contact at seven miles, no AIS, steady bearing at 337° relative. Patrol speed at eight knots. He held course. Contact passed at approximately half a mile, port beam. Visual confirmed Coast Guard cutter.

He does not say: *I held course because I was too proud to turn.* He does not say: *I didn't know what it was and I held course anyway because the alternative was admitting I didn't know.* He says the facts. He lets the facts carry the weight.

Beatty scrolls through the dead laptop as if it might offer something. Then he stands and looks out the window at the gray sea and the thin line of Kodiak on the horizon.

"You held course," Beatty says.

The words sit in the wheelhouse. Tomás waits for the rest — the nod, the grunt, the thing that will tell him what *held course* means. Whether it means *good.* Whether it means *you got lucky.* Whether it means *I'm deciding what to think about you.*

Beatty nods. Once. Goes back below.

Tomás stands at the wheel and watches Kodiak grow. The island fills the windshield — green and dark and real, the mountains shouldering up behind the coast. Gulls appear. A puffin, its ridiculous orange bill catching the early sun. The sea has changed color, the deep blue-black of open water giving way to the green-brown of the shelf.

In the galley, the stove clicks on. Propane hisses. A pan meets the burner.

Beatty comes back up with two plates. Sets one on the console next to Tomás without a word. Scrambled eggs. Canned ham. Tabasco.

They eat. The boat comes home.
