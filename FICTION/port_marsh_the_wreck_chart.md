# The Wreck Chart

The wreck was at forty-two feet, on a sandy bottom, in a current that ran sideways along the shelf and pulled at your exposure suit like a slow hand on a sleeve. Torv Halvorsen had dived on it four times before he accepted what he was looking at.

It was not in any of the records. He had been through the Lloyd's lists for the western seaboard going back to 1820. He had been through the Port Marsh Historical Society's index — seventeen file drawers, three card catalogs, two boxes of loose papers that no one had sorted since 1947. He had written to the maritime museum in Oslo, and to a colleague in Halifax who had access to the British shipping registries from before the registry fires of 1838 and 1839. Nothing. The vessel did not exist.

But it was there.

It was wooden, hand-built, copper-sheathed in places where the sheathing had survived, and it was — he had taken samples, brought them up in his drybag, sent them to a dendrochronology lab in Trondheim — old. The lab said, with a margin of error they were not happy with, between 1780 and 1820. Two hundred years, give or take a generation.

The first time he had seen it, on a clear afternoon in August with the visibility at fifteen meters and the bottom white with sand, he had thought it was geological. A piece of shelf that had broken off in some long-ago tremor. Then he had seen the curve of the keel, and the line of frames rising out of it like the ribs of something sleeping, and he had known.

He had surfaced. He had sat in the boat with the engine idling and the rod hanging over the side and the salt drying on his face. He had not radioed in. He had sat with it for a while.

The wreck was not a salvage prospect. Torv did not work for salvage. He worked, when he worked, for the historical society, charting the wrecks that lay along the shelf so that the fishermen would know where not to drag their nets and the divers who came after him would know where not to drop their anchors. It was quiet work. It was the kind of work that took a long time and did not need to be explained.

He went back down in September. He tied a small nylon line to a piece of coral at the bow, so he could find the wreck again in poor visibility, and he began to chart it. He measured the length — forty-eight feet, he thought, though the stern had collapsed into the sand and he could not be sure. He measured the beam. He measured the depth of the hold. He sketched the frames in his wet notebook, the pencil sliding on the page in the underwater world where every stroke had to be made with the kind of deliberateness that comes from knowing that the next breath is already being spent.

It was during the fourth dive, in October, on a calm morning when the water had that glassy stillness it gets in autumn between storms, that he found the container.

It was in the forward compartment, which was half-collapsed but not entirely silted. He had marked the spot with a piece of line tied to a piece of coral. He followed the line in. The compartment was small — a space for stores, maybe, or for the crew's personal effects, the kind of space a sailor keeps for the things that do not fit into the manifest.

The container was wooden, about the size of a bread box, sealed with pitch and a wrap of oiled canvas. The pitch had held. Two hundred years of saltwater, and the pitch had held.

He opened it with a small pry bar and the care of someone disarming something. The container came apart cleanly along one of the long seams. Inside, wrapped in a second layer of oiled canvas, was a book.

He had not taken the book on the fourth dive. He had only looked at it. He had opened it to the first page. He had seen the script. He had closed it. He had surfaced.

Today he was going to look.

He took the container in his left hand, with the lid off. He took the book in his right. He opened it again, to the first page, and he held it up under his dive light.

The script was not English. It was not Norwegian, not Swedish, not Danish, not Dutch, not German, not French, not Spanish, not Portuguese, not Italian, not Latin, not Greek. It was not, as far as he could tell in the green half-light of the dive, any alphabet he had ever seen. The letters were vertical strokes with small hooks and loops, and they ran, in the entries he could make out, in tight columns down the page. There were numbers — Arabic numerals, clearly. There were drawings of small objects: a fish, a hook, a piece of rope, a knot. There was, on the second page, a small map of a coastline he did not recognize.

He turned pages. He counted entries. There were, he estimated, between four and five hundred pages. He turned to the last page.

It was not the last entry. It was unfinished.

The script on the last page stopped mid-sentence. There was a handprint in the margin — a right hand, in what looked like ink or rust, pressed flat to the page. Above it, in a different hand from the rest of the log, in larger letters, was a single word he could not read. He stared at it for a long time. The air in his tank clicked softly. The wreck creaked.

He closed the logbook.

He surfaced with it in his drybag. He did not radio in. He rowed back to the slip with the outboard at half-throttle. He sat in the boat for a while with the engine off. He opened the drybag and looked at the logbook.

He took it home. He put it on his desk in the back room of the house above the cove. He sat with it for the rest of the day and most of the evening. He did not open it again until after dark.

When he opened it, he read the entries as best he could. He could not read them. The script was opaque to him. The drawings he could interpret — fish, hook, rope, knot, a kind of tally for the wind — but the words were not his.

He called Senga Vell the next morning.

Senga was the tide clerk. She had been the clerk for four years. Torv had known her since she was a girl. He told her what he had found. He asked her to come look at the handwriting.

She came up that afternoon. She sat at his desk. She looked at the logbook for a long time.

She said: *I cannot read it.*

He said: *I know.*

She said: *It is a careful hand. Whoever wrote this wrote every day. The hand does not change.*

He said: *No.*

She turned more pages. She said: *There are no crossings-out. There are no corrections. Whoever wrote this knew what they wanted to say.*

He said: *Yes.*

She closed the book. She looked at him. She said: *What are you going to do?*

He said: *I don't know.*

She left the logbook on his desk. She said: *You should not publish this.*

He said: *Why?*

She said: *Because whoever they were, they put it in a container. They sealed it. They left it in a wreck on a shelf in the sea. They did not put it in a bottle for someone to find. They put it where it was.*

He sat with this for a long time. She did not leave.

After a while he said: *I am the wreck charter. I have to file a report.*

She said: *File a report. Don't publish the log.*

He said: *The society will want to see it.*

She said: *Then tell them you couldn't bring it up. Tell them the container was too damaged. Tell them it broke apart when you tried to lift it.*

He looked at her.

She said: *It's a small lie.*

He sat with this for a long time. Then he put the logbook back in the drybag. He put the drybag in the back of the cupboard under the stairs. He closed the door.

He filed his report the next morning. He filed it as a wreck of indeterminate date, possibly nineteenth century, no artifacts recovered, currents too strong for further investigation.

The historical society accepted the report. They asked him, once, if there had been anything inside. He said no. They asked him if he wanted to go back down. He said not this season, the weather was turning. They said, all right, next year.

That was four years ago. He has not gone back.

The logbook is still in the cupboard under the stairs. He has not opened it again. He thinks about it sometimes, on winter evenings, when the wind is in from the north and the cove is white with spray and the dark is the kind of dark that makes you feel the weight of things you have decided to leave alone.

He thinks about the handprint on the last page. He thinks about the unfinished sentence. He thinks about the word he could not read above the handprint, in larger letters, in a different hand.

He does not know what it says. He has decided he will never know. He has decided this is the right decision, even though he cannot say, exactly, why.

Some weeks, he goes down to the harbor in the late afternoon and sits with Senga on the south pier while she does the 19:13 reading. They do not talk about the logbook. They talk about the weather. They talk about the boats that used to be in the harbor. They talk about Berit, who is on her way up the causeway with her torch, climbing the tower to turn on the light, the way she has done every evening for forty-one years.

Torv watches Berit climb. He thinks about the wick in its brass housing, the wick that will need someone to light it when Berit cannot climb anymore. He thinks about Senga's readings, the notebooks that go back 140 years. He thinks about his own wrecks, the dozens of them on his charts, the wrecks that are in the records, the wrecks that someone has named.

The logbook in the cupboard under the stairs is the only thing he has ever found that is not on a chart.

He has decided to leave it there.
