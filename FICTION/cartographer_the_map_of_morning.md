# The Map of Morning

Tomas Vitásek had been a cartographer for thirty-one years, and for twenty-six of them he had been mapping things that could not be mapped. He had begun, as all cartographers begin, with what is there — the river, the ridge, the town at the bend. Then he had run out of what is there. The world had been surveyed by satellites. There was no white space left on paper. So he had looked for new territories.

He found them in the things that nobody had thought to chart.

His first major project, the *Atlas of Morning*, had taken seven years. It was a work of three hundred and forty pages, hand-bound, printed in an edition of two thousand, and within a year of publication it had become the kind of book that design studios and philosophy professors and one particularly determined poet kept on their desks. Tomas had been interviewed about it twice, three times if you counted the radio piece in Vienna. He had been invited to give a talk at the Royal Geographical Society. He had declined.

The atlas was a map of what it feels like to be alive at the beginning of the day.

Not geography. *Experience*. The way light enters a kitchen in winter, sliding under the curtain in a slow yellow blade. The way a city sounds at six in the morning — the first milk float, the dog two streets over, the bus that never comes, the bus that always comes. The smell of coffee in seven apartments, each one different from the next, each one its own country. The atlas had entries for *the morning after a fight*, *the morning of a child's first day at school*, *the morning after someone has died in the night*. There was a fold-out plate called *The Continental Divide of the Alarm Clock*, which showed where, in a single household, the line falls between the sleeper who must be woken and the sleeper who wakes alone.

The methodology was his own. He had invented it because no one else had bothered.

You cannot map a morning from the outside. You cannot interview people, you cannot stand at a window with a clipboard. A morning is lived by the body that is inside it, and only by that body. So Tomas's instrument was *himself*. He rose. He paid attention. He made notes, in a small green notebook, in a shorthand he had invented at twenty-three and never taught to anyone. He made the notes while the morning was still wet, while the coffee was still cooling on the counter, while his wife Hanna was still asleep and the dog had not yet decided whether today was a day for barking.

Then, days later, sometimes weeks, he would take the notes and translate them into cartographic form.

The translation was the work. Anyone can have an experience. Few can render it in a form that another mind can enter. Tomas had spent years developing a visual language for the un-mappable: contour lines for the *density* of a feeling, color washes for its *temperature*, small typographic marks along the margin to indicate the *direction in which the morning was moving*. A reader of the atlas could, by following the marks, feel the morning of a stranger. It was not the same as living it. But it was closer than anyone had come.

The atlas had sold well. It had sold well enough that his publisher, a small press in Munich, had asked him, gently, whether he had considered a second volume.

He had.

He had already begun it. The second volume was to be called *The Atlas of Evening*, and it would be, he believed, the better of the two. Mornings were sharp, declarative, almost military. Evenings were confessions. But he had been working on the second atlas for three years now, and the work was not going well.

The problem was his instrument.

Tomas was sixty-three. He had been sixty-three for eight months. He rose, each morning, from the same bed, in the same flat, on the same street in Prague where he had lived since his marriage. Hanna was still asleep. The dog was thirteen now and slept on a tartan blanket by the radiator, and the coffee was still cooling on the counter. Everything was, by all visible measures, the same.

But the mornings had begun to drift.

The light in the kitchen was no longer a blade. It was a wash, diffuse, almost apologetic, as if the sun had lost confidence. The smell of coffee did not arrive the way it used to — quick, decisive, a summons. It arrived late, and faint, and sometimes not at all. The city outside the window sounded different too, and Tomas had spent three months trying to understand why before he realized that the city had not changed. *He* had changed. His ears were slower. His attention, which had once been a falcon, was now a sparrow: still capable of flight, but lacking the dive.

He sat at his desk in the front room, where he worked, and he opened the green notebook to the page he had been keeping for the past week, and he read what he had written.

*Tuesday. The light is grey. The radiator ticks once and does not tick again. Hanna does not stir. I stand at the counter and feel nothing particular. This is also a morning. Is it?*

He closed the notebook.

The maps he was drawing, from these notes, were coming out wrong. He could feel it. They were technically fine — the contour lines were placed where contour lines should be placed, the color washes were correct in hue and saturation. But they were *wrong* in the way a portrait is wrong when the painter has not quite caught the eyes. A reader could look at the morning map for a Tuesday in January and see that it was a January morning, that it was grey, that it was Tuesday. They could not see what Tomas had seen, which was: a man in his kitchen, holding a cup he had forgotten to fill, looking out a window at a street he no longer recognized.

The instrument was drifting. And the drift was not random. It had a direction.

Tomas had been a cartographer long enough to know what it meant when an instrument began to drift in one direction. It meant the instrument was failing. It meant the maps would, from now on, be more accurate at one edge than the other. It meant that the territory he was charting would, slowly, become foreign to him, and then unknown, and then —

He did not finish the thought. He opened the notebook again. He uncapped his pen.

He wrote: *I have been mapping morning for twenty-six years. I have been alive for sixty-three. The ratio used to be in my favor. It is no longer in my favor. I am running out of mornings to map. I am running out of me to map them with.*

He sat for a long time, looking at what he had written. Hanna slept in the other room. The dog did not bark. Outside, the city went on waking up, the way cities do, indifferent to the small dramas of the old.

He thought about the green notebook, and the thousand notebooks before it. He thought about the seven years he had spent on the first atlas, and the three he had spent so far on the second, and the way the second was turning out to be a different book than the one he had intended. The first atlas had been a celebration. The second atlas was turning out to be a chronicle of loss.

He did not know yet whether that made it a worse book. He suspected it might make it a better one.

He uncapped the pen again and began, carefully, to map the drift.

He worked through the morning the way he had always worked. He rose. He paid attention. He made notes. The light in the kitchen was grey. The coffee was faint. Hanna did not stir until almost nine, which was unusual for her, and when she came out she was wearing the blue robe and her hair was uncombed and she said, *You are up early*, and he said, *I am up at the usual time*, and she looked at him and did not say anything else.

He showed her, later that afternoon, one of the maps from the new atlas. It was a map of the morning of *being told a piece of bad news in a kitchen*. He had drawn it last year, before the drift had begun, and it was one of the better maps in the series. The contour lines fell away from a central point like ripples on a pond. The colors were the colors of winter in a city that had never quite warmed up. The margin marks indicated *inward*, *inward*, *inward*.

Hanna looked at it for a long time.

*This is the morning my mother died*, she said, finally.

*I know*, he said. *I was there.*

She looked at him. She set the map down on the table. She did not say, *How did you know?* She had been married to him for thirty-four years. She knew how he knew.

In the evening, after dinner, after the dishes had been washed and put away, after the dog had gone out and come back and gone out again and come back again, she came to his desk and said, *The new ones. The ones you are making now. Are they as good?*

He hesitated. He did not want to lie to her.

*No*, he said. *They are different.*

*Different how?*

*They are more accurate in some places and less accurate in others. The instrument is drifting.*

She sat down in the chair opposite his desk. She folded her hands in her lap. She said, *Tomas. The instrument is you.*

*I know.*

*You cannot replace the instrument.*

*No.*

*So what will you do?*

He looked at her. He looked, for a long moment, at the woman he had married, who had been his first reader and his best reader and the only reader who had ever, in the early years, when he was still inventing the notation, told him that a particular mark meant something other than what he had intended it to mean.

*I will keep mapping*, he said. *Until I cannot.*

She nodded. She stood up. She went to bed. He stayed at the desk, and he worked for another two hours, and then he went to bed too, and the morning came again, as mornings do, and he rose, and the light in the kitchen was grey, and the coffee was faint, and the dog barked once at the radiator, and the city went on waking up around him, and he began, as he had begun every day for twenty-six years, to pay attention.

The drift continued. He mapped it as best he could.

It was, he thought, the most honest thing he had ever done.
