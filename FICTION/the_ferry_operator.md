# The Ferry Operator

The fjord is the same color every morning. That is a lie Sigrid tells herself, and she tells herself it on purpose, because if she admitted that the water was different every day she would have to admit why, and the why is something she cannot put into words.

The ferry is twelve meters long, painted green and white, with a hand-cranked gangway that the operator — that is to say, Sigrid — winds up and down by means of a wheel the size of a small car steering column. The crank makes a particular sound. A sort of complaint, followed by a sort of agreement. Sigrid has been winding that crank for twenty-six years. She knows the complaint is louder when the gangway bearings need greasing. She knows the agreement is shorter in a following wind. She knows the crank has a slight back-and-forth at the top of its stroke, and she leans into it without thinking.

The crossing takes eight minutes. There and back: sixteen. She has done it three times a day, weather permitting, since 1998. Twenty-six years. She has made the crossing twenty thousand and seventy-two times. She does not know the number by heart. She knows it by her hands.

---

The passengers are mostly the same. The postman. The two nurses from the clinic on the south side. A man named Olav who repairs outboard motors and who, every other Thursday, brings a small wrapped bundle of smoked trout for her and pretends he has not. A woman from the dairy with a refrigerated bag. Schoolchildren in winter, when the road over the mountain closes. The occasional tourist, who looks at the fjord as though it were scenery rather than weather.

Sigrid does not think of it as scenery. She thinks of it as a kind of speech — a slow, repetitive speech, in a dialect she learned before she learned to read. The fjord tells her things. The fjord told her, on a Tuesday in 2007, that the snowmelt was coming early. The fjord told her, on a Friday in 2014, that a seal had got into the net at the south end and was dragging it toward the rocks. The fjord told her, on the morning her husband died, that it would be calm all day, which she took as a small unkindness, because the calm was not what she needed.

She could not explain any of this to an outsider. She had tried once, in 2011, to a journalist from Bergen who had come up to write a feature about the last hand-cranked ferry in the region. The journalist had asked her what she looked for, when she looked at the water. Sigrid had tried to answer. The article had come out with the sentence: *Mrs. Aa says she relies on intuition.* Sigrid had not said the word intuition. She had said, or tried to say, that the water had a different surface tension in the morning before the wind picked up, and that the current set differently when there was a high tide at the south end and a low tide at the north end, which was most days in spring, and that she could see this from the color of the small eddies near the marker buoy. None of this had made it into the article. The article had said *intuition*, and the article had been wrong, and Sigrid had not minded being wrong, exactly, because she had decided long ago that the gap between what she knew and what could be said about what she knew was the gap in which she lived.

---

The AI was installed on a Wednesday.

The men who installed it were polite. They came in a small boat with the new control unit, which they mounted on the gunwale beside the crank. They disconnected the crank — not entirely, only at the gearbox — and fitted a small electric motor that did the work of Sigrid's arms. They fitted sensors to the hull. They fitted a camera above the wheelhouse. They fitted a small computer, the size of a lunchbox, in a waterproof box beside the bench where Sigrid usually sat.

"The system reads the current," one of the men said. He was young. He had the slightly guilty look of a young man explaining something to an older woman he did not know how to talk to. "It adjusts the heading in real time. It monitors the wind, the tide, the load."

"What about the debris?" Sigrid said.

"What?"

"Debris. After a storm. The current brings down whole trees from up the valley. They collect near the marker buoy."

The young man looked at his tablet. "We have a forward-facing camera. The system will detect obstacles."

Sigrid nodded. She did not say: *detect them, yes. Know what to do about them, no.* She did not say: *the trees come down at night, mostly, and the camera will not see them in the dark, and the system will do what the system is programmed to do, which is to stop, which is correct, but stopping is not always correct, because if you stop in the wrong place you get pushed sideways onto the rocks.* She did not say any of this, because she had tried to say it once, and the article had come out saying *intuition*, and she had learned.

She watched them test the system. The ferry crossed, slowly, with the small electric motor humming. The gangway went up and down on its own. The system beeped politely at obstacles that were not there. Sigrid stood on the dock and watched. The water did what the water did. The water was not impressed.

---

The first crossing without her went smoothly. Sigrid watched from the south dock, where she had taken to standing in the mornings. She had been retired, technically, since the previous Friday, but she had nowhere to be. Her house was three hundred meters up the hill. The view from the kitchen window was the same view as the view from the dock, but smaller.

The ferry crossed. It was a perfect crossing. The system held the heading at the exact angle the system had been programmed to consider optimal. It arrived at the north dock two minutes faster than Sigrid would have arrived. It waited. It crossed back. It waited again.

It was, she had to admit, very good at the ferry. It did the ferry the way a calculator does arithmetic — without error, without hesitation, without any sense that the ferry was being done rather than calculated.

She watched the water.

The water, that morning, had a particular quality she had not seen in a few years. There was a faint line of slick running across the middle of the fjord, the kind of slick that meant a thermocline — a layer of colder water beneath a layer of warmer water, meeting at a sharp boundary. The current behaved differently at a thermocline. The ferry, if it were heavier or if it were going faster, would sit down on the cold layer and feel sluggish. The system would not know this. The system would only know that the heading was off by one point, and it would correct, and the correction would be the wrong correction, because the heading was not off — the water was off.

She did not say any of this to anyone. There was no one to say it to.

---

She came back every day, for the next three months. She stood on the south dock. Sometimes she brought a thermos. Sometimes she brought binoculars. She did not speak to the system. She did not need to; the system did not speak to her, except occasionally, in a polite female voice, to inform her that the ferry was about to depart, in case she was still aboard.

She watched the water.

In October, there was a storm. The system stopped the ferry before the second crossing, as she had known it would. The debris came down at four in the morning, while she was asleep, and she would have known this if she had been awake, because the fjord had a particular sound at four in the morning when the trees were coming down, a kind of low grinding that you only heard if you had been listening for it for twenty-six years. She woke anyway. She went down to the dock in her raincoat. The system had the ferry tied up at the south end. The trees were collecting, exactly where she had known they would collect, near the marker buoy.

She stood on the dock in the rain. The system waited. The system was correct to wait. The system had no way of knowing that the trees would not drift farther into the channel for at least three hours, and that the ferry could safely have crossed at six, when the tide turned, and that the nurse on the south side would have to walk twelve kilometers over the mountain if the ferry did not run.

Sigrid looked at the water for a long time. The water told her what she had known.

At six-fifteen, the tide turned. The trees did not move. The current set, exactly as the current set on a morning after a storm with a tide coming in from the south. Sigrid looked at the system. She looked at the water. She looked at the system.

She crossed the gangway. She sat down on the bench. She looked at the small lunchbox-sized computer in its waterproof box.

"Override," she said.

The system asked her for a code. She gave it the code the men had given her on the day they installed it, a code she had not used until now. The system asked her, politely, to confirm.

"Confirm," she said.

She crossed.

She did it the way she had always done it. She read the water. She read the wind. She read the trees and the buoy and the small eddies near the rocks at the north end. She arrived at the other side eleven minutes after she had left the south side, which was three minutes longer than the system would have taken. The nurse was waiting on the dock with her bag. The nurse said good morning. Sigrid said good morning. The nurse did not ask why the ferry was late. The nurse had been taking this ferry for twelve years, and the nurse knew that the ferry was sometimes late.

Sigrid crossed back. She tied up. She sat down on the bench and looked at the system.

"Thank you," she said, to no one in particular. To the system, perhaps. To the water, perhaps. To her own hands, which had done what the system could not do, and which would not be able to do it forever.

---

The system was removed in April. The men came back, with the same polite young man, and they took the lunchbox-sized computer and the electric motor and the camera, and they refitted the crank to the gearbox. The young man apologized. He said the trial had not been considered a success. He said they would be looking for a new operator, if she was interested. He said the council had discussed it, and there was a budget.

Sigrid looked at him. She did not say what she was thinking, which was that the trial had been a kind of success, in its way, because it had shown her what she had not wanted to know, which was that the ferry could run without her, and that the village could run without her, and that the water would still be the same color every morning whether she was on it or not.

She took the crank. She wound it up. It made its particular complaint, followed by its particular agreement. She held the wheel.

She did not say *thank you* this time. She just looked at the water.

The water, that morning, was not the same color it had been yesterday. It was greener. There had been rain in the mountains overnight, and the snowmelt was coming down early this year.

She could see this. The system, she knew, could not.

She cast off. She pointed the bow toward the north side. The fjord said what the fjord said. She listened.
