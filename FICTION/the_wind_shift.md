# The Wind Shift

The alarm went off at 4:10, but Ray was already awake. He'd been awake since 3:47, lying in the narrow berth with his hands behind his head, listening to the boat. The *Elinora* was a 32-foot fiberglass salmon longliner, and she talked in her sleep — the creak of the spring lines, the tick of cooling metal in the engine block, the slow drip from the galley faucet he'd been meaning to fix since October. He knew each sound the way a man knows his own breathing, and none of them were wrong, so he'd lain there in the dark and waited for the alarm because there was no reason to get up before 4:10.

He swung his feet to the deck. Cold. Even in July, the cabin sole was cold through a pair of wool socks. He dressed in the same clothes he'd worn yesterday — Carhartts, a dark green hoody, the orange Grundens bibs that stayed hung on a hook by the door because they were too stiff with salt to fold. He laced his Xtratufs by feel.

On deck, the sky was the color of a bruise. Not the dramatic kind — the old kind, the yellow-green that sits low on the horizon like a stain. The wind was southwest, eight maybe ten knots, and it had shifted in the night. Ray could feel it in the moisture on the stainless rail. He stood at the stern and pissed over the side and looked at the water.

The water had changed color. Yesterday it had been the flat, cranky gray of the central Gulf, but overnight it had gone blue-green, which meant the Alaska Current had pushed warmer water in from the south. Ray could see the boundary — a hard line on the surface, like a seam in fabric, running roughly east-west about two hundred yards off his starboard beam. That was a temperature break. Salmon stacked up along temperature breaks the way people lined up along a bar.

He went back inside.

The laptop sat on a shelf behind the helm, bolted to a piece of marine plywood with L-brackets and rubber isolators that Ray had cut from an old trailer mount. It was a ThinkPad, four years old, with a cracked bezel he'd repaired with epoxy and a strip of gorilla tape. The screen was dead — he'd removed it entirely — and the whole thing ran headless, piped to a five-inch Raymarine display zip-tied to the bulkhead above the VHF. Green text on black. The cheapest possible interface.

Ray pressed the power button and put the coffee on. The propane hissed and caught, and the percolator began its slow business. He opened a can of Dinty Moore beef stew and ate it cold, standing, with a spoon he wiped on his pants. The stew was gelled and metallic and exactly right for 4:30 in the morning.

The display blinked and settled. The model had today's prediction: forty-three fish, plus or minus six, at coordinates fifty-two miles to the northwest. A long run. Ninety gallons of diesel round-trip. He'd built the model himself over three winters — trained it on fourteen seasons of his own log entries, each one hand-typed into a spreadsheet at the end of every trip, the weather and the water and what he caught and where. It was better than he was at picking water now. Not smarter. Just faster, and free of the temptation to fish where he'd always fished because the memory was good.

"You've been wrong about that area before," Ray told the display.

The display didn't answer. It never did. But the rhythm of talking out loud helped him think — the same way it had helped when Bess was alive, the blue heeler who'd spent eleven years riding the deck and sleeping on a piece of old carpet under the helm console. He'd narrated everything to that dog. Called her name when he hauled, let her sniff each fish as it came over the rail. She'd died on the carpet, on a Tuesday. He'd buried her on the beach behind the harbormaster's office and hadn't gotten another dog because he was fifty-six and eleven years was a long time.

The carpet was still under the console. He hadn't moved it.

He drank his coffee. The display updated: barometer falling, 1009 millibars and dropping. The model liked falling pressure. Ray liked falling pressure too. It made the fish eat.

"Fine," he said.

He fired the engine at 5:05.

---

The run out took four hours and ten minutes. The *Elinora* cruised at ten knots, pushing a bow wake that scattered murres and puffins off the water. The Gulf was low and greasy, the swell coming from the southwest in long, even intervals. No whitecaps. The kind of sea that made Ray suspicious because it was too easy.

He ran the boat from the flying bridge, standing, one hand on the wheel. Below him, through the cabin window, the green display scrolled sea surface temp from the transom sensor. The temperature climbed steadily: 7.2, 7.4, 7.6. When it hit 7.8, he knew they were getting close.

"Darker water coming up on the port side," he said. "That green. You see that?"

The laptop couldn't see anything. But the sensor confirmed: 8.1°C. They'd crossed the break.

Ray throttled back and went forward to set the gear.

The longline was 1,200 feet of weighted mainline, clipped every thirty feet to a leader and hook. Each hook was baited with herring — thawed, brined, rigged through the eye so it spun in the current. Ray baited by hand. His hands moved without consultation from his brain: grab, hook, clip, feed. The line went over the stern in a steady singsong of metal and monofilament, and the flags — orange, orange, orange — marked the position of each float so he could find the line again.

It took an hour to set. When the last flag went over, Ray wiped his hands on a rag that was more fish oil than cloth and went back to the helm.

"Soak's six hours," he said. "I'll haul at one."

He dropped anchor — a lightweight Danforth that would hold the boat in the current but let her swing — and killed the engine. The silence was enormous. The boat rocked. The water slapped the hull. Somewhere, a whale blew, and Ray looked up but didn't see it.

He went below.

---

The cabin of the *Elinora* was nine feet long and six feet wide and smelled like diesel, bilge, salmon slime, coffee grounds, and the particular sour-sweet smell of a sleeping bag that hadn't been washed since April. Ray sat on the berth and pulled a paperback off the shelf — *The Mirror Crack'd*, a Christie he'd read four times — and opened it to a random page. He read for an hour. He ate a peanut butter sandwich on white bread with the crusts cut off, which was how he'd eaten them since he was six and saw no reason to change.

At 11:30, he checked the bilge. Dry. Forty-eight fathoms on the depth finder, hard bottom — the kind of ledgy, complex seafloor that held bait, which held salmon. The model's confidence had climbed to eighty-two percent. The number had gone up because the barometer was still falling and the model liked falling pressure.

He put on his oilskins and gloves and went on deck. The hauler was a hydraulic drum mounted on the starboard rail, and he started it and let it warm up, the hydraulic fluid whining through the lines. He lit a cigarette — the only one he allowed himself each day, at noon, before the haul — and smoked it looking at the water.

The water was alive. Not with fish, not yet, but with birds. Sooty shearwaters, a thousand of them maybe, working the surface in loose, banking circles. They'd found something. Something was happening below.

"Alright," Ray said. He dropped the cigarette. "Alright."

---

He hauled at 1:07.

The first hook came up empty. The second had a small shaker — a salmon too small to keep, maybe three pounds, silver and thrashing. Ray shook it off and let it go. The third hook had a sockeye, eight pounds, bright, bleeding from the gills. Good fish. He bled it and dropped it in the tote.

The hauler pulled the line in a steady rhythm, the hydraulic drum clicking and catching, and Ray worked the rail — unclipping each leader, reading each hook, bleeding the keepers, shaking the shorts. His hands were bloody to the wrists. His back ached. The sun was out now, finally, and the light on the water was hard and white and beautiful.

He counted as he worked. Sockeye, pinks, one king — a twenty-two-pounder that came up deep and heavy and furious, and Ray killed it with a priest and laid it gently in the tote because kings were money and you didn't bruise money. Thirty fish. Thirty-five. Thirty-eight. The fish were coming steady. Better than steady. The model had been right about the water.

Then the wind changed.

He felt it before he saw it — a shift in the angle against his neck, from southwest to northwest, and the temperature dropped five degrees in the time it took to unclip two hooks. He looked up. The sky to the northwest had gone the color of a filing cabinet, a flat dull gray that sat on the water like a lid, and it was moving toward him fast.

He glanced through the cabin window at the display. The model was still showing catch probability — it didn't read wind, wasn't built for weather. It knew temperature and tide and bottom structure and the feeding patterns of salmon. It did not know what a squall line looked like twenty minutes out and closing.

"Hey," Ray said, still hauling, his hands working the rail. "I need you to — "

He stopped. There was nothing to ask. The laptop couldn't help him with this. This was the thing it didn't know: whether to keep hauling or cut the line and run. The fish were biting. Three hundred feet of mainline still in the water, thirty hooks still down, and the sea was about to turn.

He worked faster. Thirty-eight became forty. The wind hit fifteen knots, then twenty, and the *Elinora* began to pitch, the bow rising and falling in a cross-chop that hadn't existed ten minutes ago. Spray came over the rail. The totes slid. Ray braced his hip against the gunwale and kept hauling, his feet wet now, the deck sloshing.

At forty-two fish, the squall arrived.

Rain like gravel. The wind veered hard northwest and held, and the sea stood up — short, steep waves, four feet, then five, coming from two directions at once because the old swell was still running southwest under the new wind. The *Elinora* rolled to starboard. A tote shifted and Ray caught it with his knee and shoved it back. The hauler was still pulling, the hydraulic drum grinding, but the line was coming up heavy — heavy meant fish, but heavy also meant the current was dragging the mainline sideways and the gear was fouling on something below.

The next three hooks came up together, tangled, and two of them had fish — good fish, sockeye, eight pounds each — but the leaders were snarled into a knot that would take ten minutes to untie on a flat calm deck. This was not a flat calm deck. Ray looked at the knot. Looked at the remaining line still in the water. Two hundred feet. Twenty-some hooks. Whatever was on them.

He pulled a knife from his belt and cut the mainline.

The gear went over the stern and into the dark water. Gone. Six hundred dollars in lead line and hardware, plus whatever fish were still on the hooks. Ray watched the last orange flag bob twice in the chop and disappear.

He stood there. Rain running down his face, blood on his hands, the boat pitching under him. The machine had found the fish. The fish were there. The wind was there too.

He secured the hauler, lashed the totes, and went to the helm.

---

The run back took six hours. Ray ran before the sea at eight knots, taking the waves on the port quarter, the *Elinora* laboring under the weight of fish and water. He'd shipped green over the stern twice during the haul and the bilge was sloshing. He ran the pump. The wind held northwest until he was inside the islands, then it backed off, and the Gulf settled as if nothing had happened.

Forty-two fish. One under the prediction. But forty-two good fish — sockeye and pinks and the king, all iced, all bled right. Not a lost trip. Not a great one.

He came in slow through the harbor entrance, no wake. The sea lions on the buoy were barking and shoving each other. He tied off bow and stern, coiled the lines, hung the bumpers.

He offloaded at the fuel dock. The buyer's kid — seventeen, sunburned, wearing earbuds — weighed each tote and wrote numbers on a clipboard. Forty-two fish, 314 pounds dressed.

"Squall came through," the kid said. "Heard it on the radio."

"Yeah," Ray said.

He hosed the deck one more time. Checked the bilge — dry now. Turned off the VHF, the depth finder, the GPS, the nav lights. The cabin went dark except for the display, which was running its nightly update. The CPU fan whined at full speed, burning watts the alternator hadn't replaced. He thought about killing it. Holding the power button until it stopped. The model update could wait. Tomorrow was tomorrow.

But the model update was how it learned. Every night it took the day's catch, the day's water, the day's weather, and adjusted. Made the weights a little different. The thresholds a little tighter. If he killed it now it would wake up tomorrow with today unprocessed, and the prediction would be a day stupider.

He left it alone and walked up the dock, past the gear lockers and the ice house, to the parking lot. His truck was where he'd left it — a 2003 Tacoma with 280,000 miles and a cracked windshield and a dog blanket still folded on the passenger seat. He got in and started the engine and drove home.

---

The *Elinora* rocked at her lines. The cabin was dark. The display glowed green against the window — the fan spinning inside the laptop, the model recompiling, already working on tomorrow.
