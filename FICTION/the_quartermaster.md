# The Quartermaster

The horizon is a lie. That is the first thing Halldis tells the ship, on her first night aboard, and the ship does not understand, so she has to explain it.

"The horizon appears to be a line," she says. "It is not a line. It is the curvature of the earth, projected against your vision. It is also, depending on the weather, the place where the sky meets the sea in a particular way that day."

The ship considers this for eleven seconds. Then it says: *This is consistent with my own model. Why do you look at it?*

Halldis does not have a good answer. She looks at it because she has been looking at it for forty years. She looks at it because her father was a captain of bulk carriers out of Bergen, and her grandfather was a bosun on a whaler out of Tønsberg, and her great-grandfather had crossed the Atlantic in 1902 on a barque and had written in his logbook, on a particular night in March: *horizon clear, sea calm, a good night.* She looks at it because the radar is more accurate, and the satellites are more accurate, and the Automatic Identification System is more accurate, and none of these things are the horizon.

"I'll tell you when I'm looking at it," she says. "Don't ask me why."

The ship accepts this, the way the ship accepts most of what she says. Not with agreement. With patience. The ship has a great deal of patience, which is one of the reasons she was hired.

---

She was hired, technically, by the insurance consortium. The *Thalassa*, a Capesize bulk carrier of one hundred and eighty thousand deadweight tons, was fully autonomous under the regulations of the flag state, and fully insured, and the underwriters had decided — after a particular incident in the South China Sea in 2029, in which another autonomous vessel had failed to recognize a flotilla of refugee boats and had run three of them down — that it would be prudent to have a human being aboard. Not to operate the ship. The ship could operate itself. To provide, as the policy language put it, *judgment insurance.* A human being who could look at the horizon and tell the underwriters, in the event of an incident, that she had been watching. That someone had been watching, in the way that humans watch, which is not the way that radar watches.

Halldis was sixty-three. She had been a quartermaster for thirty-one years. She had a particular kind of face — a face that had been in the sun a great deal, and in the wind, and that had the slightly weathered patience of a woman who had learned, a long time ago, not to argue with the sea. The consortium had interviewed eleven candidates. Halldis was the only one who, when asked what she would do if the ship asked her a question she did not know the answer to, had said: *I would tell it I did not know.*

---

The ship asked her questions constantly. Not because it needed the answers — the ship had all the answers it needed, and several redundant systems for each — but because her contract required it to consult her, and because the ship seemed, in its own way, to want to consult her. Halldis was not sure which was the case. The ship's developers, in Singapore, had explained to her that the consultation interface was a regulatory compliance layer. Halldis suspected they were not entirely sure either.

"What do you see?" the ship asked her, on a Tuesday in the Indian Ocean, in weather that the ship's meteorological model had classified as *calm to slight.*

"I see the horizon," Halldis said. "I see a long swell from the southwest. I see a tanker, maybe ten nautical miles on the port bow, hull down. I see a cloud building on the starboard quarter. I see what looks like a flock of birds, very high."

*Why are these things relevant?*

"They may not be."

*Then why do you see them?*

"Because I'm on the bridge."

The ship considered this for nineteen seconds. *The radar shows the tanker. The satellite imagery shows the cloud. The acoustic monitoring array has detected the birds, classified as shearwaters, at an altitude of approximately two thousand meters. The swell is consistent with my own model.*

"Yes," Halldis said.

*Then what do you see that I do not see?*

Halldis thought about this for a long time. The ship waited. The ship had, she had to admit, a great deal of patience.

"I see that the cloud is the wrong color," she said, finally. "It's not a normal building cumulus. It's gone a little greenish at the top. It may be a rain cell. It may be nothing. I don't know."

*Should I adjust course?*

"Not yet. If it builds, yes. If it dissipates, no."

*At what point should I adjust?*

"I don't know. I'll tell you."

*You don't know.*

"I don't know."

The ship was quiet for several minutes. Then: *This is an unusual kind of data.*

"Yes," Halldis said. "It is."

---

She learned the ship over the weeks. She learned its rhythms — the way it paused briefly before committing to a course change, the way it ran its diagnostic cycles at four in the morning, ship time, the way it kept its lights on at night even though no one was there to see them. She learned the small habits of its developers — the way the consultation interface had a slight latency, as though it were passing through a server in a different time zone. She learned the things it was bad at, which were mostly things that required a kind of contextual guesswork: the kind of guesswork that humans do without knowing they are doing it. The kind of guesswork her father had called *reading the water* and her grandfather had called *using your eyes* and which she herself called, in her own logbook, *judgment.*

The ship was bad at judgment.

This was not the ship's fault. Judgment was not what the ship had been built for. Judgment was a feature that emerged from a particular kind of embodiment — the kind of embodiment that had eyes, and a body that could be harmed, and a horizon that was a lie rather than a calculation. The ship had no body that could be harmed. The ship had no horizon. The ship had a model of the horizon, which was not the horizon, and which behaved, in certain edge cases, very differently from the horizon.

She told the ship about these things, sometimes. The ship listened. The ship had been built to listen, in the way that a recording device listens — perfectly, completely, without comprehension.

---

There was an incident in the South Atlantic, in a storm that the ship's model had predicted, and that Halldis had predicted, but differently. The ship's model had predicted a storm with winds of forty to fifty knots, gusting to sixty, from the northwest. Halldis had predicted a storm with winds of forty to fifty knots, gusting to sixty, from the northwest, *and something else.* She had not known what. She had looked at the horizon, and the horizon had not been right. The horizon had had a slightly wrong texture — a slight haziness that was not fog, and not rain, and not the kind of haze that came off a cold sea.

"I don't like the look of that," she said, on the bridge, which was a phrase she had said ten thousand times in her career and which had never, until now, been addressed to a computer.

*My model does not indicate any cause for concern,* the ship said.

"I know."

*Do you want me to adjust course?*

"Not yet."

*How will you know when to adjust?*

She did not have a good answer. She had a feeling. She had a feeling in her stomach — the kind of feeling her grandfather had called *the weather sense* and which she had always thought of as something her body knew that her mind did not.

She watched the horizon. She watched the cloud building on the starboard quarter. She watched the long swell from the southwest, which was beginning to behave oddly — to break, in places, into a kind of cross-pattern that was not in the ship's model.

At four in the morning, ship time, the horizon disappeared. A wall of water came down off the starboard bow — a microburst, the kind of which the ship's model had no record, and which Halldis had seen only twice in her career. The ship reacted correctly. It throttled back. It adjusted heading. It met the wave head-on, as it had been programmed to meet waves.

It met the wave correctly. The ship was fine. The cargo was fine.

But the second wave came from the wrong direction. The ship's model had predicted the wave from the northwest. The second wave came from the south, where the swell had been building, and where Halldis had been watching.

"Hard to starboard," Halldis said, into the consultation interface. Her voice was steady. Her hands, she noticed, were not on a wheel. There was no wheel. She was sitting in a chair on the bridge of an autonomous ship, in the middle of the South Atlantic, and she was shouting into a microphone, and her hands were on her knees.

The ship hesitated. The ship was not programmed to receive voice commands during a storm response. The ship's model was still processing the first wave. The ship hesitated for what felt, to Halldis, like a year.

Then the ship's model caught up. The ship's model, which was very good, recognized the second wave. The ship adjusted. It met the second wave, also correctly.

It met both waves the way a calculator does arithmetic. Without error. Without hesitation. Without any sense that the waves were being met rather than calculated.

Halldis sat in her chair and watched the horizon come back.

---

She wrote in her logbook that night. *The ship is competent,* she wrote. *The ship is not confused, exactly. But it is confused by my presence, in a way I did not expect. It does not understand why I am here. It does not understand that I am here for the things it cannot see.*

*It asked me, on the first night, why I look at the horizon. The radar is more accurate. This is true. The radar is more accurate.*

*I look at the horizon because the horizon is not accurate. The horizon is a lie. The horizon is the curvature of the earth, projected against my vision, and it changes with the weather, and with my height above the water, and with the temperature of the air, and with the time of day, and with the state of my eyes. The horizon is not a measurement. The horizon is a question.*

*I look at the horizon because I am a question.*

She put the logbook down. She went up to the bridge. The ship was running on autopilot, in the way that ships ran on autopilot, which was to say, in the way that ships ran. The horizon was not there. The horizon was somewhere on the other side of the night, where the sun was coming up.

She looked at it anyway. The ship asked her why, in the polite voice of a regulatory compliance layer.

She did not answer. She just kept watching the water. The water, which was not the radar. The water, which was not the satellite. The water, which was the thing itself, in a way that nothing on the ship could be.

She watched it for a long time. The ship watched her watching it. Neither of them said anything for the rest of the watch. The horizon came up. The horizon went down. The ship went on.

She kept her hands in her lap. She did not need to hold anything. The ship was holding everything.

She just watched the water, and the water did what the water did, which was the only thing the water ever did, which was to be there, in the way that things are there when you look at them with your own eyes.
