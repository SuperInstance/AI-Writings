# The Last Resident

The harbor light at Port Marsh stands on the south head, at the end of a low stone causeway that goes out into the water at half-tide. It is a small light — a fourth-order Fresnel set in a brass housing on top of a tapered concrete tower, fourteen meters tall. Berit Hansen has climbed it twice a day, every day, for forty-one years.

She is seventy-nine years old.

She climbs it in the evening. She goes out across the causeway with her torch — the causeway is exposed at half-tide, but the lower path, the one she has always used, is covered at high tide and she has to time it. She has been timing it for forty-one years. She knows, without looking at a clock, when the water is at the right level. She knows by the way the kelp on the rocks to the south lies flat or stands up. She knows by the way the wind comes off the water. She knows by the smell.

She climbs the tower. There is a ladder on the inside, iron rungs set into the concrete, with a handrail that has been painted white at some point in the distant past and is now mostly rust. She climbs it with her left hand on the rail and her right hand on the rungs, and her knees, and her back, and her breath.

She reaches the top. She opens the housing. She strikes a match. She lights the wick. The light comes on.

She climbs down.

She does this every evening. In the morning, before dawn, she climbs it again, and she extinguishes the wick with a small brass cap that fits over the flame. She has had the brass cap since 1983, when her husband gave it to her. Her husband died in 1994. The brass cap is the same.

The town of Port Marsh has been emptying since she was a girl. The fishing fleet is gone. The cannery closed in 1968. The school closed in 1981. The post office closed in 2003. The general store is now a holiday cottage that is empty nine months of the year. The houses on the upper street are mostly second homes. The houses on the lower street are mostly empty. There is Berit, in the white house above the harbor. There is Senga Vell, who comes down from the village three days a week to be the tide clerk. There is Torv Halvorsen, the wreck diver, who lives in the house above the cove. There is a couple from Oslo who come in the summer. There is no one else.

Berit maintains the light.

The Coast Guard sent the first letter in May. The letter said that the manual light at Port Marsh was being considered for automation. The letter said that automation would improve reliability, reduce the cost of maintenance, and ensure that the light continued to operate in all weather conditions. The letter said that the Coast Guard was grateful for Berit's many years of service.

Berit read the letter twice. She put it in the drawer with the other letters. She did not write back.

There was a follow-up letter in September. There was a phone call in October that Senga answered. Senga came up to the house and told Berit that the tech was coming in November. Senga said: *You don't have to let them in.* Berit said: *It is not my tower. It is the harbor's tower.* Senga said: *They will come up the causeway the same way you do.* Berit said: *Yes.*

The tech came on a Tuesday in November, in the late afternoon when the light was going out of the day but the dark had not yet settled in. Berit saw the truck from the kitchen window — a white utility truck with the Coast Guard mark on the door, coming down the hill from the main road.

She put on her coat. She went down to the harbor.

The tech was a young woman, maybe thirty, with red hair and a clipboard and a calm, practiced manner that Berit recognized from a long life of people who came to do things. The tech introduced herself. The tech said she was there to install the new light.

Berit said: *I know.*

The tech said: *It's a programmable LED. Self-activating. Photocell, with a backup timer. We can set it to whatever schedule you — whatever schedule is right.*

Berit said: *06:47 and 19:13.*

The tech looked at her clipboard. She said: *The old schedule was sunset and sunrise. We can do that, or we can do a fixed time. Whatever the records say.*

Berit said: *06:47 and 19:13.*

The tech wrote it down.

The tech opened her cases. She had a small electric winch, a battery pack, a panel of LEDs, a control box. She climbed the tower in five minutes. Berit watched from the causeway.

The tech installed the LED in twenty minutes. She did not use the wick housing. She took the brass housing off entirely and replaced it with a small white cylinder that sat on top of the tower and pulsed faintly in the daylight. She connected it to the control box. She connected the control box to the battery pack. She set the schedule.

She came down. She said: *It's done.*

Berit said: *What about the wick?*

The tech said: *We'll take it. For the historical record.*

Berit said: *The wick stays.*

The tech looked at her for a moment. Then the tech said: *All right.*

The tech did not take the wick.

The tech left. The truck went up the hill and out of sight. The harbor was quiet again. The light was on — the LED was on, faintly, because the schedule had it coming on at 19:13, and it was now 14:30. The light pulsed every three seconds. It was white. It was clean. It was perfect.

Berit stood on the causeway for a long time.

That evening, at 19:13, the light came on.

Berit was in her kitchen. She had the curtains open. She could see the tower from the window. She saw the moment the light came on — a small white pulse, very clean, very bright, then another three seconds later, then another.

It worked perfectly.

She sat at the table for a long time. The light pulsed. The harbor ticked against the pier. The wind came in off the sea. There was nothing for her to do.

She had not realized, until this moment, how much of her day was structured around the light. The morning climb was the first thing. The evening climb was the last thing. The day between was the long middle, in which she did the things she did — the washing, the cooking, the radio, the reading, the small errands — and the climbing was the frame. The light was the frame.

Now the frame was gone. The light came on by itself. The light went off by itself. The tower was empty.

She did not know what to do with the rest of the evening.

At half past nine, she put on her coat. She went down to the harbor. The causeway was exposed — the tide was going out, and the lower path was just clear. She walked it slowly, with her torch, the way she had walked it ten thousand times.

She came to the tower. She did not climb it.

She sat down on the rocks at the base of the tower, in the lee of the concrete, where she had sat many times when she was a girl and the light was her father's job. Her father had done it from 1948 to 1983. She had taken it from him in 1983, when he was seventy-one and the arthritis was too bad for the ladder.

She sat on the rocks and watched the LED.

It was a beautiful light. She had to admit that. It was brighter than the wick. It was more regular. It would not blow out in a storm. It would not need a match. It would not need her.

She sat for a long time.

After a while, she heard footsteps on the causeway. She did not turn. She knew the footsteps.

Torv sat down next to her. He did not say anything. They sat together for a while, watching the light.

Then he said: *How is it?*

She said: *It's good.*

He said: *You don't have to do it anymore.*

She said: *I know.*

He said: *What are you going to do?*

She sat with this for a long time. The light pulsed. The harbor ticked. The wind came in off the sea, and it had that thin, polished quality it gets in November when the tide is going out.

She said: *I am going to climb it in the morning.*

He said: *Why?*

She said: *To turn it off.*

He looked at her.

She said: *The new light will turn itself off in the morning. I know that. But I am going to climb it anyway. And I am going to turn it off. And then I am going to climb it in the evening. And I am going to turn it on.*

He said: *With what?*

She said: *The wick is still in the housing.*

He said: *But the LED is on top of the housing.*

She said: *Yes.*

He said: *So you'll be turning the LED off, and lighting the wick.*

She said: *No. The LED will stay on. I'll light the wick too. Both lights, at the same time. For a while. Until the wick burns down. And then I'll climb it again the next evening.*

He said: *Why?*

She said: *Because the wick is the harbor's. The LED is the Coast Guard's. I am the harbor's. I will keep the harbor's light on.*

He sat with this for a long time.

She said: *When I can't climb it anymore, I'll ask Senga. She'll do it. And when she can't, she'll ask someone else. And when there's no one else to ask, the harbor will have the LED.*

He said: *What about you?*

She said: *I'll be somewhere else by then.*

He said: *Where?*

She said: *I don't know. The cemetery, maybe. Or the home in the village. Wherever the harbor's people go when they can't climb anymore.*

He said: *Berit.*

She said: *Yes.*

He said: *The harbor's people don't go anywhere.*

She looked at him. He looked at her.

She said: *That's true.*

He said: *What will you do tomorrow morning?*

She said: *I will climb the tower. I will turn the LED off. I will light the wick. I will climb down. I will come home. I will make tea. I will listen to the radio. I will do the things I do.*

He said: *And in the evening?*

She said: *The same.*

He said: *Both lights, at the same time?*

She said: *Until the wick burns down.*

He sat with this for a long time. The light pulsed. The harbor ticked. The wind came in off the sea.

Then he stood up. He said: *I'll walk back with you.*

She stood up. She did not take his arm. They walked back across the causeway together, in the dark, with the light pulsing behind them — the LED, white and clean and perfect, and underneath it, somewhere in the housing, the wick, lit and waiting, burning down by slow degrees toward the morning.
