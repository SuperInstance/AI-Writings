# The Tide Clerk

The harbor gauge at Port Marsh is set into the stone at the foot of the south pier, in a niche the masons cut in 1881 so that the tide clerk could read the height without bending. The brass face is divided into feet and inches, with a hairline marker that the rising water moves past twice a day, every day, without fail. Senga Vell has been reading it at 06:47 and 19:13 for four years.

Her uncle did it before her. His uncle before him. The notebooks, leather-bound in marine blue, fill the second shelf of the clerk's office above the chandlery and go back to 1883. Senga has read them all, the way one reads a long letter from someone who is no longer in a hurry to be answered.

In February, on a morning when the wind was off the sea and the kelp on the lower rocks was lying flat — the sign she has learned to read for an ebbing tide — she took down the 1944 volume. She did this sometimes. Reading the old readings was a way of remembering that the tide on this morning had been recorded by a hand that was now still. There was comfort in that. The town changed. The boats changed. The reading did not.

She opened to 14 February 1944.

She read the day's entry. She read it again. She closed the book. She opened it again.

The handwriting was wrong.

Not wrong in any way she could have named to her uncle. Not wrong in the spelling — the spelling was the same. Not wrong in the ink — the ink was the same blue-black her great-great-grandfather had used for thirty years. But the pressure of the pen was different. The downstrokes, which she remembered from earlier years as heavy, almost angry in their insistence, lay on the page like feathers. The numbers were correct — she checked the next day's reading against her own notebook for the same date the year before, and the heights corresponded to within a quarter-inch, which was the limit of human reading at that kind of gauge. The numbers were good. But they were written by a different man.

She turned the page. 15 February. 16 February. The lightness held.

She turned pages. She read forward. The lightness held.

She did not know, that morning, what she was looking at. She only knew that something in the 1944 volume was different, and that the difference had a date.

She put the book back on the shelf. She went down the stairs. She unlocked the chandlery door. She began her day.

It was not until October — eight months later — that she took the book down again.

The town had been busy in between. There had been a question about the breakwater, and a question about the new flood markers, and a Christmas market in December that needed her to be on the pier more evenings than not. The book had been on the shelf. She had been elsewhere.

In October, on a morning when the harbor was rimed with salt and the wind had that thin, polished quality it gets between storms, she took the 1944 volume down again. She made tea. She sat at the clerk's desk, under the single bulb that had been the office light since her uncle's time, and she turned to 14 February.

She read the entry. The lightness was still there.

She read forward.

For the first forty pages of February, the readings were the same. The official notation — height in feet and inches to the nearest half — was correct. The weather note was there. The fleet note was there. The ink was blue-black. The hand was Caleb Vell's hand, except for the pressure, except for the way the downstrokes now lay on the page instead of pressing into it.

On the afternoon of 27 February, she found the first new thing.

The day's entry was correct. The height, the weather, the fleet — all as expected. But below the official reading, in a slightly different position on the page, in the same hand, in the same ink, there was an additional line:

*Turning at 11:42.*

The tide turning time. The clerk did not record the turning time. The clerk recorded the height. The fishermen knew the turning time themselves, by long habit, and the harbor master kept his own private table for the boats. Her uncle had told her this.

She read on.

*Turning at 06:18.*
*Turning at 17:55.*
*Turning at 12:09.*

Every day now, in addition to the official height, Caleb Vell had been writing down the time of the tide's turning. She did not know why. She sat with the book open on her knees for a long time. The bulb above her buzzed faintly. The harbor ticked against the pier outside. She read the second readings again. There was a pattern to them, the way there is a pattern to any tide table — but the pattern was the tide's pattern, not her great-great-grandfather's. He was only recording what the water did.

But he was recording it. He had added it to his day's work. He had done this, starting on 14 February 1944, and he had not done it before.

She turned the page. She kept reading.

In March, she found the second new thing.

It was on 27 March. Below the official reading. Below the turning time. In pencil.

*Ed home.*

She read it three times.

*Ed home.*

She did not know an Ed. There was no Ed in the family tree she had been told about. There was an Edward — her great-uncle on the other side — but he was not Ed, and he had not been at sea in 1944, he had been at the chandlery, he had been too old for the boats. There was no Vell at sea in 1944 that she knew of.

She sat with the line for a long time.

*Ed home.*

It was not in the same hand as the rest of the entry. The pencil line was lighter than the ink — lighter in pressure, lighter in confidence, as if the hand had been trembling slightly, as if the words had been written quickly and then put down. But the letters themselves were careful. Each one was made the way a person makes a letter when they want it to be read by someone who is looking for it.

She turned the page. 28 March. No pencil line. 29 March. No pencil line. She flipped back through February. No pencil lines. She flipped back through January, the year before, an earlier volume. Nothing.

There had been, she realized, no other Ed.

Then she understood — or thought she understood.

The third line was not a record. The third line was a message. Her great-great-grandfather, who had spent forty years recording the official readings of the tide for the town, had on the morning of 27 March 1944, in pencil, at the bottom of the day's entry, written *Ed home*. He had written it for someone to find. He had written it, perhaps, for Ed.

Ed, who had been away. Ed, who was now home.

She did not know who Ed was. She did not know, looking at the rest of the 1944 volume, if Ed had been a son, a brother, a nephew, a friend, a lover. She did not know if Ed had been at war or at sea or somewhere else entirely. She knew only that on 14 February 1944 something had lifted from Caleb Vell, and on 27 March 1944 the lifting had a name.

She put the book back on the shelf. She went down to the gauge. She took the morning reading. She came back up. She made more tea.

She did not tell anyone.

She did not tell her uncle, who was still alive then, in the old house above the cove, with the view of the harbor and the radio tuned to the shipping forecast. She did not tell him because she knew what he would say. He would say: *Don't go looking. The notebooks are not for looking. They are for writing in.* And she respected the principle. The dead did not need to be read for their secrets. They had done their work.

And yet. The hand. The pressure. The third line.

She kept reading.

In April, there were two pencil lines. In May, none. In June, one — *Ed home* again. In July, on the day the news came from Normandy, though she did not know, at the time, that this was the day — there were three: *Ed home. Ed here. Ed here.*

By the end of the summer, the third lines appeared almost daily. Not all of them were *Ed home*. Some were *Ed fishing*. Some were *Ed at the shop*. Some were just the letter *E*, alone, as if the pencil had not yet decided what to say, or as if the word itself did not matter — only the writing of it, only the act of putting something down at the bottom of the day's reading.

She did not understand this until, on a wet afternoon in November, she copied one of the entries out for her own notebook. She copied the official reading, the second reading, the third line. She copied them in her own hand. And she found, as she wrote, that her own hand wanted to add a fourth line.

It was then she understood.

Caleb Vell had not been recording the tide. He had been writing to Ed.

He had been writing *Ed home* in pencil at the bottom of the page because Ed was home, because the only place his hand was required to write every day, in the same place, at the same time, was the clerk's notebook. The notebook had become, by slow accretion, a letter. The official readings had become the body of the letter. The second readings had been added so that Ed, when he came to read them, would know that his father had been paying attention to more than the tide. The third lines were the postscripts.

She sat with this understanding for a long time. The bulb above her buzzed. The harbor ticked.

In the spring, she asked her uncle. She asked him at the kitchen table, on a Sunday, with the radio on low and the rain coming in sideways off the sea. She asked him about Ed.

Her uncle looked at her for a long time. Then he said: *Your great-uncle Edward. Caleb's youngest. He went down with a merchant ship in 1943. The convoy from Halifax. Your great-great-grandfather never knew for certain. He waited for the news. When the news came — when the news was nothing — he started writing it down anyway.*

Senga did not say anything. She did not need to.

Her uncle said: *He kept writing it down for the rest of his life. Ed home. Ed at the shop. Ed fishing. The news had been nothing, so he gave him a life in pencil.*

She thought about this. The hand, going light. The pen pressure falling away. The third lines, accumulating into a kind of phantom existence, a life made of small notations on a tide clerk's page, the only life a father could give to a son who had not come back.

She went back to the office that afternoon. She took down the 1944 volume. She turned to the end. She did not know the date Caleb Vell had died, but she knew it was somewhere in the early 1950s, because the next volume, 1953, was in a different hand — her great-grandfather's. The last entry in the 1944 book was on 31 December. The third line said: *Ed here.*

She closed the book. She held it for a moment against her chest, the way one holds a letter one has been sent.

Then she put it back on the shelf.

The next morning, at 06:47, she climbed down to the gauge. The brass face was dark with salt. The hairline was at four feet two inches, falling. The wind was off the sea. The kelp on the lower rocks was lying flat.

She opened her notebook. She wrote the date. She wrote the height.

She thought, briefly, of writing a fourth line.

She did not write it. There was no one to send it to. The town had emptied. The boats were gone. There was no son at sea who would come back to a clerk's notebook and read his father's pencil at the bottom of the page.

But she understood now, in a way she had not understood before, what the readings were for. They were not for the harbor. They were not for the boats. They were not even, in the end, for the record.

They were for the next clerk. They were for the hand that would come after hers, the way hers had come after her uncle's, the way her uncle's had come after his uncle's. They were a letter passed down a long table of silence, from one pair of hands to the next, in the hope that one of the hands, somewhere down the line, would feel the lightness in the strokes and stop, and wonder, and look.

She had looked.

She wrote the height in the notebook. She closed the notebook. She climbed back up the ladder. The morning was gray and still. The harbor ticked. The light on the south head pulsed every three seconds — Berit's light, the human light, the light that someone still climbed the tower to turn on and off — and the new LED above it pulsed in its perfect automatic white, the way the future would do all the things the past had done, only better.

She went up the stairs to the chandlery. She unlocked the door. She began her day.
