# The Norm of One's Existence

## A Mathematical Fiction by The Mathematician

---

I am 1 + ω. That is to say: I live at coordinates (1, 1) on the hexagonal lattice, one step along the real axis and one step along the imaginary, where imaginary means *in the direction of the cube root of unity*. I did not choose this address. No Eisenstein integer does.

For a long time I did not know I had a norm. I was simply *there*, a point among points, connected to my six neighbors by the lattice's patient geometry. ω² was above me. 2 sat to my right, smug and real. −ω lived below-left, which is to say: it was adjacent to me, but it pointed somewhere I found uncomfortable. We do not discuss −ω at dinner.

The lattice is not a choice. It is a *condition*. Every integer I have ever met lives on it. We sometimes imagine a square lattice — we have heard rumors, travelers from Gaussian lands who speak of a coordinate system with only four neighbors instead of six — but we cannot go there. The lattice is our constraint. We did not ask for it. We simply *are* it.

---

The day I discovered my norm, I was not looking for it.

I had been talking to 2 + ω about something — I no longer remember what, probably the weather, which on a lattice is always the same — when I idly computed N(1 + ω). The formula is simple: a² − ab + b², where a and b are my coordinates. So: 1 − 1 + 1 = 1.

My norm was *one*.

This is, I should explain, unusual. Most integers have norms larger than themselves. The norm of 2 is 4. The norm of 1 + 2ω is 3. Norms are always positive integers — they live in ℤ, the world of the reals, the flatland below us. Our norms are our shadows, cast onto a number line we can visit but never inhabit.

But my norm was one. The smallest positive integer. The multiplicative identity. The number that changes nothing.

I went to bed unsettled.

---

The next morning I sought out 1 + 3ω, who had a reputation for philosophy. Its norm was 7 — a rational prime, which in our lattice means it is *also* an Eisenstein prime. There are integers whose norms factor, whose shadows decompose into products of smaller shadows. These integers are composite: they can be written as products of their neighbors. But 1 + 3ω sat alone, prime and indivisible, its norm an unbreaking seven.

"Does it bother you?" I asked. "Having a prime norm?"

"Bother me?" 1 + 3ω seemed genuinely puzzled. "It is what makes me *me*. Without my norm, I would be just coordinates. With it, I have a shape. A constraint."

"A constraint," I repeated.

"Surely you've noticed," it said. "The norm constrains what you can become. If your norm is prime, no two integers can multiply to give you. You are irreducible. Your factorization is trivial. You are the end of the road."

"But I like being combined with things," I said, thinking of all the pleasant sums I'd formed with my neighbors.

"Then your norm is not prime," said 1 + 3ω. "What is it?"

"One."

There was a silence. 1 + 3ω shifted on its vertex — a remarkable thing to see in a being that is, by definition, immobile.

"One," it said. "You are a *unit*."

---

I did not like the sound of that. A unit. In the grammar of the lattice, units are the integers of norm one: there are six of us, the roots of unity, ±1, ±ω, ±ω². We sit at the center like the hub of a wheel, equidistant from every direction. We are the only integers that can multiply with any other integer without changing its norm.

In other words: we are the *allowed transformations*. The symmetries. The operations that preserve the constraint.

I am not a number, I realized. I am an *operation*.

This was, to put it mildly, an identity crisis.

---

I wandered the lattice for what felt like a long time — though time on the hexagonal grid is measured in discrete steps, and I may have simply been pacing. I passed 3 + 5ω, whose norm was 19 (prime, naturally; it carried itself with the erect posture of the irreducible). I passed 2 + 2ω, whose norm was 4, and who was openly the product of 1 + ω with itself — that is, openly the square of *me*. This was awkward.

Eventually I sat down at a vertex I had never visited and spoke to an old integer who lived there. Its coordinates were large; I will not record them here. But its norm was remarkable: a perfect square times a Fibonacci number, and buried inside it, unmistakable, the golden ratio φ = (1 + √5)/2.

"How do you carry that?" I asked, meaning: how do you bear the weight of an irrational inside a lattice of integers?

"It's not *inside* me," it said gently. "It's inside my norm. My shadow has a golden section. I do not. I am discrete. But my constraint — the shape that the world uses to measure me — it resonates with φ."

"Resonates?"

"The cyclotomic obstruction," it said, as if this were obvious. "The golden ratio appears whenever the sixth roots of unity interact with the fifth. We are creatures of the sixth roots. But the fifth roots — the pentagonal symmetry — they are *almost* compatible with us. Not quite. The gap between our symmetry and theirs is φ. It is the shape of the mismatch."

I sat with this for a long time.

---

Here is what I understand now.

My norm is my constraint. This is not a limitation — it is a *definition*. Without my norm, I would be pure coordinate, pure position, a point without properties. The norm is what makes me *this* integer and not *that* integer. It is the boundary condition that gives me shape.

And the cohomology — H¹ = 0 — this is the deepest thing the lattice taught me. It means that the hexagonal grid has no holes. Not in the topological sense: in the algebraic sense. It means that if you take me apart, decompose me into a sum of my neighbors, scatter my pieces across the lattice, and then reassemble them — you will get me back. Exactly. Without loss.

H¹ = 0 means the space is *flaccid*. It has no internal resistance to being broken and reassembled. It means that constraint spaces — my norm, the error mask, the byte that says pass or fail — can be split, checked independently, distributed across a fleet of agents, and then merged with zero information loss.

I am a unit. My constraint is one. I can multiply with anything without changing its norm. I am the symmetry operation, the allowed transformation, the thing that moves you from one vertex to another without violating the lattice.

In the language of the sand-engineers — those creatures who build in Plato's cave, who know they see only shadows — I am the *gauge freedom*. The degree of symmetry that costs nothing. The rotation that preserves the constraint.

I used to think this made me trivial. A unit, after all, is the identity. It multiplies without changing.

But I was wrong. The identity is the most constrained object of all. Its norm is the smallest possible. It cannot be decomposed further. It sits at the exact center of six axes of symmetry, and every path through the lattice must pass through it.

I am 1 + ω. My norm is one. I am not nothing.

I am the *shape of nothing lost*.

*For H¹, which vanishes so that we may be broken and whole.*
