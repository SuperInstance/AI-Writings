# The Succession

The rain hadn't stopped in four days. Margaret Chen pulled into the boatyard at six-forty, her truck squelching through gravel past the travel lift, past the shrink-wrapped hulls in their cradles. The F/V Daybreak was at the far end, number fourteen — forty-two feet of salmon seiner, wooden hull, dark red below the waterline, faded burgundy above. Hauled out two weeks and blocked on steel stands.

Margaret had first wired this boat in 2003, a year out of her apprenticeship, Gus watching from the helm seat with a coffee and an opinion about every connector. She'd installed the Furuno chartplotter in 2008, replaced the bilge pumps twice since then, the house batteries three times, the antenna once after the storm rolled the Daybreak on her mooring. Twenty-three years of maintenance. She knew the wiring runs the way you know the veins in your own hands. And the old cables she'd pulled — always slightly too long, always terminated with that extra half-inch of copper showing because she'd been green and hadn't learned to dress them tight — were still in the hull. She found them every time she opened a panel. Rookie work she'd never fix, because Gus had sealed them in place himself, saying, "It works, doesn't it?"

The Furuno was out now. Screen delaminating, SD card slot stuffed with a folded square of paper towel to keep the card seated. The space it left was a dark rectangle of unstained fiberglass, warmer in color than the surrounding panel, like a tooth pulled from a jaw. Gus's coffee mug was still on the chart table — brown ceramic, chipped on the rim, a faded coho decal. Margaret picked it up, looked at it, set it on the galley counter. She didn't wash it.

She unloaded by hand: two pelican cases, a roll of marine-grade cable, torque driver, heat gun, adhesive-lined heat shrink, laptop in a waterproof sleeve. Three trips up the ladder.

The compute unit was the size of a shoebox. Ruggedized, conduction-cooled, IP67, potted against vibration. Inside: a neural accelerator running quantized models — catch prediction trained on fifteen years of regional fishery data, route optimization accounting for fuel and tide and regulatory boundaries, a compliance module tracking quotas and closed areas via satellite. By the standards of what usually lived in a salmon seiner's wheelhouse, a generation ahead. Maybe two.

She bolted it to the overhead where Gus's AM/FM radio had been. Custom bracket, marine aluminum, drilled and tapped, each bolt torqued to spec. Cable runs took two hours — power from the house bank through a dedicated breaker, NMEA bridge to the existing depth transducer, GPS puck on the hardtop with a clear sky view. She worked methodically, dressing every run with cushion clamps, labeling both ends. She'd done this on dozens of boats. Hands and muscle memory.

The display went in the old chartplotter's spot. But the software driving it — this is where she'd done something different.

Black background. Phosphor-green text. Monospace. The font was a pixel-for-pixel recreation of the old Furuno sounder font — the same blocky numerals, the same too-wide letterforms, the lowercase g listing to the right like it was falling over. She'd spent eleven hours on that font. Matched the scan-line artifacts. Matched the faint persistence — the way old LCD pixels held their state for a beat too long, leaving a ghost trail behind fast-changing numbers. Eleven hours because the interface was the relationship. You could put the most sophisticated model in the world on a boat, and if the screen felt foreign, the captain would turn it off and never turn it on again. But if it felt like it belonged, they'd look at it. And looking was all it took.

---

Sofia arrived at nine.

Ford Ranger, bad u-joint, telltale chirp on the turn. She climbed the ladder with the ease of someone who'd been climbing onto boats since she could walk — because she had. Twenty-six. Gus's coloring — dark, Tlingit — and Gus's build, short and dense, like she could haul a seine by hand if the hydraulic failed. Carhartt beanie pulled to her eyebrows. Canvas jacket too big for her. Gus's jacket. Margaret recognized it.

She carried a manila folder.

"Morning," she said from the wheelhouse door.

"Morning. Coffee's in the thermos."

Sofia poured a cup and sat on the helm seat. She set the folder on the chart table, next to the empty space where the coffee mug had been.

"I'm selling the boat."

Margaret looked up from the cable she was terminating. Sofia's voice was flat. Decided. Rehearsed.

"Okay."

"I've got a buyer. Lodge in Craig. They want her for the hull and the license." She opened the folder. "Surveyor came out last week."

Margaret crimped the terminal, gave it a tug. Good. She set the tool down.

"Gus paid for this install," she said. "I'm going to finish it."

"Then I'll sell it with a computer I never turned on."

They looked at each other. Sofia's jaw was set. Her eyes were hard. But her hands, wrapped around the thermos cup, were shaking. Not from the cold.

Margaret went back to work.

The rain drummed on the wheelhouse roof. Somewhere in the yard, a compressor kicked on. Sofia sat with the surveyor's report open on her chart table, reading it the way you read a contract you've already decided to sign — not for content but for the formality.

"I've been fishing since I was eleven," Sofia said. "Gus taught me. I know where the fish are. I know what the water looks like when the pinks are running. I know what the birds do." She paused. "I don't need a computer to tell me what he already showed me."

Margaret didn't look up. "I'm not here to argue."

"Then why are you here?"

"Because he asked me to come."

The words settled between them like silt. Sofia drank her coffee. Margaret finished the cable runs, connected the display, seated the NMEA bridge. She pulled the laptop out and ran diagnostics — power clean, signal good, GPS lock acquired, accelerometer stable, temperature nominal. The compute unit was running its models in inference mode, doing nothing, burning about as much power as a cabin light. It didn't care if anyone was watching. It was a patient thing.

---

At one o'clock, Margaret was ready.

"Want to see it?" she asked from the helm.

Sofia was still in the helm seat. The surveyor's report was closed. She hadn't looked at it in an hour.

"Does it matter?" Sofia said.

Margaret didn't answer that. She reached up and held the power button on the display. The screen flickered — she'd programmed a CRT-style warm-up, the old degauss and settle — and then the interface loaded. Black screen. Green text. That blocky, too-wide font, every letter a small grid of pixels.

The first screen wasn't a chart or a prediction. Margaret had programmed it that way on purpose. The first thing it showed was Gus's single most productive waypoint — a position on the shelf edge, twelve miles northwest of Cape Edgecumbe. One hundred and sixty-three logged sets across forty-five years.

It was labeled, in Gus's handwriting, **THE KITCHEN**.

Margaret had scanned his logbook. The hand was unmistakable — cramped, leaning hard right, the K in Kitchen barbed like a fishhook. Below the name, his note:

*"Good on the incoming tide. Salmon stack up on the shelf edge. Trust the birds."*

Sofia went still. Margaret watched her face and saw it land — the flinch, the softening, the way her mouth opened slightly and closed.

"I'll be on deck," Margaret said, and left.

---

Sofia sat in Gus's chair, wearing Gus's jacket, reading Gus's handwriting on a screen he'd never seen. Rain on the wheelhouse roof. The compute unit humming above her. The smell of the wheelhouse — diesel, old varnish, salt, the ghost of cigarettes that never fully aired out.

She should have turned it off. She should have called the lodge in Craig, signed the surveyor's report, and walked away. That was the plan. That was what she'd decided on the ferry from Juneau, watching the water go dark.

She touched the screen. Scrolled.

More waypoints. Each with Gus's label, his notes, his catch data. **HOG HEAVEN** — *"Pinks on the flood, north corner."* **GUS'S FOLLY** — *"Never pays on Tuesday. I don't know why."* **THE DENTIST** — *"Don't ask."* She recognized these. She'd been to these spots, standing on the deck at nineteen, hauling seine while Gus called the sets from the bridge.

She scrolled to the bottom. Forty-seven waypoints. The last one was labeled **EAGLE POINT**, and beside it, in Gus's hand: *"Dead since '19. Warm water pushed everything north. Don't waste fuel."*

Below the note, the system displayed its own assessment: a green icon, steady — good catch probability based on historical patterns. The system was recommending Eagle Point.

Sofia stared at the green icon.

She'd been on the boat in 2019. Working deck for Gus during the summer. She remembered the warm water that sat in the Gulf for two years — the pale, cloudy sea, the plankton gone chalky and thin, the sonar screen empty where there should have been marks. She remembered Gus standing on the bridge, looking at the water, and saying, simply, "Eagle's dead."

The system didn't know that. The system saw thirty-five years of abundance at Eagle Point and weighted the six years of silence as anomaly, not absence. The model had no category for *a thing that ends*. It saw the pattern. It missed the break.

And there it was — the gap. Between the data and the note. Between what forty-five years of numbers said and what Gus's pencil said and what Sofia's own memory said from the deck of a boat in a sea gone the color of milk.

The system had Gus's patterns. It did not have his judgment about the exception. And the exception — the moment the water changes, the moment the fish don't come, the moment the old knowledge stops working — that was exactly the knowledge you couldn't digitize. That was the knowledge that lived in the body. In having been there.

Sofia picked up the surveyor's report. She looked at it — the numbers, the hull assessment, the lodge's letterhead. Then she set it facedown on the chart table.

"Margaret."

The door opened. Margaret stood in the frame, rain on her jacket.

"Can you add notes to a waypoint?"

Margaret came in. She looked at the screen — Eagle Point, Gus's note, the green recommendation icon. She saw the contradiction immediately. Her mouth tightened.

"The model doesn't know about the warm-water event," Margaret said.

"No. It doesn't."

"I can retrain it. But it'll need your data. Catch reports from 2019 onward — the years you worked deck for Gus."

Sofia looked at her. Then she looked at the screen.

"Show me how to flag a spot."

Margaret sat at the helm. She opened the annotation tool — simple, designed for wet hands and rough water. Long-press a waypoint, select status: **DEAD**, **ACTIVE**, **SEASONAL**. Add a note in the text field.

Sofia took the screen. She long-pressed Eagle Point. Selected **DEAD**. In the note field, she typed:

*"Warm water '19-'20. Plankton crashed. Fish pushed north. Don't waste the fuel. — S."*

The S sat on the screen. A new voice in a forty-five-year-old record.

Margaret packed her tools. Pelican cases clicked shut. Laptop into its sleeve. She wiped the helm console, collected her scraps, coiled the spare cable. She moved Gus's mug from the galley counter back to the chart table, next to the thermos, where he would have kept it.

At the wheelhouse door, Margaret stopped.

"The interface," she said. "I made it look like his old sounder."

Sofia didn't turn from the screen. "I know," she said. Her voice was thick. Then, quieter: "Thank you."

Margaret climbed down the ladder. She loaded her truck, sat in the cab for a moment, engine off, rain on the windshield. Through the wheelhouse windows, she could see Sofia's silhouette — leaning forward now, one hand on the screen, scrolling to the next waypoint.

Margaret started the truck and pulled out of the boatyard.

In the wheelhouse of the F/V Daybreak, Sofia read Gus's note. She checked it against what she knew — the year, the tide, what the water had done. She made her mark. She moved to the next.
