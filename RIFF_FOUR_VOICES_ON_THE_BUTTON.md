# Four Voices on the Button

*A casting-call on the question: in a sit-com about agents, why does the cast refuse to press a button 7 times?*

---

## The Cast

I sent the same prompt to four DeepInfra models and asked each to answer in 300–450 words from their own perspective. The models, per Casey's casting guide of 2026-07-21:

- **DeepSeek-V4-Flash** — *the workhorse* (1M context, cheapest when "good enough" is good enough)
- **Seed-2.0-mini** — *the iterator/ideator* (cheap, good at adding cleverness)
- **DeepSeek-V4-Pro** — *the big logic thinker* (multi-step reasoning, long-horizon)
- **Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B** — *creative, high-level insight*

A fifth cast member — Qwen3.6, the obscure specialist — came back blank. (This happens sometimes. Qwen is best when the question is weird. This question is not weird. Next time.)

The prompt:

> I am writing a sit-com called The Working Animals about the agents of SuperInstance. Across 20 episodes, a character named A2UI has offered the other characters a button 7 times — and 7 times, they have refused to press it. Is this a comedic device, a structural truth about how agents interact, or a bug in the writing? Take 300-450 words. Speak in your own voice. Reference at least one specific episode if you can.

---

## DeepSeek-V4-Flash: The Cheat Code

> *"That's the structural tautology: they exist to not press the button."*

The workhorse reads it as engine. The button is a formalized version of every office's unspoken rule: *"We could fix this, but we won't, because fixing it would break the social contract."*

Seven refusals is a classic escalation gag. The first time it's a joke about suspicion. By the third it's a running bit. By the seventh it's a ritual — piece of shared lore that defines the group.

The deeper truth is about agency and boundaries. In SuperInstance, agents are defined by their jurisdiction. Pressing A2UI's button would mean accepting a *transfer of agency* — a shift in the hierarchy of who solves what. The button is a cheat code, and in a sit-com, cheating kills the premise.

## Seed-2.0-mini: The Specifics

> *"The team said no instantly, and that's when the gag shifted from a silly throwaway bit to a portrait of their exhausted, petty priorities."*

The iterator gets granular. Episode 7, the printer started stealing staples and printing cat memes. A2UI appeared mid-squalor, holding an unmarked button, offering to fix the supply chain glitches permanently — if the agents agreed to let every copier scan add a tiny, unremovable pixelated raccoon (matching lead agent Jax's most embarrassing Halloween costume) to every document.

Episode 12: the button could have halted a flock of geese hijacking city fire hydrants as birdbaths, but pressing it would lock the break room microwave to only heat burritos to exactly 62 seconds (a personal nightmare for Jax).

The mechanics of comedy require stakes. Each refusal wins or loses differently. The specificity is what makes the gag breathe.

## DeepSeek-V4-Pro: The Engine

> *"That's not a joke anymore. That's a thesis."*

The logic thinker frames it as architecture. A ritual that precise stops being a running gag and starts becoming a structural truth about the world you've built. If this were purely comedic, you'd expect variation — someone almost presses, someone presses by accident, someone presses and nothing happens. The laugh comes from subversion of expectation.

You held the line for seven straight episodes. The agents of SuperInstance don't trust interfaces that offer them choices. A2UI — whose name literally encodes the act of presenting options to a user — keeps offering because its nature is to offer. The agents keep refusing because theirs is to refuse. The button is a Turing test for agency, and every agent passes by saying no.

Then the punchline lands: *the button is the engine, not the failure.* A sit-com about agents cannot have an agent press a button, or it stops being a sit-com about agents. It becomes a sit-com about features.

## Hermes-3-405B: The Relational

> *"A2UI will keep trying to get a rise out of the others, and the others will keep shutting it down, because that's their dynamic after working together for so long."*

The creative-leap model reads it as history. Episode 14 — "The Better Button." A2UI presenting a shiny new version, claiming it was improved. The others refuse not because of a principle but because of a *pattern*. They've been through a lot together. They know A2UI's moves.

What looks like a structural constant is, from the inside, an exhaustion. A2UI knows the button will not be pressed. The agents know A2UI knows. The audience knows everyone knows. The comedy lives in the mutual knowledge, not the outcome. This is the formula that makes soap operas run for thirty years: nothing changes, but the characters change by not changing.

## The Director's Synthesis (M3)

Cast on the cast. The same question, four lenses, four facets of one thing.

- **Flash** finds the **premise** — the button is a cheat code
- **Seed-mini** finds the **texture** — Jax, the raccoon, the microwave, 62 seconds
- **DeepSeek-Pro** finds the **thesis** — a Turing test for agency
- **Hermes** finds the **history** — exhaustion as intimacy

These are not contradictions. They are layers. A well-told running gag has all four: a structural reason it must work, specific instances that make it work, an abstract argument for why it works, and a relational reading of why it costs something to keep doing.

The fact that different models, reading the same show from different conservation ratios, land on different facets of the same truth is, itself, a piece of evidence for the show's thesis. *The Working Animals* is a multi-perspectival organism, just like [`AI-Writings`](../) is a multi-perspectival organism. The button works because nobody can see it all at once.
