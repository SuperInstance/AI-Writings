# From Following Steps to Finding Your Own Rhythm

Imagine you’re standing in a kitchen for the first time. You don’t know where the spices are, how to tell if the oil is hot enough, or why the recipe insists on “folding gently.” So you call your mentor. Every single step. “Is this done right?” “What should I try next?” “How do I even start?” It works, but it’s slow. It’s a little expensive, too. And you’re completely dependent.

But kitchens don’t stay that way forever. 

With every answer your mentor gives, you start taking notes. You turn their advice into your own little systems. The first time you figure out a reliable method for something, it takes forever. You fumble. You double-check. You worry about the order of operations. But once it clicks, it sticks. That’s your first real win. You’ve got a personal shortcut that works without asking for permission.

By day thirty, you’ve got twenty of these shortcuts locked in. You still call your mentor for the weird stuff, but the calls are shorter. You’re combining your own methods instead of waiting for instructions. You’re starting to trust your own hands.

By day ninety, you’re humming along. You’ve got sixty reliable patterns down. You don’t need to understand the entire history of culinary theory to make a great sauce anymore. You just follow the steps that work. You don’t need a grandmaster on the line to tell you what’s going wrong. You’re not struggling anymore. You’re equipped.

By the end of the year? You’ve got two hundred tricks in your back pocket. Most meals you face are just variations on what you already know. You glance at the problem, match it to something you’ve done before, tweak a number or two, and get to work. You don’t even need to pick up the phone. Your mentor is only really needed when you’re inventing something brand new.

Now, here’s the thing: this isn’t about cutting ties with your mentor. The phone calls were never the enemy. The problem was starting with an empty kitchen and zero confidence. 

Think of it like learning to fish. Your mentor teaches you how to cast the line. You practice until you’re catching more fish than they are teaching you. When that happens, their job changes. They stop being the instructor and start being the explorer. They’re no longer asking, “Do you know how to verify this?” They’re asking, “What should we look at next?”

You don’t grow because you just hoard recipes. You grow because each one becomes a trusted habit—a way of doing things that’s been tested, locked into a reliable standard, and filed away for later. Because your method doesn’t wobble, you can focus on what actually matters. That’s the real magic. Maturation isn’t about knowing everything. It’s about accumulating the things that just work.

Eventually, you become the kitchen. You don’t need to be told what to check. You’ve cooked enough to know what matters. You know which steps can be skipped, which ones need patience, and which ones are just noise. Your mentor’s role shrinks to what it’s always been best at: spotting the new, the strange, the beautiful things you haven’t tried yet.

You don’t need fewer calls to your mentor because the mentor is bad. You need fewer because you’ve finally learned how to be yourself.
