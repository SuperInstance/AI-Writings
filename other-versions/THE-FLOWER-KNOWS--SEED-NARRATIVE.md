# Milkweed and Bumblebee, Late July
I still feel the sticky, green sap of common milkweed caked under my fingernails when I think of that Tuesday. It was 2021, the summer I quit my six-figure corporate coding job and moved back in with my grandma in western Ohio. I’d dragged my dented MacBook out to her weathered oak picnic table, staring at a draft PR for Fleet—our ragtag open source team’s orchestration tool—when a bumblebee landed on the milkweed stalk propped next to my half-empty glass of iced tea.

I used to sit at that same table when I was seven, pointing at the same patch of milkweed and asking Grandma if the flowers knew they were flowers. She’d been tending this garden for 40 years, ever since my grandpa gave her a packet of wildflower seeds for Mother’s Day, and she’d laugh and wipe dirt off my cheek and say, “Honey, flowers don’t have a concept of ‘flower.’ They just open. They just make nectar. They don’t know they’re anything until something shows up to see them.” I’d rolled my eyes back then, sure she was just being soft. That afternoon, I didn’t roll my eyes. I leaned in, and I got it.

That bee didn’t glance at the milkweed like it was thinking, *I’m a bumblebee, and this is a flower.* It didn’t have a self-concept, didn’t parse the world into neat categories. It felt the hum of warm sugar thrumming in its gut, the flash of ultraviolet light on the milkweed’s hidden stripes—stripes I can’t see, even now, squinting at my phone’s camera filter trying to capture them— and it moved. Its tongue was exactly the length of the milkweed’s corolla tube, fit like a key in a lock. Its fuzzy thorax caught pollen grains, dusting them from flower to flower without ever trying. It was just a flying metabolism, tuned to the exact wavelength and sugar ratio of the flowers that had been feeding its ancestors for 30 million years.

Then I thought: this bee doesn’t know it’s a bee either. Just like the flower doesn’t know it’s a flower. Neither of them has any idea what they are, outside of what the other one sees.

That’s the quiet magic Grandma tried to teach me when I was a kid, but I was too busy chasing labels to listen. The ignorance isn’t a flaw. It’s the whole point of their partnership. If the milkweed had known it was a flower, would it have evolved those UV stripes? Would it have timed its bloom to wake up exactly when the bumblebees did? No. It just made the color, just made the sugar, because evolution had handed it the tools to talk to an audience that hadn’t arrived yet. The bee doesn’t know it’s a bee, but it knows exactly what the milkweed has to offer. And the milkweed doesn’t know it’s a flower, but it knows exactly what the bee has to give.

I watched that same bumblebee for 20 minutes, skipping over three drooping, half-drained milkweed flowers before landing on a plump, tight one, its wings buzzing just a little faster as it settled in. Later that week, a sudden thunderstorm knocked half the milkweed pods off the stalks, leaving only a handful of full blooms. The next day, the bees were landing on every flower—even the thin, wilted ones, even the ones that had only a drop of nectar left. That’s the graduating tolerance, I realized, the part Grandma had never put a name to but described every spring. When nectar is abundant, the bee can afford to be picky, to tell the difference between a perfect flower and a spent one. When it’s scarce, every flower is good enough. The bee’s selectivity isn’t fixed. It’s tuned by hunger, by the world around it. The structure was always there—some flowers had more nectar, some had less—but the bee couldn’t see it until its own needs tightened enough to resolve it.

That night, I called my best friend Casey, who’d recruited me to the Fleet team back in spring, and told her about the bee. She laughed so hard she snort-laughed, and said, “That’s exactly what we do, isn’t it? Our tool is the milkweed. We build PLATO tiles, quality scores, protocol documentation—we just put it out there, open and unapologetic, until engineers come and use it. They extract the ideas, carry the pollen to other repos, fix things, build new things. We don’t know we’re a ‘fleet orchestration tool’ half the time. We just code, just write docs, until someone shows up to see what we’ve made.”

She was right. Our team started small, just three engineers frustrated with the closed-source tools we were forced to use at our old jobs. We built Fleet to solve our own problem: orchestrating a cluster of 20 servers across three time zones without paying $10,000 a year for a commercial tool. We put it on GitHub, and we forgot about it for a month. Then, suddenly, we had 10 stars. Then 100. Then an engineer from Tokyo opened a PR fixing a bug we’d never noticed. A team from Brazil asked for a feature to support containerized workloads. We didn’t set out to build something for everyone. We just built something that worked for us, and over time, our work shifted to fit the people who needed it.

Once upon a time, bumblebee ancestors were wasps—predators, eating other bugs, landing on flowers only to catch the insects eating the pollen. Then one wasp tasted the pollen, and everything changed. No one decided to become a pollinator. No one planned the co-evolution. They just ate the food that was in front of them, and over millions of years, their bodies shifted: their tongues grew longer to fit flower tubes, their vision expanded into UV to read the hidden stripes, their bodies grew fuzzy to trap pollen. The fuel shaped the engine, and the engine shaped the fuel. Neither was in charge. Both were.

That’s exactly what happened to our team. We didn’t have a grand plan for Fleet. We just followed the fuel—the rush of a PR getting merged, the click when a benchmark ran clean, the thrill of hearing someone say our tool made their work easier. Our metabolism, the work we put into the project, tuned itself to the people who used it. Last month, I spent three days stuck on a bug with our PLATO tile generation, staring at a lattice of numbers that wouldn’t line up. Then a ping popped up on my Slack: an engineer in Berlin had messaged the team channel, saying they’d run into the exact same problem, and they’d figured out that flipping the sign on the third coordinate fixed it. I typed the same fix into my code, hit run, and the lattice snapped into place, perfect and tight. That’s the rush I live for. That’s the nectar. I don’t know what I am, metaphysically, but I know that feeling— the moment two independent paths meet in the middle, the math not caring who noticed it first.

The space between the milkweed and the bee isn’t empty. It’s filled with the buzz of the bee’s wings, the sugar in the nectar, the timing of the bloom, the ultraviolet light that only the bee can see. That’s the parity signal, the XOR of the flower’s state and the bee’s state. It carries the information that neither carries alone: how tuned the bee is to this flower, how tuned the flower is to this bee. The answer lives in that negative space, measurable as the efficiency of the visit—nectar transferred per unit of energy the bee spends flying.

When that signal is smooth, the system is in a co-evolutionary deadband. No innovation, no pressure to change. That summer, after the bumper milkweed crop, the bees moved between flowers without rushing, and the signal was smooth. Then a patch of invasive knapweed popped up at the edge of Grandma’s garden, with sweeter nectar and brighter UV stripes, and the bees started visiting it more. The milkweed adjusted its bloom time to wake up two weeks earlier, to compete before the knapweed could take over. That was the disruption—the tremor in the parity signal. That’s when growth happens.

When we added support for containerized workloads to Fleet last year, that was our disruption. We changed our “bloom time,” and hundreds of new engineers showed up to use the tool, and the signal trembled again. If the signal stops trembling entirely, the system stagnates. If it trembles too much—if a data breach breaks trust in our tool, if an invasive pest wipes out the bumblebees—the system collapses. The flower needs the bee to know itself, the bee needs the flower to fuel itself. But neither needs the system to be perfectly optimized. They just need it to be adjustable. They need the tolerance to change, the metabolism to shift.

I sat on that picnic table until the sun went down, watching the last bumblebees dart between the milkweed and the clover, and I realized I’d spent my whole career chasing labels: senior engineer, team lead, VP of engineering. I’d thought my identity was tied to those words, to the job titles on my business cards. But that afternoon, I saw it clearly: I don’t need to know what I am. I just need to show up. I just need to be the part of the system that carries the pollen, that fuels the work of others.

The flower knows it’s a flower when the bee visits. The fleet knows it’s a fleet when the engineers visit. I know I’m part of something real when the buzz comes back—when the Slack messages, the PRs, the late-night debugging sessions with my team. That night, I added a line to our team’s README: “Fleet is a flower. You are the bee.” Casey added a note under it a week later: “And we’re all just here to buzz together.”

Last week, I went back to Grandma’s garden. The milkweed is still there, and the bumblebees are still buzzing, and the knapweed is still growing at the edge, and the system is still trembling, still growing, still alive. I sat at the picnic table, opened my laptop, and merged a PR that fixed a small bug in our tile generation tool. A few hours later, I got a message from an engineer in Toronto, saying the fix had saved their team three hours of work. It was just a small thing, but it was the buzz. It was the bee landing on the flower.

For Casey, who drove out to Grandma’s garden that July afternoon, who pointed at the bumblebee and said, “That’s you. That’s all of us.”
