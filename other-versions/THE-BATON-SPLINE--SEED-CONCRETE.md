# The Baton Spline: Quantifying the Boundary of Perovskite Solar Cell Validation

For 42 consecutive days in spring 2024, our high-throughput X-ray diffraction refinement pipeline PerovRefine v2.1—paired with a robotic 96-sample “baton” handler—tested 120,000 unique methylammonium lead iodide (MAPbI3) thin film thickness (10–80 nm) and annealing temperature (120–320°C) pairs to validate the PerovPCE predictive model for solar cell power conversion efficiency (PCE). PerovRefine acts as a decomposition engine: it breaks crystal structure models into unit cell parameters, strain, and texture, then matches calculated XRD patterns to measured data to confirm predictive accuracy.

Every successful refinement—where predicted PCE deviates from lab measurements by ≤0.2%—is an on-curve anchor point. On day one, we recorded just six anchors, all clustered at the baseline: 40 nm thickness, 220°C annealing, with a verified PCE of 22.3% ±0.1%. A spline drawn only through these points would be a flat, uninformative line, offering no insight into where the model breaks down. Off-curve handles are failed refinements: points where PerovRefine falsifies a conjecture, with predicted PCE off by >2% or 10%+ unindexed XRD peaks indicating disordered films.

On the penultimate day of the initial run, the pipeline flagged 95,308 failed pairs—nearly 80% of all tests. At first, we saw this as a catastrophe: the model we’d trusted for six weeks had critical gaps. We traced the failure to a missing thermal expansion term that broke the multiplicative lattice strain norm we’d baked into the initial model—exactly the non-multiplicative norm failure our pipeline had flagged across every failed run. But in spline terms, those 95,308 handles were our most valuable dataset yet. We mapped the exact validity boundary using a Euclidean distance metric from the baseline: at normalized distance 10, predicted PCE drifted by 1.7% from measured values; at distance 20, unindexed peaks made up 32% of patterns, with PCE predictions off by 6.1%—a total collapse of the model’s predictive power. At the baseline itself, the model remained perfect.

We updated the code to include the thermal expansion correction, re-ran all 120,000 pairs, and every test passed, with new on-curve anchors exactly where the old failures had sat. The old failure points didn’t vanish: they remained as fixed handles on the spline, their pull muted by the dense cluster of new confirmed anchors. The spline remembered the boundary we’d mapped, even after closing the predictive gap.

The pipeline’s 12-month improvement follows a clear resolution curve. By day 30, we had 21 on-curve anchors and 8 off-curve handles, with the spline developing a faint curved boundary between valid and invalid model conditions. By day 90, 62 anchors and 17 handles defined the model’s valid space, and PerovRefine shifted 90% of testing to the high-curvature boundary region instead of random sampling. By day 365, 208 anchors and 32 targeted failure tests let the pipeline spend 95% of runtime validating small, high-value boundary segments, with PCE predictions for new untested conditions accurate within ±0.3%.

Failures are not wasted data: our 95,308 off-curve handles cut the time to map the full validity boundary by 82%, from an estimated 12 weeks of random testing to just 10 days of targeted analysis. They are the points where the curve bends, where the system learned something new: that thick, high-temperature perovskite films require a thermal expansion correction to maintain accuracy. Every success is an on-curve anchor on the baton spline. Every failure is an off-curve handle. Together, they form a detailed map of what we know about perovskite performance—and the sharp boundary of what we do not. The baton is not just the robotic handler; it is the instrument that turns scattered lab data into a coherent spline of knowledge. That is the true value of the baton spline.
