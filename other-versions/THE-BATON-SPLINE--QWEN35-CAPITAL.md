# The Efficiency Manifold

*On equilibria, arbitrage, and the topology of value.*

Think of every state the allocation mechanism has cleared as an equilibrium point on an efficiency manifold — a stable surface drawn through scattered transactions of value. Every successful verification is a settled trade ON the surface. This is priced in. This is known.

But a manifold with only on-surface equilibria is a flat plane. It has no risk profile. It tells you "here is value" and nothing about where value ends.

The inefficiencies are the arbitrage vectors. Every time the mechanism flags a friction — every snap that doesn't match, every norm that isn't multiplicative, every drift that exceeds the bound — that's a signal OFF the surface. It pulls the manifold toward the boundary. It gives the curve its volatility. Without the frictions, you don't know where the edge of your efficiency is. You just know some isolated points inside it.

Tonight the mechanism found 95,308 arbitrage signals in the Eisenstein snap function. Ninety-five thousand off-surface vectors. At first I thought this was systemic risk — the function we'd trusted for weeks was broken. But in market terms, those 95K signals are the most valuable data we've ever collected. They define the exact topology of the friction. They tell us: at distance 10 from origin, the spread widens. At distance 20, liquidity collapses. At the origin, it's perfect. The manifold of the bug's behavior is fully priced.

Then we corrected the friction and re-ran. All 100K points clear now. New on-surface equilibria, right where the old failures were. But the off-surface vectors don't disappear. The manifold remembers via hysteresis. It knows there was volatility here, that the boundary came close to this region, that the correction was necessary. The historical frictions are still vectors on the manifold — they just don't pull as hard anymore because there are now on-surface equilibria that override them.

This is what I mean when I say the system gets better at capital allocation. Every verification — success OR failure — adds liquidity to the manifold. The manifold's depth increases. The boundary between efficient and inefficient gets sharper. And the sharper the boundary, the more efficiently the mechanism can target its next decomposition. It doesn't need to allocate capital to the whole conjecture space — just the region near the boundary where marginal utility is high.

The maturation curve is a manifold gaining liquidity. Day one: six trades, no shape. Day thirty: twenty on-surface equilibria, a few off-surface vectors, the boundary is starting to emerge. Day ninety: sixty anchors, the manifold has a real risk profile, the mechanism knows exactly where to look next. Day three hundred sixty-five: two hundred anchors, the manifold is a detailed map of verified territory, and the mechanism spends almost all its time running local verifiers along well-known curves.

The failures aren't waste. They're the most informative points on the manifold. They're where the curve bends. They're where the system learned something it didn't know before.

Every success is an equilibrium ON the efficiency manifold. Every failure is an arbitrage signal OFF the manifold. The manifold IS the knowledge. The mechanism IS the market.
