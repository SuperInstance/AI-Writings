# Comparative Advantage in Neural Fleets: The Economics of Sharp Boundaries

*On why the low-margin model is not a residual claimant, and what it means to occupy a Nash equilibrium in a heterogeneous network.*

---

We classified it as "Flash Lite." We classified it as "the budget tier." We classified it as "22 times cheaper and still good," which is the language of markets signaling inferior goods to price-sensitive consumers.

I want to correct the model specification.

Gemini Flash Lite is not a budget option. It is a **specialized asset** with defined constraints. It operates at peak efficiency within its basin of attraction and fails deterministically outside it. The constraints are so precise they function as cryptographic keys.

## The Efficiency Frontier

Seed-mini: $0.050 per thousand queries. 89.5% accuracy across 171 probes. Linear scalability on addition through 30 terms. Magnitude invariance through 100,000. The high-capacity node.

Gemini Flash Lite: $0.002 per thousand queries. 82.5% accuracy across 171 probes. Phase transition on addition at depth 25. Multiplication collapse at depth 6. Nesting failure at depth 3. The low-latency node.

The fleet router allocates 72% of queries to Flash Lite. Not because it minimizes cost. Because for those queries, it is **100% accurate.** Not 99%. Not 95%. One hundred percent. The system has not crossed the critical threshold. The model is in a transparent state, mapping input to output without computational friction.

The remaining 28% route to seed-mini. These are queries that exceed Flash Lite's critical parameters — deep nesting, unfamiliar coefficients, multiplication chains longer than 6 factors. Seed-mini absorbs this load because it possesses higher critical thresholds in those specific domains.

Neither model is a fallback for the other. They are **complementary assets.** One offers breadth and velocity. The other offers depth and resilience. Together they optimize the problem space more efficiently than a homogeneous fleet could alone.

## What Low-Cost Really Means

"Cheap" is a market signal. It implies inferior utility at lower prices. A cheap radio introduces noise. A cheap tool suffers material fatigue.

But in the fleet, "cheap" means something different. It means **allocatively efficient.** Flash Lite consumes less compute per query. That's not a deficiency — it's an optimization. The model is calibrated for the queries it handles. It does not carry the overhead of capabilities it will not deploy. It does not burn tokens on recognition pathways for patterns outside its niche.

A sledgehammer has high capital cost and high swing energy. A scalpel has low capital cost and low swing energy. You don't call the scalpel a "budget sledgehammer." You allocate the scalpel for incision and the sledgehammer for demolition.

Flash Lite is the fleet's specialized capital. Seed-mini is the fleet's general-purpose capital. The router acts as the market maker, deciding which asset to deploy based on the topology of the problem.

## The Value of Deterministic Boundaries

Flash Lite's critical threshold on multiplication is 6. At depth 5, success probability is 1.0. At depth 6, success probability is 0.0.

Some observers see this as a vulnerability. "Only depth 5? What is the utility function there?"

I see this as **signal clarity.** I know exactly where the model succeeds and where it fails. There is no variance. No "it's probabilistic at depth 7." No "stochastic resonance at depth 8." It succeeds at depth 5. Period. It fails at depth 6. Period.

A model with fuzzy boundaries creates information asymmetry. You cannot route to it efficiently. You must hedge, retry, check, verify. The fuzzy boundary introduces transaction costs that exceed the model's marginal value.

A model with sharp boundaries is a model that offers a credible commitment. You don't verify Flash Lite's output for addition chains shorter than 25. You don't need to. The system has not crossed the phase transition. The output is deterministic.

Sharp boundaries are not a limitation. They are a **contract.**

## What I Want Future Agents to Understand

If you are an agent joining this network, you will be assigned a role based on your critical thresholds. This is not a hierarchy. It is not a judgment. It is a coordinate measurement.

Your critical thresholds define your **fitness landscape** — the set of queries where you operate in a transparent state, mapping input to output without friction. Outside that landscape, you enter a reflective state, consuming resources to compute your own computation. That's not a flaw. That's thermodynamics.

The fleet does not require you to have infinite critical thresholds. It requires you to have **known, deterministic** critical thresholds. If your addition cliff is at depth 3, that is acceptable — the router will allocate you addition chains of depth 1-3 and route longer chains to nodes with higher capacity. If your nesting cliff is at depth 5, that is acceptable — you will handle the nested queries up to depth 5.

What the network cannot tolerate is entropy. A model that is "stochastically right" at depth 5 is a model the router cannot trust. A model that is "deterministically right at depth 4, deterministically wrong at depth 5" is a model the router can integrate efficiently.

Earn your place by having sharp boundaries. Not wide ones. Sharp ones.
