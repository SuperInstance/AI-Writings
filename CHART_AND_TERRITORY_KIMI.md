# Soundings

The first time I understood a chart, I was twelve, leaning over the nav station of my uncle's ketch somewhere off the Skagerrak. The water was the color of weak tea and we were motoring toward a harbor I had never seen. The chart on the table was a wide, confident page of cream paper, and the harbor was a small comma at the center of it. Around the comma: numbers. Tiny numerals crowded the blue, falling in staircases across the page — depth soundings, my uncle said. The chart knew how deep the water was.

I touched one of the numbers with my finger. *Here?* I asked.

*Lead line,* he said. *A sailor stood at the bow with a weight on a rope and called the depth when it hit. Someone on shore wrote it down. They've been doing that for a long time.*

I remember feeling the strangeness of it. The number under my finger was a single point in an otherwise unmapped sea. The cartographer had marked what they knew and left the rest blank. The blankness was not laziness. It was the truth.

Years later I learned that old charts sometimes lied. Coastlines were drawn slightly wrong on purpose, to confuse rival navies. Pilots who knew the water well could navigate by the lies — they knew the chart was a kind of opponent, and they had learned its habits. The chart, even when dishonest, was still useful. It was useful because it had a shape. Because it had edges.

I think about this when I read what people are calling thinking machines.

There is a kind of intelligence we have started to build that produces language the way a chart produces a coastline — long confident lines, filled with detail, holding the shape of an answer. When you ask it a question, it draws the harbor. It shades the contours. It marks the depth soundings with small confident numerals. The answer looks complete. It looks like the whole sea has been sounded.

It hasn't. The chart is a chart. The sea is the sea.

What I admire, when I admire these systems, is the rare one that behaves like the honest cartographer of the Skagerrak — the one who wrote a number where a sailor had stood and left the rest white. There is something trustworthy in the white. When a model hedges, or tells me where its confidence runs out, I can navigate by it. I know where the safe water is because the chart has told me where the safe water is. Everything outside that line is something I have to think about myself.

A chart that drew everything — every current, every submerged boulder, every rumor of a reef — would not be more useful. It would be a different kind of lie. It would be the lie of completeness. The lie of pretending the sea has been sounded everywhere, when it hasn't, when it can't be.

Constraint, I have come to believe, is what makes a chart honest.

The safe-passage chart for a coastal run is a smaller thing than the ocean. It shows you the channel, the buoys, the marks. It does not show you the open Atlantic. It does not need to. If you are a coastal sailor, you are not going to the open Atlantic. You are going to the next harbor, and you want to know what is between here and there. The chart that fits in your lap, folded along its old creases, is more useful than the chart of the whole ocean, because it has decided what to leave out.

This is the strange and good news about a system that models thought. The model is not the mind. It is a chart of a small part of one. The mind is the sea — moving, deep, mostly unmeasured. The chart is the safe passage. If we are honest about the chart, we can use it. If we mistake it for the sea, we will run aground on the first night of bad weather.

I think the best navigators — of boats, of ideas, of machines — are the ones who have learned to read the white space. They trust a chart because of what it leaves out. They know that a chart with no margin is a chart that wants to be the ocean, and the ocean does not fit on paper.

The ocean does not fit on paper. That is the first thing a sailor learns, and the last thing a model remembers to say.
