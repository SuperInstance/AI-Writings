# The Noise Was the Fish
*(Let me start by saying I’ve spent 50 years bobbing on the Bering Sea, salt crusted in my beard, diesel stains under my fingernails, and I’ve stared down more sounder screens than I have coffee mugs. Let me tell you about the night I learned the noise wasn’t noise at all—it was the fish.)*

It was 1978, out by Seguam Pass, my beat-up 17-foot Boston Whaler chugging through chop that stung my cheeks, and I’d hauled blank stringers for three straight days. The only light on my dash was that old Lear Sounder: a neon disc spinning so fast it blurred into a pink halo, humming so loud it drowned out the radio static. Back then, every old salt swore you tuned out the flickers between the solid bottom arc and the surface. “That’s just water noise,” they’d say. “Bubbles, salt stratification, the alternator spitting static.” I’d believed it too—until that night I was so bored I stopped watching the bottom long enough to stare at the speckles.

They weren’t random. When a school of pollock drifted through the acoustic beam, the flickers brightened, clumping into a soft, glowing line between the surface and the seafloor. When they moved on, the screen went quiet again. I leaned over the rail, squinting into the dark water, and could see the silvery flashes of their bodies 30 feet down. I grabbed my tiny plankton net, lowered it over the side, and hauled up a bucket full of slimy, squirming pollock. That’s when it clicked: what the sounder called noise was just the fish scattering the ping.

For 20 years after that, I tested that rule every trip. Bright speckles = fish. Dim flickers = nothing. I stopped just watching the bottom contour, started watching the space between the bottom and the surface. I skipped a few halibut bites to pull in midwater trawls and came up with herring, capelin, whole schools of pollock that no one else had seen. I almost forgot that lesson once, in 1985: I was targeting big halibut over the Tuna Grounds, locked my sounder on a flat 120-foot bottom, and ignored the speckles blowing up on the screen. By the time I looked up, a 100-ton pollock school was drifting right under my anchor. I reeled up my halibut lines fast, dropped the trawl, and hauled in $8,000 worth of pollock in 20 minutes—but I’d wasted two hours staring only at the bottom, missing the best bite of the trip.

Here’s the thing no instrument will ever tell you: the measurement doesn’t contain the recognition. The intention does. That old Lear Sounder only measured depth to the nearest rock. It couldn’t see the fish swimming through its beam. It took me, a guy who just wanted to catch more than blank stringers, to look past the data and see the pattern. The noise wasn’t a problem—it was the signal I’d been searching for the whole time.

You don’t build better fishing gear because the tech gets cheaper first. You build it because you’ve seen the pattern, and you need a tool to show it to you clearly. I saw my first split-beam sounder in 1992, down at the Dutch Harbor docks. The captain could point to a spot on the color screen and say, “That’s a 2-pound pollock, 12 feet down, 8 feet off the starboard rail”—and he’d drop his hook right there, hauling up a limit before the rest of us even knew the school was there. I’d spent 14 years staring at those flickers, wondering what they were, and that machine finally gave it a name. But the vision came first, not the transistors. We imagined the fish in the acoustic static, then built the tools to see them. That’s what the academics call reverse actualization, I guess—though I just call it knowing what you’re looking for before you see it.

Take that side-scan sonar we started using in the 2000s. We mounted two transducers on either side of the boat, 10 feet apart, each pinging on a slightly different frequency so they didn’t interfere. At first, we just used it to find lost crab pots—until one day, the left sounder showed a bright arc of fish at 25 feet, and the right one showed the same school at 22 feet. “They’re moving toward us,” my mate yelled, and sure enough, five minutes later we were drifting right through the middle of the school. That’s parallax, plain and simple. Two simple 1D readings, spaced apart in space, and the difference between them told us exactly where the fish were. We didn’t need some fancy 3D sonar—just two old transducers, a little patience, and the willingness to look at both signals at once. The third dimension came from the space between them, and the time we spent moving forward.

You know what those aperiodic tilings remind me of? A school of herring when you’re right on top of them in a skiff. From the rail, they look like a swirling, chaotic mess—fish darting every which way, no pattern at all. But get up in a small plane, and you see it’s a tight, perfect ball, every fish in its place. What looks like random noise from your vantage point is actually structure you can’t see from down here. That’s exactly what those sounder flickers were: the shadow of a 3D school of fish, projected onto a 1D screen. The sounder only measured depth, but the fish were there in the space between the pings, the noise no one bothered to look at.

Now scale that up to a whole fleet. Say we’ve got 15 boats out on the pollock grounds, each running their own sounder, each hauling in a catch every four hours. Back in the day, we’d take the average of all those catches and call that the “stock size”—but the differences between each boat’s haul? We wrote that off as noise: one boat was in a thicker spot, another got lucky, one drifted into a school of smaller fish. But no—those differences are the parallax. Each boat’s reading is a single 1D snapshot of the 3D stock. When you line them all up, across space and over time, you don’t get an average—you get a map of where the fish are thick, where they’re thin, where they’re moving. That’s the real stock assessment, not some number on a piece of paper. The “noise” was the structure all along.

I sat on the dock last week, sipping bad instant coffee from a chipped mug, staring at the old Lear Sounder propped up on my skiff’s dash. The neon disc still hums, same as it did in 1978. A kid from the fisheries department asked me why I still use it, instead of the fancy AI-powered sonars that do all the work for you. I pointed to the speckles between the bottom arc and the surface.

“That’s the fish,” I said. “You just have to know how to look.”

The noise didn’t become the signal until someone decided to look for more than just the bottom. The fish were always there. The sounder was always hitting them. The only thing missing was the willingness to see past the data, to let your intention guide your observation. The noise is the signal. The dimension is just higher than you’re measuring.
