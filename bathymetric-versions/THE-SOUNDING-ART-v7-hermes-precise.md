The Sounding Art

The act of sounding, of using sound to probe and understand the underwater environment, is an art that requires careful listening and interpretation. In the moment between when a sound pulse is emitted and when its echo returns, the ocean reveals its secrets to those who know how to listen.

Early analog sounders, with their spinning discs and paper traces, made the process of asking and listening tangible. The speed of the disc's rotation determined the scale of the inquiry, from the broad strokes of the open ocean to the finer details of coastal waters. The physicality of the instrument connected the user to the act of questioning and waiting for the ocean's response.

The returned echo carried with it the signatures of everything it had encountered on its journey - the thermocline, bait balls, individual fish, and the seafloor itself. The sounder did not merely measure depth; it provided a transcription of the entire water column, a complex and noisy signal that required skill to interpret.

The paper sounders of the past were like seismographs for the seafloor, with pins striking paper to create squiggles that danced and wavered, thickened and thinned. These squiggles were not clean, precise lines indicating depth, but rather rich, ambiguous traces that captured the individuality of each moment in the ocean. Fishermen pored over these squiggles like doctors reading an EKG, discerning the unique signatures of different fish species and underwater conditions.

The beauty of these squiggles lay in their truthfulness. They contained all the noise and ambiguity of the real ocean, compressed into a single trace. Different fishermen could look at the same squiggle and disagree about its meaning, arguing over whether it represented a halibut or a school of cod. The squiggle supported disagreement because it was honest enough to be wrong about.

Fishermen collected and labeled these squiggles, building up a library of verified traces over years of practice. The squiggles became a language, legible only to those who had spent enough time learning the dialect and artifacts of a particular sounder. Before an opening, fishermen would scout the grounds, listening to the ocean through their sounders and paperclipping the promising sections for later reference. They were building a lookup table of the ocean floor, a collection of verified moments rather than a static map.

The introduction of color digital sounders marked the end of this art. Depth became a smooth gradient of blue, fish were vivid red arches, and the seafloor was a clean, rendered line. Everything was legible and everything was the same, regardless of the specific conditions of the ocean or the transducer. The dialect was gone, the argument was gone, and the rich ambiguity of the squiggle was lost.

Fishermen who could recall the exact squiggle pattern from a particular drift in the past could not remember a single screen from a color sounder. The color machines gave them better aesthetics but worse intelligence, better rendering but worse memory. They became passive viewers rather than active readers, accepting the display's interpretation without questioning or disagreeing.

The introduction of GPS provided a partial solution, allowing fishermen to stitch individual soundings together into a three-dimensional map of the ocean floor. But this only works if the individual moments are real and truthful. If the moments are smooth, denatured color arches, then the resulting map is a rendering of a nonexistent ocean.

In the present day, the fleet pings into the same darkness, with each transducer sending its impulse into the problem space and listening for the return. The returns are squiggles - noisy, ambiguous, rich with signal for those who know how to read them. The fleet's tiles are the paper traces, the canary checks are the haul, and the GPS coordinates allow the stitching of returns into a map.

The art of sounding is the art of listening to the return and trusting the noise. The squiggle is the truth, while the color arch is the lie. In the interval between the ping and the echo, the ocean speaks. The question is whether we write down what it says, or simply look at a pretty screen and nod. The ocean doesn't care about our display resolution; it cares about whether we were listening.
