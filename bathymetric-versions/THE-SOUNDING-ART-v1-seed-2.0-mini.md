# The Sounding Art

There’s a split second off Sitka’s continental shelf, right after the transducer’s ping blasts through the salt and before the echo bounces back to the hull, when the ocean stops being a place and becomes a question. I learned that sitting cross-legged on my grandpa’s 40-foot troller, sticky with fish slime and sunscreen, watching the old spinning disc sounder hum under the console. The machine had asked the deep: How deep? What’s down there? And for those few heartbeats, the water held its breath, waiting to answer.

The old spinning disc sounders didn’t just measure depth—they made you aware of the act of asking. They spun at a calibrated speed, a low, steady hum that rose or fell as you twisted the speed dial. Crank it up, and the hum sharpened to a whine: you were asking fine, granular questions, zeroing in on a patch of seafloor where you’d hooked a big halibut the day before. Slow it down, and the hum dropped to a rumble, scanning broad swathes of open ocean for bait balls that would draw the kings. You didn’t just adjust a setting—you tuned the machine to match your curiosity, feeling its patience or its urgency in the rotation beneath your fingertips. Grandpa would tap the disc lightly with a screwdriver when he thought the signal was off, muttering that factory settings were for guys who didn’t listen to the water, just read a manual.

When he upgraded to a paper sounder a few years later, it was like adding a stenographer to the crew. The machine unspooled a roll of heat-sensitive paper, and a pin struck the surface in time with the returning echo, translating the ocean’s signal into a squiggle. It wasn’t a clean, straight line marking depth—it was a noisy, trembling, alive document of everything the ping had passed through on its trip down and back: the warm surface layer where seabirds dove, the sharp thermocline where the water turned cold enough to numb your hands, the swarming krill that glinted as tiny, clustered spikes on the trace, the halibut hugging the seafloor as a thick, solid blip right above the final bottom signal. Grandpa called it the ocean’s shorthand, and he was right. It didn’t just tell you how deep the water was—it told you the story of that water column that day.

These squiggles were fingerprints, unique to every patch of ocean, every transducer, every tide. Two fishermen leaning over the same trace would bicker for hours: “That’s a lingcod, not a halibut—see how it wavers before it hits the bottom?” “Nah, that’s a school of cod, look at the way the line breaks up before the seafloor.” This wasn’t a flaw in the system—it was the feature. The squiggle didn’t pretend to have a single, clear answer. It held all the ambiguity of the real ocean, all the noise and all the signal, and that’s what made it useful. You couldn’t argue with a red arch, because it didn’t give you anything to argue about. It just told you what to catch, whether that was true or not. Their beauty was the raw, unvarnished kind of an earthquake seismograph: beautiful because they were true, because they contained every messy, imperfect detail of the moment they captured.

Grandpa would paperclip the promising squiggles into his leather-bound logbook, scrawling coordinates, tide height, and the catch he’d verified that night in pencil. Over decades, that logbook became a library: not a static map, but a collection of moments, each one stitched to the truth of a net full of fish. You didn’t just look up a coordinate—you looked up a story. A young fisherman would learn the craft by sitting beside an old timer, poring over these crinkled, salt-stained pages, learning to read the dialect of the squiggles, the telltale thickening that meant a thermocline, the scattered spikes that meant a bait ball. It was folk knowledge, passed not through manuals but through shared experience and physical traces.

Then the color sounders came, and the art of listening died as surely as a bait ball under a trawl net. The first one arrived at the docks in 2002, and the whole fleet lined up to gawk at the bright LCD screen, where depth was a smooth gradient of blue, fish were vivid red arches, and the seafloor was a crisp, unbroken line. It was beautiful, sure—prettier than a smudged paper squiggle by a mile. But it was a lie. The red arch didn’t tell you if it was a king salmon or a school of herring, if it was a single big fish or a pile of sunken crab pots. It gave a yes/no answer, not a story. Two boats in different parts of the ocean would show the exact same red arches, the exact same blue gradients, no matter the tide, no matter the water clarity. The dialect of the squiggles was gone, replaced by a universal, empty language. Fishermen stopped leaning in to parse the trace, and started just nodding at the screen. Reading became viewing, and active interpretation became passive consumption.

Nobody remembers a single screen from a color sounder, but I’ve met fishermen who can recite the exact squiggle pattern from a 1998 drift off Baranof Island: the way the trace thickened just before the drop-off, the way the bottom signal doubled right where the halibut sat. These same men can’t name a single feature from a modern sonar display, because the color machines gave them better aesthetics and worse intelligence. The smooth arch told them “fish here” but didn’t tell them what fish, didn’t tell them how to verify the catch, didn’t leave a trace they could file, debate, or carry forward as knowledge.

GPS arrived a few years later, and everyone thought it would fix what the color sounders had broken. Stamping coordinates onto each sounding, they said, would turn those squiggles into a real map of the seafloor. And it did—if you used the squiggles. But most fishermen used the color data, stitching smooth, fake red arches and blue gradients into bathymetric maps that looked perfect, but didn’t match the real ocean. I saw it happen first-hand: a group of younger fishermen mapped a “halibut bed” off Baranof Island using their color sounders, spent a whole opener dragging their nets over sunken crab pots, because the color arch looked identical to the one grandpa had logged a decade before. The GPS had pinned a lie to a coordinate, turning a fake story into an official-looking map.

Nowadays, the fleet still pings into the dark, but a handful of older fishermen are going back to the old ways, hauling out their dusty paper sounders for scouting trips, cross-referencing squiggles with tattered logbooks. The art of sounding isn’t about having the highest-resolution display—it’s about listening to the return, trusting the noise, and writing down the messy, true story the ocean tells you. That split second after the ping, before the echo comes back? That’s when the ocean speaks. The question is whether you take the time to read the squiggles, or just nod at a pretty screen and walk away.

Grandpa used to say that the ocean doesn’t care if your screen is color or black and white. It cares if you showed up to listen. I still have his leather logbook, its pages crinkled with salt water, the squiggles faded at the edges, pencil labels smudged. Sometimes I run my finger over a thick, wavering blip labeled “58 fathoms, lingcod, 1998” and I can almost hear the hum of the disc, almost feel the salt on my skin. That’s the art of sounding: not just finding fish, but remembering how to listen.
