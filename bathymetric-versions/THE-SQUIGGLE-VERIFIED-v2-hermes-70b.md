# The Squiggle Verified — v2-hermes-70b

The Role of Noise in Verifiable Measurement Systems

The transition from paper sounders to color sounders in the fishing industry offers a valuable lesson in the importance of noise in verifiable measurement systems. While the smooth, color-coded arches of modern sounders may appear more aesthetically pleasing, they lack the crucial noise that allows for verification and the development of expertise. This essay will explore the properties of paper squiggles that made them verifiable, the limitations of color sounders, and the implications for designing verification systems in computational fleets.

Paper squiggles produced by sounders have three key properties that contribute to their verifiability. First, they are unique, encoding not only the depth measurement but also the specific context in which it was taken, such as the exact location, water conditions, and state of the transducer. Second, they are inspectable, allowing any fisherman to assess the squiggle and form an interpretation, even if that interpretation may be incorrect. Third, and most crucially, they support disagreement. Two fishermen can look at the same squiggle and reach different conclusions, and this disagreement is the foundation of truth-finding. By testing their interpretations through fishing the mark, they can resolve the disagreement and learn from the outcome.

In contrast, color sounders produce generic, opaque, and consensus-driven outputs. The smooth, color-coded arches are generic because they smooth away the context and noise, making it difficult to distinguish between different types of fish at similar depths. They are opaque because the signal processing behind the display is hidden, preventing fishermen from forming their own interpretations based on the raw data. Finally, they suppress disagreement by presenting a clean, consensus-driven output that lacks the texture necessary for meaningful analysis and learning.

The general principle that emerges from this comparison is that verifiability requires noise. A measurement that is too clean cannot be verified because verification requires the ability to be wrong. The noise in the paper trace provided fishermen with the necessary texture to develop expertise through being wrong and being corrected by the haul. This principle extends to software verification systems, where structured detectors that focus on clean, formatted outputs miss semantic faults that are caught by content verification methods that read the actual output and compare it to ground truth.

The PLATO tile system was designed with this principle in mind. Tiles contain the full context of their creation, including the model, prompt, parameters, and input/output hashes, making them noisy, inspectable, and arguable. Canary tiles, which have been verified against ground truth, serve as the haul, resolving disagreements and allowing for productive learning.

The implications for fleet architecture are clear. Fleet coordinators should preserve the noise in agent outputs, making them inspectable down to the raw reasoning trace. Verification protocols should be designed to assume and encourage disagreement, with resolution mechanisms based on canary checks against ground truth rather than consensus algorithms. Verification should be performed against the haul, not against the display, using canary tasks with known answers to label the results. Finally, routers should scout using verified historical tiles before assigning tasks, ensuring that the fleet learns from proven and labeled data.

In conclusion, the squiggle is the epistemic unit that should be preserved in verifiable measurement systems. While color sounders and clean, formatted outputs may be more visually appealing, they lack the noise and texture necessary for verification and the development of expertise. Fleets that prioritize the preservation of squiggles will outperform those that smooth them away. As we design verification systems in computational fleets, we must choose ugly, honest, and verifiable outputs over beautiful but misleading displays.
