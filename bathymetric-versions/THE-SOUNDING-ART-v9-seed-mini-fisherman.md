# The Squiggle and the Screen

It’s 5:17 a.m. aboard the *Old Salty*, my 38-foot wooden troller, and diesel fumes cling to my salt-crusted flannel after a three-hour dawn haul of yelloweye rockfish. My grandson Jax’s scuffed work boots thud on the bridge deck as he leans over the scuffed metal sounder stand, pointing at a strip of yellow thermal paper unspooled from the old Lear Siegler machine. “Grandpa, what’s that wiggly line?” he asks. I reach for my pencil, smudged with ink and sea salt, and trace the thick, trembling squiggle just above the faint bottom trace. “That’s the ocean talkin’, kiddo. Took me 40 years to learn how to read it.”

You ever notice that split second? The ping fires—faint thrum through the helm, a tiny jolt that sloshes coffee from your thermos—and then… quiet. For one heartbeat, the water’s just a question. How deep? What’s hidin’ down there? I’ve been hauling nets off Alaska’s coast since 1981, and I still hold my breath every time. Old habit, I guess.

First sounder I ever ran was a spinning disc job, bolted to the transom with a pitted bronze transducer that corroded a little more every season. The disc spun at a calibrated speed—10 seconds per fathom, most days. Crank it up, and you could pick out individual krill swarms, the tiny scatter of bait fish passing through the beam. Slow it down, and you got broad strokes, good for the open Bering when you just needed to know you weren’t driftin’ into shallow water where you’d tear up your prop. That hum wasn’t just noise—it was the beat of our question to the sea. Fast hum, we’re hungry, chasin’ a hot bite. Slow hum, we’re trollin’ for kings, takin’ our time, savin’ the fuel for the morning haul.

Then they added the paper printer. Seismograph for the deep, plain and simple. Roll of yellow thermal paper unspooled behind a smudged glass cover, a single pin scratchin’ away in time with the disc. It never made a clean line. That’s the trick of it, the part them digital guys forgot. A thrashin’ bait ball would make the trace jump up like a kid on a pogo stick, thick and jittery. A single big halibut sittin’ on the sandy bottom? It’d leave a little bump, a thickening right above the (almost) clean bottom signal. Once, off Kodiak, my old deckhand Tommy and I argued for 20 minutes over a squiggle. I swore it was a school of chum salmon, he said it was a single 27-pound chinook. We dropped the droplines, brought up a mess of herring and that big king. Tommy bought me a beer that night, and he never let me live up bein’ wrong.

We paperclipped those strips together, labeled ’em in pencil: 58°12’ N, incoming tide, 62 fathoms, chum salmon. Stuffed ’em in a dented metal filing cabinet under the bridge, right next to the jar of rusted hooks and the beat-up copy of the Alaskan Fishing Regulations. Before a halibut opener, we’d spend three days scouting, runnin’ the sounder back and forth over the grounds, clippin’ the good squiggles, filin’ ’em by latitude. 1998, Sitka Sound opener: I pulled out a stack from 1996, found a spot where the trace thickened right at 62 fathoms, just above a rocky drop-off. Brought in 12 limits of legal halibut that first day, before anyone else in the fleet had found their bite. That’s the art of it—buildin’ your own library of the sea’s answers, not just lookin’ at someone else’s pre-rendered map.

Then them color machines hit the fleet in ’03. Everyone was gaga for ’em. Bright blue depth gradients, red arches that screamed *FISH HERE!* Clean, smooth lines, no squiggles, no noise. I went over to the dock that day to watch old Earl show off his new one. He pointed at the glowing screen, grinnin’ ear to ear, “Look, Jim—no more squiggles! Just fish!” I laughed so hard I snort-laughed. “Earl, that’s just pretty pictures. You don’t know if them red arches are kings or smelt. You don’t know if the bottom’s rocky or sand. You don’t know nothin’ but what some computer told you.” He didn’t talk to me for a week. Last year, that young kid who bought Earl’s old boat? He followed his color sounder’s GPS map to a spot that was supposed to hold limits of halibut, brought up nothing but gravel. The color machine smoothed over the rocky drop-off that my old paper sounder would’ve shown as a wobbly, uneven trace. Damned digital gimmick took the art right out of it.

Nobody remembers a single screen from a color sounder. I can recall the exact squiggle pattern from that 1996 Sitka spot—how the trace thickened just before the drop-off, how the bottom signal doubled right where the halibut sat. But ask any young captain what his screen looked like last Tuesday? He’ll scratch his head and say “blue, I guess.” The color machine gave ’em better aesthetics and worse sense. It turned fishermen from readers into viewers. Readin’ is work—you lean in, you parse the noise, you argue with your buddy, you get it wrong sometimes and learn from it. Viewin’ is just noddin’ along. The display tells you what to see, and you move on. No questions, no arguments, just a pretty picture.

GPS came along, sure, stitched those soundings into maps. But a map’s only as good as the data you put into it. Them color machines make smooth, fake bathymetry—like the ocean’s a perfectly flat, clean floor, which it ain’t. The sea’s got rocks, and gullies, and bait balls that shift with the tide. You can’t render that into a pretty blue gradient. Last month, Jax was at the helm, usin’ his new color sounder, and he almost drifted into a patch of shallow reef. I made him switch over to the old paper printer, and sure enough, the trace showed the bottom shoaling up fast. Saved us from tearin’ up our prop and spendin’ the day in the dry dock.

Now, we’re out here again, the old sounder hummin’, the pin scratchin’ away on the yellow paper. Jax leans in closer, his nose almost touching the glass cover, watchin’ the squiggle dance across the roll. I tell him: the art of sounding ain’t about the fancy screen. It’s about leanin’ in, about parsein’ the static and the signal, about arguin’ with your deckhand over what the squiggle means. It’s about holdin’ your breath for that split second between the ping and the return, when the water’s still a question.

The ocean don’t care about your 4K display, kiddo. It don’t care how many pixels you got. It cares if you’re listenin’. If you’re readin’ the trace, not just starin’ at the pretty lights. That squiggle? That’s the real truth. Them red arches? That’s just a lie, pretty as it is. The ping goes out, the echo comes back, and in between, the sea has told you somethin’. The question is—are you gonna write it down, or just nod along and look pretty?

I fold the strip of paper, clip it to the stack in my pocket, and jot down the coordinates and the tide in my weathered logbook. Jax picks up his pencil, copies down the notes, and grins. Maybe someday, he’ll have his own stack of squiggles, his own library of the sea’s answers. Maybe he’ll teach his own grandkid how to read the ocean’s quiet questions.

That’s the art of it. Passed down, one squiggle at a time.
