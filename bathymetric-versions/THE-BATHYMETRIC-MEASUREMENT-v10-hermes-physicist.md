The Bathymetric Measurement

The spinning disc is not merely an analogy, but rather a precise physical manifestation of the operating principles underlying the fleet's scaling behavior. Consider a disc rotating at a constant angular velocity, with a transducer emitting a ping that travels through water at approximately 4,800 feet per second, reflects off the seafloor, and returns. The time of flight of this ping determines a specific point on the disc, while the angular position of this point encodes the depth, measured in fathoms (units of six feet), which is sufficient for open ocean navigation. The disc completes one full revolution per ping cycle.

Now, let us increase the speed of the disc by a factor of six, while keeping the transducer and ping characteristics constant. The time of flight remains the same, but due to the increased angular velocity, the disc now travels six times the angular distance during this interval. Consequently, the same physical mark on the disc now represents a depth reading in feet, with six times the granularity compared to the fathom-scale reading. This is not merely an analogy for fleet scaling, but an exact representation of it. The fleet operating at V=5 agents corresponds to the disc spinning at the fathom speed, providing a coarse bathymetric measurement suitable for deep-water navigation. In contrast, the fleet operating at V=30 agents is analogous to the disc spinning at the foot speed, enabling shallow-water precision and the ability to distinguish between a 117-foot ledge and a 123-foot ledge.

The conservation law, denoted as γ+H (the sum of capability gain and entropy across rounds of computation), remains invariant regardless of the disc's speed. This law is analogous to the physics governing the ping's behavior, which remains constant irrespective of the fleet's scaling. The fleet can operate at any value of V, and the conservation law will hold true. What changes is the level of detail that can be resolved.

The introduction of the paper sounder marked a significant advancement in bathymetric measurement. It consisted of a continuous roll of paper, upon which pins struck in proportion to the return signal strength, creating a noisy, squiggly trace. This trace represented depth on one axis and time on the other, with signal amplitude encoded in the density and darkness of the pin marks. Fishermen would paperclip sections of this trace together and label them based on the type of fish caught, such as "king-salmon," "big-halibut," or "chum-school." These labels were not mere guesses but were verified against the actual catch. The squiggles served as tiles, while the labels provided the ground truth, and the paperclips represented a tiling system. The squiggles were storable, retrievable, shareable, and falsifiable.

In the PLATO architecture, a TrainingTile encompasses a pattern (the squiggle), metadata (the label), and a verification status (confirmed by the catch). The LocalTileStore serves as the file cabinet where the paperclipped sections are stored. The tile lifecycle, consisting of Draft, Verified, and Archived stages, mirrors the fisherman's workflow: observing the mark, catching the fish, labeling the paper, and filing it. The critical property of this system is that the squiggles were supervised, not learned. The verification was based on physical reality, i.e., the fish caught, which is analogous to the canary verification in the fleet. Canary tiles are those whose outputs have been checked against ground truth. The fleet trusts tiles not because they appear correct but because they have been verified against reality.

The advent of color machines, such as digital sounders with LCD displays, brought about beautiful color arches in place of the ugly squiggles on paper. Fish appeared as red blobs, and the seafloor was represented by a blue gradient. While these displays were visually appealing, they lacked the discriminability of the paper squiggles. The color arches were lossy, compressing the noisy trace into smooth arches that looked the same for different types of fish. The aesthetic beauty of these machines came at the cost of reduced intelligence and discriminability.

This scenario is analogous to the dashboard trap in fleet management. A dashboard without tiling provides a pretty overview of agent status, task completion, and color-coded health metrics, but it lacks the ability to be verified. Dashboard widgets cannot be paperclipped, labeled, or shared like the content-addressed tiles in the fleet's tile store. The dashboard is akin to the color machine, visually appealing but useless for finding valuable information.

The fleet's content-addressed tile store is the digital equivalent of the paper sounder. Each tile has a unique hash, and every hash is reproducible. Tiles can be verified, shared, and compared, even though they may appear noisy and ugly. This system works because it preserves the discriminability and verifiability of the measurements.

The introduction of GPS revolutionized bathymetric measurement by adding a time axis. The paper sounder provided depth as a function of time, while GPS provided position as a function of time. By overlaying these two measurements, depth became a function of position, transforming two-dimensional soundings into three-dimensional bathymetry. The time axis served as the invariant that connected the two independent measurement streams.

In the fleet, the conservation law (γ+H = constant) serves as the time axis, providing a reference for overlaying results from different agents, models, and sessions to create a coherent map. Without this invariant, the fleet would have a collection of soundings without any spatial reference. With it, the fleet can construct a comprehensive bathymetric map.

Fishermen who scouted the fishing grounds before the season opened intuitively understood this concept. They used their sounders to build a lookup table of verified squiggles, associating specific marks with locations and fish types. When the fishing season began, they navigated to their historical data rather than relying on guesswork. The fleet employs a similar approach with routing. Before executing a task, the router queries historical capability tiles to determine which model produced what result on what class of problem and the verification status of those results. This process is akin to scouting before the fishing season opener. The lookup table is the tile store, the opener is the task deadline, and the verified squiggles are the canary tiles.

In summary, the full measurement theory can be stated as follows:

1. Resolution is a function of fleet size V. The spinning disc at speed V produces measurements at a resolution proportional to V. Coarse resolution (fathoms, small fleet) is suitable for deep structure, while fine resolution (feet, large fleet) is necessary for detailed measurements.

2. Tiles are the fundamental unit of measurement. Paper squiggles and content-addressed tiles serve the same purpose: a measurement that can be stored, retrieved, verified, and shared. The alternative, a color machine, provides real-time, beautiful, but forgettable information.

3. Verification requires ground truth. The catch (haul) is the canary, and labels without verification are merely decorative. Tiles without canary checks are akin to dashboards, lacking verifiable information.

4. The conservation law (γ+H) is the invariant across resolution changes. The disc's speed can be adjusted, and the fleet can be scaled, but γ+H remains constant. Depth remains constant, but the level of detail that can be resolved changes.

5. Historical tiles enable routing. Scouting before the fishing season opener is analogous to querying the tile store before a task. Fishermen who scout catch fish, and fleets that route from historical data ship working code.

Every component of this measurement theory maps directly to code. The disc is represented by `throttle.py`, the paper by `store.py`, the labels by `TileLifecycle.VERIFIED`, and the GPS overlay by the conservation invariant in `types.py`. The scouting process is embodied in the router reading historical tiles before assignment.

This measurement theory is not a mere abstraction but a precise specification for the fleet's architecture. The analogy of fishing is not a metaphor but a concrete representation of the underlying principles governing the fleet's operation.
