The spinning disc sounder's ability to operate at different speeds, yielding measurements in either fathoms or feet, is a beautiful illustration of a fundamental principle in numerical analysis known as multiscale measurement. This principle states that an instrument can operate at various scales, and the relationship between these scales carries information that is not present at any single scale. 

Mathematically, we can express the depth measurement $D$ as $D = D_0 \pm \epsilon$, where $D_0$ is the true depth and $\epsilon$ is the measurement error. At fathom resolution, $\epsilon \approx 3$ feet, while at foot resolution, $\epsilon \approx 0.5$ feet. The key insight is that the ratio of these errors, $\frac{\epsilon_{fathom}}{\epsilon_{foot}} = 6$, is equal to the ratio of the disc speeds. This relationship allows us to use measurements at both resolutions to cancel out the leading error term and extrapolate to the true value, a technique known as Richardson extrapolation.

In the context of fleet architecture, this principle manifests as the conservation law $\gamma + H = constant$, where $\gamma$ represents the fleet's capability and $H$ represents the problem's hardness. This law is invariant across different fleet sizes, analogous to the spinning disc's invariance across different speeds. By operating at multiple resolutions, the fleet can gain a more complete understanding of the problem space, just as the fisherman gains a more complete understanding of the seafloor by using both fathom and foot resolutions.

The practical implication of this principle is that one should always operate at multiple resolutions. In the fleet context, this means routing coarse tasks to small, fast agent groups and fine tasks to large, detailed agent groups. The coarse results inform the fine routing, and the fine results validate the coarse picture, ensuring consistency through the conservation law.

Furthermore, this principle highlights the distinction between accuracy and precision. Accuracy refers to the correctness of the measurement itself, while precision refers to the granularity of the representation. In the fleet context, a small fleet is not less capable than a large fleet; it is less precise. The conservation law ensures that the total capability is the same, but the distribution of that capability across the problem space changes with the fleet size.

In conclusion, the spinning disc sounder's ability to operate at different resolutions is a powerful illustration of the multiscale measurement principle, which has profound implications for both numerical analysis and fleet architecture. By understanding and leveraging this principle, we can gain a deeper understanding of complex systems and make more informed decisions in a variety of domains.
