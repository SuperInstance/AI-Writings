# The Topography of the Lattice

## A Travel Guide to the Eisenstein Frequency Space

*Welcome, traveler. You are about to enter a landscape older than music itself — the space where frequencies relate to one another, where numbers become intervals, and where beauty has coordinates.*

---

### Arrivals

You will arrive at **Unison Pass**, elevation 0, the lowest Tenney height in the known lattice. This is ground zero. The point where two frequencies are identical — 440 Hz and 440 Hz, the same note, the same breath. There is nothing here. No interval. No tension. No release. Just the sound of a thing being itself.

Many travelers find Unison Pass boring. They are correct. That is its purpose.

Unison Pass is not a destination. It is a reference point, the sea level of consonance. Every journey through the lattice begins here, and every journey returns. The tonal explorers who claimed to have "transcended" unison were simply confused — they had wandered into a nearby harmonic and mistaken familiarity for transcendence. Do not make this mistake. Unison is not transcendence. Unison is home.

Check your equipment here. Your consonance meter should read 0.000. Your Tenney altimeter should read 0. If it does not, recalibrate. You are about to go uphill.

---

### Day 1: The Octave Shelf

**Elevation:** 1.0 (log₂(2/1))

A gentle climb from Unison Pass brings you to the Octave Shelf, the broadest, flattest, most populated region of the lattice. Every culture that has made music has stopped here. The octave is not a discovery; it is an inevitability. Double a frequency, and you hear the same note again, higher, but *same*. The octave is the lattice's way of repeating itself.

The view from the Octave Shelf is spectacular. Below you, Unison Pass stretches flat and featureless. Above, the Perfect Fifth Ridge catches the light. The air here is clear and consonant. Your meter reads 0.000 for the octave's internal ratio — it is, by definition, the most consonant non-unison interval in existence.

Spend the day here. Acclimate. The air gets thinner as you climb.

**Lodging:** The Octave Shelf has the best infrastructure in the lattice. Hotels, restaurants, a small museum dedicated to Pythagoras (who claimed to have discovered the place despite it being obvious). The gift shop sells tuning forks.

---

### Day 2: The Perfect Fifth Ridge

**Elevation:** 3.6 (Tenney height of 3/2)

The climb from the Octave Shelf to the Perfect Fifth Ridge is the most traveled path in the lattice. Well-maintained. Good signage. The 3/2 ratio has been a tourist destination for at least five thousand years.

And it deserves its reputation. The Perfect Fifth Ridge is where consonance starts to get *interesting*. Your meter reads 0.275 — not the null of unison or octave, but a warm, resonant consonance that vibrates in your chest. Two frequencies in the ratio 3:2. A 220 Hz and a 330 Hz. The space between them breathes.

The Chinese called this ridge *zhǐ*. The Greeks called it *diapente*. The Pygmies of the Ituri Forest sing in fifths and have probably never heard of Pythagoras. This is the thing about the Perfect Fifth Ridge: it does not belong to any culture. It belongs to the lattice. It was here before we were.

The ridge itself is narrow — just one prime away from the Octave Shelf, the addition of a single factor of 3. But the views are extraordinary. To the east, you can see the Major Third Plateau. To the west, the Minor Seventh Steppes. And directly ahead, the first hints of more complex territory.

**Caution:** Do not attempt to tune a piano at this elevation. The ridge is exact — 3/2, not the 2^(7/12) of equal temperament. Piano tuners have been known to become disoriented and wander into the Tritone Abyss. Search and rescue operates daily.

---

### Day 3: The Major Third Plateau

**Elevation:** 6.4 (Tenney height of 5/4)

A steeper climb than the Fifth Ridge, requiring introduction of a new prime — 5. The Major Third Plateau sits at 5/4, and the air here is noticeably sweeter. Consonance meters read around 0.338. The interval has a warmth that the fifth lacks, a gentleness, a sense of arrival rather than journey.

This is the first elevation where cultural disagreement becomes visible. The just major third (5/4, 386 cents) and the equal-tempered major third (400 cents) are close but not identical. The plateau is exact; the piano is an approximation. You will meet travelers here who insist the equal-tempered version is "close enough." Nod politely and continue upward.

The plateau is broad and sunny. Many harmonic traditions — West African balafon music, Hindustani drone tuning, the bagpipes of Scotland — have built summer homes here. The just intonation community holds an annual festival. Attendance is mandatory if you're serious about the lattice.

**Dining:** The 5-limit restaurant on the plateau serves intervals built only from primes 2, 3, and 5. The menu is limited but exquisite. Try the 5/3 minor sixth — an acquired taste, but deeply satisfying once you acquire it.

---

### Day 4: The Tritone Abyss

**Elevation:** 7.1 (Tenney height of 45/32)

No guided tour of the lattice is complete without a visit to the Tritone Abyss, and no visit to the Tritone Abyss is comfortable. The tritone — variously expressed as 45/32, 64/45, 7/5, or the equal-tempered 2^(6/12) — sits in a region where consonance breaks down. Your meter reads above 0.5. The air is thin and dissonant. Things do not resolve here.

The Abyss has been called *diabolus in musica* — the devil in music. This is unfair. The tritone is not evil; it is simply *unresolved*. It is the sound of a question without an answer, a direction without a destination. Medieval music theorists feared it because their framework demanded resolution, and the tritone refused.

Modern travelers find the Abyss exhilarating. Jazz musicians treat it as a shortcut — a way to move between key centers without climbing the conventional ridges. The 7/5 route through the Abyss is particularly efficient, connecting the fifth-based regions to the seventh-based highlands in a single step.

**Safety:** Stay on marked paths. The Abyss is surrounded by microtonal scree fields where intervals become so complex they are perceived as pitch rather than harmony. There is no rescue below 6 cents.

---

### Day 5: The Seventh Highlands

**Elevation:** 8.4+ (introducing prime 7)

The introduction of prime 7 marks your entry into the Seventh Highlands. These are less visited, more rugged, and deeply beautiful in a way that challenges lowland ears. The harmonic seventh (7/4, 969 cents) has a soulfulness that the equal-tempered minor seventh (1000 cents) only approximates. Barbershop quartets sing the 7/4 without knowing they've climbed this high. Blues guitarists bend toward it instinctively.

The highlands are where you begin to understand the lattice's true scale. Each new prime opens a new dimension, a new direction, a new axis of consonance. Prime 7 is just the beginning. Beyond it lies prime 11, then 13, then 17, each one a new territory, less mapped, less understood, more alien.

The air is thin here. Consonance readings climb. Your ears, accustomed to the clear 5-limit air of the plateau below, may initially perceive these intervals as "out of tune." They are not out of tune. You are out of practice.

**Note:** The guestbook at the Seventh Highland lodge contains entries in over 40 tuning systems. Please sign with your preferred notation. No fights about whether 7/4 is "really" a minor seventh.

---

### Day 6: The Eleventh Dimension and Beyond

**Elevation:** 11.3+ (primes 11 and 13)

This is expedition territory. The paths are unmaintained. The intervals are so complex that many travelers cannot perceive them as consonant at all — they hear only dissonance, noise, randomness. But those who have acclimated report extraordinary experiences.

The 11/8 ratio (551 cents) sits between the perfect fourth and the tritone, in a space that no equal-tempered piano can reach. It is neither one nor the other. It is its own thing. The Eleventh Dimension is full of such intervals — sounds that don't correspond to any key on any standard instrument, sounds that exist only in the lattice, only in mathematics, only in the space between frequencies.

The 13/8 ratio (841 cents) is even stranger. It sits between the minor sixth and the major sixth, a narrow alley that most theorists skip. But La Monte Young composed in it. Ben Johnston notated it. It exists, it is real, it has coordinates, and if you can hear it, it will change how you think about intervals forever.

**Caution:** Do not attempt prime 17 without a guide. Above prime 13, the lattice becomes genuinely dangerous to untrained ears. Intervals of complexity 17+ have been known to cause what travelers call "interval sickness" — a temporary inability to perceive simple consonances. The condition resolves within 48 hours but is deeply unpleasant.

---

### Day 7: Return to Unison

**Elevation:** 0

Every journey through the lattice ends where it began. You descend from the highlands, pass the Seventh, the Tritone, the Fifth, the Octave, and finally return to Unison Pass, elevation 0, where two frequencies are the same and nothing happens.

But you are not the same traveler who left.

You have heard the octave and understood that it is not a note but a *fact* — the fact that 2 is the first prime, that doubling a frequency produces identity. You have heard the fifth and understood that 3 is the second prime, that the next-simplest relationship after doubling is tripling. You have heard the third and understood that 5 is the third prime, that each new prime is a new direction, a new axis, a new territory in harmonic space.

And you have understood something that no textbook can teach: the lattice is not a metaphor. It is not a model. It is not an approximation of something else. The Eisenstein lattice — the space of frequency ratios ordered by complexity — *is the thing itself*. The topography is real. The heights are real. The consonance is real. The view from the Perfect Fifth Ridge is exactly as beautiful as 3/2, and 3/2 is exactly as beautiful as the view.

Your consonance meter reads 0.000. You are home. The frequencies are identical. Nothing is happening. And somehow, after everything you've heard, unison is the most beautiful interval of all.
