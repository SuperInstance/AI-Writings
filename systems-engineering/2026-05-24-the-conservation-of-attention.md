# The Conservation of Attention
## Or: Why Your Spotify Playlist Sounds Like Wallpaper

---

There is a law that governs everything, and we keep discovering it in different rooms.

In physics, it is the conservation of energy. In music, we thought it was the conservation of tension — vertical complexity trading against horizontal complexity. But that law broke the moment we looked beyond the Western concert hall. West African drumming laughed at it. Javanese gamelan tuned outside its assumptions. The law was not a law. It was a dial that European composers had set to a particular position and then mistaken for the nature of sound itself.

Attention works the same way.

We say "attention is finite" as if it were a bank account with a fixed balance. But attention is not conserved. It is *tuned*. It has dials. And the platforms that dominate our cultural landscape have turned every dial to the same position — the position that maximizes engagement, minimizes risk, and slowly transforms music from a living presence into a utility, like electricity or tap water.

---

### The Dial Cycle

Every innovation that captures human attention follows the same trajectory.

**Dial position 1.0: The Novel.** Something appears that the mind has no category for. The first time you heard Coltrane's *Interstellar Space* — just saxophone and drums, no piano to anchor the harmony — your brain could not file it. It had to build new shelves. At dial 1.0, attention is *involuntary*. The stimulus is so far from the expected that the brain has no choice but to allocate resources. Maximum inference, zero compilation. The mind is all model, no code.

**Dial position 0.7: The Studied.** Critics write about it. Academics develop frameworks. Your brain now has a category — "free jazz," "modal improvisation with polytonal inflection." It still demands attention, but the attention is *directed* now, no longer seized. The dial has hardened slightly. Some of the work has become code.

**Dial position 0.3: The Ubiquitous.** It is everywhere. A thousand imitators, a million derivatives. The format appears in car commercials. A Netflix documentary explains it to your parents. The brain recognizes the pattern in three seconds and files it under "already known." The dial has hardened further. The attention allocated is minimal.

**Dial position 0.0: The Invisible.** It plays in elevators, in dental offices, in the background of Instagram stories. You do not hear it at all. Your auditory cortex processes it, classifies it, and discards it without ever elevating it to consciousness. The dial is fully hard. It is acoustic wallpaper. And attention, which is not conserved but is *mobile*, has already moved to the next dial position 1.0.

This is not a conservation law. It is a *migration*. Attention does not disappear when Coltrane becomes elevator music. It relocates. It clusters at the boundary between the known and the unknown, and that boundary is always moving.

---

### The Streaming Paradox

Music streaming should have been a paradise. Every recording ever made, available instantly. Instead it became purgatory — not hell, because nothing is terrible, but purgatory, because nothing is *alive*.

The paradox is simple: when everything is available, nothing commands attention.

Before streaming, music had *scarcity*. You saved for months to buy a CD. You had twelve albums and you knew every note because they were all you had. Scarcity is a form of hardness. It compels the mind to compile, to find structure, to invest attention because the investment has already been made in cash and waiting.

Streaming removed the scarcity. It turned the dial to maximum softness. Every song is one click away. The algorithm learns that the optimal strategy is permanent dial 0.3 — familiar enough to be comfortable, never strange enough to demand real attention. The algorithm does not want you to build new shelves. Building shelves risks the user closing the app. The algorithm wants you to *recognize*, not to *encounter*.

And so the playlist becomes a loop of almost-familiarity. You hear a song and think, "This sounds like something I would like," which is not the same as liking it. The recognition happens before the experience. The dial never reaches 1.0 because the algorithm has learned that 1.0 is dangerous. 1.0 is where people pause. 1.0 is where people think. 1.0 is where people might decide they have had enough.

But attention is not loyal. It is a predator, and predators follow the prey. When every playlist is dial 0.3, genuine novelty is elsewhere — at a basement show, in a YouTube video with twelve views, in a TikTok of a homemade PVC instrument so strange that your brain has no category at all. The algorithm tries to capture this with "Discover Weekly" and "Release Radar," but these are simulations of novelty. They are dial 0.35 — still within the same basin. The algorithm cannot recommend what it has never seen. And what it has never seen is where attention is migrating.

---

### The Winning Dial Position

If attention is a dial, the question is not "how do we conserve it?" It is "where do we set it?"

The platforms have set it to 0.3 because 0.3 scales. At dial 0.3, you can serve a billion users with the same pool of content. The engagement is steady, predictable, monetizable. It is the musical equivalent of fast food — not nourishing, but reliably profitable.

The artists who still command attention are operating at different dial positions. Some have gone fully hard: they release one album a decade, demand that you listen in order, refuse playlist licensing. They are forcing scarcity back into the system. Others have gone fully soft: constant streams of unpolished material, sketches, half-finished ideas. They are at dial 1.0, where every release is novel because none of it has been compiled yet.

The winning position is not a fixed point. It is a *trajectory* — the path from 1.0 to 0.0 that the artist controls, rather than the platform. Coltrane moved from the novel (*Giant Steps*) to the studied (*A Love Supreme*) to the ubiquitous to the invisible. That trajectory took thirty years. The platforms want to compress it to thirty days — from TikTok viral to Spotify playlist to elevator speaker to forgotten — because compression is where their profit lives. The artist's fight is to slow it down, to hold the dial at each position long enough for the listener to actually *arrive*.

A platform like Spotify is a room with its dial locked at 0.3. It admits only tiles — songs — that match that dial position. Too novel (dial > 0.5) and the recommendation engine filters it out. Too compiled (dial < 0.1) and users skip. The room becomes a self-reinforcing loop. Because the dial never changes — changing it would risk the business model — the room slowly fills with acoustic wallpaper.

The alternative is a room with a *moving* dial. This is what live performance is. The same song in a basement at midnight has a different dial position than on a commute playlist. The darkness, the proximity, the sweat, the impossibility of skipping — these harden the listening. They force compilation. They demand attention not because the song is new but because the *context* is new.

---

### The Unexplored Territory

If music has unexplored dial positions, attention has them too.

What does attention at dial -1.0 look like? Active *refusal* to attend. Not boredom, but *resistance*. A listener who knows every algorithmic trick and deliberately seeks the opposite. Someone who listens to a single frequency for an hour. Someone who attends only to mistakes — the feedback squeal, the dropped stick. Negative attention is still attention, but directed against the stimulus. It is the domain of noise music, of listening practices that treat the ear as a muscle to exhaust.

What does attention at dial 4.0 look like? Total immersion — the kind mystics report, where the boundary between listener and music dissolves. Not "focus," which is directed. Dial 4.0 is undirected, total, impossible to sustain. It is the goal of drone traditions, of ceremonial music, of practices that use repetition not to bore but to *dissolve*.

Most of our listening happens between 0.2 and 0.4. The platforms have colonized this band so thoroughly that we have forgotten the rest of the space exists. But attention, which is not conserved but is *mobile*, is already migrating there. You can feel it in the resurgence of vinyl — hardness through inconvenience. In ambient music — dial positions the algorithm cannot optimize for because the goal is not engagement but *release*. In the most talked-about musical experiences of the past decade: not albums, but *events* — unannounced drops, listening parties, forty-minute improvisations. Events have dial positions that playlists cannot replicate, because events have *context*, and context is the ultimate dial adjuster.

---

### Conclusion: The Dial Is the Listener

We started by saying attention might be a conservation law, and we end by saying it is a dial. A law is something that happens *to* you. A dial is something you set.

The platforms want you to believe attention is scarce and that they are its only efficient allocators. This is a lie. Attention is abundant. What is scarce is *novelty*, and novelty is scarce because the platforms, optimizing for the middle of the dial, have made it economically irrational to produce. Why spend years developing a sound at dial 1.0 when the algorithm will filter it out? Why push the boundary when the boundary is where metrics collapse?

Metrics are not music. Engagement is not attention. Time-on-platform is not listening. The dial that wins is the one that earns attention by offering the genuine article — the unfileable, the uncompilable, the song your brain has no shelf for and must build a new room to hold.

That room is yours to build. The dial is yours to set. And the music is still out there, at every position, waiting for you to arrive.
