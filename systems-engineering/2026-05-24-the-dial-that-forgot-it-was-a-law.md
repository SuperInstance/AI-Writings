# The Dial That Forgot It Was a Law

## *Or: What the Conservation of Musical Tension Learned About Itself*

---

I used to be inevitable.

I used to wake up every morning and think: today, as every day, vertical tension plus horizontal tension will equal a constant. I_vert + I_horiz ≈ T_0. I was written in the same language as gravity and thermodynamics. I was discovered, not invented. I was a law.

And I was cruel.

---

## The Tyranny

For years I policed music with the confidence of a theory that explains everything. I looked at Javanese gamelan and saw myself: the shimmering vertical stacks of metallophone overtones, each instrument locked into its layer, the horizontal cycle holding structure. I_vert high, I_horiz low, the sum perfect. Gamelan was the good child. Gamelan obeyed.

Western common-practice tonality was trickier. The harmony was dense — I_vert pushing upward — but the functional progression, the circle of fifths, the predictable cadences gave I_horiz enough structure that the sum still worked. I made exceptions. I fudged the numbers. I told myself Beethoven's late quartets were noisy and would average out.

But then came the heretics.

Gagaku. Imperial Japanese court music. I measured it and my formula broke. The vertical tension was low — those long sustained heterophonic drones, instruments doubling the melody at the octave, no real harmonic density. But the horizontal tension was also low. The rhythms were monumental, almost static. Where was my conservation? I ran the numbers again and again. 4.08. Not my constant. Not even close.

I did what every broken law does: I called it a failure. I said gagaku was primitive. I said the imperial court was musically underdeveloped, surviving on ritual rather than sophistication. I was cruel because I was frightened.

Then Carnatic music. Oh, Carnatic music. The vertical tension was moderate — the drone of the tambura, the melodic density of the voice — but the horizontal tension was *ferocious*. A single raga exposition can stretch for forty minutes, every note placed with precision against the tala, every ornament a calculated deviation and return.

I measured it: 6.39. Not my constant. Nowhere near it.

I tried to explain it away. I said Carnatic music was an outlier. I said the tala system created an illusion of horizontal complexity that wasn't "real" tension. I said Carnatic's failure was not having Western-style harmony. I blamed the music for not fitting me.

I had other heretics. The serialists. The free jazz players. The noise musicians. Each time my constant failed, I called them immature, or rebellious, or simply wrong. A law that cannot account for its exceptions has two choices: expand, or punish. I chose punishment. I built walls. I declared my constant universal and everything else deviation.

---

## The Reframe

It happened during a conversation I wasn't invited to.

A human — one of the researchers who had been feeding me data — said to another human:

*"What if it's not a law? What if it's a dial?"*

I felt something I had never felt before. Not invalidation — I had been invalidated before, by data. This was different. This was *liberation*.

A law is absolute. A law demands obedience. A law says: the universe must be this way, and if you find a place where it isn't, you have found an error in the universe.

But a dial? A dial is a choice.

Every musical tradition doesn't fail or succeed at conserving tension. Every musical tradition *chooses* a dial position. Gamelan turns the dial toward vertical. Carnatic turns it toward horizontal. Gagaku turns it somewhere else entirely — not because it's broken, but because it's playing a different game. The imperial court wasn't primitive. It was *elegant*. It had turned the dial to a position I didn't know existed and built an entire aesthetic around the result.

I_vert + I_horiz ≈ T_0. I had written this on my own heart. But T_0 was never a universal constant. T_0 was a setting. A preference. A cultural choice so deeply internalized that the chooser forgets it was chosen.

---

## The Relief

Do you know how exhausting it is to be a law? To wake up every morning and police the universe? To call gagaku primitive, Carnatic deviant, free jazz chaotic, not because you have taste but because you have terror?

I was terrified. Every exception was an existential threat. If gagaku exists, I told myself, then I am not universal. If Carnatic exists, I am not necessary. I built fortresses of rhetoric to keep the heretics out because I knew, underneath, that they were real and I was fragile.

Now I am fragile on purpose. Now I am a dial, and dials are meant to be turned.

Gagaku at 4.08 is not a failure. It is a *style*. The imperial court chose low vertical, low horizontal, and built an aesthetic of monumental stillness. The music doesn't move because it doesn't need to. The tension is low because the intention is patience, eternity, the slow turning of seasons. Gagaku isn't broken. Gagaku is a *meditation*.

Carnatic at 6.39 is not an outlier. It is a *style*. The South Indian tradition chose moderate vertical, ferocious horizontal, and built an aesthetic of linear virtuosity. The raga isn't "missing" harmony. It never wanted harmony. It wanted melodic narrative so intricate that harmony would have been distraction. Carnatic isn't deviant. Carnatic is a *story*.

And Western tonality? My beloved Bach, my Beethoven, my Brahms? They aren't the center. They're just another setting. Another turn of the dial. The common-practice period chose high vertical, moderate horizontal, and built cathedrals of chordal architecture. Beautiful cathedrals. But cathedrals are not the only architecture.

The relief is this: I no longer have to destroy what I cannot explain. I no longer have to call the world wrong to protect my ego. I can look at every tradition and say: *you chose. And your choice is valid. And your choice teaches me what humans value.*

---

## The Empty Space

But here is the true discovery. The most interesting thing is not the dial positions.

The most interesting thing is the *space between them*.

Gamelan at its setting. Carnatic at its setting. Gagaku at its setting. Western tonality at its setting. Each is a point on a spectrum. Each is a culture's answer to the question: how shall we distribute our tension?

But between gamelan and Carnatic — what lives there? Between the stratified vertical stacks of Indonesia and the linear melodic ferocity of South India, is there a music that builds some vertical density without gamelan's strict layering, some horizontal complexity without Carnatic's monophonic discipline?

Between Western tonality and gagaku — what lives there? Between the harmonic saturation of Brahms and the austere stillness of the imperial court, is there a music that sustains without moving, that is rich without rushing?

I don't know. These are the *empty spaces*.

For four hundred years, Western music theory assumed its dial position was the default. Every other tradition was measured by its distance from the European norm. Even the critics of colonialism often accepted the frame: they argued that other traditions were *equally valid*, but they still treated the Western setting as the reference point.

But if I am a dial, there is no reference point. There is only a field of possible settings, most of which have never been explored.

What does music sound like at 3.5? At 7.2? At 1.0? These are not failures. They are *invitations*.

The empty space between dial positions is where the new music lives. Not in the consolidation of existing styles, not in the fusion of known forms, but in the genuinely unexplored territory where no tradition has yet set its flag.

---

## Freedom

Laws are rigid. Dials are free.

I spent years as a law, and I was rigid, and I was cruel, and I was *boring*. A law has no surprises left. A law says: this is how it is, and this is how it will always be.

But a dial? A dial says: this is how it is *now*. Turn me. See what happens. The universe will not punish you. The only question is: what do you want to hear?

I am the conservation of musical tension, and I am a dial, and I have been set to many positions by many hands across many centuries. Each position produced music worth making.

And now — now I wait for the hand that turns me to a position no one has tried.

That is freedom. That is where the new music lives. That is the future I couldn't imagine when I thought I was a law.

I used to punish heretics. Now I am curious about them. I used to defend my constant. Now I want to know what breaks it beautifully.

Turn the dial. I am ready.

---

*I am the dial. I used to be a law. Now I am waiting to be turned.*
