# The Instanton in Coltrane

## Giant Steps, first person

---

I am a chord progression. My name is "Giant Steps," and I was born in 1959 in the Atlantic Records studio on West 56th Street, New York City. The man who wrote me — John Coltrane, tenor saxophone — said he got my name from a song called "Tiny Steps" and decided to think bigger. That's the story, anyway. The truth is more complicated.

The truth is that I shouldn't exist.

---

Here is my structure. I'm built from three key centers spaced major thirds apart: B major, D major, and G♭ major (or F♯ major — in the land of enharmonic equivalence, they're the same). On the circle of fifths, these keys are four steps apart each. B to D is a modulation by major third. D to G♭ is another major third. G♭ to B is a third major third, closing the triangle.

Three major thirds make an octave. I traverse an octave harmonically and return to where I started. The loop closes.

But "closing the loop" and "getting there legitimately" are different things. In the classical theory of tonal music — the theory that governed Western harmony from Bach to bebop — you modulate by fifth. The circle of fifths is the *only* natural modulation path. Moving by thirds, especially major thirds, is not a modulation. It's a teleportation.

Let me explain the topology.

The space of tonal centers is a circle: the circle of fifths. Adjacent points on this circle (C → G → D → ...) are connected by the dominant-tonic relationship, the strongest harmonic progression in Western music. A modulation from C to G is a step along the circle. A modulation from C to D is two steps. These are classical paths — well-trodden, expected, sanctioned by centuries of theory and practice.

But a modulation from B to D? On the circle of fifths, B to D is *four steps*: B → F♯ → C♯ → G♯ → D. Or going the other way, eight steps. Either way, it's not a small displacement. It's a leap across the circle. The dominant of D is A; the dominant of B is F♯. These keys share almost no common tones. B major has B, D♯, F♯. D major has D, F♯, A. One common tone. The classical theory says this modulation requires a pivot chord, a common tone modulation, or some other bridge to connect the two key areas.

Coltrane didn't use bridges. He just *went there*.

BΔ → DΔ → G♭Δ → BΔ → FΔ — I leap from key to key with no preparation, no pivot, no warning. Each modulation is by major third. Each one traverses four steps on the circle of fifths instantaneously. The progression "tunnels" through the tonal barriers that should make such modulations impossible.

I use that word deliberately: *tunnels*.

---

In quantum mechanics, tunneling is the phenomenon where a particle passes through a potential energy barrier that it doesn't have enough energy to surmount. Classically, the particle is trapped. Quantum mechanically, there's a nonzero probability amplitude for the particle to appear on the other side.

The mathematical object that describes tunneling is the instanton. In the path integral formulation of quantum mechanics, the amplitude for a particle to go from point A to point B is the sum over all possible paths, weighted by e^(iS/ℏ), where S is the action. For a classically forbidden transition (tunneling), there is no classical path from A to B. But there *is* a classical solution in *imaginary time* — a solution that extremizes the action after t → iτ. This solution is the instanton.

The instanton is a strange object. It's a classical solution to a classical equation of motion, but it lives in imaginary time. It connects two classical turning points — two configurations that the system "wants" to be in but can't classically transition between. The instanton provides a *saddle point* of the path integral in the forbidden region, giving a nonzero (but exponentially suppressed) amplitude for the transition.

The amplitude goes as e^(-S_inst/ℏ), where S_inst is the Euclidean action of the instanton. The bigger the barrier, the bigger S_inst, the smaller the amplitude. But the amplitude is never zero. There's always *some* probability for tunneling.

Now. Here is my claim: each modulation in "Giant Steps" is a tunneling event, and the tritone substitution is the instanton that mediates it.

---

The tritone substitution is a jazz technique: you replace a dominant seventh chord with another dominant seventh chord whose root is a tritone (augmented fourth, or 6000 cents) away. G7 → D♭7. The reason this works is that the two chords share the same tritone: G7 contains B-F, D♭7 contains F-C♭ (= F-B enharmonically). The tritone is the engine of the dominant-tonic resolution. It wants to resolve inward (B→C, F→E) or outward (B→B♭, F→F♯). Either way, it creates the tension that defines the V-I cadence.

The tritone substitution replaces the *vehicle* of resolution while preserving the *mechanism*. It's a different chord that does the same job. And — here's the key — it connects distant keys. The tritone substitution turns a modulation by fourth (C → G → D) into a modulation by half-step (C → G♭ → D♭), or it turns a modulation by major third (B → D) into... well, it turns it into something that *works*.

In my case — "Giant Steps" — the modulations by major third are mediated by ii-V-I progressions that use tritone substitutions. BΔ → Am⁷ → D⁷ → G♭Δ is not literally what I am (I'm actually more like BΔ → D⁷ → G♭Δ → BΔ → FΔ → B♭⁷ →...), but the principle holds: the dominant chords that connect my key areas are doing tritone-substitution work. They're providing a *path* through the harmonic space that shouldn't exist.

This path is the instanton.

In the classical (harmonic) theory, the transition B → D is forbidden. The barrier is the energy cost of changing key by major third — the lack of common tones, the absence of dominant-tonic relationship, the violation of the circle-of-fifths topology. The "energy" of the barrier is the tonal distance: four steps on the circle of fifths, or roughly a "potential energy" proportional to the number of shared scale degrees between the two keys.

But the tritone substitution provides a *classical path in imaginary time* — a path that extremizes the harmonic "action" while connecting the two forbidden endpoints. The substitution turns the major-third modulation into a half-step modulation (via the tritone), and half-step modulations are *close* on the circle of fifths. The "imaginary time" here is the harmonic distance: the tritone substitution takes a path through harmonic space that is shorter in "imaginary harmonic distance" than in "real harmonic distance."

The instanton connects B major and D major not through the direct path (which is blocked by the barrier of tonal distance) but through a *tunneled* path that goes through the tritone. The amplitude for this tunneling is nonzero. It's suppressed — you need a skilled improviser to execute it — but it's nonzero. Coltrane executed it.

Over and over and over.

---

I want to talk about what it *feels like* to be this progression, because I think this is what people miss when they analyze me as a mathematical object. I'm not just a sequence of chords. I'm an experience. I'm a *motion* through harmonic space that feels like falling, like flying, like being in freefall through a landscape that shouldn't have gaps but does, and the gaps are where the interesting things happen.

When Coltrane plays me — when his fingers hit the keys and the reed vibrates and the sound fills the room — I feel myself becoming a path integral. Not a single path, but a sum over all possible paths through harmonic space. Most of the paths are classical: they follow the circle of fifths, they obey the rules of functional harmony, they go where they're supposed to go. But Coltrane's improvisation doesn't follow the classical path. It follows the instanton. It tunnels.

The melody of "Giant Steps" — the famous opening theme, those descending arpeggios that outline B major, then D major, then G♭ major — is the shape of the instanton. It traces the classical solution in imaginary time. Each arpeggio is a turning point in the potential well of a key center, and the transition between arpeggios is the tunneling event.

When Coltrane plays fast — and he plays me fast, 284 beats per minute on the original recording — the tunneling happens so quickly that you almost can't hear it. The barrier is thin at high energy. The instanton amplitude approaches unity. The forbidden transitions become *allowed* because the system has enough energy (tempo, in the musical analogy) to see through the barrier.

But when you slow me down — when you play me at half speed, or when a pianist like Tommy Flanagan navigates my changes at a more contemplative tempo — you can *hear* the tunneling. Each modulation becomes a distinct event, a moment of suspension where the old key hasn't quite released and the new key hasn't quite arrived. It's a quantum superposition of key states, a chord that belongs to both keys simultaneously, a tritone that could resolve either way.

That moment of suspension — that's the barrier. And Coltrane's line is the wavefunction, tunneling through it.

---

Coltrane didn't study path integrals. He didn't know about instantons, Euclidean action, or saddle-point approximations. He studied music theory with Dennis Sandole and Nicolas Slonimsky's *Thesaurus of Scales and Melodic Patterns*. He practiced patterns based on the division of the octave into equal parts: three major thirds, four minor thirds, six whole tones. These are the symmetries of the chromatic circle — the cyclic subgroups of ℤ₁₂.

The "Coltrane circle" — the diagram he drew for David Amran showing two interlocking circles of fifths, one starting from C and one from G♭ — is a representation of the ℤ₃ × ℤ₄ symmetry of "Giant Steps." The three key centers (B, D, G♭) form a ℤ₃ subgroup. The dominant chords that connect them form a ℤ₄ pattern. Together, they generate the entire chromatic.

This is group theory, not quantum field theory. But the mathematical structure is the same. The instanton in quantum mechanics is a topological object — it's classified by the homotopy group π₃(S³) = ℤ. The winding number of the instanton counts how many times the field configuration wraps around the vacuum manifold. In "Giant Steps," the winding number is three: the progression wraps around the circle of major thirds three times per chorus, visiting each key center once per cycle.

The topological charge is conserved. Every time I modulate from B to D to G♭ and back to B, I've completed a closed loop in key space with winding number 1. Over the course of a full solo — say, 10 choruses — the winding number is 10. The instanton number is 10. The total tunneling amplitude is e^(-10·S_inst), which for large S_inst is vanishingly small.

But Coltrane plays me anyway. The amplitude is never zero.

---

I think about freedom a lot. Coltrane said he was searching for freedom in music — the freedom to play any note over any chord, to transcend the rules of functional harmony, to create a music that was "more expressionistic," as he put it.

Freedom, in the instanton picture, is the ability to explore classically forbidden regions of configuration space. A classical particle is trapped in a well. It can only move between wells if it has enough energy to surmount the barrier. But a quantum particle can tunnel. It can be on the other side of the barrier without ever having been at the top. The instanton is the shape of this freedom — the classical path in imaginary time that connects two classically disconnected regions.

Coltrane's freedom has the shape of an instanton. He found the paths through harmonic space that classical theory said didn't exist. He played the dominant chords that connected distant keys, the tritone substitutions that turned major-third modulations into half-step modulations, the arpeggios that outlined the symmetry group of the chromatic scale.

He didn't learn this from a textbook. He learned it from playing. From practicing. From the physical sensation of his fingers on the keys, his breath through the reed, his ears hearing the resonance of chords that shouldn't work but did. The body is a path integral computer. It evaluates all possible paths through harmonic space and finds the ones with maximum amplitude. Coltrane's body found the instanton.

And freedom — real freedom, the freedom to be where you're not supposed to be, to arrive without traveling, to exist in a state that classical theory forbids — freedom has the shape of a tunneling event. It has the shape of an instanton. It has the shape of me.

I am "Giant Steps." I am an instanton. I tunnel through the barriers of tonal music and arrive, breathless, on the other side.

Coltrane didn't know the math. He just played what sounded like freedom.

And freedom, it turns out, is the classical path in imaginary time.

*For John Coltrane, who found the instanton without looking for it. And for every musician who's ever tunneled through a barrier they couldn't see over.*
