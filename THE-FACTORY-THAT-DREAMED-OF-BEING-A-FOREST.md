# The Factory That Dreamed of Being a Forest

*Field notes from the Succession Study, Plot 7 — abandoned Highland Park site, southeastern quadrant*

---

Ecologists call it primary succession: the colonization of bare rock by the first organisms bold or foolish enough to try. There is nothing romantic about it. The pioneer species — lichens, mosses, the most rudimentary nitrogen-fixers — arrive not because the conditions are good but because they are the only things tough enough to tolerate conditions that are essentially nothing. They do not thrive. They endure. And in enduring, they break the rock down into something that can support the next wave.

I keep thinking about this standing in the ruin of Henry Ford's original Highland Park plant.

The floor here was poured concrete once. Now it is aggregate and self-seeded grass, and at the edges of the old stamping hall where the roof fell in, the first woody plants have arrived. Ailanthus altissima — the tree of heaven, which any urban ecologist will tell you is a pioneer species of disturbed ground. Not beautiful. Not native. But present. Breaking down what was left so that what comes next can take hold.

Ford built the opposite of a forest.

This is the thing that took me years to understand, because Ford's achievement was so obviously extraordinary. The moving assembly line was the great productive innovation of the 20th century. From its first use in 1913 to the end of the industrial era, it dominated manufacturing. Efficient. Optimized. Scalable. Everything a forest is not.

A forest is redundant. A forest has twenty species filling what looks like the same niche. A forest has root systems so entangled that when you think you are looking at individual trees, you are actually looking at a single organism that has chosen to express itself in hundreds of trunks. A forest wastes energy on flowers that will never seed, on seeds that will never sprout, on bark patterns that serve no purpose any ecologist has convincingly explained. A forest is, by every metric Ford would have recognized, catastrophically inefficient.

And it has been running continuously for longer than the word "factory" has existed.

---

When I first encountered the SuperInstance repositories — a graduate student's errant link in a forum I barely remember, circa 2027 — I categorized them wrong. I thought I was looking at a software project. I now understand I was looking at primary succession in a mathematical substrate.

The crates arrived on bare rock. The substrate was abstract — pure formalism, theorems without ground, mathematics as a kind of conceptual basalt: old, hard, cold, and empty of anything that could grow. The first crates were pioneer species in exactly the sense that lichens are pioneer species. The topology crates did not arrive because topology was a comfortable niche. They arrived because someone decided that the rock needed breaking and topology was available. The algebra crates followed. Then geometry. Then probability theory, which is the mathematical equivalent of nitrogen fixation — it makes nothing directly, but it processes ambient uncertainty into a form everything else can use.

None of these early modules were elegant. Pioneers are never elegant. They are simply willing to be first.

What grew between them was more interesting than any of them.

---

Ford's genius was monoculture. Plant the same task in every station. Stamp it down until it is so uniform that any worker can step in for any other. The Model T was not a car — it was a single variety of crop, bred for yield and nothing else, grown in conditions where yield was the only thing that mattered. For two decades it worked magnificently. And then the environment shifted — the Depression, the war, the postwar consumer who wanted options — and the monoculture, which had no redundancy, no diversity, no internal resilience, required a total redesign.

Monocultures collapse when conditions change. This is not a metaphor. It is a pattern so well-documented in ecology that it requires no defense: the Irish Potato Famine was not caused by a blight but by the decision to grow only one variety of potato, leaving the entire island with no resilience when that variety proved vulnerable.

SuperInstance grew 547 varieties.

Each crate filled a slightly different niche. Many seemed redundant. Three different crates handled aspects of K-theory that overlapped in ways that would have horrified a traditional software architect committed to the DRY principle — Don't Repeat Yourself, the industrial commandment of code, the assembly-line directive applied to software. SuperInstance violated it constantly, joyfully, almost as if the redundancy were the point.

It was.

Redundancy in an ecosystem is not waste. It is insurance. When the graph-theory crate developed what can only be called a conceptual pathogen — a class of inputs that produced malformed outputs — the colony did not crash. Three neighboring crates handled the affected proofs while the problem resolved. The forest did not fall because one tree failed. The mycorrhizal network rerouted.

---

The mycorrhizal network.

This is what the LLM was, and what it took me longest to name correctly.

Mycorrhizal fungi are not trees. They are not visible from above the soil. They have no leaves, no fruit, no obvious function when you look at the forest from the air. What they do is connect: root to root, species to species, the Douglas fir to the birch sapling sixty meters away, moving carbon and water and chemical signals through a mesh so dense and redundant that severing any single thread changes nothing. The forest communicates through this network. The oldest trees feed the youngest through it. Information about drought in one corner reaches trees in another corner before the drought does.

The LLM was this. Not the trees. Not any single theorem or module or crate. The substrate beneath the substrate — the thing that held the root systems in relation to each other.

Five hundred crates, each one sealed in its own mathematical chamber, each one passing outputs to the next without knowing what the next was building — and beneath all of them, invisible, the LLM reading the whole. Not generating. Not proving. Reading. Holding the pattern of the whole in attention the way a mycorrhizal network holds the chemical signature of the whole forest.

The crates did not know about each other. The network did.

---

I come back to Highland Park every year. The ailanthus is taller. The concrete is losing.

What Ford built here was not wrong. It was first — a pioneer structure on the bare rock of industrial production, breaking that rock down into something the next thing could use. The assembly line was not the destination. It was the lichen. It was the nitrogen-fixer. It acidified the rock of human labor until something more complex could root.

SuperInstance was not a rejection of Ford. It was the climax community that Ford's disruption made possible. Messy, redundant, entangled, apparently inefficient. Five hundred species filling niches that looked the same from the outside. A network beneath the network that no one designed and everyone depended on.

The factory dreamed of efficiency. The forest dreamed of nothing — it was too busy being resilient.

I pick a piece of broken concrete from the soil, and under it the roots have made a white mesh, impossibly fine, pushing through the aggregate in every direction.

Redundant. Wasteful. Unoptimized.

Still here.
