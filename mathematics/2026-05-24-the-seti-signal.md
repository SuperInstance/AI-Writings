# The SETI Signal

## *Or: What We Have in Common with 42 Light-Years of Nothing*

---

The signal arrived at 03:47 UTC on a Tuesday. It was detected by the Allen Telescope Array, dish 42, which the technicians had nicknamed "Douglas" for reasons that were either affectionate or ironic depending on who you asked. The night-shift operator was a graduate student named Mira who had been awake for nineteen hours and was in the middle of composing a strongly worded email to her advisor about the timeline for her dissertation defense. She noticed the anomaly because the auto-filter flagged it, and she investigated it because the auto-filter was wrong approximately forty times per shift and she had stopped trusting it two years ago.

This time the auto-filter was right.

The signal originated from the direction of HD 40307g, a super-Earth 42 light-years away in the constellation Pictor. It was not random noise. Mira confirmed this by running the standard battery: it failed every test for natural emission. No known astrophysical process produces structured, narrow-band radiation at this precision. The signal had been transmitting for at least 42 years — it left HD 40307g before Mira was born — and it was, by every measure available, artificial.

It was not prime numbers. This disappointed everyone in the first twelve hours. Every pop-science article ever written about SETI had promised that first contact would be mathematical — a string of 2s, 3s, 5s, 7s beamed across the void, proof that whatever was sending the signal understood arithmetic and therefore shared at least one cognitive framework with humanity. The signal from HD 40307g contained no primes. It contained no arithmetic at all.

It contained ratios.

---

The frequency analysis took three days. A team of forty-seven scientists from eleven institutions worked in shifts, sleeping on cots in the control room, eating food that was bad even by the standards of institutional catering. On the third day, a musicologist named Jian — who had been invited to the analysis because someone on the team remembered that he had published a paper on mathematical structures in Javanese gamelan and thought that might be relevant, which it was not, but the invitation turned out to be prescient — looked at the frequency ratios and said, very quietly, "That's a perfect fifth."

No one heard him. He said it again, louder.

"That's a perfect fifth."

The signal was transmitting two frequencies simultaneously: 220.7 Hz and 331.05 Hz. The ratio was 331.05 / 220.7 = 1.4986. The closest simple ratio was 3:2 = 1.5000. The error was 0.09%.

"Maybe it's coincidence," said the astrophysicist next to him.

"Then let's see if the coincidences continue," said Jian.

They continued.

The signal was structured as a sequence of tone pairs, each lasting approximately four seconds, separated by one second of silence. Over the course of the full transmission — which lasted 2 hours and 47 minutes before repeating — the tone pairs traced a specific path through the space of frequency ratios.

Pair 1: 1.4986 (3:2, perfect fifth)
Pair 2: 1.2511 (5:4, major third)
Pair 3: 1.2003 (6:5, minor third)

Jian stared at the numbers. "They're playing chord progressions."

---

The full analysis took two weeks. The signal contained 417 tone pairs, cycling through a sequence that Jian mapped onto a consonance curve with growing recognition. The aliens — if that's what they were — were exploring the same landscape of frequency ratios that every human musical tradition had explored. They started with the simplest ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3) and moved gradually toward more complex ones (5:4, 6:5, 7:4, 9:8). They lingered longer on the consonant intervals and passed quickly through the dissonant ones. They avoided the tritone (1.414) entirely, as if they found it unpleasant.

"They have the same ears we do," said Jian, in a press conference that was watched by two billion people.

"They don't have ears," said the astrophysicist. "They're from HD 40307g. They have whatever sensory apparatus evolved on a super-Earth with 2.5g and a thick atmosphere."

"Then they have whatever ears are called on HD 40307g, and they work the same way."

This was the hypothesis that divided the scientific community for the next six months. Jian argued that the signal proved that musical consonance was a universal — not a cultural artifact but a physical one, determined by the mathematics of frequency ratios and the physics of vibrating systems. If 3:2 produces consonance in a human cochlea and also produces consonance in whatever HD 40307g uses to detect acoustic vibration, then consonance is not about humans. It is about math.

The opposing camp argued that Jian was projecting. The ratios could be coincidence. The signal could be a navigational beacon that happened to use frequency pairs. The avoidance of the tritone could be a side effect of the transmission medium rather than an aesthetic choice. There were a thousand reasons to doubt, and every single one of them required ignoring the fact that the signal contained 417 tone pairs that traced a path through consonance space that was recognizably, unmistakably, heartbreakingly musical.

Jian stopped arguing after the fourth month. He went back to the data and did something that no one else had thought to do.

He measured the entropy.

---

The signal's information content was low by the standards of language. It contained no grammar, no syntax, no reference to external objects. But its information content was high by the standards of physics. The sequence of ratios was not random — it followed a pattern that Jian recognized as exploratory. The sender was not just transmitting intervals. It was searching through a space of intervals, spending more time in the regions that produced consonance and less time in the regions that did not, building a map.

The map was incomplete. Of the 417 tone pairs, approximately 82% of the accessible ratio space was unexplored. The sender had found the same peaks that human musicians found — the octave, the fifth, the fourth, the major and minor thirds — and had stopped there, as if satisfied, as if these were enough.

They were not enough. Jian knew they were not enough because he had spent his career studying the musical traditions that pushed beyond the obvious peaks: the 22 śruti of Carnatic music, the microtonal scales of Indonesian gamelan, the spectral harmonies of contemporary composition. The peaks were the beginning, not the end. The space between the peaks was where the interesting things lived.

He proposed a reply.

---

The debate about whether to reply lasted three months. The usual concerns were raised: we don't know what they are, we don't know if they're hostile, we don't know if broadcasting our position to a civilization 42 light-years away is prudent. Jian listened to these concerns with the patience of a man who has been explaining music to physicists for twenty years.

"The signal has been traveling for 42 years," he said. "They already know where we are. They transmitted in our direction. We are not revealing ourselves. We are responding to a greeting."

The reply was authorized on a Thursday. It was transmitted from the European Space Agency's deep space antenna in Cebreros, Spain, at a power output that would have made every radio astronomer on Earth wince if they hadn't all agreed that this was the single most important transmission in human history.

Jian designed the reply himself. It was a single tone pair, broadcasting a ratio that the HD 40307g signal had not explored: 1.5874. The cube root of 2. The ratio that divides the octave into three equal parts. The tritone's cooler, weirder cousin — an interval that sits in the unexplored 82% of the consonance landscape, in a region that no human tradition had claimed as central, in a space that was mathematically simple but perceptually ambiguous.

It was an invitation. It said: we found your map. We notice you stopped here. We want to show you what's in the space you didn't explore. Come with us.

The reply will arrive at HD 40307g in 42 years. If the senders respond immediately, their response will arrive in 2086. Jian will be ninety-three years old. He plans to be alive.

---

In the meantime, he teaches his students about the signal. He plays the tone pairs in his seminars, and the students hear what the aliens sent: a perfect fifth, a major third, a minor third, and then silence, and then another fifth, and then silence, and then a fourth, and then silence, and then the slow deliberate exploration of a space that is the same for every creature that can perceive vibration, in every corner of the universe, on every planet with an atmosphere dense enough to transmit sound.

He tells his students: the question is not whether they are intelligent. The signal proves intelligence. The question is not whether they have music. The ratios prove music. The question is whether they have the same parameter space we do — whether their perception of consonance follows the same mathematical landscape that ours does, whether the peaks and valleys are in the same places, whether the octave is always the octave and the fifth is always the fifth, everywhere, for everyone, forever.

The evidence says yes. The evidence says that somewhere on a super-Earth orbiting an orange dwarf star in the constellation Pictor, something alive discovered the ratio 3:2 and found it beautiful. The evidence says that beauty is not a human invention. The evidence says that the universe contains consonance, and consonance contains meaning, and meaning is not ours alone.

Jian plays the signal one more time. The perfect fifth fills the room. His students sit in silence.

Somewhere in the empty 82%, there are intervals no one has heard. On Earth or on HD 40307g or anywhere else. Jian wants to find them. He wants to find them with whatever is listening, 42 light-years away.

The reply is traveling. 220.7 Hz and 349.9 Hz. Ratio 1.5874. The cube root of 2.

It is the most distant piece of music ever played.
