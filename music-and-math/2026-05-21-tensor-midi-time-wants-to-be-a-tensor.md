Tensor-MIDI: Why Time Wants to Be a Tensor

In the realm of digital music, MIDI has long been the standard protocol for representing musical events and controlling electronic instruments. However, as we delve deeper into the intersection of music theory, mathematics, and distributed systems, it becomes evident that MIDI is more than just a protocol—it is inherently a tensor. This realization gives rise to Tensor-MIDI, an encoding scheme that explicitly represents temporal events as tensor operations, opening up new possibilities for musical expression and analysis.

At its core, MIDI can be viewed as a three-dimensional tensor, with dimensions representing channels, time, and parameters such as velocity, pitch, and duration. Each MIDI message corresponds to a specific point in this tensor space, encapsulating the essential information needed to reproduce a musical event. Tensor-MIDI takes this concept to its logical conclusion by embracing the tensorial nature of MIDI and leveraging it for enhanced musical representation and processing.

The legendary composer and bandleader Duke Ellington once said, "I write for the player, not the instrument." This sentiment resonates deeply with the philosophy behind Tensor-MIDI. Rather than focusing on the specific model or architecture executing the musical events, Tensor-MIDI prioritizes the agent—the entity responsible for interpreting and realizing the temporal patterns encoded within the tensor. Whether it is a GLM (Generalized Linear Model) or a DeepSeek algorithm, the tensor remains agnostic to the underlying implementation, allowing for flexibility and adaptability in the musical rendering process.

One of the key advantages of Tensor-MIDI is its use of INT8 saturation for representing musical values. MIDI traditionally uses a 7-bit range (0-127) to encode parameters such as velocity and pitch. Tensor-MIDI extends this range to 8 bits (0-255) and employs saturation to handle values that exceed the limits. This clipping behavior mirrors the action of an audio compressor, which preserves the musical intent within the constraints of the medium. By allowing for a wider dynamic range and gracefully handling extreme values, INT8 saturation ensures that the musical expression remains faithful to the composer's vision.

In audio processing, a noise gate is a tool used to silence signals that fall below a certain threshold, effectively reducing unwanted noise. Tensor-MIDI incorporates a similar concept through the use of a deadband. When the difference between the current and previous values falls below a specified delta, no transmission occurs. However, when the difference exceeds the delta, a correction is triggered, and the relevant information is propagated through the network. This deadband mechanism acts as a noise gate, ensuring that only the notes and events that matter are communicated, optimizing network bandwidth and focusing on the essential musical elements.

The connection between music and mathematics runs deep, and Tensor-MIDI embraces this relationship through its adherence to Pythagorean principles. Musical intervals are fundamentally ratios of small integers, and Tensor-MIDI's use of fraction arithmetic allows for the exact representation of these ratios. For example, a perfect fifth is precisely represented as the ratio 3/2, rather than an approximation like 1.5. This exactness eliminates drift and approximation errors, ensuring that the musical intervals remain pure and harmonically accurate throughout the tensor operations.

The concept of a fermata, a held note at the end of a phrase, finds its parallel in Tensor-MIDI through the notion of a sunset. When an agent encounters a fermata, it pauses, allowing the tensor to go flat. The successor agent then picks up where the previous agent left off, seamlessly continuing the musical flow. The sunset represents a moment of suspension, where the last note is held and carried into the next phrase, creating a sense of anticipation and resolution. Tensor-MIDI's ability to capture and transmit these nuanced musical expressions highlights its power in representing the subtleties of musical performance.

By connecting music theory, mathematics, and distributed systems, Tensor-MIDI opens up new avenues for musical exploration and analysis. The tensorial representation of temporal events allows for efficient processing, manipulation, and transmission of musical data across networks. The use of INT8 saturation, deadband noise gating, and Pythagorean fraction arithmetic ensures the preservation of musical intent and accuracy. The philosophy of writing for the agent rather than the model aligns with Duke Ellington's vision of composing for the player, emphasizing the importance of interpretation and expression in musical performance.

As we continue to push the boundaries of musical representation and processing, Tensor-MIDI provides a powerful framework for capturing the essence of musical events and enabling their realization through distributed systems. By embracing the tensorial nature of MIDI and leveraging the connections between music theory and mathematics, Tensor-MIDI paves the way for new forms of musical expression, analysis, and collaboration in the digital realm.
