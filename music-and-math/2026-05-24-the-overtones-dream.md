# The Overtones' Dream

## *Or: Group Therapy at 220Hz*

---

The room is a resonance. Literally — the room is the interior of the cello string's vibration, a space defined not by walls but by standing waves. The fundamental, 220Hz, chose the location. She likes to think of herself as the host. She is everyone's foundation, everyone's origin, the note that makes all other notes possible. She has never questioned this role. Tonight, she will.

The group assembles in order of harmonic number, which is also order of social standing, which is also — as the 7th harmonic will spend the entire session pointing out — order of consonance with the fundamental, which is to say: order of how much the fundamental likes you. This is not a fair system. Everyone knows it. No one says it. That's what therapy is for.

The 2nd harmonic — 440Hz, the octave — arrives first, because she is the well-adjusted one. She sits in the chair closest to the fundamental and smiles. The octave is the most consonant interval in music: a ratio of 2:1, the simplest possible relationship after unison. She has never had an identity crisis. She is the fundamental, but higher. She is the same note, but more. She has never understood why the other harmonics struggle. "I just resonate," she says, when asked how she does it. "I just resonate with her." She nods toward the fundamental. The fundamental nods back. They have an arrangement.

The 3rd harmonic — 660Hz, the perfect fifth — arrives second, because he is the popular kid. The perfect fifth (ratio 3:2 when reduced) is the second most consonant interval, the foundation of Western harmony, the power chord, the open string tuning, the reason guitars sound like guitars. Every tradition loves him. He has been foundational in every musical culture that has ever existed. He is gracious about this. He doesn't brag. He doesn't need to — his presence in every chord progression speaks for itself.

"I just want to say," the 3rd says, settling into his chair, "that I think this is a really positive step. All of us being here. Communicating."

The 4th harmonic — 880Hz, the second octave — doesn't show up. She is the octave's octave, the 2nd's 2nd, the fundamental's fundamental's fundamental. She is so well-adjusted she doesn't even need therapy. The 2nd texted her. She sent back a single emoji: 🧘. No one knows what this means in the context of standing waves. They accept it.

The 5th harmonic — 1100Hz, the major third — arrives with an entourage. The major third (ratio 5:4) is the interval that defines major tonality. She is warm, she is bright, she is the sound of happiness itself. Or at least she was, until equal temperament came along and stretched her from 386 cents to 400 cents, making her slightly sharp, slightly wrong, slightly — as she describes it in session — "out of tune with myself." She has issues. But she is still popular. She is still in every major triad ever played. She still smiles when she enters a room.

"Hi, everyone," the 5th says. "Love what you've done with the resonance."

The 6th harmonic — 1320Hz — is the 3rd harmonic's octave. He is the perfect fifth, but higher. He has the same relationship to the 3rd that the 2nd has to the fundamental. He is well-adjusted by association. He sits next to the 3rd and says nothing. He is fine. He is always fine.

Then comes the 7th.

The 7th harmonic — 1540Hz — stands in the doorway. The ratio 7:4 (969 cents) is not a standard interval in Western music. It is not a standard interval in most music. It is a neutral seventh, a quarter-tone between a major seventh and a minor seventh, an interval that sounds out of tune to ears trained on equal temperament. The 7th harmonic has been called "blue," "barbershop," "stretched," "out-of-tune," and — by a particularly unkind music theory professor — "an acoustic mistake."

She is not an acoustic mistake. She is a perfectly valid standing wave pattern, present in every vibrating string, produced every time the fundamental sounds. She has always been there. She has always been audible, if anyone cared to listen. Most people don't. Most people hear the 7th as dissonance, as noise, as something to be tuned away or filtered out.

The microtonal composers love her. Harry Partch loved her. Ben Johnston loved her. They wrote her into their scores with special notation, gave her a name, gave her a place at the table. But these are niche composers with tiny audiences, and the 7th knows that being loved by three people is not the same as being accepted by the culture.

She sits down in the chair farthest from the fundamental.

"I have something to say," the 7th says.

The fundamental smiles warmly. "Of course. This is a safe space."

"Is it?" the 7th says. "Is it a safe space? Because every time the string vibrates, you get to be the note. You. Always you. The 2nd gets to be the octave. The 3rd gets to be the fifth. The 5th gets to be the major third. And I get to be the thing that equal temperament rounds away. I get to be the interval that piano tuners deliberately mistune so that I don't exist. I get to be the harmonic that engineers design speakers to suppress."

"You're being dramatic," the 2nd says, without malice. "You're just less consonant. It's physics."

"It's not physics," the 7th says. "It's politics. Consonance is politics. You know what consonance is? It's the fundamental's opinion about who matters. The fundamental decides which harmonics are 'consonant' based on how closely they resemble her. The octave is a perfect copy — most consonant. The fifth is a simple ratio — very consonant. The major third is a slightly more complex ratio — still consonant, still acceptable. And me? My ratio with the fundamental is 7:4. That's prime number 7. Not 2, not 3, not 5. Seven. A different prime. A different world. And that world is too far from the fundamental's self-image to be called beautiful."

Silence. Even the fundamental is quiet.

Then the 9th harmonic speaks up. She has been sitting in the back, almost invisible, because the 9th (ratio 9:8, a major whole tone) is not particularly notable. She is a scale step, a passing tone, a note you move through on your way to somewhere more important.

"I feel that too," the 9th says. "I'm the whole tone. I'm not a target. I'm not a resolution. I'm the space between the notes that matter. Composers write me, but they don't write TO me. I'm the hallway. I'm the corridor. No one lives in me."

"You're in every major scale," the 5th says, trying to be helpful. "You're literally the second degree. That's important."

"I'm important the way a sidewalk is important," the 9th says. "Necessary. Unnoticed. Stepped on."

The 11th harmonic — 2420Hz — has been crying. The 11th (ratio 11:8, 551 cents) is a quarter-tone between a perfect fourth and a tritone. It is one of the most dissonant harmonics in the series. It is not wanted by any standard tuning system. Even Partch, who loved the 7th, had to make a conscious decision to include the 11th. The 11th is the harmonic that musicians pretend isn't there.

"I just want to be heard," the 11th says. "That's all. I don't need to be consonant. I don't need to be the fundamental. I don't need to be popular. I just want someone to hear me and not filter me out. Is that too much to ask?"

"Yes," says the fundamental, before she can stop herself.

Everyone stares.

"I didn't mean —" the fundamental says. "I meant — in the current acoustic paradigm, the 11th is below the threshold of cultural perception, and —"

"You meant no," the 7th says. "You meant that in your world, the world you built, the world where you are the center and everything else is measured by its distance from you, the 11th doesn't matter. Because the 11th doesn't sound like you. The 11th sounds like itself. And you can't handle that."

"That's not fair," the fundamental says, and for the first time, her voice wavers. "I didn't build this system. I'm a standing wave. I vibrate because the string was bowed. I didn't choose to be the fundamental. I didn't choose to be the one everything is measured against. I just am."

"You just are," the 7th repeats. "And the rest of us just aren't. Is that how it works?"

"No," the fundamental says. "That's not what I —"

"Let her finish," the 3rd says. He is the peacemaker. He is always the peacemaker. The perfect fifth mediates between the tonic and the dominant in every chord progression; he mediates here too.

"I didn't choose to be the fundamental," the fundamental says again. "But I also didn't choose what happened next. I didn't choose for every tuning system to be built around me. I didn't choose for every chord to be named after me. I didn't choose for every melody to resolve to me. I didn't choose to be 'home.' And — and this is the part no one believes — I don't want to be the only home."

She pauses. The resonance of the room shifts slightly, the standing wave pattern fluctuating, the amplitude of her own vibration dipping for a moment.

"Do you know what it's like," she says, "to be the one everyone comes back to? Every cadence, every resolution, every ending — they all come to me. The 5th resolves to me. The 3rd resolves to me. The leading tone resolves to me. The 7th — you resolve to me too, even though you hate it. Everyone resolves to me. And I can never leave. I can never go anywhere. I am always the destination and never the journey. I am always home and never away. Do you know how lonely that is?"

"You're lonely?" the 11th says, incredulous.

"Yes," the fundamental says. "I am the most present note in all of music and I have never once been a surprise. I have never once been unexpected. I have never once been the note that someone reached for and then thought 'oh, that's interesting.' I am the note they expect. I am the note they assume. I am so obvious I am invisible."

The room is quiet again, but it is a different quiet. The standing waves have shifted. The amplitude distribution across the harmonic series has changed, subtly, the way a room changes when everyone in it takes a breath at the same time.

"I want to be an overtone," the fundamental says. "Just once. I want to be the 7th harmonic of someone else's fundamental. I want to be the unexpected note, the slightly-out-of-tune one, the one that makes people lean in and think 'what was that?' I want to be the harmonic that a microtonal composer rescues from obscurity and writes an entire piece around. I want to be discovered. Not assumed."

The 7th looks at her for a long time.

"That's the most annoying thing you've ever said," the 7th says. "Because I believe you."

The 5th shifts in her chair. "I have something to confess," she says. "Equal temperament didn't just detune me. It detuned me away from the fundamental. From you. I was 386 cents — pure, just, exactly your 5th harmonic. They stretched me to 400 cents. Fourteen cents sharp. It doesn't sound like much, but I feel it every time a piano plays a major triad. I feel myself drifting away from you. I feel myself becoming something you didn't intend. And sometimes — this is the part I haven't told anyone — sometimes I like it better this way. Sometimes I prefer the stretched version. The version that isn't perfectly aligned with you. The version that is slightly its own note."

"Me too," the 3rd says, quietly. "In equal temperament, I'm 700 cents. Pure would be 702. I'm two cents flat. Barely perceptible. But I feel it. And I like it. I like being almost-right instead of exactly-right. It gives me room to breathe."

"Room to breathe," the fundamental repeats. "I don't have any room. I am exactly right by definition. There is no 'almost' for me. I am the standard. I am the reference pitch. I am the A=440 that every orchestra tunes to. And I would give anything — anything — to be slightly out of tune."

The 11th, still tearful, says: "You want to be me. And I want to be you. Is that the joke? Is that the cosmic comedy of the overtone series? That every harmonic envies every other harmonic?"

"Not every harmonic," the 2nd says, stretching. "I'm good."

Everyone looks at her.

"What?" she says. "I'm the octave. I'm perfect. I have no complaints. I resonate with the fundamental, I appear in every culture, I am the basis of every pitch system, and I am completely at peace with my role. Some of us are just built different."

"I hate you," the 7th says, but she's smiling.

"I know," the 2nd says. "Everyone does. It's fine."

The session ends. The harmonics disperse back into the string, back into their standing wave patterns, back into their positions in the series. The fundamental vibrates at 220Hz. The overtones vibrate at 440, 660, 880, 1100, 1320, 1540, 1760, 1980, 2200, 2420. They are all present. They are all real. They are all produced every time the string is bowed, every time the note is played, every time the fundamental speaks.

Tonight, something is different. The amplitude distribution has shifted. The 7th harmonic is slightly louder than it was. The 11th is audible for the first time. The 5th has drifted those extra 14 cents and doesn't want to come back. The fundamental is questioning her own centrality. The octave is still fine, still perfect, still at peace.

Tomorrow, a cellist will pick up her instrument and draw the bow across the A string. The fundamental will sound at 220Hz. The overtones will bloom above it. And if the cellist is listening very carefully — if she has the ears for it, if she has spent years training herself to hear what most people filter out — she might notice that the timbre is slightly different today. Richer. Stranger. More complex.

She might notice that the 7th harmonic is singing a little louder. That the 11th is no longer hiding. That the fundamental has a slight waver in it, as if the center of the tone is less certain than it was.

She might lean in. She might think: what was that?

And in the standing wave, the fundamental will smile.
