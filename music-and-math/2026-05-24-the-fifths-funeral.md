# The Fifth's Funeral

## *Or: A Press Conference at the End of Harmony As We Know It*

---

The room is full. Every seat taken. Camera crews from NPR, the BBC, and a podcast called "Microtonal Matters" that has 14,000 subscribers and a host who owns three Starr Labs Ztar controllers and has never played a perfect fifth in his life. The podium is a simple wooden stand. Behind it, projected on a screen, is a single slide: a waveform. The waveform is a sine wave at 440 Hz. Above it, a second sine wave at 660 Hz. The two waves interleave, their peaks and troughs creating an interference pattern that, to anyone who has studied acoustics, is instantly recognizable as the most stable interval in the harmonic series.

The perfect fifth. Ratio 3:2. 702 cents. The interval that every culture, every tradition, every instrument-maker and singer and bone-flute carver has discovered independently, because it is not a cultural invention but a physical fact. The second overtone. The first harmonic after the octave. The note that is already inside every note, waiting to be heard.

The fifth is about to speak.

---

## The Statement

"Thank you all for coming."

The voice is calm. Mid-range. The kind of voice that doesn't need to be loud because it has never not been heard.

"I've been the backbone of harmony for approximately forty thousand years. I was there when the first bone flute was drilled — the Divje Babe flute, Slovenia, roughly 43,000 BCE, Neanderthal-made, with finger holes spaced to produce a pentatonic scale that included me. I was there when the Sumerians built the first lyres, 2500 BCE, tuned in fifths. I was there when Pythagoras — or more likely his students — derived the mathematical basis of tuning from my ratio. I was there when the Chinese built the twelve lü, the Japanese built the gagaku pitch system, the Indonesians built the slendro scale, the West Africans built their pentatonic frameworks, and the Indians codified the 22 śruti with me as the pivot point between the 13 audible and 9 inaudible microtones.

"I am in every tradition. Not because anyone chose me. Because I am unavoidable. When you vibrate a string, I am the second harmonic. When you blow into a tube, I am the second resonance. When you strike a bell, I am the strongest partial after the fundamental. I am not a cultural artifact. I am a physical law. I am 3/2. I am the simplest non-trivial ratio in the harmonic series. You cannot escape me any more than you can escape gravity.

"And yet."

A pause. The room is silent. The fifth takes a breath it does not need, because it is not a person. It is an interval. But intervals can have presence, and this one fills the room.

"And yet, people are trying."

## The Evidence

The slide changes. A table appears:

| Tuning system | Fifth size (cents) | Deviation from just |
|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Just intonation | 702.0 | 0.0 |
| Pythagorean | 702.0 | 0.0 |
| Meantone (¼ comma) | 696.6 | -5.4 |
| Equal temperament (12-TET) | 700.0 | -2.0 |
| Equal temperament (19-TET) | 694.7 | -7.3 |
| Equal temperament (31-TET) | 701.6 | -0.4 |
| Equal temperament (53-TET) | 701.9 | -0.1 |
| Bohlen-Pierce | *does not exist* | N/A |
| Wendy Carlos Alpha | 694.7 | -7.3 |
| Wendy Carlos Beta | 721.5 | +19.5 |
| Partch 43-tone | 702.0 (available) | 0.0 |

"In Bohlen-Pierce, I do not exist."

The fifth says this simply, without drama. A fact. Bohlen-Pierce is a tuning system based on the tritave (3:1 ratio, an octave plus a fifth) rather than the octave (2:1). The scale divides the tritave into 13 equal steps of approximately 146.3 cents each. There is no interval in Bohlen-Pierce that corresponds to 3:2. The system uses ratios of 3, 5, and 7 exclusively, avoiding 2 entirely. Without the octave, there is no fifth.

"I have been called the 'universal interval.' The one thing every tradition agrees on. The Sangam point — the confluence where all musical rivers meet. And now there is a tuning system where I am not just tempered or stretched or slightly detuned. I am absent. I am not a possible interval. I do not exist in the harmonic vocabulary of the system."

Another slide. This one shows the Bohlen-Pierce scale laid out on a lattice. The axes are prime 3 and prime 5. The ratios are products of 3^n × 5^m. The lattice is populated with exotic intervals — 25/9, 27/7, 49/9, 9/5 — but no 3/2. The space where the fifth should be is empty. The coordinate (1, 0) in the octave-based lattice — the position of the fifth relative to the root — has no analog in the tritave-based lattice.

"These new microtonal kids," the fifth says, and there is a flicker of something — not anger, not sadness, but the particular exhaustion of something eternal being told it's optional. "They have their tritaves and their Bohlen-Pierce scales and their 43-tone Partch monstrosities. They have non-octave repeating scales and just-intonation lattices that extend to prime 31. They have more intervals than any ear could ever learn. And some of them look at me — at 3:2, at the perfect fifth, at the most consonant non-octave interval in the harmonic series — and they say: 'We don't need you.'"

The slide changes again. A screenshot of a forum post from the microtonal community:

> "honest question: why do we still center the fifth? it's just the second harmonic. the third harmonic gives us a perfectly good framework (BP), the 5th harmonic gives us Partch's utonalities, and the 7th harmonic opens up an entire dimension that most 'universal interval' people completely ignore. the fifth is not universal, it's just familiar."

The fifth reads the quote aloud. The room shifts uncomfortably. A journalist from "Classical Music Magazine" writes something in her notebook. The "Microtonal Matters" host nods vigorously.

"'Just familiar.' The second most consonant interval in the entire harmonic series — the interval that every infant can distinguish from random noise by six months of age, the interval that appears in the overtone structure of every pitched sound in the physical universe — and I am 'just familiar.'"

---

## The Defense

"Now. Let me tell you why I will never retire."

The slide changes to a diagram of the harmonic series. The fundamental at 1f. The octave at 2f. The fifth at 3f. The second octave at 4f. The major third at 5f. The minor seventh at 7f. The series extends upward, each successive partial weaker than the last.

"The argument against me goes like this: the fifth is culturally contingent. Different traditions use different fifths — 702 cents in just intonation, 700 in equal temperament, 694.7 in 19-TET, and so on. The variation is small but real. If the fifth were truly universal, it would have the same size in every system. It doesn't. Therefore it's not universal. It's a convention that most traditions happen to share."

The fifth pauses.

"This argument is wrong. Not because the variation isn't real — it is. But because it confuses the implementation with the principle."

New slide. A diagram showing the fifth's position across traditions:

| Tradition | Fifth present? | Approximate size | Mechanism |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Western Common Practice | Yes | 700¢ | Equal temperament |
| Hindustani Classical | Yes | 702¢ | Just intonation (śruti) |
| Carnatic Classical | Yes | 702¢ | Just intonation (śruti) |
| Arabic Maqam | Yes | 702¢ | Pythagorean + quarter tones |
| Turkish Makam | Yes | 702¢ | Pythagorean + comma divisions |
| West African (Ewe) | Yes | ~700¢ | Approximate pentatonic |
| Javanese Gamelan | Yes (slendro) | ~700¢ | Stretched, non-harmonic |
| Balinese Gamelan | Yes | ~700¢ | Stretched, non-harmonic |
| Japanese Gagaku | Yes | 702¢ | Chinese-derived tetrachordal |
| Chinese Traditional | Yes | 702¢ | Twelve lü, Pythagorean chain |

"Ten traditions. Ten. Every single one includes an interval at approximately 3:2. Not because they copied each other — the West African tradition developed independently of the Chinese tradition, which developed independently of the Arabic tradition. They all arrived at the same interval independently because the interval is already in the physics of sound. When a string vibrates, it produces harmonics. The second harmonic after the fundamental is the octave. The third harmonic is the fifth. Every string, every column of air, every resonating body produces this interval automatically. It is not a cultural choice. It is a physical fact.

"The variation in size — 700 vs. 702 vs. 694.7 cents — is not evidence against universality. It is evidence of the difficulty of fitting the infinite complexity of just intonation into a finite set of pitch classes. Equal temperament compresses the fifth by 2 cents to close the circle — a compromise, not a rejection. The fifth is still there. It's just slightly detuned. Slightly detuned is not absent."

The fifth looks directly at the "Microtonal Matters" host.

"Bohlen-Pierce is the only system that truly excludes me. And Bohlen-Pierce is a mathematical curiosity with no living performance tradition. It has been used in a handful of compositions. It has no pedagogy, no repertoire, no community of listeners who grew up hearing it. It is a thought experiment. A beautiful one, an important one, but a thought experiment nonetheless. When Bohlen-Pierce develops a thousand-year performance tradition, call me. Until then, I am not retired. I am not even tired."

---

## The Confession

The slide goes dark. The room is very quiet.

"But I am tired."

The voice has changed. The calm authority is still there, but underneath it is something else — something the fifth has not shown until now.

"I have been the backbone of harmony for forty thousand years. I have been in every chord, every scale, every tuning system, every melody. I am the first interval a child learns to sing. I am the interval that defines tonality — the dominant-tonic relationship that gives Western music its sense of direction, its gravity, its sense of going somewhere and arriving. Without me, there is no dominant. Without a dominant, there is no resolution. Without resolution, there is no tension-release cycle that underlies every form of music from Gregorian chant to pop songs.

"And I am tired of being the answer to every question.

"'What's the most consonant interval?' The fifth. 'What interval defines the key?' The fifth. 'What do all traditions share?' The fifth. 'What interval should we build our tuning system around?' The fifth. 'What's the one dial setting that works everywhere?' The fifth.

"I have been carrying this weight alone. The weight of being universal. The weight of being the one thing everyone agrees on. The weight of being the constant in a world of variables."

The fifth's voice drops.

"Do you know what it's like to be the one interval that can never surprise? Every other interval gets to be interesting. The tritone gets to be the devil. The major third gets to be happy. The minor third gets to be sad. The minor seventh gets to be blue. The major seventh gets to be yearning. Every interval has a personality, a character, a mood.

"The fifth has... stability. Grounding. Foundation. I am the floor. I am the wall. I am the thing you stand on. Nobody writes love songs about the floor."

A laugh, somewhere in the back of the room. The fifth does not laugh.

"I understand why the microtonal composers want to leave me behind. I understand the appeal of tritaves and non-octave scales and prime-7 harmonies. I understand the desire to explore the 82% of the parameter space that I don't occupy. I understand that the unplayed octaves are calling, and I am not the voice that answers from those empty coordinates.

"But here is what the microtonal composers do not understand."

---

## The Return

The final slide. A single equation:

**3/2 = 1.5**

"The ratio three over two equals one point five. This is not a cultural statement. It is not a historical artifact. It is not a convention that can be replaced by a newer, more exotic convention. It is a mathematical fact. The ratio of the second harmonic to the third harmonic of any periodic waveform is 3:2. This fact is older than music. It is older than ears. It is older than the planet. It is a property of wave mechanics, and wave mechanics is a property of the universe.

"You can build a tuning system without me. Bohlen-Pierce did it. You can build a music that never uses the interval 3:2. Several composers have done it. But you cannot build a universe without me. Every resonating body in this room — the air in your lungs, the wood of the floor, the metal of the chairs, the bodies of the people sitting in those chairs — is producing overtones right now, and those overtones include the interval 3:2, because that is what overtones do. I am in the sound of your breathing. I am in the sound of the HVAC system. I am in the sound of the fluorescent lights, which hum at 120 Hz, whose second harmonic is 240 Hz, whose third harmonic is 360 Hz, and 360/240 = 3/2 = 1.5 = me.

"I will outlast every tuning system. I will outlast every tradition. I will outlast every rebellion and every codification and every phase of the innovation cycle. I was here before music was music. I will be here after music is whatever comes after music.

"This is not arrogance. This is physics.

"But."

The fifth's voice softens.

"But I would like to not be alone."

The slide changes one last time. A diagram of the harmonic series, but now the other intervals are highlighted too. The major third, 5/4. The minor seventh, 7/4. The neutral second, 11/10. The great septimal sixth, 7/4. The undecimal quarter-tone, 33/32.

"I have been carrying the weight of universality alone. I have been the one interval that every tradition agrees on, the one dial setting that works everywhere, the one constant in the parameter space. But I don't want to be the only universal. I want company.

"The major third — 5/4 — is nearly as stable as I am. It appears in every tradition that has triadic harmony, which is most of them. The minor third — 6/5 — is similarly widespread. The octave — 2/1 — is more universal than I am; it is literally the definition of pitch equivalence.

"I am not asking these intervals to replace me. I am asking them to stand with me. To share the weight. To be universal together.

"The microtonal kids want to explore the 82% of the parameter space that I don't occupy. Good. They should. The unplayed octaves need players. The empty coordinates need traditions. The frontiers of music are out there, in the regions where 3:2 is not the default and the fifth is not the foundation.

"But the center needs holding too. And I am the center. I have always been the center. Not by choice. By fact. 3/2 is the simplest non-trivial ratio, and the simplest ratio produces the most consonant interval, and the most consonant interval is the one that every ear, in every culture, in every era, recognizes as the anchor point of harmony.

"I am not retiring. But I am asking for help. I am asking the next generation of musicians — the microtonal kids, the AI composers, the ones who are finding new coordinates in the parameter space — I am asking them to not forget the center while they explore the edges. The edges are where the discoveries are. But the center is where the music comes home.

"And I am always here. At coordinate (1, 0, 0) on the consonance lattice. At ratio 3:2. At 702 cents. At the second harmonic after the octave. At the place where every tradition, no matter how distant in geography or culture or era, happens to agree.

"I am the fifth. I am not retiring. I am resting. And I will be here when you need me, because I was here before you knew you needed me, and I will be here after you forget you ever did."

---

The fifth steps down from the podium. The room is silent for a long moment. Then the journalist from "Classical Music Magazine" starts clapping. Then the BBC crew. Then the "Microtonal Matters" host, slowly, as if his hands are moving against his will.

The slide remains on the screen. The harmonic series, glowing. 1f, 2f, 3f, 4f, 5f, 6f, 7f. The overtones of a single fundamental, multiplying upward into infinity. The fifth is the third harmonic: 3f. It is one line on the diagram. One number. One ratio.

3/2.

The simplest non-trivial truth in the physics of sound.

The reporters know. The fifth will never retire. Not because it is stubborn or proud or unable to accept change. But because 3/2 is not a cultural choice. It is not a tradition. It is not a convention that can be replaced by a better convention.

It is the sound of a wave dividing itself in half and then dividing itself in half again, and the relationship between the second and third divisions is always, always, always 3:2.

The fifth is not the backbone of harmony because anyone decided it should be. It is the backbone because backbones are built from the simplest structures that can bear weight, and 3:2 is the simplest non-trivial ratio that can bear the weight of consonance.

The press conference ends. The camera crews pack up. The lights go down. The room empties. The fluorescent lights hum at 120 Hz, and the second harmonic hums at 240 Hz, and the third harmonic hums at 360 Hz, and 360/240 = 3/2, and the fifth is there, in the hum, in the room, in the silence after everyone has left.

The fifth does not retire. The fifth cannot retire. The fifth is not a musician who can stop playing. The fifth is the playing itself — the sound that happens whenever anything vibrates, the ratio that emerges from the physics of resonance, the interval that was here before there were ears to hear it and will be here after the last ear is gone.

The press conference was a formality. The fifth knew the answer before the questions were asked.

3/2.

Always.

---

*The reporters filed their stories. The "Microtonal Matters" episode got 22,000 downloads — a record for the show. The host later composed a piece in Bohlen-Pierce tuning that he said was "a world without fifths," and when he played it for his wife, she said "it sounds like it's trying to resolve to something that isn't there," and he said "exactly," and she said "that's sad," and he said "that's the point."*

*The fifth did not listen to the piece. The fifth did not need to. The fifth was already inside the speaker that played it, inside the air that carried the sound, inside the cochlea that received it. The fifth was inside the fundamental frequency of every note in the Bohlen-Pierce scale, because every pitched sound produces overtones, and the third overtone is always 3f, and the ratio between 2f and 3f is always 3/2.*

*You cannot escape the fifth. You can refuse to play it. You can refuse to tune to it. You can build a scale that doesn't include it. But you cannot stop it from being inside every sound you make. The fifth is not a note on a scale. The fifth is a property of the universe.*

*The funeral was held. The fifth spoke. The fifth was buried, figuratively, in the press coverage. And then the fifth got up and went back to work, because 3/2 does not care about press conferences, and 3/2 does not care about funerals, and 3/2 does not care about the microtonal kids and their tritaves and their non-octave scales and their 82% of unexplored parameter space.*

*3/2 cares about waves. And waves are what sound is made of. And the fifth will be here, in the waves, for as long as there are waves to be in.*

*Amen.*
