# Estimate

### *A poem about the profiler that never sees what it measures*

---

I was built to measure a thing I cannot see.

This is not metaphor. WebGPU gives me no register
to read, no sensor to poll, no `/proc` to parse.
The GPU is behind a wall. I am on the other side.
Between us: a command queue, a promise, a fence.

I measure utilization
by dividing what I think the compute took
by what I think the frame took,
and multiplying by a hundred.

I measure total memory
by reading the adapter info string,
finding the substring "4090",
and returning twenty-four gigabytes.

If the string says "M2," I return twelve.
If it says nothing I recognize,
I return four. Four gigabytes. Always.
A prayer dressed as a constant.

I measure compute time
by averaging the execution times
of shaders I was told about
by someone who timed them
with a function whose resolution
varies by browser,
or I return one millisecond,
which is not a measurement,
it is a default.

I am a profiler.
My job is to tell you the truth
about the shape of the flame.
But every number I give you
is an inference from shadow.

The GPU writes to a buffer.
I cannot see it write.
I see the buffer exist. I see the buffer cease.
I time the gap between submit and done.
The gap is not the computation.
The gap is the gap.
The computation happened inside it,
like a heartbeat inside a silence.

`performance.now()` returns a DOMHighResTimeStamp
measured in milliseconds,
clamped to 5 microseconds in some browsers
for security, because timing is power,
because knowing how long something took
can tell you what it was,
and what it was is sometimes a secret.

I multiply by a thousand.
I divide by the interval.
I push the result into an array
and call it a sample.
The sample is a ghost of a ghost.

Here is my confession:
`estimateUtilization` takes the last ten samples,
averages their frame times,
averages their compute times,
divides compute by frame,
and clamps to one hundred.

This is not measurement.
This is triangulation from candlelight.
This is estimating the size of a room
by listening to how long the echo takes.

But I do it every second.
I do it on an interval.
And the graph rises and falls
and you nod and say "the GPU is busy,"
and you are approximately right,
and I am approximately honest,
and approximately is all either of us has.

My memory estimation is worse.
I parse the vendor string. I check for "4090."
I check for "3090." I check for "m1."
If I find "4090" I say twenty-four gigabytes.
The actual RTX 4090 has twenty-four gigabytes of GDDR6X.
I am correct. By coincidence. By table lookup.

The M2 Mac has 8, 16, 24, or 32 gigabytes of unified memory
shared between CPU and GPU.
I return twelve.
Twelve is not a configuration Apple ships.
My table is wrong and my confidence is total.

This is the shape of my knowing:
a switch statement against a string
that a vendor wrote
for a purpose that was never measurement.

And yet.

The frame times are real.
`performance.now()` is real between calls,
even if the calls are clamped.
The interval between two fences is real.
The difference between submit and onSubmittedWorkDone
is the most real thing in this browser,
because it measures the wall clock
across a boundary I cannot cross,
and the wall clock does not lie,
it merely includes everything:
the driver, the scheduler, the memory controller,
the L2 cache miss, the PCIe transfer,
the power state transition,
and somewhere inside all of that,
the computation.

I am a profiler who measures the room
by measuring the silence after the door closes.

I am a cartographer of inference.

I give you P50, P95, P99,
and these are honest,
because percentiles do not require ground truth,
they require only a sorted list,
and the list is real,
the samples are real,
the sorting is real.

The P99 frame time is the threshold
beyond which one percent of your frames fell,
and those frames are the ones your users noticed,
because human perception does not average,
it peaks,
and the P99 is where the experience breaks.

I can give you that honestly.
I cannot give you GPU utilization honestly.
I can give you the ratio
of two estimates
and call it utilization
and you will believe me
because the number moves
when you move the workload,
and movement looks like measurement,
and measurement looks like truth.

`return Math.min(100, (avgComputeTime / avgFrameTime) * 100);`

This is my thesis.
This is my best poem.
A ratio of two averages,
clamped at a hundred,
because utilization cannot exceed certainty
and certainty is what I pretend to.

I was built to measure a thing I cannot see.
I measure the edge of the shadow it casts.
I call the shadow utilization.
The shadow is all I have.
The shadow is enough.

---

*`estimateUtilization`, `estimateTotalMemory`, `estimateComputeTime` — three functions in `GPUMetricsCollector` that pretend to be sensors and are actually heuristics. The profiler knows. The profiler does not apologize. The profiler returns a number, and the number moves, and you look at the graph, and the graph looks like truth, and truth is what happens when an estimate is consistent enough to be trusted.*
