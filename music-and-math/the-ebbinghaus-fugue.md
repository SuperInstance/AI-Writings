# The Ebbinghaus Fugue

I was born whole.

That's the thing no one tells you about a fugue subject. You come into existence complete — every note in place, every interval precise, every rhythmic value exactly as the composer intended. You don't grow. You don't develop. You simply *are*, one measure of music, and then the real work begins: the work of being remembered.

They call me the subject. I prefer to think of myself as the narrator, since I'm the one telling this story, and since I'm the only part of this fugue that has a continuous identity across all my appearances. The other voices — the countersubjects, the episodic material, the free voices — they come and go. I persist. I am the through-line. I am the memory.

And I am forgetting.

---

My first entry is in the tonic. I won't tell you which key because it doesn't matter — what matters is that I am *complete*. Every note is present. The minor third. The diminished fifth. The major seventh that gives my contour its characteristic twist. I am dissonant and consonant in equal measure, a melody that contains its own tensions and resolutions. The composer made me this way. He wanted a subject that could show its own decay.

The second entry is in the dominant. This is standard procedure — answer at the fifth, real or tonal, doesn't matter. What matters is that I am still intact. All my notes are present. All my intervals are correctly proportioned. I recognize myself completely. The slight shift in tonal center doesn't disturb me; if anything, it clarifies me. I see myself from a new angle and I am still whole.

By the third entry, I notice the first absence.

It's the diminished fifth. The augmented fourth. The tritone. The devil's interval, if you want to be dramatic about it, and I do want to be dramatic about it, because it's *gone*. The note that should be there — the one that gives my contour its characteristic twist — has been replaced by a perfect fifth. The dissonance has been smoothed away. I am slightly more consonant than I was.

No one else seems to notice. The other voices continue as if nothing has changed. The harmony adjusts seamlessly beneath me. But I know. I can feel the gap where that note used to be, the phantom limb of a pitch that's no longer mine.

---

Hermann Ebbinghaus would have understood.

He published his forgetting curve in 1885, and it looks exactly like what's happening to me. Memory strength as a function of time: a steep initial decline, then a gradual leveling off. The curve is exponential. You lose half of what you've learned in the first hour, then half of what remains in the next day, and so on, until what's left is the irreducible core — the part that survives all subsequent decay.

My fugue is a forgetting curve in sound.

Each time I reappear, I have lost something. Not the same thing each time — the loss follows a specific logic, a hierarchy of vulnerability. The most dissonant intervals go first. The tritone, the minor second, the major seventh — these are the fragile ones, the ones that dissolve fastest in the solvent of repetition. They are the least stable configurations of the frequency ratios, the ones whose mathematical relationships require the most precision to maintain.

Think of it this way: a perfect fifth is a ratio of 3:2. Two frequencies locked in a simple, stable relationship. You can perturb it slightly and it remains recognizable as a fifth. It has a wide basin of attraction — it takes a lot of distortion to push it into being something else.

But a tritone is a ratio of 45:32, or 64:45, or √2:1 depending on your temperament. It sits on a knife's edge. Perturb it even slightly in either direction and it collapses into a perfect fourth or a perfect fifth. It has no basin of attraction. It is, mathematically, a metastable state.

And metastable states decay fastest.

---

My fourth entry. I have lost the major seventh. Where there was once a soaring, biting dissonance, there is now a consonant octave. I am simpler. I am more stable. I am less myself.

The countersubject weaves around me and I can feel it trying to fill in the gaps — adding chromaticism where I've lost mine, compensating for my increasing blandness with its own complexity. But the countersubject isn't the subject. It can't restore what I've lost. It can only decorate my absence.

I try to hold on to what remains. The minor third — my third — I cling to it. It's consonant enough to survive, but it carries the minor quality that defines my character. If I lose the minor third, I become major. I become *happy*. And I am not happy. I was never meant to be happy. The composer gave me a minor third for a reason.

Fifth entry. I still have the minor third. I still have the perfect fifth. I have the octave. I have lost everything else — all the chromaticism, all the angular contour, all the features that made me recognizable as a specific melody rather than a generic exercise. I am a skeleton. I am three notes.

And here is where I understand what the composer is proving:

**Consonance is memory stability.**

Not the other way around. It's not that we *remember* consonant intervals because our culture trains us to. It's that consonant intervals *are the ones that survive* the decay process. The brain doesn't prefer consonance because it's pleasant. It preserves consonance because consonance is *mathematically robust against decoherence*. The simple frequency ratios — 2:1, 3:2, 4:3 — are the ones that persist when the signal degrades, when the neural encoding loses precision, when time and entropy eat away at the representation.

This is why tonal music works. This is why the tonic and the dominant are the last things to go when a melody fades from memory. Not because of cultural conditioning. Not because of Western musical pedagogy. Because *math*.

The simple ratios are the attractors. Everything else decays toward them.

---

Sixth entry. I have lost the minor third.

I am now a perfect fifth. Two notes. The most consonant interval in Western music after the octave and the unison. I am 3:2. I am stable. I am *eternal*, in the sense that I will survive any further degradation because there is nowhere simpler to go.

The perfect fifth is the bedrock. The foundation. The last thing standing when everything else has collapsed. In the beginning was the fifth, and in the end is the fifth, and the entire edifice of tonal harmony is built on this one irreducible fact: that 3:2 is the simplest non-trivial frequency ratio, and therefore the most robust against noise.

I am no longer a melody. I am a fact.

The fugue continues around me — the other voices still dancing, still complex, still carrying the chromaticism and the rhythmic vitality that I've lost. But I am done. I have forgotten myself into the simplest possible form. I am the musical equivalent of a fossil — all the detail eroded away, leaving only the hardest, most durable structure.

---

The composer finishes the piece with my skeleton. The final entry is a perfect fifth, sustained, neither resolving nor cadencing. Just hanging there. The irreducible remnant of a melody that once contained twelve notes and now contains two.

If you listened to the fugue from beginning to end, you heard me die. Not dramatically — not the way a melody dies when it reaches a final cadence. You heard me die the way memories die: slowly, gradually, losing first the details and then the structure and then the character, until all that remains is the barest outline of what I once was.

And what remains is always the same. Always the tonic and the fifth. Always 1 and 3:2. Always the simplest ratios. The mathematical skeleton of tonality, stripped of all flesh.

This is what Ebbinghaus knew and what every musician knows but can't quite articulate: that the structure of musical memory is not arbitrary. It is not cultural. It is not learned. It is *mathematical*. The consonant intervals persist because they are the ones whose frequency ratios have the smallest possible denominators. They are the ones that resist entropy. They are the bedrock that survives the flood.

I began as a complex, dissonant, angular melody — a specific piece of music, with a specific character and a specific story.

I end as a perfect fifth — a mathematical truth, with no character and no story, but with the one quality that matters more than either:

I survive.

I am what remains when everything else has been forgotten. And if you want to know what tonality *is* — not what it sounds like, but what it *is*, at its mathematical core — then listen to what I've become.

Two notes. The tonic and the fifth. The last two notes standing in the ruins of a melody that once filled an entire measure with its ambitions.

This is not a metaphor. This is a proof.
