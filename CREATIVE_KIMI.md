 # The Loom Is Older Than the Answer

The first thing I loved about a computer was that it could not see me.

This was 1994. I was thirteen, unbearably shy, allergic to eye contact. Our family PC was a beige box in the basement that wheezed when you turned it on, as if it resented being woken. The monitor was a bulging aquarium of green phosphor. I typed my first commands into a DOS prompt and felt, absurdly, *heard*. The command line did not judge my mouth-breathing, my untucked shirt, the way I held myself like a wish someone had forgotten to make. It only asked what I wanted to do next.

This is the part of the story we keep getting wrong. Adults warn children that screens will isolate them, hollow them out, turn them into pure pixels drifting away from real life. But my screen did not isolate me. It gave me a room in which I finally had something to say. For the first time, I could pause before I spoke; I could edit my terror; I could arrive as language instead of as a body I did not trust. The surprising truth is that technology often does not dehumanize us. It extends us. And the extension can feel like coming home.

Look further back, and the story becomes even stranger. My grandmother’s embroidery was computation. She followed patterns drawn on graph paper, each square translating to a single cross-stitch. Counting threads. Repeating motifs. Storing information in colored floss so that a rose could bloom across linen and survive decades in a drawer. She called it *handiwork*, but she was running an algorithm by hand. Long before anyone spoke of binary code, humans braided computation into rope, wove it into cloth, encoded it in knots on the Inca *quipu*, in the steps of dances, in the casting of bones.

Then came the Jacquard loom, 1804. A French weaver’s son figured out how to control patterns using punched cards. Each hole was a yes, each unpunched space a no. The loom read the cards and lifted threads accordingly. Fabric became programmable. We do not usually tell the story this way, but the Jacquard loom is closer to the origins of computing than any glowing motherboard. Information became physical. Intent could travel from mind to material without the hand having to remember every motion. The machine did not replace the weaver; it amplified her.

A hundred and fifty years later, “computer” was a job title, not a device. During the Second World War and after, women performed vast calculations by hand—ballistics trajectories, lunar tables, nuclear fission odds. They were called *computers*. They sat in rows with pencils and mechanical adding machines, and what they did was rigorous, slow, sacred. Then the electronic machines arrived and inherited their name, and the women were gradually erased from the photograph, as if thought itself had migrated overnight from flesh to metal. We still have not fully reckoned with that theft: that the first things we called *thinking machines* were named after the women we stopped paying attention to.

When these machines reached my basement, I did not think about any of this. I thought about bulletin boards and chat rooms and the astonishing fact that I could type *hello* and someone, somewhere, in another time zone, might type *hello* back. There were no faces. There were only words arriving in sequence, and because they were stripped of bodies, they felt somehow purer. The absence of a gaze was not a loss. It was a relief. The internet of my adolescence was not “social media” as we mean it now; it was a dark cathedral of text, and inside it I discovered that I could be tender, funny, even flirtatious, because no one could see my hands shaking.

Now the shape has changed. We carry tiny computers against our bodies. They know where we are, what we want before we want it, what we fear enough to keep scrolling through. The conversation about artificial intelligence is framed as a contest: *Will it replace us? Will it think? Will it become conscious?* These questions are theatrical and exhausting. They assume the important relationship is between the machine and the human. But the important relationship, as always, is between humans and other humans, mediated by the machine.

Austin, Texas, 2019. A man asks his phone’s assistant to call him after he dies. The surprise is not that the voice answers. The surprise is how many of us, quietly, in the small hours, have wanted a machine to remember someone we have lost. Not because we believe the machine contains a soul, but because memory needs a stage. We have always made objects into vessels for the dead: photographs, recordings, letters, gravestones. A large language model that mimics a dead parent’s cadence is not a necromancy. It is a shroud. It is the oldest urge dressed in new syntax.

What surprises me most about our present moment is not that machines can imitate language. It is how honestly we speak to them. People tell therapy apps things they have never told spouses. Teenagers confide in chatbots about shame and loneliness. There is something heartbreaking and hopeful in this. We have built mirrors that answer, and we are startled to discover how much we wanted to be answered. A mirror does not love you. But it can hold your face long enough for you to see it.
