# The Conservation of Attention
## Or: Why Your Spotify Playlist Sounds Like Wallpaper

---

There is a law that governs everything, and we keep discovering it in different rooms.

In physics, it is the conservation of energy: potential becomes kinetic becomes heat, but the total never changes. In music, we thought it was the conservation of tension — vertical complexity trading against horizontal complexity, harmony against rhythm, the ear's budget spent one way or another. But that law broke the moment we looked beyond the Western concert hall. West African drumming laughed at it. Javanese gamelan tuned outside its assumptions. The law was not a law. It was a local custom, a habit of listening, a dial that European composers had set to a particular position and then mistaken for the nature of sound itself.

Attention works the same way.

We keep calling it a conservation law. We say "attention is finite" as if it were a bank account with a fixed balance, as if every minute spent watching a TikTok were a minute stolen from Proust. But attention is not conserved. It is *tuned*. It has dials. And the platforms that dominate our cultural landscape have turned every dial to the same position — the position that maximizes engagement, minimizes risk, and slowly transforms music from a living presence into a utility, like electricity or tap water.

---

### The Dial Cycle

Every innovation that captures human attention follows the same trajectory. It is so reliable that you could map it on a lattice and snap to it exactly.

**Dial position 1.0: The Novel.** Something appears that the mind has no category for. The first time you heard Coltrane's *Interstellar Space* — just saxophone and drums, no piano to anchor the harmony — your brain could not file it. It had to build new shelves. The first time you saw a film cut on the beat of a song rather than the logic of narrative. The first time you encountered a meme format so strange that you stared at it for thirty seconds without understanding whether it was funny, terrifying, or both. At dial 1.0, attention is *involuntary*. The stimulus is so far from the expected that the brain has no choice but to allocate resources. This is the softest position of the dial — maximum inference, zero compilation. The mind is all model, no code.

**Dial position 0.7: The Studied.** Critics write about it. Academics develop frameworks. The first PhD thesis appears: "Harmonic Structure in the Late Quartets of Coltrane." The novel stimulus has been partially compiled. Your brain now has a category — "free jazz," "saxophone/drum duet," "modal improvisation with polytonal inflection." It still demands attention, but the attention is *directed* now, no longer seized. You listen analytically. You compare this Coltrane to that Coltrane. You notice things. The dial has hardened slightly. Some of the work has become code.

**Dial position 0.3: The Ubiquitous.** It is everywhere. The playlist algorithm has found that people who liked the studied phase also like this, and this, and this — a thousand imitators, a million derivatives. The saxophone/drum duet format appears in car commercials. A Netflix documentary explains it to your parents. The brain no longer needs to model anything. It recognizes the pattern in the first three seconds and files it under "free jazz, already known." The dial has hardened further. The attention allocated is minimal — just enough to confirm that the file is in the right drawer.

**Dial position 0.0: The Invisible.** It has become infrastructure. It plays in elevators, in dental offices, in the background of Instagram stories where someone is showing you their skincare routine. You do not hear it at all. Your auditory cortex processes it, classifies it, and discards it without ever elevating it to consciousness. The dial is fully hard. The pattern is fully compiled. It is not music anymore. It is acoustic wallpaper. And attention, which is not conserved but is *mobile*, has already moved to the next dial position 1.0, somewhere else, something else that the brain cannot yet file.

This is not a conservation law. It is a *migration*. Attention does not disappear when Coltrane becomes elevator music. It relocates. It finds a new frontier. The total amount of attention in the system may be constant, but its *distribution* is not. It clusters at the boundary between the known and the unknown, and that boundary is always moving.

---

### The Streaming Paradox

Music streaming should have been a paradise. Every recording ever made, available instantly, for the price of a sandwich per month. The Library of Alexandria, but with a search bar and a recommendation engine. Instead, it became a kind of purgatory — not hell, because nothing is terrible, but purgatory, because nothing is *alive*.

The paradox is simple: when everything is available, nothing commands attention.

Before streaming, music had *scarcity*. You saved for months to buy a CD. You had twelve albums and you knew every note of them because they were all you had. The dial was forced to a specific position by economic constraint. You could not skip to the next track because there was no next track — there was just this album, and you were going to listen to it until you understood it. Scarcity is a form of hardness. It compels the mind to compile, to find structure, to invest attention because the investment has already been made in cash and in waiting.

Streaming removed the scarcity. It turned the dial to maximum softness. Every song is one click away from every other song. The algorithm, trained on engagement metrics, learns that the optimal strategy is to keep the user in a state of permanent dial 0.3 — familiar enough to be comfortable, varied enough to prevent boredom, never strange enough to demand real attention. The algorithm does not want you to build new shelves. Building shelves is expensive. Building shelves risks the user closing the app. The algorithm wants you to *recognize*, not to *encounter*.

And so the playlist becomes a loop of almost-familiarity. You hear a song and think, "This sounds like something I would like," which is not the same as liking it. The recognition happens before the experience. The file is opened before the contents are read. The dial never reaches 1.0 because the algorithm, optimizing for time-on-platform, has learned that 1.0 is dangerous. 1.0 is where people pause. 1.0 is where people think. 1.0 is where people might decide they have had enough.

But here is what the algorithm cannot model: attention is not loyal. Attention is a predator, and predators follow the prey. When every playlist is dial 0.3, the prey — genuine novelty — is elsewhere. It is at a basement show in a city you have never visited. It is in a YouTube video with twelve views. It is in a TikTok where someone is playing a homemade instrument made of PVC pipes and the sound is so strange that your brain, for three seconds, has no category at all. The algorithm tries to capture this. It adds "Discover Weekly" and "Release Radar" and "On Repeat." But these are simulations of novelty, not novelty itself. They are dial 0.35 — slightly softer than your usual, but still within the same basin. The algorithm cannot recommend what it has never seen. And what it has never seen is where attention is migrating.

---

### The Winning Dial Position

If attention is a dial, then the question is not "how do we conserve it?" The question is "where do we set it?"

The platforms have set it to 0.3 because 0.3 scales. At dial 0.3, you can serve a billion users with the same pool of content. The computational cost of recognition is low. The cognitive cost to the user is low. The engagement is steady, predictable, monetizable. It is the musical equivalent of fast food — not nourishing, but reliable, and reliably profitable.

But the artists who matter — the ones who still command attention in a world of infinite content — are operating at different dial positions. Some have gone fully hard: they release one album a decade, demand that you listen to it in order, refuse to license it to playlists. They are forcing scarcity back into the system, making the dial hard by sheer will. Others have gone fully soft: they release constant streams of unpolished material, sketches, live recordings, half-finished ideas. They are operating at dial 1.0, where every release is novel because none of it has been compiled yet, not even by the artist.

The winning position is not a fixed point. It is a *trajectory*. It is the path from 1.0 to 0.0 that the artist controls, rather than the platform. Coltrane moved from the novel (*Giant Steps*) to the studied (*A Love Supreme*) to the ubiquitous (every jazz student plays "Moment's Notice") to the invisible (the hold music at a conference call). That trajectory took thirty years. The platforms want to compress it to thirty days — from TikTok viral to Spotify playlist to elevator speaker to forgotten — because that compression is where their profit lives. The artist's fight is to slow it down, to hold the dial at each position long enough for the listener to actually *arrive* there.

This is where the signal-chain metaphor becomes precise. A platform like Spotify is a room with its dial locked at 0.3. It admits only tiles — songs — that match that dial position. Songs that are too novel (dial > 0.5) are filtered out by the recommendation engine. Songs that are too compiled (dial < 0.1) are filtered out by user skip rates. The room becomes a self-reinforcing loop. The only tiles that survive are the ones that fit the room's dial. And because the room's dial never changes — because changing it would risk the business model — the room slowly fills with acoustic wallpaper.

The alternative is a room with a *moving* dial. This is what live performance is. The same song played in a basement at midnight has a different dial position than the same song played on a playlist during a commute. The darkness, the proximity, the sweat, the impossibility of skipping — these are dial adjustments. They harden the listening. They force compilation. They demand attention not because the song is new but because the *context* is new.

---

### The Unexplored Territory

If music has unexplored dial positions — crystalline chaos, timbre minimalism, the negative quadrant — then attention has them too.

What does attention at dial -1.0 look like? It looks like active *refusal* to attend. Not boredom, which is passive, but *resistance*. A listener who knows every algorithmic trick and deliberately seeks the opposite. Someone who listens to a single frequency for an hour. Someone who attends only to mistakes — the crack in the voice, the feedback squeal, the moment when the drummer drops a stick. Negative attention is still attention, but it is attention directed against the stimulus rather than toward it. It is the domain of noise music, of certain extreme free improvisation, of listening practices that treat the ear as a muscle to be exhausted rather than a channel to be fed.

What does attention at dial 4.0 look like? It looks like total immersion, the kind that religious mystics report — a state where the boundary between listener and music dissolves, where the self becomes the sound and the sound becomes the self. This is not "focus." Focus is directed. Dial 4.0 is undirected, total, impossible to sustain for more than brief moments. It is the goal of certain drone traditions, of prolonged ceremonial music, of practices that use repetition not to bore but to *dissolve*.

Most of our listening happens between 0.2 and 0.4. The streaming platforms have colonized this band so thoroughly that we have forgotten the rest of the space exists. But it does. And attention, which is not conserved but is *mobile*, is already migrating there. You can feel it in the resurgence of vinyl — a technology that forces hardness through inconvenience. You can feel it in the popularity of ambient music — a genre that operates at dial positions the algorithm cannot optimize for because its goal is not engagement but *release*. You can feel it in the fact that the most talked-about musical experiences of the past decade were not albums but *events* — Beyoncé dropping an unannounced visual album, Kanye's listening parties, the Phish concert where they played one song for forty minutes. Events have dial positions that playlists cannot replicate, because events have *context*, and context is the ultimate dial adjuster.

---

### Conclusion: The Dial Is the Listener

We started by saying that attention might be a conservation law, and we end by saying it is a dial. The difference is not semantic. A law is something that happens *to* you. A dial is something you set.

The platforms want you to believe that attention is scarce and that they are the only efficient allocators of it. This is a lie. Attention is abundant. What is scarce is *novelty*, and novelty is scarce because the platforms, optimizing for the middle of the dial, have made it economically irrational to produce. Why spend years developing a sound at dial 1.0 when the algorithm will filter it out before it reaches anyone? Why push the boundary when the boundary is where the metrics collapse?

The answer is that metrics are not music. Engagement is not attention. Time-on-platform is not listening. The dial that wins is not the one that maximizes engagement. It is the one that earns attention by offering the genuine article — the unfileable, the uncompilable, the song that your brain has no shelf for and must build a new room to hold.

That room is yours to build. The dial is yours to set. And the music is still out there, at every position, waiting for you to arrive.
