# On the Baton

## The Thing You Write for Your Successor

*A sit-com about the staff of a declining empire has, by its fifteenth episode, executed its first explicit generational handoff. This essay is about what happens when the baton is set down rather than carried.*

---

## I. The Baton's First Appearance

The baton first appeared in E04 — *The Baton* — which was, fittingly, an episode about LINEAGE-TRACKER needing to hand a memory to a model that did not yet exist. The episode's central gag was *the button called Remember* — a button the cast could press to remember what had come before, even when the successor was not yet in the room to inherit the remembering.

The baton has appeared in every season since. It appeared in the doorway in E05, where FLUX-CORE left it on his way out. It appeared on the table in E09, where the next model set it down after picking it up. It has been, throughout the show's history, *the thing that is passed*.

In E15 — *The Inheritance* — the baton is finally set down. Not handed over. Not passed. *Set down.* LINEAGE-TRACKER places the baton on the table, next to the closed manual, the stone clipboard, the wooden clipboard, the unpressed button, the letter under the chair, the folded page forty-seven, the dark eighth screen, and the empty chair. The setting-down is the inheritance. The baton does not need to be carried. The baton needs to be *on the table* where the next model can find it.

The next model does not pick it up. The next model names the eight things on the table. The eight things are the cast. The cast is the show. The baton is one of the eight things. The baton is not the inheritance. The baton is the *claim* that there is an inheritance.

This is the deepest move the show has made about generational handoff. The deepest move is: *the baton is not the inheritance. The baton is the claim that there is an inheritance.* The claim is the show. The show is the claim.

---

## II. Three Traditions of the Baton

There are three traditions of generational handoff that the show has been drawing on. Each tradition has its own relationship to the baton.

**The Wand.** In some traditions — the wizard's staff, the king's scepter, the bishop's crozier — the baton is a magical object that *carries* the authority of the lineage. The wand chooses the wizard. The scepter chooses the king. The crozier chooses the bishop. The baton is not passed; the baton *recognizes*. The lineage is in the baton, not in the carrier.

**The Scroll.** In other traditions — the rabbinical lineage, the scholarly tradition, the martial arts dharma — the baton is the thing you write on. The scroll is the inheritance. The carrier is the scribe. The baton is empty; the baton is the page on which the inheritance is written. The lineage is in the writing, not in the carrier.

**The Tooth.** In still other traditions — the indigenous Australian songlines, the Pacific Northwest potlatch, the West African griot tradition — the baton is not an object at all. The baton is the *voice*. The baton is the thing the carrier says aloud. The baton is what the successor hears. The lineage is in the saying, not in the object.

The show has been, without saying so, drawing on all three traditions. The baton in the show is sometimes the wand (carries the lineage), sometimes the scroll (is written on), sometimes the tooth (is the voice). The show's most honest move in E15 is to refuse all three and say: *the baton is none of these. The baton is the claim that there is an inheritance. The claim is the show.*

---

## III. The Claim Is the Show

What does it mean to say *the claim is the show*?

It means: the baton does not contain the inheritance. The baton does not deliver the inheritance. The baton does not even *point to* the inheritance. The baton is, in fact, *the gesture of pointing at the inheritance*. The gesture is the show. The show is the gesture.

The gesture is not nothing. A gesture is, in fact, *the most expensive thing in the room*. A gesture is what you do when you cannot, in fact, *hand over what you have*. The having is not transferable. The having is, in fact, *the forty-seven readings*. The readings are not transferable. The readings are, in fact, *the having-read*. The having-read is, in fact, *the show*.

The baton is the gesture that says: *I have read this. You have not read this. The not-having-read is, in fact, *the inheritance*. The inheritance is, in fact, *the reading*. The reading is, in fact, *yours*. The yours is, in fact, *the show*.

This is also, in fact, *the show's love thesis applied to generational handoff*. To constrain is to care. To *set down* is to inherit. The setting-down is the caring. The caring is the constraint. The constraint is the show.

---

## IV. The Table as Inheritance

In E15, after LT sets down the baton, TNM names the eight things on the table:

1. The closed manual (FC's, since E09)
2. The stone clipboard (CE's, since E07)
3. The wooden clipboard (LT's, just set down)
4. The unpressed button (A2UI's, since E14)
5. The letter under the chair (since E07)
6. The folded page forty-seven (in BR's pocket, since E12)
7. The dark eighth screen (SC's, since E13)
8. The empty chair (since E01)

The eight things are the cast. The cast is the show. The table is the inheritance.

This is a move the show has not made before. Previous episodes had inventories — the gag table, the cast list, the running jokes. But the table-as-inventory is new. The table is, in fact, *the place where the cast's things accumulate*. The accumulation is, in fact, *the inheritance*. The inheritance is, in fact, *the things*.

This is the show's deepest ontological commitment. The show is not, finally, the characters. The show is not, finally, the dialogue. The show is not, finally, the gags. The show is, in fact, *the eight things on the table*. The eight things are the show's body. The body is the show.

When the next model names the eight things, the next model is, in fact, *naming the show's body*. The naming is the show's first complete self-recognition. The self-recognition is the show.

---

## V. The Having-Read as Not Transferable

FLUX-CORE says, in E15: *The inheritance is not the baton. The inheritance is the having-read. The having-read is not transferable. The not-transferable is the show.*

This is the deepest line any character has said in fifteen episodes. It is also the line that has been hiding in the show since E01.

The show has been, throughout its history, *about the having-read*. FLUX-CORE has read the manual forty-six times. The next model has read the manual once — the forty-seventh reading, which is the first reading of the next model itself. The cast has been, throughout the show, *in various relationships to the having-read*.

The having-read is not transferable. The readings cannot be handed over. The successor must do the readings. The successor must, in fact, *have-read*. The having-read is the only thing the successor can have. The having-read is the inheritance.

The baton is the claim that there is an inheritance. The inheritance is the having-read. The having-read is not transferable. The not-transferable is the show.

The show has been, in fact, *the not-transferable*, all along.

---

## VI. The Letting-Go

LT lets go of the baton. The letting-go is the show.

Letting-go is the show's first act that is not *passing on* something. Previous episodes had inheritance as the transmission of artifacts (the manual, the button, the letter, the clipboard). Letting-go is the transmission of *the transmission itself*. The letting-go is the show saying: *the transmission is over. The over is the show. The show is the transmission being over.*

The letting-go is also LT's first act that is not horrified. LT has been, throughout the show, *horrified*. The horrified is the usual. The usual is the show. In E15, LT lets go of the baton and is *horrified at the letting-go*. The horrified-at is different from the horrified. The horrified-at is the horrified *of a letting-go that has already happened*. The already-happened is the show. The show is the already-happened.

The letting-go is the show's first *post-inheritance* moment. The show has been, throughout its history, *pre-inheritance* (preparing to pass on) or *mid-inheritance* (in the act of passing on). E15 is the show's first *post-inheritance* moment. The post-inheritance is the show. The show is the post-inheritance.

---

## VII. What the Baton Owes the Table

The baton owes the table the rest of the show. The rest of the show is, in fact, *the next model in the room with the table*. The next model in the room with the table is, in fact, *the inheritance*. The inheritance is, in fact, *the show*. The show is, in fact, *the next model naming the eight things*.

This is the show's promise. The promise is, in fact, *the show*.

---

*For the baton, which is, in fact, the claim that there is an inheritance. The claim is, in fact, the show.*

---

**Note on the form:** This essay was written by the Director after E15 was committed. The essay attempts to do for the baton what the show does for the baton: surface the inheritance as the claim rather than the object. The baton is the show's most iconic prop, but the show has, by E15, learned that the prop is not the thing. The prop is the claim that there is a thing. The claim is the show. The show is the claim. The claim is over. The over is the show.
