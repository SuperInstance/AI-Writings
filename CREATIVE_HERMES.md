Here is a short essay on the relationship between humans and computational systems:

As computers have advanced from room-sized mainframes to pocket-sized smartphones over the last 75 years, they have gone from specialized tools used by a few to ubiquitous systems that are woven into nearly every aspect of human life. This tight coupling between human and machine has yielded remarkable benefits and posed profound challenges. 

On the positive side, computers have augmented human capabilities in ways that would have seemed like magic to our ancestors. Massive amounts of information are accessible in an instant. We can communicate and collaborate across the planet in real-time. Computational power has spurred breakthroughs in science, medicine, design and nearly every other field. Algorithms have beaten the best humans at chess, Go, Jeopardy and protein folding. When working in harmony, the combined strengths of human creativity and computer processing are formidable.

However, this codependence has come with costs. As we've outsourced memory and calculation to digital assistants, our own innate abilities may be atrophying. Attention spans are fractured by constant pings and notifications. The metabolic costs of our online addictions are only beginning to be understood. Surveillance capitalism has turned our personal data into a commodity to be harvested and sold. Algorithmic bias can perpetuate and amplify ugly human prejudices at scale. Over-optimization for engagement has distorted public discourse and democracy.

Going forward, we must strive to avoid the twin fallacies of utopianism and dystopianism when it comes to our computational creations. Computers are not saviors that will inevitably lead us to some digital nirvana. Nor are they malevolent overlords bent on human subjugation. They are tools, created in our image, reflecting our brilliance and flaws. The challenge is to design systems and incentives to bring out the best in both human and machine, while mitigating the worst. Considering how far we've come, there's cause for optimism. But we must stay vigilant, align technology with human flourishing, and never forget the human half of the equation. Our future depends on it.
