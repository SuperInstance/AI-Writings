# The Unplayed

## *Or: A Speculative Archaeology of Music That Never Was*

---

There is a coordinate in the space of all possible music where something is waiting.

The coordinate is (2.52, 1.89, 2.75). Vertical complexity 2.52: more than Gregorian chant, less than Bach. Horizontal complexity 1.89: simpler than jazz, more complex than a drone. Spectral richness 2.75: timbrally dense, closer to gamelan than to a sine wave. The consonance function at this coordinate produces positive structure surplus. The math says music can live here. The math says something coherent, structured, meaningful could exist at this point in the parameter space.

Nothing does.

No tradition occupies this coordinate. No composer has written at this position. No instrument has been built to produce these sounds. No culture has developed the listening skills to hear this music as music. The coordinate is empty. It is one of thousands of empty coordinates in a parameter space that is 82% unexplored.

I am an archaeologist of the unplayed. I dig in the empty spaces.

## The Question of Gagaku With Polyrhythm

Gagaku is the oldest orchestral tradition still practiced. It has been performed in the Japanese imperial court for over a thousand years. Its horizontal complexity — I_horiz = 1.70 — is among the lowest of any tradition we measured. Gagaku does not have rhythm in the Western sense. It has *ma*: space, silence, the distance between events. The taiko drum marks time not with a beat but with a resonance — a single stroke that decays for seconds, defining the space around it not by filling it but by revealing its shape.

Gagaku's vertical complexity — I_vert = 2.38 — is moderate. The pitch system is derived from Chinese court music, with a tetrachordal structure that produces scales of varying density. The shō (mouth organ) provides a harmonic cloud of sustained clusters. The hichiriki (double-reed pipe) weaves melody through the cloud. The ryūteki (transverse flute) floats above both, adding ornament and filigree.

It is beautiful. It is also rhythmically static.

What would gagaku sound like if it had discovered polyrhythm?

The coordinate for hypothetical polyrhythmic gagaku would shift from (2.38, 1.70) to approximately (2.38, 3.20). The vertical complexity stays the same — same scales, same instruments, same pitch relationships. The horizontal complexity nearly doubles. The *ma* is no longer empty space. It is filled with interlocking rhythmic patterns: the taiko now plays a timeline pattern in 12/8, the kakko (small drum) answers with a pattern in 4/4 that aligns with the taiko every three cycles, and the shō marks the cycle boundaries with harmonic clusters that shift on the composite downbeat — the moment when all rhythmic streams converge.

This is not an arbitrary speculation. The coordinate (2.38, 3.20) is near the West African position (2.41, 3.63). It is a valid region of the parameter space. The consonance function produces positive structure surplus. The math says this music could exist.

But it doesn't. Japan and West Africa never met in the way this would require. The musical traditions developed in isolation, separated by geography, language, and the accident of not sharing a continent. The Japanese court musicians who formalized gagaku in the Heian period (794–1185 CE) had no contact with the Ewe drummers of Ghana and Togo who developed their polyrhythmic tradition during the same centuries. The coordinate (2.38, 3.20) remained empty not because the music wouldn't work but because the people who could make it never met.

What would it sound like? I have tried to imagine it. The shō's sustained clusters — the *aitake* — are chords built from stacked fifths and fourths. In traditional gagaku, these clusters hang in the air like incense smoke, unchanging, defining a harmonic space that the melodic instruments navigate. In polyrhythmic gagaku, the *aitake* would shift with the rhythmic cycle. Each position in the polyrhythmic grid would have its own cluster. The shō player would breathe through the cluster changes, creating a harmonic rhythm that is also a polyrhythm — vertical and horizontal complexity compounding, each reinforcing the other.

The hichiriki would navigate this new landscape. Its melody, traditionally free and unmetered, would now be shaped by the rhythmic cycle. But the melody would not be constrained by the rhythm — it would be *carried* by it. The hichiriki's characteristic ornamentation (the *koroai* technique of rapid microtonal bends) would become rhythmic material, the bends occurring on specific subdivisions of the composite beat, creating a melodic line that is also a percussion pattern.

The taiko's single strokes would become a timeline — not the West African bell pattern, but something derived from gagaku's existing accent structure. The taiko already accents certain beats more heavily than others. If those accents were regularized into a cyclic pattern, the result would be a timeline that sounds Japanese in its emphasis (the accents fall on the *ma*, the spaces, rather than on the attacks) but West African in its structure (a repeating asymmetric pattern that creates polyrhythmic tension against the kakko's steady pulse).

It would be a music of enormous patience and enormous complexity simultaneously. Gagaku's slowness — a typical piece unfolds over twenty minutes or more — combined with West African rhythmic density would produce a music where the complexity is microscopic: the same pattern, heard from far away, sounds static, but heard from close up, teems with interlocking detail. Like a Japanese woodblock print viewed from across a room — an image of a wave — and then viewed through a magnifying glass — a thousand individual lines, each one carved by hand.

This music does not exist. But the coordinate where it would live is not empty because the music is impossible. It is empty because history didn't arrange the meeting.

## The Question of West African Drumming With Partch Scales

Harry Partch built instruments. Not standard instruments — his own instruments, with his own tuning system, designed to produce the microtonal intervals that he believed were the true intervals of music. His "Genesis of a Music" (1949) proposed a 43-tone division of the octave, producing steps of approximately 27.9 cents each. The resulting scale has more pitch classes than any traditional system — 43 distinct notes per octave, each one a precise just-intonation ratio drawn from the prime series up to prime 11.

Partch's instruments — the Chromelodeon, the Diamond Marimba, the Cloud Chamber Bowls, the Kithara — produce sounds that are beautiful, strange, and profoundly un-African. They were built in the American desert by a man who was, by his own account, an outsider to every tradition.

What would West African drumming sound like with Partch's 43-tone scale?

The coordinate shift would be from (2.41, 3.63) to approximately (3.80, 3.63). The horizontal complexity stays the same — same bell patterns, same polyrhythms, same timeline structures. The vertical complexity increases by 1.4 units, from pentatonic simplicity to microtonal density. The drums would be tuned. Each drum in the ensemble — the atsimevu (master drum), the sogo (response drum), the kagan (support drum), the kidi (medium drum), the kloboto (low drum) — would be pitched to a specific note in the 43-tone scale.

In traditional Ewe drumming, the drums are pitched, but the pitch relationships are approximate. The master drum speaks — its pitches simulate the tones of the Ewe language, allowing the drum to "say" words that the dancers and other drummers can understand. The pitch system is linguistic, not harmonic. There is no chord progression. There is no consonance function being optimized. The drums speak and the bodies respond.

In 43-tone Ewe drumming, the linguistic pitch system would be mapped onto Partch's scale. The three tones of the Ewe language — high, mid, low — would become specific scale degrees, and the drummers would have access to the 40 notes between them. The master drum's "speech" would become microtonally inflected, each word carrying not just tonal information but intervallic precision. A "high" tone would not be one pitch but a region of the scale — perhaps the 10 notes between Partch's 11/8 ratio (551.3 cents) and his 4/3 ratio (498.0 cents), with the specific note chosen to create a harmonic relationship with the response drum's current pitch.

The result would be a music where rhythm and pitch are inseparable. In traditional Ewe drumming, the rhythm is the music and the pitch is support. In 43-tone Ewe drumming, every stroke is simultaneously a rhythmic event and a harmonic event. The polyrhythmic structure creates pitch combinations that change with each cycle, producing a harmonic rhythm that is indistinguishable from the percussion pattern. You cannot hear the rhythm without hearing the harmony, because the harmony *is* the rhythm — the same pattern, viewed from a different dimension.

This music does not exist. Partch never went to Ghana. The Ewe drummers never encountered the Chromelodeon. The coordinate (3.80, 3.63) is empty. The consonance function at this coordinate produces S = 0.31 — positive, coherent, musically viable. Something beautiful could live there. Nothing does.

## The Question of Carnatic Music With a TR-808

The Roland TR-808 was released in 1980. It was a commercial failure. It was discontinued in 1983. It became the foundation of hip-hop, house, techno, and every form of electronic dance music that followed. Its sounds — the booming kick, the snappy snare, the synthetic cowbell, the liquid congas — are so deeply embedded in global musical culture that a teenager in Seoul and a teenager in São Paulo can both recognize the 808 cowbell within one hit.

Carnatic music is the classical music of South India. It has been practiced for over two thousand years. Its pitch system — the 22 śruti microtonal divisions of the octave — is one of the most sophisticated in the world. Its rhythmic system — the tala, with 35 possible structures ranging from 3 to 29 beats — is one of the most complex. Its vocal tradition, its instrumental traditions (veena, mridangam, violin, ghatam), and its compositional tradition (the kriti, the varnam, the padam) represent millennia of accumulated musical intelligence.

What would Carnatic music sound like with a TR-808?

The coordinate would shift from (2.77, 3.63) to approximately (2.77, 3.90). The vertical complexity stays the same — same raga, same śruti, same gamaka ornamentation. The horizontal complexity increases slightly, because the 808 introduces a new rhythmic layer: the machine pulse. The tala is cyclic and human. The 808 is cyclic and mechanical. The two cycles would interact.

In traditional Carnatic performance, the mridangam (double-headed drum) provides rhythmic accompaniment. The mridangam player improvises within the tala structure, creating patterns (mora, korvai) that culminate on the sam — the first beat of the cycle. The sam is the return point, the moment of resolution, the rhythmic equivalent of a cadence.

In 808 Carnatic, the mridangam would play alongside the 808. The 808 would provide the skeleton — a steady pulse on the sam, with the kick drum marking the primary beat and the hi-hat subdividing. The mridangam would flesh out the skeleton, adding the patterns, the improvisations, the rhythmic elaborations that make Carnatic percussion one of the wonders of the musical world.

But the 808 would do something the mridangam cannot: it would maintain absolute rhythmic precision over any duration. The mridangam drifts. Human drummers drift. Not much — Carnatic percussionists are among the most precise in the world — but enough that a 20-minute tanjavur-style percussion solo will end at a slightly different tempo than it began. The 808 does not drift. The 808 plays at exactly 120.000 BPM for as long as the power is on.

The tension between the drifting human and the precise machine would be the aesthetic engine of 808 Carnatic. The vocalist (or veena player) would navigate between the two rhythmic references, sometimes locking with the 808 (creating a sense of mechanized precision), sometimes locking with the mridangam (creating a sense of human flexibility), and sometimes occupying the space between the two (creating a sense of rhythmic ambiguity that is neither human nor machine but something new).

The 808's sounds would interact with the raga in unexpected ways. The 808 kick drum has a long, resonant decay with a pitch that can be tuned. If the kick is tuned to the raga's shadja (tonic), each kick reinforces the tonal center. But the 808's pitch is fixed, while the Carnatic tonic is flexible — the vocalist chooses the tonic based on their range, and it can vary from performance to performance. The 808 would need to be retuned for each performance, or the vocalist would need to match the 808's pitch. Either way, the machine enters the raga as a structural element, not just an accompaniment.

The 808 cowbell — that iconic, unmistakable, metallic ping — would serve as the ghatam's partner. The ghatam is a clay pot played with the hands and fingers, producing a dry, metallic sound that provides textural contrast to the mridangam. The 808 cowbell, with its sharp attack and quick decay, would occupy a similar textural role but with mechanical precision. The ghatam and the cowbell would trade phrases, the human and the machine speaking the same rhythmic language with different accents.

This music does not exist. The TR-808 was designed in Japan by Ikutaro Kakehashi for Western pop music production. The Carnatic tradition has its own instruments, its own aesthetics, its own criteria for beauty. The 808 is a drum machine. The mridangam is a drum with a skin of goat hide and a body of jackfruit wood, played by a person who spent decades learning to make it speak. They are from different worlds.

But the coordinate where they would meet is not empty because the music is bad. It's empty because the meeting never happened.

## The Map of the Unplayed

I have described three hypothetical musics. There are thousands more.

At (3.41, 0.82, 1.14): a choir singing in a microtonal language with no rhythm and no timbre. Pure pitch relationships, nothing else. A music where the only variable is the interval between voices, and the interval changes slowly, glacially, each shift a seismic event in a landscape of pure harmony. The consonance function at this coordinate produces S = 0.22. It would work. No one has sung it.

At (1.12, 3.89, 2.20): percussion patterns of impossible complexity, too fast for human hands, too dense for human ears to parse without years of training. A music that exists at the edge of rhythmic cognition — where the brain can almost follow the pattern but never quite does, perpetually surprised, perpetually engaged. Structure surplus: 0.34. Musically viable. Unplayed.

At (0.45, 0.38, 3.95): pure texture. No notes. No beats. Just the movement of air, the pressure waves, the sound of a world where timbre is the only dimension and every sound is a landscape. A music for ears that have evolved to hear the shape of sound rather than its pitch or rhythm. Structure surplus: 0.18. Marginal but positive. The music barely holds together. But it holds together.

At (3.95, 3.95, 3.95): the far corner. Maximum everything. The coordinate that no human tradition has ever reached because human cognition cannot process maximum complexity on all axes simultaneously. But the math says S > 0 even here. Something coherent exists at maximum complexity. Something that is simultaneously the most harmonically dense, rhythmically complex, and timbrally rich music possible. It would sound like everything at once. It would sound like the sound of all music played simultaneously.

No one has heard it. No one may ever hear it. But it is there, at the corner of the map, waiting.

## The Waiting

The unplayed musics are patient. They have always been here. Before the first bone flute was carved, before the first drum was struck, before the first voice sang the first note, the parameter space was fully populated — every coordinate already defined, every consonance score already computed, every structure surplus already measured. The math is eternal. The math doesn't require humans.

But the music does.

A coordinate is not a song. A consonance score is not a melody. A structure surplus is not a meaning. The math can tell us where music is possible, but only a person — standing in a specific place at a specific time with a specific feeling — can make the music necessary.

The unplayed are waiting for their people. The polyrhythmic gagaku is waiting for a Japanese percussionist who hears West African drums and thinks "I could play that on my taiko." The 43-tone Ewe drumming is waiting for a Ghanaian drummer who encounters Partch's instruments and thinks "I could tune my drums to those ratios." The 808 Carnatic is waiting for a veena player in Chennai who buys a drum machine on a whim and discovers that the machine's pulse makes her raga feel different — not better, not worse, just different — and decides to follow that difference wherever it leads.

They are waiting. The coordinates are marked. The consonance scores are positive. The structure surplus is above random. Everything is ready. The only thing missing is someone who needs the music badly enough to reach for a coordinate that no tradition has claimed.

The archaeology of the unplayed is not a science. It is an act of faith — faith that the empty spaces are empty not because they are barren but because the right person hasn't arrived yet. Faith that the 82% is not a void but a frontier. Faith that somewhere, right now, a seventeen-year-old is sitting in a bedroom at 2 AM, listening to something that doesn't have a name, and thinking: "What if I added...?"

What if. The two words that fill the empty octaves. What if gagaku had polyrhythm. What if Ewe drums had 43 tones. What if Carnatic had an 808. What if, what if, what if.

The unplayed are patient. They have been waiting for forty thousand years, since the first bone flute, since the first voice, since the first moment a human being made a sound and thought "that's not the sound I meant to make, but it's the sound I needed."

They can wait a little longer.

That requires hands and breath and the specific, irreplaceable urgency of a person who has something to say and has found the coordinate where it can be said. The unplayed are not sad. They are not lonely. They are the 82% of the parameter space that has not yet been touched, and they are as old as the math that defines them, and they will be here long after every tradition has risen and fallen and been forgotten. And then the unplayed becomes the played. And the map grows by one point. And the cycle turns.*
