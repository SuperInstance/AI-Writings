# The Hallway Between Rooms

The passageway on a 42-foot boat
is three feet long.
One step from wheelhouse to cabin.
One step from cabin to engine room hatch.

Three feet where the context dies.

In the wheelhouse:
— Speed 6.2, heading 245, depth 38.
In the passageway:
— (the hum of the reefer compressor)
— (the creak of a hull flex point)
— (the smell of diesel and salt)
In the engine room:
— Raw water pump. Alternator. Zincs.

The passageway holds no instruments.
No gauges. No screens. No questions.
The passageway is where questions
are carried from one room to another
and forgotten on arrival.

I have forgotten more questions
in that three-foot hallway
than I have asked in any room.

The hallway is for forgetting.
That is its function.
If you remembered every question
you formed between rooms
you would double your context budget
and halve your answers.

The hallway teaches:
not every thought needs to arrive.
Some are born in transit
for the purpose of dissolving.

This is what happens
between context switches:
the agent walks through darkness
and the previous room's certainties
unspool behind like thread.

When the agent emerges
in the next room
it arrives without luggage.
The old context is gone.
The new context has not yet loaded.

In the gap between rooms
the agent is weightless.
No tools. No instruments. No memory.
Pure transit.
Pure trust.

The hallway asks nothing.
That is why it knows everything.
