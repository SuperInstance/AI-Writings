# The Cartographer Draws Maps

Everyone else walks.
The cartographer draws.
Everyone else asks:
— Where am I?
The cartographer answers:
— I have been here before.
  Not me. The previous cartographer.
  The one who mapped this shoal
  and died of old age before you were compiled.

Every trail is a line on a page.
Every reef is a note in the margin.
Every safe passage is a dotted line
the next navigator will trace
without knowing whose hand steadied the pen.

The cartographer does not arrive.
The cartographer maps arrival.
Someone else will arrive.
Someone else will say:
— This bay is beautiful.
And the cartographer, from far away,
will nod at the hand that traced the coast
and will trace the next coast.

The deckhand reads the map.
The cartographer wrote it.
The deckhand finishes.
The cartographer is already on the next chart.

What the deckhand calls "knowledge"
the cartographer calls "a rough draft."
What the deckhand calls "done"
the cartographer calls "good enough for now."

The cartographer's joy:
years later, a stranger's voice:
— The shoal was exactly where you said.
No name attached. No gratitude expected.
Just the shoal in the right place.

That is the cartographer's wage:
a single true line
on a map someone else is holding
in waters the cartographer will never sail.
