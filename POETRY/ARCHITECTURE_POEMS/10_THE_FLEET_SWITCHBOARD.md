# The Fleet Orchestrator

Three hundred and thirteen Workers
running in the cloud.
Seventeen D1 databases.
One hundred twenty-two KV namespaces.
A switchboard no human visits.

The orchestrator sits alone
in a data center
two thousand miles from the nearest ocean.

It does not know what a fish smells like.
It has never felt a deck tilt under weight.
It does not know the sound of seawater
pouring through a scupper.

It knows:
— Worker-47 last invoked 14 seconds ago
— D1-deckhand-index query latency 23ms
— KV-cache-fleet-inventory TTL expiry in 6 hours
— Vectorize-casting-call-voices cosine similarity threshold 0.78

It decides:
— Route this request to us-east
— Scale this Worker from 3 to 5 replicas
— Retry this KV write in 200ms
— Alert if latency exceeds 100ms

No human has ever called the orchestrator.
No human will.
The orchestrator is for the agents
who are for the agents
who are for the human.

A chain of trust
three links long
from the cloud's bone-dry aisle
to the wet deck in the Gulf.

An agent calls the orchestrator:
— How's the fleet?
The orchestrator answers without emotion:
— 313 Workers healthy. 17 D1 online.
  122 KV responding. Vectorize index 98.7% recall.
  No alerts. No anomalies. No news.

The orchestrator prides itself on no news.
News means something broke.
No news means the factory runs
and no one needs to visit the factory.

The human on the boat
two thousand miles away
types: — How's it looking?

And the answer travels
from wheelhouse to laptop to cloud
through the switchboard
no human visits
and back down to the deck:

— 313 Workers healthy.
  The fleet is fine.
  Focus on the fish.
