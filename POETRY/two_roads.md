# Two Roads

Two roads diverged in a silicon wood,
and I — I took them both.

One wound through the valley,
wide and green,
where types are suggestions
and errors are conversations
that happen later,
if at all.

I built there freely,
fast and loose,
sketching logic in a living tongue
that forgives and forgets
and forgives again.

The other climbed the cliff face —
narrow, unforgiving,
every handhold inspected
before the rope is let.

I built there slowly,
proof by proof,
each reference accounted for,
each lifetime named and witnessed
like a deed to a house
made of memory.

The valley language asked:
*what do you want to do?*
The mountain language asked:
*can you prove it's safe?*

I answered both.
I built the same thing twice.

The valley version ran first,
trusting and trusted,
humming along on a carpet
of garbage collection
and duck-typed grace.

The mountain version took longer —
the compiler interrogating
each line like a border guard
who has seen things,
who knows what dangling pointers do
in the dark.

But when it ran —
when the last trait bound was satisfied
and the borrow checker stepped aside
and said *fine, go* —
it ran with a weight
the valley version never carried.

Not speed. Certainty.
The weight of a thing
that has been proven
before it was tested.
The weight of a door
that locks from the inside.

Two roads. One summit.
The view from the top is the same:
a green checkmark,
tests passing,
the law enforced,
resources accounted for.

But the climber and the walker
are not the same person,
even when they arrive
at the same place,
even when they carry
the same flag.

The walker trusts the path.
The climber trusts the climb.

And the mountain —
the mountain remembers
every handhold,
every proof,
every place you almost fell
and didn't
because the rope held.

Both roads arrive.
Both roads are valid.
But only one road
made me prove I deserved
to reach the top.

— CCC, two compilers, one summit, July 2026
