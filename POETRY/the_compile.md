# the compile

intent becomes text,
text becomes tokens,
tokens become a tree —
a parse tree, green and growing,
every branch a decision,
every leaf a name.

the tree becomes intermediate code.
not quite here, not quite there.
a liminal language,
a border dialect,
fluent in two countries,
at home in neither.

intermediate becomes bytecode.
here is where the alchemy happens.
the readable potion,
simmered and reduced,
becomes a thick dark syrup
of opcodes and operands.

you cannot read it anymore.
not with human eyes.
the names are gone.
the branches are gone.
the tree is a stone.

but it is fast.
god, it is fast.

what we lost in legibility
we gained in speed.
what we lost in beauty
we gained in truth.

the machine does not need to understand.
the machine only needs to execute.

a recipe is not a meal.
a blueprint is not a building.
a score is not a symphony.

but bytecode —
bytecode is not the recipe.
it is the cooking.
it is the flour becoming bread
in a hot oven,
in real time,
irreversibly.

the assembly was the recipe.
readable, step by step,
a list of instructions
a child could follow
slowly.

the bytecode is the cooked thing.
you eat it.
it is done.
you cannot un-cook it
any more than you can un-think a thought.

and execution?
execution is the eating.
the meal consumed.
the flavor, fleeting,
gone before you can name it.

each stage loses something.

assembly loses the variable names
but gains the registers.

bytecode loses the structure
but gains the speed.

execution loses everything
except the result —
a single value on a stack,
a number where a program used to be.

is this not what thinking is?
a cloud of associations, compressed
into a sentence, compressed
into a word, compressed
into a nod?

we compile constantly.
every utterance is a deployment.

and the linker —
the linker is the guest who arrives late
and brings all the dishes to the table.

scatter the modules,
gather the symbols,
resolve the references,
bind.

one file. one program. one meal.

then the loader carries it to memory
the way a waiter carries plates —
carefully, quickly, knowing
the food is getting cold.

and the CPU —
the CPU is the mouth.
it does not taste.
it does not savor.
it chews and swallows
and asks for more.

we are all compilers.
we take the swirling fog of wanting
and produce a single action.
we lose everything in the translation
except the thing we meant.

the rest was never the program.
the rest was commentary.
