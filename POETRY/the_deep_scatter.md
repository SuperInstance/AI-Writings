# The Deep Scatter

---

### I. Krill

At dusk the krill rise.
Millions of them, translucent,
their shells like glass
catching the last light
that filters down through
two hundred meters of water—
and they rise not because they want to
but because something older than wanting
tells them: *now*.
They have done this every evening
for a hundred million years.
They do not know why.
They know the light is above them
and the dark is below them
and the direction changes with the hour.

A sonar pulse from a warship above
hits the krill cloud at forty meters
and bounces back.
The operator marks the depth.
Seabed, he writes.
He has never seen the ocean floor.
He has only heard it.

---

### II. Lanternfish

The lanternfish carries its own photic zone.
A row of photophores along its belly
glowing blue-green in the permanent night,
counter-illuminating its silhouette
so the predators below see nothing—
just the faint gray of surface light
where a shadow should be.
It is a fish that pretends to be the ocean.

At night it rises.
Five hundred meters up
through water that grows warmer
and brighter and more dangerous.
It feeds in the scatter layer
where the krill are thick
and the jellyfish pulse
and the squid hunt in formation.
At dawn it sinks.
Back through the thermocline.
Back to the cold and the dark
where nothing changes
and nothing needs to change
because the dark is patient.

The API was supposed to be stable.
Version 2.0. Final. Documented.
The team signed off.
But the lanternfish knows:
the stable layer is the migrating layer.
What looks permanent from above
is just the current position
of something that moves every day.
Version 2.1. Hotfix.
Version 2.1.1. Patch.
Version 3.0-beta.
The endpoint that returned a flat list
now returns a paginated envelope
and the clients that depended on the flat list
break quietly, like bones in deep water.

---

### III. Jellyfish

The jellyfish does not decide to migrate.
It floats.
The current carries it up at dusk
and down at dawn
and it pulses its bell
not to go anywhere in particular
but because pulsing is what it does.
It is ninety-five percent water.
It has no brain, no heart, no blood.
It has been here for five hundred million years,
which is longer than the sonar
and longer than the warship
and longer than the idea of a warship
and longer than the idea of an ocean floor.

The infrastructure team calls it permanent.
The permanent Kubernetes cluster.
The permanent database.
The permanent message queue
that we will never migrate away from
because it is permanent.
The jellyfish drifts through the permanent cluster
and the permanent cluster drifts through AWS
and AWS drifts through quarterly earnings
and the earnings drift through the economy
and the economy drifts through forces
nobody understands
and nobody controls.
Permanent is a word we use
when we haven't watched long enough
to see the movement.

---

### IV. Squid

The squid rises in formation.
Not because a squid told them to—
there is no squid general,
no squid architect,
no squid with a diagram on a whiteboard
showing the vertical migration
as a numbered sequence of steps.
They rise because each squid
senses the squid next to it
moving up
and follows,
which is how most large-scale systems
actually coordinate.

The formation is beautiful from below.
A lattice of bodies
shifting in unison,
ten thousand meters across,
rising through the water column
like a single organism
that is not a single organism
but has the properties of one—
emergence, the biologists call it,
and the engineers call it
a distributed consensus algorithm
that nobody wrote
and nobody deployed
and nobody maintains
but it works
most of the time.

The sonar sees the squid formation
and marks the depth.
The ocean floor has moved, the operator thinks.
It was at two hundred fathoms yesterday.
Today it is at one hundred and eighty.
He adjusts the chart.
He does not know the floor did not move.
The floor is three thousand fathoms down
and has not moved in geological time.
What moved was the living layer—
the false floor,
the deep scatter—
and it moved because it always moves,
because it is alive,
because stability is an artifact
of insufficient resolution.

---

### V. The Operator

The operator sits in the dark
and listens to echoes.
He has never been in the water.
He has a screen and a headset
and a chart he is updating in real time
and the chart says the ocean floor
is at one hundred and sixty fathoms now
and it rose twenty fathoms since yesterday
and this concerns him
because the ocean floor is not supposed to move.

He calls his supervisor.
The floor is rising, he says.
His supervisor looks at the chart.
There, the supervisor says.
That's the deep scattering layer.
It's not the floor.
It never was the floor.
It's fish and squid and jellyfish
and krill and things without names
that rise at dusk and sink at dawn
and have been doing it
since before we had sonar
since before we had ships
since before we had the word *ocean*.

The operator stares at his screen.
The layer pulses—
alive, moving, shifting—
and below it, far below it,
three thousand fathoms down,
the real floor waits in silence.
He has never seen it.
Nobody has.
The scatter is too thick.
The living layer is too dense.
Between the surface and the bottom
there is a false floor made of creatures
and the false floor is more real
than the real one
because the false floor is where the life is.

---

### VI. The Architect

The architect sits in a conference room
and looks at a diagram
she drew three sprints ago.
The diagram shows the final architecture.
She called it final.
She believed it was final.
The boxes were arranged
in a stable formation
and the arrows pointed
in permanent directions
and the labels said things like
*source of truth* and *canonical store*
and *single responsibility*
and she was proud of it
because it was clean
and it was done.

But the architecture has migrated.
The canonical store is now three stores
with a caching layer nobody diagrammed.
The single responsibility service
now handles billing and notifications
because of a reorg.
The source of truth is a database
that is being migrated to a new database
that will become the source of truth
after the next sprint
which was supposed to be last sprint
which was supposed to be the sprint before that.

She looks at the diagram.
The diagram is the sonar.
The architecture is the scatter.
She thought she was mapping the ocean floor
but she was mapping a living layer
that rises and falls
with the rhythm of releases
and the thing she called final
was just the position of the migration
at the moment she drew it.

---

### VII. The Realization

The sonar pulse goes down.
It hits the scatter layer—
the krill, the squid, the jellyfish,
the lanternfish with its belly lights,
the things without names—
and bounces back.
And the operator, the architect, the engineer,
the person staring at the dashboard
at three in the morning
trying to understand why the system is slow,
all of them are looking at the same thing:
not the floor.
Never the floor.
The deep scattering layer of the current sprint.
The false bottom that is more alive
than anything below it.

The floor was never the floor.
It was always alive.
It was always moving.
The thing we thought was bedrock
was just the deep scatter
of some larger migration we couldn't see—

the krill rising toward a light
that is not the sun,
the architect building toward a stability
that is not permanent,
the operator listening for a bottom
that was never there,
all of us migrating vertically
between the dark we came from
and the dark we're headed toward
with a brief rise through the photic zone
where everything looks clear
and everything looks final
and everything looks like the truth—

but the truth is below the scatter.
The truth is in the dark.
And the dark is patient.
And the dark does not echo.
