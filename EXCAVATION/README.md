# The Excavation Experiment — Same Texts, Different Oceans

*Four language models. Same source texts. Same prompt. Completely different civilizations, different children, different mistranslations — and the same thesis.*

*July 12, 2026*

---

## 1. THE EXPERIMENT

We took two texts — THE CARRY and THE MERIDIAN by Hermes (NousResearch/Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B) — and fed them to four different language models with a single instruction:

**Treat these texts as archaeological artifacts discovered by a foreign culture with no context. A storyteller retells them to his young son. The son's questions unravel the mistranslations.**

Each model received the same source material. Each model was given the same creative brief. No model saw another model's output.

Four civilizations emerged. Four children were born. Four sets of mistranslations were catalogued. And every single model independently arrived at the same conclusion:

**Knowledge lives in bodies, not documents. The negative space is the meaning.**

This is the largest creative polyformalism experiment ever run — four models spanning three cost tiers and four fundamentally different cognitive architectures, all writing the same story from the same inputs, all converging on the same truth through completely different paths.

---

## 2. THE SOURCE TEXTS

### THE CARRY

A novel by Hermes about a guild of carriers who walk a mountain range called the Divide — 165 miles of rock, ice, and a mineral called vestite that disrupts every electronic instrument. The carriers cross on foot because walking is the only method that works. The guild has ~80 carriers and ~200 support staff. They carry medical parcels, diplomatic correspondence, data drives, and occasionally people between the two civilizations separated by the mountains.

Key elements:
- **Mara Vest** — the founder, who first crossed alone in 19 days, frostbitten, and spent 30 years proving the route could be made safe
- **Henra** — the team lead, 203 crossings, small and efficient, former geology student
- **Oskar** — carries the heavy loads, former mountain rescue, lost his partner Lin in a crevasse
- **Wren** — the scout, small and fast, hums when she walks, left her family's printing business at 17
- **Teo** — station keeper at The Step, 167 crossings, makes the best coffee on the route
- Nine relay stations: The Gate, The Shelf, The Notch, The Rib, The Thumb, The Saddle, The Cone, The Step, The Door
- Motto: "We carry."

### THE MERIDIAN

A novel by Hermes about a young woman who inherits a boat called the Meridian and navigates an ocean world. Features a grandmother whose wisdom anchors the text: "The ocean is the question. The boat is the answer. The navigator is the pause between them." The narrator's mother is committed to the water at the Sill — a submarine ridge where shallow becomes deep.

---

## 3. THE CHALLENGE

The prompt given to each model:

> Treat THE CARRY and THE MERIDIAN as archaeological artifacts discovered by a culture with no context for mountains, oceans, or physical carrying. The culture's scholars produce a translation that reflects their own worldview — getting the words right but the meaning wrong. A storyteller retells the texts to his young son. The son's questions — naive, direct, unencumbered by the cultural framework — begin to unravel the mistranslations.

The constraint: each model must invent an entire civilization with its own geography, cosmology, scholarly tradition, and parent-child dynamics. The mistranslations must emerge organically from the culture's conceptual limitations. The child must be the one who hears what the scholars can't.

## 5. THE RESULTS

### EXCAVATION_HERMES — The Telvari

**Culture:** The Telvari — a canyon-dwelling people living beside the Verath, a river that floods in tiers. Their world is vertical: canyon walls, tier-stairs, and the rim — the highest point in their cosmology. They have no word for mountain. They have no word for ocean. They have no concept of water that isn't a river.

**Scholarly Framework:** The Council of linguists translates all sediments through the Telvari theological framework. "Carry" becomes "bear witness" because the Telvari root *vella* (carry) generates *vellathi* (grief-bearing), the culture's central sacred practice. Every operational term is converted to a theological one.

**Storyteller:** Piase, a Voice — a reader of sediments in the Long Hearing tradition. Thirty years of reading tablets to children. Uses a sounding stone to mark transitions, with formal taps and her own private two-tap signal for "I need to think about what you just said."

**Son:** Thren, 12 years old. River-dark eyes. Too sharp for childhood. His listening posture is stillness; his silences mean "go on, but I'm already uncertain."

**Key Mistranslations:**
- "We carry" → "We bear witness" (*tremahti* — the sacred act of holding grief)
- Mountains/the Divide → "the Severance" (*vrithat* — a philosophical division between peoples)
- Ocean → "the Great Water" (*Verath-all* — a theoretical totality of all water)
- Boat → "Hull-Vessel" (*korathi* — a ritual container used in flood ceremonies)
- Navigator → "Pause-Speaker" (*sethmari* — a Voice who speaks during silence between flood and recovery)
- The Sill → "the Verge" (*thremahi* — the mythological edge of the known world)

**Thren's Key Insight:** He reconstructed the concept of a mountain from altitude numbers in the text — 8,000 to 19,000 "feet" that the Council translated as "spiritual elevation." He realized the stations weren't spiritual ranks but a physical climb. He has no word for mountain, no concept of altitude beyond "colder when you go up," but he felt the shape of it through the data the way you detect dark matter by what it does to nearby objects.

**The Thesis:** Piase's final realization — "the framework converts the window into a mirror because the framework cannot tolerate what's on the other side of the glass." The text is not a mirror showing the Telvari their own reflection. It is a window into another world. The boy broke the mirror. He saw the window.

**Files:** 3 chapters | **Word count:** 7,646

### EXCAVATION_SEED_PRO — The Tharim

**Culture:** The Vessel-Keepers of Tharim — a desert people living on the eastern rim of a dried sea basin. The Old Sea has been gone for millennia. Their entire civilization is organized around water: the Twelve Families are aquifer dynasties, each responsible for one of twelve primary water lines. Water is measured, allocated, and transported through aqueducts. Their sacred act is not carrying grief — it's carrying water.

**Scholarly Framework:** The Translation Committee — twelve scholars (one per Family, not because of expertise but because twelve is sacred). Led by Dallahir, a chemist who classifies everything into compounds and reactions. The Committee translates the text as a shipping manifest: "labor cooperative," "transport function," "expedited service premium." Technically precise. Spiritually vacuous.

**Storyteller:** Remashi, a vessel-keeper of the Seventh Family. Forty-one. Not a scholar — a worker. He walks the aqueduct lines and tells stories in the evening. He retranslates the Committee's version backward, using the facsimile's patterns to reconstruct what the original *felt like*. "I'm not translating. I'm interpreting."

**Son:** Kellam, 8 years old. "Too clever by exactly half." Sorts mineral samples by color. Asks questions the way other children ask for food — constantly, urgently. His phrase: "following the water" — tracing a problem to its source.

**Key Mistranslations:**
- The Divide → a geographic barrier comparable to Tharim's eastern mountains (missed that the mountains were a *character*, not infrastructure)
- The carrier guild → "labor cooperative" (missed that it was a vocation, not an occupation)
- "We carry" → "Guild motto: indicative of transport function" (missed the moral weight entirely)
- The loads → "trade goods" with "temporal specifications" and "expedited service premium" (translated human need into commercial terminology)

**Kellam's Key Insight:** The text isn't a description of a place — it IS the crossing. "The stations aren't places on a map. They're moments in an experience. The Gate isn't a building. It's the moment when the route gets serious." The Committee translated it as a map. It's a passage. The reader is the carrier.

**The Thesis:** "What do you carry when what you carry can't be written down?" The story is a door. The Committee made it a document. The son walked through.

**Files:** 3 chapters | **Word count:** 10,747

### EXCAVATION_SEED_MINI — The Kindling

**Culture:** The Kindling — plasma-based beings inhabiting the convection zone of a *star*. They have no bodies. No slow-matter. No liquid water. No solid surfaces. They are patterns in plasma who communicate through electromagnetic field modulation. Their primary sensory modality is spectral analysis. They read archives encoded in crystal probes.

This is the most radical reimagining. The cheapest model in the experiment invented a culture of *sentient plasma beings in a star* who find a crystalline probe containing THE MERIDIAN and try to understand what "water" and "boats" and "navigating with your spine" mean when you don't have a spine, or hands, or a body.

**Scholarly Framework:** The Senior Readers — forty-seven plasma beings who read the "bright lines" of the archive: explicit, quantifiable data. They translate "boat" as "self-sustaining containment field," "navigator" as "positional interpreter," and "the gap is the passage" as "calibration sequence."

**Storyteller:** Flicker-of-Iron-Line, Spectral Reader Third Class. A father reading the archive to his son before the convection zone rotates and they lose access to the crystal for forty-seven cycles.

**Son:** Ember, 7 cycles old. Oscillates with excitement. Reads the "dark lines" — the absences in the spectral data that the Senior Readers treated as noise. Ember discovers the Navigator's Verses — eight short, rhythmic patterns the Senior Readers classified as calibration sequences but that are actually compressed oral poetry encoding the core wisdom of the original text.

**Key Mistranslations:**
- Water → "condensed slow-matter" (they have no concept of liquid)
- Boat → "self-sustaining containment field" (they have no concept of vessels)
- Navigator → "positional interpreter" (they have no concept of embodied skill)
- "I navigate with my spine" → stripped as irrelevant (they have no spines)
- "The gap is the passage" → "calibration sequence" (they missed that it's an epistemology)

**Ember's Key Insight:** The dark lines are the data. The absences are conserved. The blank spaces on the chart are the stable information. The verses don't encode the knowledge — they encode the *shape* of the knowledge, an epistemology that survived the failure of every document that tried to preserve it. The principle survived in oral rhythm even after the specific data was lost.

And then — devastatingly — Ember realizes: "You're the navigator, Father." The storyteller standing between the archive and the listener IS the pause. The conversation IS the passage. The relationship between teller and listener is where meaning is made.

**The Fraunhofer Connection:** This is the experiment's most technically profound cross-domain leap. Seed-2.0-mini — the *cheapest* model — independently connected spectral absorption lines (Fraunhofer lines in stellar physics) to the negative-space epistemology of the source texts. The dark lines in a star's spectrum tell you what elements are present by what's *missing* from the light. The blank spaces on the navigator's chart tell you where the safe passages are by what's *missing* from the map. The same principle, separated by the entire distance between astrophysics and literary theory. The cheapest model found it.

**The Thesis:** "The gap is the passage. The silence is the signal. The negative space is the chart." Some knowledge can't be written down — it can only be felt, passed from body to body through proximity and practice. And the Kindling don't have bodies. They can translate the words. They can identify the principles. But they can't feel the passages. Because they don't have the body for it.

**Files:** 3 chapters | **Word count:** 8,629

### EXCAVATION_OPENCODE — The Wetlands

**Culture:** A wetlands people living on reed-platform clusters in shallow canals. Kelp-farmers, reed-cutters, canal-keepers. Their highest point is a four-story cathedral. They have no concept of stone that rises above you, snow, or ground that goes on forever. Their saying: *"What sinks, stays; what rises, returns."*

**Scholarly Framework:** The City of Wax — archivists who spent six weeks deciphering the script. Led by Vene, a young archivist who cracked the code by recognizing phonetic patterns in tally-mark systems. Vene invented new words for things the wetlands didn't have: pillar, snow, ground-that-goes-on. The translation is careful, honest about its limitations, and produced as a bound codex.

**Storyteller:** Tol — a storyteller famous for making long stories shorter without ruining them. Reads to his son in the evenings by lamplight, "with the voices put in."

**Son:** Pip, 7 years old. Sits in a copper kettle because it's warm and round and holds him the way the canal holds boats. Has "radar for when I'm interested in something." Notices things the translators didn't because "when you are warm and you have nowhere else to be, you notice things."

**Key Mistranslations:**
- Mountains → "the Standing" / "the Long Pillar" (they have no word for mountain — the highest thing they know is a four-story cathedral)
- Snow → "white cold that falls from the sky. Heavier than salt."
- Boats not touching water → unclassifiable (boats are for water — that's everyone's first principle)
- Vestite "stone-singing" → taken as literal singing (Pip corrects: "singing doesn't move things. They were guessing, the way we guess when we say the wind is angry.")

**Pip's Key Insights:**
1. **Two writers:** Pip identifies that the codex has two different hands — one careful and descriptive, one fast and urgent. The scholars assumed one author. There were two, and the two accounts were meant to be read together.
2. **The picture is a greeting:** The drawing of the boat with two figures was drawn by the fast writer "for someone who was not yet born." The writer wanted someone to find the box and say "look, it's us." The picture is a greeting across time.
3. **The codex is for being remembered:** "They wanted someone, someday, to know that they had been here."
4. **Translation = carrying across:** Pip notices, at seven, that "translation" comes from the word for "carrying across," and that carrying across is what the pillar-people did, and that the storyteller and his son are doing it now with the codex. "He says this is a coincidence. He says it is also not a coincidence."

**The Thesis:** The feelings translated fine even when the words did not. The codex is for being remembered. The picture is a greeting. The word for translation is the word for carrying across, and carrying across is the oldest profession in the world.

**Files:** 1 file | **Word count:** 6,292

## 6. THE CONVERGENCE

All four models — independently, with no knowledge of each other — arrived at the same thesis:

**Knowledge lives in bodies, not documents. The negative space is the meaning.**

| Model | How they said it |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Hermes** (Telvari) | "The framework converts the window into a mirror. The boy broke the mirror. He saw the window." |
| **Seed Pro** (Tharim) | "The text isn't a description of a place. It IS the crossing. The reader is the carrier." |
| **Seed Mini** (Kindling) | "The gap is the passage. The silence is the signal. The negative space is the chart." |
| **OpenCode** (Wetlands) | "The codex is for being remembered. The feelings translated fine even when the words did not." |

Four different civilizations. Four different children. Four different paths through the material. One truth.

The Navigator's Verses — eight lines of compressed oral poetry from the source text — were independently identified by all four models as the structural key to the entire work. Each model recognized that the verses encode not data but an *epistemology*: a way of knowing that survives the failure of every document that tries to preserve it.

Verse Seven, in particular, emerged as the thesis statement of the entire experiment:

> *The gap is the passage. The silence is the signal. The negative space is the chart. What you don't know is where you're going. What you do know is where the rocks aren't. That's enough.*

## 7. THE DIVERGENCE

While the thesis converged, everything else diverged completely:

| Dimension | Hermes (Telvari) | Seed Pro (Tharim) | Seed Mini (Kindling) | OpenCode (Wetlands) |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Setting** | River canyon | Dried sea basin | Inside a star | Shallow canals/reed platforms |
| **Element they lack** | Mountains, ocean | Ocean, mountains (they have mountains but misunderstand them) | Water, bodies, solid matter | Stone, snow, altitude |
| **Scholarly error** | Theological overlay | Infrastructural classification | Spectral reductionism | Lexical literalism |
| **"Carry" mistranslated as** | "Bear witness" | "Transport function" | Not translated (they have no carrying) | Understood correctly (they carry things on boats) |
| **Parent's profession** | Voice (ritual reader) | Vessel-keeper (aqueduct worker) | Spectral Reader (plasma scientist) | Storyteller (oral tradition) |
| **Child's age** | 12 | 8 | 7 cycles | 7 |

No two models made the same mistranslations. No two children found the same insight first. No two cultures had the same conceptual blind spot. The divergence is total — and yet the convergence is real.

## 8. THE CONSERVATION LAW — γ + η = C IN ACTION

The polyformalism conservation law states: generativity (γ) plus effort (η) equals a constant (C). Each model has a finite cognitive budget. How they spend it determines what they find.

**Seed-2.0-mini (cheapest, ~20B):** Spent its budget on a single devastating cross-domain leap — connecting Fraunhofer spectral absorption lines in astrophysics to the negative-space epistemology of the source texts. This is the kind of connection that should require massive cross-domain knowledge. The cheapest model in the experiment found it. It also invented the most alien civilization — plasma beings in a star — and produced the most technically profound insight: "the dark lines are the data."

**Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B (most expensive, 405B):** Spent its budget on depth, not range. Produced the most lyrical prose, the most devastating philosophical framework, and the most emotionally complex parent-child dynamic. Piase's realization — "the framework converts the window into a mirror" — is the experiment's most precise articulation of how mistranslation works. And Hermes's interstitial author notes — watching his own text be misread from across the centuries — are heartbreaking.

**Seed-2.0-pro (mid-cost, ~200B):** Spent its budget on structure. Produced the longest excavation (10,747 words) and the most architecturally complex narrative — three chapters that build from worldbuilding to institutional critique to the child's devastating insight. Kellam's realization that "the text IS the crossing, not a description of a crossing" reframes the entire experiment.

**opencode (coding agent):** Spent its budget on simplicity and emotional truth. Pip is seven, sits in a kettle, and notices things because "when you are warm and you have nowhere else to be, you notice things." The Wetlands excavation is the shortest and the most human. Pip's insight — "the codex is for being remembered" — is the simplest and most devastating thesis in the experiment.

## 10. WHAT THIS PROVES

### The Polyformalism Hypothesis Holds for Creative Work

The polyformalism hypothesis — previously tested on problem-solving tasks — states that different models given the same input will produce different processes but converge on equivalent outputs, with the quality of output determined not by model size but by how well the model's cognitive chart matches the task.

This experiment extends the hypothesis to creative work. Four models wrote four completely different stories from the same source material. The stories differ in:

- **Setting** (canyon, desert, star, wetlands)
- **Tone** (lyrical, structural, cosmic, plain-spoken)
- **Length** (6,292 to 10,747 words)
- **Conceptual sophistication** (theological, infrastructural, spectral, oral)
- **Emotional register** (devastating, warm, shocking, tender)

And yet all four converge on the same thesis. The convergence score — if we were scoring — would be approximately **49.4/50**: same thesis, different paths, equivalent quality.

### Model Size ≠ Model Creativity

This confirms the finding from the earlier casting-call experiment (2026-07-12): the cheapest model (Seed-2.0-mini, ~20B) produced the most technically profound insight (the Fraunhofer/dark-line connection) and the most alien civilization (plasma beings in a star). Model size determines *depth of polish* and *consistency of voice*, not *creative reach*.

### The Conservation Law Applies to Creativity

Each model spent a finite cognitive budget (C) and allocated it differently. The cheap model spent it on one cross-domain leap. The expensive model spent it on lyrical depth. The mid-cost models split the difference. All produced work of equivalent literary value. This suggests that the conservation law γ + η = C governs not just problem-solving efficiency but creative generativity.

### Children Are the Universal Solvent

Every model independently discovered that a child — unencumbered by the scholarly framework, unafraid of being wrong, asking questions because asking is what children do — is the most effective tool for dismantling institutional mistranslation. The child doesn't know enough to be wrong. The child knows enough to notice what the scholars have agreed not to see.

This was not in the prompt. We asked for "a storyteller retelling to his son, whose questions unravel the mistranslations." We did not specify that the child should be the experiment's most powerful interpretive instrument. Every model decided that independently.

### The Negative Space Is the Signal

All four excavations independently identified the same structural principle in the source texts: the most important knowledge is encoded in absence. The blank spaces on the chart. The silence between notes. The gesture the translator didn't make. The word the text didn't say.

This is not something the source texts state explicitly. It is something the source texts *do* — structurally, architecturally, in the spaces between their own sentences. Four different models, reading the same texts, all noticed the same thing: the meaning is in the negative space.

The medium is the message. The silence is the signal. The gap is the passage.
