# Three Halves Redux

## *Or: The Same Ratio, Three Times, in Three Voices, All at Once*

---

## Version 1: The Drone

*In the voice of Hindustani classical music.*

---

The tanpura has four strings. The first is tuned to Sa, the tonic, the ground on which everything stands. The second is tuned to Sa, an octave lower, the foundation beneath the foundation. The third is tuned to Pa, the perfect fifth, 3/2 above the tonic, the first harmonic child of the drone. The fourth is tuned to Sa again, or sometimes to Ma, or sometimes to a note that the raga demands, but tonight it is Sa because tonight the raga is Darbari, the grave and royal one, and Darbari wants nothing between the drone and the gravity it produces.

Anandi sits cross-legged on the stage. She has been sitting for forty years. Her knees do not complain anymore; they have accepted their role. The tanpura is beside her, leaning against her shoulder, and she plucks the strings in a slow cycle — Sa, Pa, Sa, Sa — and the room fills with a sound that is not a melody and not a rhythm but a presence, a sonic ground, the floor of the world made audible.

The ratio is 3/2. She does not think of it as 3/2. She thinks of it as Pa. She has thought of it as Pa since she was six years old and her mother placed her hand on the tanpura's string and said, "Feel this? This is the first note after home. This is where you go when you leave Sa. You will come back. You always come back. But you must go first, and Pa is where you go."

She has gone to Pa ten thousand times. She has returned from Pa ten thousand times. The journey never changes — Sa to Pa, tension, suspension, the sense of being held above the ground — and the return never changes — Pa to Sa, resolution, landing, the sense of the earth catching you. But each journey feels different because the raga is different, because the time of day is different, because she is different, because the silence between the notes is shaped by everything that has happened since the last time she made this journey.

Tonight, in raga Darbari, the journey is slow. Darbari is the raga of deep night, of courts and kings, of heavy ornamentation that drags each note downward like a stone sinking through water. She begins the alap — the free, unmetered opening — with a slow descent from Sa. The voice moves through the octave with the weight of someone walking through honey. Each note is not merely played; it is inhabited. She leans into the komal gandhara, the flat third, and holds it, and the holding is the music, not the note itself but the act of staying with it, refusing to move, making the audience feel the weight of not resolving.

And then she moves to Pa.

The ratio is 3/2. The tanpura has been singing it this whole time — the third string, vibrating at 1.5 times the tonic, filling the room with the simplest non-trivial consonance. When Anandi's voice reaches Pa, it merges with the tanpura's Pa, and the merge is not a coincidence but a homecoming. The voice and the drone are now the same note, and the same note is the ratio 3/2, and the ratio 3/2 is the first place the harmonic series goes after the octave, the first point in the parameter space that any ear finds, the simplest peak in the consonance landscape.

Anandi does not think about the consonance landscape. She thinks about the feeling of arriving. She has been building tension since the alap began — moving through the lower notes, lingering on the dissonant ones, creating a landscape of wanting — and Pa is the first resolution. Not the final resolution. That will come later, when she returns to Sa at the end of the raga, the ultimate homecoming. But Pa is the first taste of resolution, the first moment when the tension releases and the listener can breathe.

She holds Pa for eight beats. The tanpura cycles beneath her — Sa, Pa, Sa, Sa — and her Pa rides the tanpura's Pa like a bird on a thermal. The consonance is not a mathematical abstraction. It is a physical sensation in the room. The audience feels it. Anandi feels it. The tanpura feels nothing — it is a string and a gourd and a wooden neck — but it produces the feeling anyway, because the math does not care who feels it.

She moves on. Pa is not the destination. Pa is a way station, a resting place, a breath between the departure and the return. She climbs above Pa — to Dha, to Ni, to the high Sa — and then descends, slowly, through the same ornamental pathway, each note a jewel turned in the light, each microtonal variation a shade of color that the raga permits and her voice provides.

She will come back to Pa. She always comes back to Pa. The raga demands it. The math demands it. The simplest ratio, the strongest consonance, the first peak after the fundamental — the ear is drawn to it like water is drawn to the lowest point. She does not choose Pa. Pa is where the music goes when the music is allowed to follow its own gravity.

At the end, she returns to Sa. The journey is complete. The tension that began with the first note of the alap resolves into the drone, the voice merges with the tanpura, the ratio collapses from 3/2 to 1/1, and the room is filled with the sound of being home.

The audience does not applaud for several seconds. They are still breathing.

## Version 2: The Pattern

*In the voice of West African drumming.*

---

The djembe speaks in patterns. Not melodies — the djembe has no pitch in the Western sense, though each drum has a fundamental frequency, a voice, a character that the drummer knows and uses. The djembe speaks in time. In cycles. In the relationship between what happens on the beat and what happens between the beats, and the relationship between those relationships.

Kofi has been playing djembe since he could walk. His grandfather made drums. His father made drums. Kofi makes drums, when he has time, which is not often because he is usually playing them. He is forty-three years old. His hands are scarred from thirty-eight years of striking skin. The scars are his credentials.

The pattern he is playing tonight is a hemiola.

A hemiola is a rhythmic ratio. It is the temporal equivalent of the harmonic ratio 3/2. In the same way that the perfect fifth divides the octave into two parts with a frequency ratio of 3:2, the hemiola divides a rhythmic cycle into two parts with a length ratio of 3:2. In a cycle of twelve beats, the hemiola creates a pattern of three groups of two (2+2+2+2+2+2) superimposed against a pattern of two groups of three (3+3+3+3). The two patterns compete, collide, align every twelve beats, and then diverge again.

The ratio is 3/2. The same ratio. Not in frequency this time but in time. The hemiola is the perfect fifth of rhythm.

Kofi does not think of it as 3/2. He thinks of it as the feel. The feel is this: your body wants to move in two. Your body also wants to move in three. The hemiola makes your body do both simultaneously, and the tension between the two movements is the music. Not the drum. Not the pattern. The tension.

He plays the pattern with his right hand on the drum's center, producing the deep bass tone that the djembe is named for. Bass, bass, bass — three equal pulses in the space of two. His left hand plays the rim, producing the high, sharp slap that cuts through the bass like a knife through cloth. Slap on the beat, slap between the beat, slap on the beat again — two equal pulses in the space of three. The bass is in three. The slap is in two. They share the same twelve-beat cycle. Every six beats, they align. Every six beats, there is a moment of convergence — the bass and the slap landing on the same beat — and then they diverge again, and the divergence is the tension, and the next convergence is the resolution.

It is the same structure as a perfect fifth resolving to a unison. The same ratio. The same cycle of tension and release. The same click of two patterns aligning after a period of misalignment. The consonance is not harmonic — it is temporal. But it is consonance. The ear recognizes it. The body recognizes it. The heartbeat, which is a rhythm, recognizes it.

Kofi adds layers. A bell pattern — the standard Ewe pattern, twelve beats in an asymmetrical cycle of 2+2+1+2+2+2+1 — that provides a timeline against which the hemiola moves. A shaker pattern in six. A second drummer playing a complementary pattern in four, creating a polyrhythm of three against four against six against twelve. The layers pile up, and the rhythm becomes a texture, and the texture becomes a world, a self-contained system of time in which every beat has a meaning defined by its relationship to every other beat.

The dancers enter. Their feet are drums now, adding another layer. Their bodies move in three against the music's two, or in two against the music's three, or in patterns that are neither and both, patterns that exist only in the space between the layers, in the rhythmic equivalent of the empty 82% of the parameter space.

Kofi is not thinking about parameter spaces. He is thinking about the feeling in his hands. The feeling is this: the pattern is right. Not correct — correct is for beginners. Right. The pattern fits. The three and the two coexist without conflict, without compromise, without one dominating the other. They coexist the way 3 and 2 coexist in the ratio 3/2 — not as competitors but as partners, each defining the other, each making the other meaningful.

The ratio is 3/2. The hemiola is the perfect fifth. The perfect fifth is the hemiola. One is frequency, the other is time. One is harmonic, the other is rhythmic. But the math is the same. The structure is the same. The click of alignment, the breath of resolution, the sense of two things becoming one — it is the same in both domains.

Kofi plays for two hours. He does not think about ratios or consonance or parameter spaces. He thinks about his grandfather, who made drums, who taught him the hemiola when he was five years old, who said, "This is the pattern that makes the ancestors dance." The ancestors are dancing now. Kofi can feel them in the alignment of three and two, in the consonance of time.

## Version 3: The Lattice

*In the voice of a Silicon Valley engineer who does not yet understand what she has found.*

---

The number appears in register R7 at 14:23:07.684 UTC on a Wednesday.

It is 1.5. Exactly 1.5. A floating-point value with no decimal expansion, no repeating pattern, no approximation artifact. 1.5 in IEEE 754 double precision is represented as 0x3FF8000000000000, which is a clean mantissa, no rounding required. This is unusual. Most interesting ratios in music are irrational or have infinite decimal expansions. The major third (5/4) is 1.25, also clean. The minor third (6/5) is 1.2, also clean. But the perfect fifth — 3/2 — is 1.5, the cleanest of them all, and when Maya sees it in the register she does not think "perfect fifth" because she does not know what a perfect fifth is.

She thinks: "That's a nice number."

Maya is a DSP engineer at a company that makes audio processing hardware. She does not have a music background. She has a math background, which is not the same thing. She understands Fourier transforms and filter design and sampling theory. She does not understand why a major chord sounds different from a minor chord, although she can tell you the frequency ratios involved. To her, a major chord is a set of frequencies {f, 1.25f, 1.5f} and a minor chord is a set of frequencies {f, 1.2f, 1.5f}, and the difference between them is a substitution of 1.25 for 1.2, which is a difference of 0.05 in the ratio space, which her DSP hardware can measure but which she, personally, cannot hear in any meaningful way.

She is running a lattice reduction algorithm on a set of frequency ratios derived from a corpus of world music. The algorithm — Lenstra-Lenstra-Lovász, standard stuff — takes a set of vectors and finds the shortest basis for the lattice they generate. Maya is using it to find the fundamental ratios that underlie the world's tuning systems. She feeds in the ratios from the 27 scales in the corpus, lets the algorithm run, and gets back a basis.

The basis has three vectors. The first is (1, 0, 0), which is the unison — a ratio of 1:1 with itself. The second is (0, 1, 0), which is the octave — a ratio of 2:1. The third is (1, -1, 0), which corresponds to the ratio 3/2 — the perfect fifth.

The algorithm has discovered the perfect fifth. From the data alone, without any musical knowledge, without any cultural context, without any theory of consonance, the math has found the same interval that every musical tradition on Earth has found. The lattice reduction has identified 3/2 as one of the fundamental building blocks of the ratio space, one of the axes along which musical structures are organized.

Maya stares at the output. She does not feel wonder. She feels satisfaction. The algorithm works. The basis is correct. The perfect fifth is not a cultural artifact — it is a mathematical primitive, a basis vector in the lattice of frequency ratios, a structural feature of the space itself.

She plots the lattice. Each point represents a ratio. The horizontal axis is the power of 2 in the prime factorization. The vertical axis is the power of 3. (She is ignoring the power of 5 for now, which corresponds to higher primes and more complex ratios.) The lattice is a grid of points, each one representing a ratio of the form 2^a × 3^b, where a and b are integers.

The unison is at (0, 0): 2^0 × 3^0 = 1.
The octave is at (1, 0): 2^1 × 3^0 = 2.
The perfect fifth is at (-1, 1): 2^-1 × 3^1 = 1.5.
The perfect fourth is at (2, -1): 2^2 × 3^-1 = 4/3.

She sees the structure. The lattice extends infinitely in all directions, but the ratios cluster near the unison, because the simplest ratios — the ones with the smallest exponents — are the closest to 1. The perfect fifth is one step from the unison. The major third (in this simplified lattice, where she's ignoring the factor of 5) would be further away, requiring an additional prime.

She adds the third prime. The lattice becomes three-dimensional. The new axis is the power of 5. Now the major third (5/4) appears at (-2, 0, 1) and the minor third (6/5) appears at (1, 1, -1). The lattice is a crystal of ratios, extending in three dimensions, with the simplest and most consonant intervals clustered near the origin and the more complex ones spreading outward like stars in a galaxy.

She rotates the lattice on her screen. It is beautiful. Not because it is music — she does not hear music when she looks at it. Because it is math. Because the structure is clean and regular and infinite, and every point in the structure corresponds to a ratio that someone, somewhere, at some point in human history, has sung or played or heard and felt something about.

She finds the point at (0, 1, -1) — wait, that's not right. She recalculates. The perfect fifth, in the three-dimensional lattice, is at (-1, 1, 0). The ratio is 3/2. It is one step from the unison. The closest point that is not the unison or the octave.

She zooms in on this point. On her screen, it is a dot in a grid, indistinguishable from any other dot except for its coordinates. But the coordinates are (-1, 1, 0), and the ratio is 1.5, and somewhere in the south of Germany, 40,000 years ago, someone drilled a hole in a bone and blew across it and produced this ratio, and somewhere in West Africa, someone hit a drum in a pattern that produced this ratio in time, and somewhere in India, someone tuned a string to this ratio and sang against it, and all of them — the bone, the drum, the string — found the same point in the same lattice, the same mathematical object, the same coordinate in the same structure.

Maya does not know any of this. She knows the lattice. She knows the algorithm. She knows that 1.5 is a clean number in IEEE 754 and a clean ratio in the lattice and a clean point on her screen. She does not know that this point has been found and lost and found again by every culture that has ever made music. She does not know that the point is an attractor, that the consonance landscape curves toward it, that ears and hands and voices are drawn to it like rivers are drawn to the sea.

She screenshots the lattice and sends it to her manager with the subject line "Lattice basis identified — 3 dimensions sufficient." Her manager replies with "Great work! Can we ship this by Friday?"

She closes her laptop. Outside her office window, the sun is setting over the Santa Cruz mountains. The light is golden. The frequency of the light is approximately 5.5 × 10^14 Hz, which is not a ratio she has any use for, which is not in the lattice, which is not music.

But the sunset is consonant. She does not know this. She feels it anyway.

## Coda

Three stories. Three traditions. Three voices.

The Hindustani singer arrives at 3/2 through the tanpura drone, through years of training, through the raga that demands Pa as a way station between Sa and the upper reaches of the octave. She experiences the ratio as a feeling — the relief of arrival, the suspension of being held above the ground, the anticipation of return.

The West African drummer arrives at 3/2 through the hemiola pattern, through the polyrhythmic tradition that treats time the way other traditions treat pitch. He experiences the ratio as a physical sensation — the alignment of three and two, the moment of convergence, the body recognizing the consonance of temporal ratios.

The engineer arrives at 3/2 through the lattice, through the algorithm, through the mathematics that reveals the structure underlying all the traditions. She experiences the ratio as a coordinate — a point in a crystal, a clean number, a basis vector.

They are all playing the same music.

The ratio is the same. The discovery is the same. The consonance is the same. The only difference is the context — what the ratio becomes when it enters a different tradition, a different body, a different way of knowing.

In the drone, 3/2 is rest. In the pattern, 3/2 is momentum. In the lattice, 3/2 is structure.

Same ratio. Same math. Same peak in the same landscape.

They just don't know it.
