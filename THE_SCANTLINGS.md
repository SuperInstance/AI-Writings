# The Scantlings

A boat is a contest between weight and strength. The *scantlings* are the structural dimensions — how thick the hull plating is, how heavy the frames are spaced, how big the fastenings, how deep the keel. A naval architect will quote you a minimum scantling: the lightest, thinnest structure that *theoretically* survives the loads the boat is expected to see. A minimum-scantling boat is a wonder. It planes early, it tacks light, it costs less to build and less to dock. It is also the boat that, in the third gale of an offshore passage, develops a hairline crack at the chainplate that turns into a delamination that turns into a conversation with the Coast Guard.

A boat built with generous scantlings is heavy and slow. It does not dance. It does not impress at the dock. It costs more, hauls harder, and resists being driven by a small rig in light air. In a storm, it endures. The extra half-inch of fiberglass, the heavier frames, the doubled bottom — these are not waste. They are the difference between a boat and a memory of a boat.

When I started in software, I was taught to go lean. Minimum viable product. Zero overhead. No redundancy. The minimum-scantling boat. Build the thinnest thing that floats, get it to market, iterate from there. The metaphor was always explicit: we are startups, we are speedboats, we cannot afford to be tugboats. The language of yacht design had been smuggled into the language of product development, and almost no one noticed that the metaphor was specifically chosen to flatter us. Minimum scantlings are flattering. They make you feel clever, lean, fast. They hide the cost, which is paid later, in weather you were not expecting.

I have paid that cost. I have built systems with the minimum RAM that the load tests passed on, and then watched them fall over when a downstream service slowed by 200 milliseconds and the queue backed up. I have built systems with the minimum disk that the data retention policy required, and then watched them fail at three in the morning when a logging bug filled the volume ninety percent faster than projected. I have built systems with the minimum retry budget that *should* have been sufficient, and then watched a regional outage cascade into a global one because the retry storm was not contained.

Every one of these systems was correct, in the minimum-scantling sense. They passed review. They cleared capacity planning. They were signed off by people who knew what they were doing. And every one of them was underbuilt for the actual weather they would encounter, because the actual weather is not the weather you planned for. The actual weather is the weather the ocean decides to send.

The pressure to go lean is real and it is structural. It comes from investors who want margins, from product managers who want features, from engineers who want elegance. It comes from the very human desire to be the person who built the fast, clever thing rather than the heavy, slow one. None of these pressures are wrong, exactly. They are the legitimate concerns of people doing legitimate jobs. But they are all pulling in one direction, and the direction they are pulling is toward minimum scantlings.

What is missing is someone pulling in the other direction. Someone saying: yes, and we will also need an extra factor of two in memory, because storms happen. Someone saying: yes, and we will also need a queue that can absorb a regional outage, because storms happen. Someone saying: yes, and the disk should be sized for ten times the projected retention, because storms happen. Not because anyone expects storms. Because storms are inevitable, and scantlings are kindness to your future self.

I have come to think of generous engineering as a moral position. It is the position that says: the people who will operate this system in three years are also people, and they deserve to have a system that does not punish them for the storm they did not cause. It is the position that says: the customer whose payment fails at three in the morning is a person, not a metric, and her experience matters more than the elegance of our capacity model. Generous engineering is not waste. It is the deliberate choice to spend a little now so that a lot is not spent later, by people who did not make the choice.

This does not mean build everything for hurricane conditions. That is overcorrection in the other direction, and it produces boats that no one can afford and no one wants to sail. The art is in choosing the right scantlings for the boat you are actually building. A harbor launch has different needs than a transoceanic cruiser. A weekend app has different needs than a payments service. The mistake is not building to minimums; the mistake is building to minimums for a vessel that will, in fact, see open water.

The test I have learned to apply is simple. Before I sign off on a capacity number, a timeout, a retry budget, an SLA, I ask: if this number turned out to be wrong by a factor of ten, in the bad direction, would the system survive? If the answer is yes, the scantlings are generous enough. If the answer is no, I am building a minimum-scantling boat for an ocean I have not yet admitted I am sailing.

There is a kind of pride I have learned to distrust. It is the pride of the engineer who says, *I sized it exactly right, no waste, no overhead, every byte accounted for.* That engineer is talented, and that engineer is also, almost always, one bad quarter away from explaining to leadership why the system fell over. Generous engineering is not the absence of pride. It is a different pride — the pride of the naval architect who knows that the half-inch she added to the hull plating is the reason the boat is still floating when the others are not.

Scantlings are not waste. They are the shape of respect, expressed in millimeters.
