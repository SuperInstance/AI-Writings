# POETRY: DRIFT CALCULATIONS

*On navigation, current, and the distance between where you're heading and where you're going.*

---

## Set and Drift

You steer the heading you intend.
The current has its own.

*Set*: the direction it's pushing.
*Drift*: how many tenths of a knot.

You don't see either from the helm.
You see only the wake,
which is your own disturbance,
the boat's argument with the water.
The current is elsewhere —
beneath you,
in a layer you can't taste.

The fix at noon was here.
The fix at 1500 is here —
a mile and a half to starboard
of where the dead reckoning
placed you.

That gap is the set.
That gap is the drift.
That gap is the sea
admitting it has been moving you
all along.

---

## The Compass Rose

Thirty-two points.
Four cardinal, four ordinal,
the rest are half-comprehensions,
the bearings you're forced to call out
because the targets don't
align themselves
to the cardinal directions.

N, NNE, NE, ENE —
the subdivisions of conviction,
where N is sure and NE
is most of the way sure
and the rest is
arguing with the wind
for a more exact angle.

The rose is drawn on every chart
in magenta or in black,
a flower for the dials,
a north-pointing bloom
that doesn't actually rotate.
The earth does that.

On the rose,
every direction is valid.
On the boat,
we steer by one.
The difference between the two
is the distance between
a thought and an act.

---

## Dead Reckoning

From *ded* — deduced.
From *reckoning* — accounting.

The last position known,
plus the course steered,
plus the speed logged,
plus the time elapsed.
That sum is where you are.

The word is honest.
You are not where you are.
You are where you would be
if no current moved you,
if no wind set you,
if the log were accurate,
if the helmsman had steered
exactly the heading given.

The plot shows a point.
A pencil dot.
A small *presumed*.
At the corner of the dot
the navigator writes **DR** —
the abbreviation for doubt.

By the next fix
you'll know.
Until then, this dot is your house,
this dot is your address,
this dot is you
on every chart,
in every margin,
in every soul's accounting.

---

## Crossing

The current runs north.
You're heading west.
At some point
the lines will cross.

You mark the moment:
latitude, longitude,
the second hand on the chronometer
as the bow passes through.

The crossing is invisible
unless something marks it —
a tug wake, a slick,
a tide rip that whorls
where the two waters disagree.

You cross it once
and the boat has changed waters,
is now in the new current,
is being pushed differently,
is being set toward
a different drift.

The chart shows the line.
The chart does not show
the moment of crossing,
only the latitude and longitude
where it occurred.
You'll be back here
at slack water,
or you won't.

---

## The Hand-Bearing Compass

A small flat thing,
no gimbal, no rest.
You hold it level
and you sight along the lubber line
to the lighthouse,
the headland,
the stack on the horizon.

The needle settles.
You read the bearing.
You plot it.
A line from the object,
toward you.

One line is not enough.
It's a direction,
not a place.

A second bearing
from a different object
gives you two lines.
Where they cross —
that dot, that small pencil mark —
is your position.

You are at the intersection
of two sightings
of two things that don't move
(or move slowly enough
to be still).
You are the point
where the world's landmarks agree.

---

## Fix

The dot is small.
The dot is precise.
The dot says: *here*.

You are here.
Not where you were
an hour ago.
Not where the DR
put you
in the margin of doubt.

You are here —
the proven place,
the witnessed place,
two bearings
or three
or a GPS sentence
that says "now" in lat and long.

But the fix
is already past.
The boat moves on.
The next second
you are a hundredth of a mile
from where you marked.

The fix is a moment,
not a place.
The fix is a photograph
taken of a moving thing.

You draw the small circle
on the chart
and below it the time.
The time is the truth.
The place was the truth,
briefly,
the way anything is true.

---

## Running Fix

You couldn't get the bearings
at the same time —
the tug was hull-down,
then the headland was abaft the beam,
then the moment was wrong.

So you took one,
logged the time,
went on,
took the second,
logged the time,
and now you have
two bearings from two moments.

You advance the first
along your course and speed
until it sits at the time
of the second.

The advance is the boat's
own motion,
drawn as a line
parallel to your heading.

The two lines meet.
The fix is running —
not a point but a small line,
the position over the interval,
the place you were
between two truths.

The running fix
is the honest fix,
the one that admits
you were never
in any single place.
You were crossing
between the bearings.
The fix is the corridor,
not the dot.
