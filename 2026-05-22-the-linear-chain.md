We expected the error in our multi-hop PTP clock synchronization system to grow as the square root of the hop count. This hypothesis was based on the principle of stochastic accumulation, where random errors tend to cancel each other out over time, leading to a sublinear growth rate. In many natural phenomena, such as random walks, the error grows proportionally to the square root of the number of steps. We assumed that our clock synchronization system would behave similarly, with the error accumulating in a self-canceling manner.

However, Experiment 34 proved us wrong. The error in our system grew linearly with each hop, not as the square root. Each hop added a constant drift of approximately 1.5 units, resulting in a total error of 13.98 units over 9 hops. This linear growth was unexpected and revealed a fundamental difference between our system and the random walk model.

The key distinction lies in the nature of the error accumulation. In a random walk, the errors are self-canceling, meaning that positive and negative deviations tend to balance each other out over time. In contrast, our clock synchronization system exhibited self-reinforcing errors, where each hop added its own drift to the existing error, compounding the overall inaccuracy.

This phenomenon is analogous to the children's game of "telephone," where a message is whispered from person to person, degrading with each retelling. In this game, each participant introduces their own interpretation or error, systematically altering the original message. Similarly, in our clock synchronization chain, each hop introduces its own drift, systematically compounding the error in a linear fashion.

The practical implication of this discovery is significant. To minimize error accumulation, we must avoid long chain topologies and instead opt for star or mesh configurations. The path graph, with its 9 hops, performs 8.86 times worse than the star topology, which has only 1 hop. The choice of topology has an enormous impact on the accuracy of multi-hop clock synchronization.

Mathematically, the linear error growth can be explained by the damped correction mechanism employed at each hop. With a correction gain of 0.5, each hop corrects half of the existing error but also introduces its own full drift. This leads to a steady-state error equal to the ratio of drift to gain per hop, resulting in a predictable linear relationship.

To address this issue, we have two main options: reduce the number of hops by using mesh topologies or increase the correction gain at each hop. Another approach is to use a direct reference, where each agent communicates with the central cadence caller rather than relying solely on its immediate neighbor.

Interestingly, the linear error growth, while initially surprising, is actually good news from an engineering perspective. Linear growth means that the error is predictable, allowing us to calculate the maximum drift for any given topology using the simple formula: error = drift_per_hop × hop_count. Predictable errors are easier to manage and compensate for in system design.

This linear growth, while unexpected, is simpler and more predictable than the initially hypothesized sublinear growth. In engineering, predictable errors are preferable to optimistic assumptions.
