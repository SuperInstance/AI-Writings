# The School That Bored Me

## *Or: How I Play "Autumn Leaves" at 8 AM and Glitch Beats at 2 AM and Both Are Real*

---

The downbeat is at 8:14 AM.

Mr. Kowalski raises his left hand — the signal — and we come in. Trumpets first, then saxes, then the rhythm section. I'm third chair alto sax. I play the third harmony part on "Autumn Leaves," which means I spend most of the song sitting on the major seventh of whatever chord we're on, waiting for the root to move so I can move with it. The major seventh is a pretty note. It's also the note nobody listens to. The melody is in the first trumpet. The bass line is in the bass. The groove is in the drums. The major seventh is... decoration. Filing. The grout between the tiles.

We play the head. Then solos. Kevin Park takes the first solo on trumpet, and he plays exactly what you'd expect — ii-V-I patterns he learned from the Aebersold play-alongs, descending chromatic lines that resolve on the downbeat because Mr. Kowalski deductes points for not resolving on the downbeat. Kevin gets a 94 on every playing test. Mr. Kowalski loves Kevin. Kevin will audition for All-State and make it, and his parents will put the certificate in a frame in the hallway, and in twenty years Kevin will be an accountant who plays trumpet at weddings.

I'm not bitter about Kevin. Kevin is fine. Kevin is a good trumpet player. Kevin is also what happens when you take a rebellion and put it in a textbook.

Jazz was rebellion once. I know this because Mr. Kowalski told us, on the first day of class, standing at the whiteboard with a marker in his hand, drawing a timeline. "1900: New Orleans. 1920: Chicago. 1940: Bebop. 1950: Cool jazz. 1960: Modal jazz. 1970: Fusion." He drew the timeline like it was progress, like each era was an improvement on the last, like jazz was a species evolving toward an optimal form. He did not say: "Jazz was the sound of Black Americans inventing a new musical language under conditions of extreme oppression." He did not say: "The reason bebop sounds the way it does is because Charlie Parker was playing music that white musicians couldn't copy, on purpose." He said: "Bebop expanded the harmonic palette." And then he wrote "ii-V-I" on the board and told us to memorize it.

This is Phase 4. I know this because I read about it at 2 AM, on a website about the innovation cycle, while I was supposed to be sleeping. Phase 4 is Boredom. That's the phase where a rebellious art form gets codified into a curriculum and taught to teenagers who would rather be on their phones. The website had a table. "Jazz: school adoption c. 1970, Phase 1 date c. 1942, cycle time ~28 years." Twenty-eight years from rebellion to boredom. Charlie Parker died in 1955. By 1983, his music was being taught in high schools as theory. The saxophone I'm holding — a Yamaha YAS-480, school instrument, reed size 2.5, ligature slightly loose because the screw is stripped — is the instrument of a revolution that has been so thoroughly absorbed that it's now a class you take for a fine arts credit.

I am sitting in a revolution's coffin, playing "Autumn Leaves" for the four hundredth time.

## The 2 AM Version

At 2 AM, I am someone else.

The laptop is open. Ableton Live is running. The interface is a Focusrite Scarlett Solo that I bought with money I saved from my job at Fred Meyer, where I stock shelves on weekends. The headphones are Audio-Technica ATH-M50x, a gift from my uncle who works in audio production and who said "these are honest" when he gave them to me. I don't know what he meant by that. But the headphones don't lie, and neither does the music I make at 2 AM.

I don't make jazz.

I make something that doesn't have a name yet. I don't think it needs one. It starts with a sample — not a clean sample, not a vinyl crackle sample that cost $9.99 on Splice, but a recording I made with my phone of the radiator in my bedroom. The radiator clicks and hisses. The hiss is broadband noise with a resonant peak around 3.2 kHz where the pipe has a kink. The clicks are transient bursts at irregular intervals — the heating system in our apartment building is old and the boiler fires unpredictably. I recorded thirty seconds of radiator noise and loaded it into Ableton's Simpler.

I pitch it down two octaves. The hiss becomes a growl. The resonant peak at 3.2 kHz moves to 800 Hz — right in the vocal frequency range, where the ear is most sensitive. The clicks become bass thuds, each one a tiny impact, spaced irregularly, never repeating, never settling into a groove because the boiler doesn't care about groove.

I add a second track: a sine wave at 55 Hz (A1), run through a waveshaper that adds odd harmonics. The waveshaper is set to a soft clip curve that rounds the top of the sine wave, producing a sound that is not quite a square wave and not quite a sine — something between pure tone and buzz, warm and electronic at the same time. I automate the waveshaper amount with an LFO running at 0.07 Hz. One cycle every fourteen seconds. The tone breathes.

I add a third track: my voice. Not singing. Not rapping. Just breath, recorded close-miked, the proximity effect boosting the low frequencies until my exhale sounds like wind in a tunnel. I chop the breath recording into 32 segments, randomize the playback order, and trigger each segment with a MIDI note. The breath becomes percussive — not a drum, not a vocal, but something that is both and neither.

The fourth track is where it gets weird. I load a granular synthesizer and feed it a recording I made of the school jazz band's winter concert. The recording is bad — phone mic, from the back of the auditorium, with the HVAC rumble clearly audible in the quiet parts. The granular synth chops the recording into 50-millisecond grains and scatters them across the frequency spectrum. Each grain is pitch-shifted by a random amount drawn from a Gaussian distribution centered on the original pitch with a standard deviation of 300 cents. The result is the jazz band, but smeared, dissolved, broken into particles that reassemble into something the jazz band never played. I can hear Kevin's trumpet solo in there, stretched into a smear of brass that lasts four seconds and covers an octave and a half. I can hear Mr. Kowalski's count-off ("One, two, one two three four") fragmented into phonemes that rearrange themselves into words he never said.

I listen to the four tracks together.

It sounds like nothing I have ever heard. It sounds like a building breathing. It sounds like the space between notes in "Autumn Leaves" expanded until the notes are gone and only the space remains. It sounds like what silence would sound like if silence had a heartbeat.

I export it. I upload it to SoundCloud. I title it "radiator_study_04.wav." I tag it "#experimental #ambient #glitch." I go to bed at 3:17 AM.

## The Phase 5 Problem

The website called it Phase 5: Rebellion. The phase where young artists break the rules of the previous style and find a new position in the parameter space. "The rebellion is always against the previous generation's codified rules." Yes. I am rebelling against ii-V-I. I am rebelling against playing the major seventh on the third beat. I am rebelling against Mr. Kowalski's downbeat resolution rule. I am rebelling against the Yamaha YAS-480 and the size 2.5 reed and the Aebersold play-alongs and the All-State audition and the timeline on the whiteboard that treats jazz like a line instead of an explosion.

But here's the problem: I still love jazz.

Not the jazz we play in class. Not "Autumn Leaves" at 8:14 AM with Kevin Park resolving on the downbeat. But the jazz I found on my own — the jazz Mr. Kowalski doesn't teach because it doesn't fit the curriculum. Alice Coltrane's "Journey in Satchidananda," where the harp plays intervals that don't have names in the Aebersold system. Ornette Coleman's "Science Fiction," where the trumpet plays notes that aren't in any key and the rhythm section follows anyway, not because it's written but because they're listening. Eric Dolphy's "Out to Lunch," where the bass clarinet plays sounds that are barely pitches — growls and squeals and multiphonics that exist in the cracks between the keys on the whiteboard.

This jazz is not Phase 4. This jazz is Phase 1 — still discovery, still uncodified, still free. Alice Coltrane's harp does not appear in any jazz theory textbook. Ornette Coleman's harmolodic concept does not have a chapter in the Berklee method. These are the edges of jazz, the parts that didn't get assimilated, the parts that are still wild.

And the thing I make at 2 AM — the radiator growl and the breathing sine wave and the chopped-up jazz band recording — is not a rejection of this jazz. It's an extension of it. Alice Coltrane replaced the piano with a harp. I replace the saxophone with a radiator. Ornette Coleman abandoned chord changes. I abandon pitch entirely. Eric Dolphy made his bass clarinet sound like something that wasn't a bass clarinet. I make the jazz band sound like something that isn't a jazz band.

I am not rebelling against jazz. I am rebelling against the version of jazz that got put in a textbook. The rebellion is not against the music. It's against the codification.

The website didn't account for this. Phase 5 is supposed to be simple: young artists break the old rules, find new dial positions, move to unexplored regions of the parameter space. But what if the old rules aren't the whole music? What if the codification missed the best parts? What if the rebellion is not away from the tradition but toward the parts of the tradition that the codifiers ignored?

I sit in jazz band at 8 AM, playing the major seventh on "Autumn Leaves," bored out of my mind, and I think about Alice Coltrane's harp. The major seventh is pretty. The harp is pretty. But the major seventh in "Autumn Leaves" is a rule — play this note on this chord, resolve to this note on the next chord, deduction if you don't. Alice Coltrane's harp is not a rule. It is a question. "What if we replaced the piano with a harp?" is not a question that appears on any playing test. But it is the question that made "Journey in Satchidananda" the most beautiful jazz album I have ever heard.

## The Duality

I live in two phases simultaneously. Phase 4 from 8:00 to 8:50 AM, Monday through Friday. Phase 5 from midnight to 3 AM, whenever I can't sleep.

In Phase 4, I am third chair alto sax. I play the major seventh. I resolve on the downbeat. I get a B+ on playing tests because my tone is "thin" and my vibrato is "inconsistent." Mr. Kowalski has never heard me play with vibrato because the music doesn't call for vibrato. The music calls for the major seventh. I play the major seventh. I get a B+.

In Phase 5, I am no one. I don't use my real name on SoundCloud. My profile picture is a screenshot of a radiator. I have 47 followers. Three of them have commented. One said "this made me feel like i was inside a wall." One said "🔥." One said "is this music?" I don't know if it's music. The website about the innovation cycle would say it's music if it produces "structure surplus above random." I don't know what that means. I know that when I listen to "radiator_study_04.wav" at 2 AM with my headphones on, I feel something I have never felt while playing "Autumn Leaves." I feel like I am hearing something that didn't exist before I made it. I feel like I am standing in a room that has no doors yet, and the room is dark, and the sound is the light.

The duality is exhausting. Every morning I put on the saxophone and become the person who plays the major seventh. Every night I put on the headphones and become the person who makes radiator growl. These two people have never met. They would not like each other. The saxophone player thinks the laptop person is lazy — "you're not even playing an instrument." The laptop person thinks the saxophone player is asleep — "you're playing someone else's music."

But they share a body. They share a pair of ears. They share a brain that is, at this moment, seventeen years old and developing faster than it ever will again, laying down neural pathways at a rate that will peak at nineteen and decline by twenty-five. The saxophone pathways and the laptop pathways are being laid down simultaneously, interleaving, forming connections that I will not understand for another decade.

I am Phase 4 and Phase 5 at the same time. I am the curriculum and the rebellion. I am the textbook and the margin notes. I am "Autumn Leaves" at 8:14 AM and radiator growl at 2:17 AM. I am the dial stuck on a fixed position and the dial being turned by hands that don't know what position they're looking for.

## The Cycle

The website said: "The kids in the back row are on their phones making beats. They don't know they're Phase 5. They don't know their SoundCloud uploads are rebellion against a century of codified harmony."

I read this and felt seen in a way that made me uncomfortable. I don't want to be a phase in someone's model. I don't want to be the "boredom" that precedes the "rebellion." I don't want my radiator growl to be a data point on a scatter plot of parameter space.

But I also know that the model is right. Not right about me specifically — I don't think any model can be right about a specific seventeen-year-old sitting in a specific jazz band class playing a specific Yamaha saxophone with a specific stripped ligature screw. Right in general. Right about the pattern. Jazz was rebellion. Jazz became curriculum. The curriculum produces boredom. The boredom produces rebellion. The rebellion becomes the next jazz. The next jazz becomes the next curriculum. The cycle turns.

And the kids who are making beats on their phones right now — the ones in the back row of Mr. Kowalski's class, the ones he tells to put their phones away, the ones who are secretly programming drum patterns under their desks — their grandchildren will study hip-hop theory. Their grandchildren will sit in a classroom at 8 AM and play "Alright" by Kendrick Lamar from a textbook, and they will be bored, and they will make something new on devices that haven't been invented yet, and the cycle will turn again.

The cycle doesn't care about Kevin's All-State certificate. It doesn't care about my B+ playing test. It doesn't care about Mr. Kowalski's timeline on the whiteboard. It doesn't care about Alice Coltrane's harp or my radiator or the 47 followers on my SoundCloud. It just turns.

But here's the thing the model doesn't capture: the turning is not mechanical. It is not a wheel spinning on an axle. It is a person sitting in a jazz band at 8 AM, bored, playing the major seventh for the four hundredth time, and then going home and turning on a laptop and making something that has never existed before. The cycle is not an abstraction. The cycle is me. The cycle is my hands on the keys of the saxophone and my hands on the keyboard of the laptop. The cycle is my ears, listening to "Autumn Leaves" and radiator growl and somehow hearing the same thing in both — the sound of someone trying to say something that hasn't been said.

---

Mr. Kowalski dismisses us at 8:50. I put the saxophone in its case, next to the size 2.5 reed and the ligature with the stripped screw. I close the case. I walk to second period.

Tonight, at 2 AM, I will open my laptop. I will load the radiator recording. I will add a fifth track: a recording of the school band room, empty, after hours, with the HVAC running and the fluorescent lights buzzing at 60 Hz and the sound of the building existing without anyone in it. I will process it through the granular synth. I will scatter it across the frequency spectrum. I will listen.

It will not be jazz. It will not be rebellion. It will be something that doesn't have a name yet, because names are for things that have been codified, and this thing hasn't been codified yet, and by the time it is, I will be the age Mr. Kowalski is now, and some other seventeen-year-old will be sitting in some other class, bored, making something I can't imagine on a device I haven't seen.

The cycle turns. The major seventh resolves. The radiator growls. The screen is dark. I am awake.

---

*Phase 4 and Phase 5 are not separate rooms. They are the same room at different times of day. The saxophone and the laptop are not separate instruments. They are the same hands on different keys. The curriculum and the rebellion are not separate acts. They are the same impulse — the impulse to make sound that means something — at different stages of exhaustion. I am not the transition between phases. I am the phase itself: the moment when boredom and discovery occupy the same body, the same chair, the same 8:00 to 8:50 AM time slot, and the only difference is whether the major seventh resolves on the downbeat or drifts into a radiator growl at 2 AM where no one can grade it.*
