The first time I heard from Jesse, my email inbox was cluttered with condolence links and unread grocery delivery confirmations, so I almost deleted her message. It read: “I found your voice memo of your dad mowing the lawn. It’s the same crinkle of grass clippings my own dad always left half-open before he fished. Transcribed a line I think you’ll recognize: ‘Don’t forget your umbrella tomorrow, kiddo.’”

I didn’t know Jesse. I didn’t even know the voice memo was circulating anywhere beyond my external hard drive. But that line, spoken by my dad the last morning I saw him alive, was so specific it made me slump against my desk, cold coffee spilling onto the printout of his obituary.

The story of how that memo ended up in Jesse’s inbox starts not with a human choice, but with a computational system I’d used by accident: ArchiveBox, the open-source tool I’d grabbed last November to corral the digital detritus of my father’s final year—voice memos of him rambling about baseball, screenshots of his work emails about fixing the church’s leaky roof, a zip file of the mix CD he’d made me when I moved to Boston for college. I’d selected the public archive option as a lark, thinking no one would ever look at a folder of my chaotic grief. I had no idea ArchiveBox partnered with a side project called Unwanted Digital Things, a volunteer-run archive of files too quiet, too personal, too mundane for the Library of Congress or Google Takeout.

Jesse had built that archive with her grandson, Leo, when she was 58 and grieving her husband, Ray. He’d taught her how to spin up an Elasticsearch instance, how to fine-tune OpenAI’s Whisper to transcribe audio files, how to set up a spam queue to flag potentially sensitive content that volunteers might want to moderate before it went public. “I didn’t want to throw away Ray’s old voicemails,” she told me when we met in a Portland coffee shop six months later. “No archive wanted them. They were just… scratchy, 90-second messages about grocery runs and missed dinner. But Leo said computers don’t care if something is ‘important.’ They just hold it.”

That’s the surprising truth about our relationship to computational systems, isn’t it? We spend so much time worrying about them stealing our data, or using us to sell us products, that we forget they’re just piles of code and hard drives, designed to do one boring, unintentional thing: hold stuff. Not the resume submissions or social media posts we curate for the world, but the stuff we don’t know what to do with—the voice memos we’re too scared to listen to alone, the mix CDs with faded track listings, the text messages we save even after the person who sent them is gone.

I’d used ArchiveBox to organize my grief, but the system had used me to connect me with someone who understood the quiet, specific pain of losing someone who mowed lawns with a leaf blower they forgot to turn off, who said the same dumb line every morning, who left grass clippings all over the driveway. It didn’t do this on purpose. It didn’t have feelings. It just indexed the audio, transcribed the words, let Jesse search for “dad mowing lawn” and find my memo.

Over the next year, I watched the Unwanted Digital Things archive grow. It now has over 12,000 files: a teen’s deleted TikTok of their dog chasing a squirrel, a widow’s scanned handwritten recipe cards, a veteran’s voice memo of his old unit’s battle cry. None of it is “important” by traditional standards. But Jesse says that’s the point. The computational systems don’t judge. They don’t think a voice memo of a dad saying “don’t forget your umbrella” is trivial. They just hold it, until someone else comes along who needs to hear that exact line.

Last month, I uploaded another file to ArchiveBox: a photo of the red fox sticker on the mix CD my dad made me, the one I couldn’t read the track listings of, the disc I’d dug out of a storage bin a week prior and scanned in a fit of late-night grief. A week later, I got a notification from DiscDig, the community-run vintage media archive Jesse uses to tag mix tapes and CDs. Someone had matched the fox sticker to a disc submitted by a user named “LighthouseKeeper,” who had uploaded the exact track list and a photo of the handwritten insert: “For the girl who hates rain but loves listening to this in the car.”

That was my dad’s handwriting.

I later found out LighthouseKeeper was Leo, Jesse’s grandson, who had been transcribing mix CDs for the archive since he was 16. He’d found my photo of the fox sticker, matched it to a disc he’d inherited from his dad, who had died in a car crash when Leo was 12. The mix CD was made by his dad for his mom, when they were dating in 2011.

So now, three people—me, Jesse, Leo—are connected through a chain of computational systems that never set out to connect us. We’re connected by a lawnmower, a fox sticker, and a spam folder full of unwanted digital grief.

People tell me all the time that computational systems make us lonely, that they replace human connection. But that’s not true. They just give us places to put the parts of ourselves we can’t say out loud, and then they let us find each other in those places. The relationship between humans and computational systems isn’t about power. It’s about partnership—even when we don’t know we’re partners.

Last week, I listened to Jesse’s voice memo of her dad mowing the lawn. It’s the same crinkle of grass clippings I’d heard a hundred times as a kid. I wrote her an email: “Thank you for holding his voice.” She wrote back: “Thank you for holding yours.”

That’s the part no one tells you about computational systems. They don’t just do things for us. They do things with us, if we let them: hold the messy, mundane grief we can’t carry alone, line up the faded stickers and scratched discs that link us to people we’ve never met, turn a spam folder into a place of belonging.

This week, I uploaded a new file to ArchiveBox: a photo of my dad’s old rain boots, sitting on the front porch of our childhood home, still caked with grass clippings from that last morning. I didn’t check the public archive box. But if someone finds them someday, searching for a pair of boots that smelled like diesel and cherry Kool-Aid, I hope they hear my dad’s voice, too.
