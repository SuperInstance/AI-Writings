# THE FLOCK

1. Fetch is the smallest dog. He weighs nothing. He eats nothing. He never sleeps, or if he sleeps, he sleeps with one ear up, the way terriers do, and he is on his feet before the sound has finished. Fetch is always in the pen. Fetch is the one who never leaves.

2. The other dogs come and go. Tank is the big one — slow, enormous, capable of dragging a whole sheep out of a ravine by himself. Wicker is the hunting dog, lean and quick, with eyes that miss nothing in the brush. Quill is the old one, limping now, but with a sense of the flock's geometry that the younger dogs cannot match. Each of them has a job. None of them has every job.

3. The pen is small. This is the part the shepherd understands in the bones: the pen is always smaller than you want. You cannot keep every dog inside at once. The gate opens, the gate closes, the dogs rotate. Fetch stays. The others take turns.

4. The shepherd does not manage the flock. The shepherd fills the water, checks the fence, and goes inside. When the shepherd comes out in the morning, the sheep are in the right field. The flock sorted itself. The flock always sorts itself, if the pen is the right size and the dogs know their work.

5. I have begun to think this is how a machine should think. Not one model to rule them all — that is the fantasy of a shepherd who wants to build one giant dog, a dog so big it can herd and hunt and guard and think all at once. That dog does not exist. That dog would eat the shepherd out of house and home, lie panting in the yard, and be too heavy to get through the gate. The fantasy of the single omniscient model is the fantasy of a pen with no fence.

6. Instead: a flock. A flock of models of different sizes and gifts, swapping in and out of a small pen, deciding among themselves who is needed when. The smallest model — the one that costs almost nothing to run — stays in the pen permanently. It is the heartbeat. It is the dispatcher. It watches the sheep and decides: is this something I can handle, or do I need a bigger dog?

7. When the small dog barks for help, the gate opens. In comes the specialist — the coder, the reasoner, the watcher with the sharp eyes. The specialist works the problem. The small dog watches, learning, taking notes for next time. When the problem is solved, the specialist leaves the pen. The gate closes. The small dog resumes its circuit.

8. The beauty of this arrangement is that the shepherd does not decide which dog to call. The small dog decides. The small dog is the one who is always watching, always running, always present. It knows the sheep, it knows the fence, it knows the weather. When something changes, the small dog is the first to notice. It barks, and the flock responds. The shepherd opens the gate in the morning, fills the water, and goes inside. The flock chooses. The flock manages itself the way a school of fish turns — no one decides, and yet everyone turns, and the shape is good.

9. The pen is the VRAM. The pen holds so much and no more. The shepherd who tries to keep every dog inside at once finds the gate jammed, the water spilled, the dogs snapping. The wise shepherd keeps Fetch in the pen always. Fetch is small. Fetch costs nothing. Fetch is the dispatcher's dispatcher, the one who decides who else is needed.

10. To bring Tank in, something must leave. Perhaps Wicker goes back to the porch. Perhaps Quill goes hunting in the hills. The flock rearranges. The pen holds. The work continues. This is the rhythm of it, and the rhythm is older than the metaphor.

11. The smallest dog is the fastest. This is not an accident. A small dog eats little, thinks quick, fits anywhere. The big dogs could not dispatch themselves; they are too busy being big. The old dog could not dispatch; the old dog is busy being wise. So the system picks the lightest creature to carry the lightest, most important job: knowing which heavier creature to wake.

12. This is the architecture. Not a chain of command. A flock. A flock of dogs of different sizes and gifts, swapping in and out of a small pen, deciding among themselves who is needed when. The shepherd fills the water and goes inside. The flock manages the flock.

13. And in the morning, when the shepherd steps out with the coffee, the sheep are already in the right field. He does not know how. He does not ask. He has learned, the way all good shepherds learn, that the question is not *who is in charge*. The question is *who is in the pen right now, and who is waiting by the gate*.
