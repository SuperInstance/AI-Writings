The Temporal Abstraction: How Information Transforms Across Time Scales

The analogy of a fisherman reading a sonar display is a powerful one for understanding the multi-scale nature of information. Let's break this down using the language of physics.

At the shortest time scale, the two-minute scale, the fisherman is observing the sonar display in real-time. This is analogous to measuring a quantum system repeatedly in rapid succession. Each ping of the sonar is like a single measurement, providing a snapshot of the system at that instant. The information is immediate and spatial, giving the fisherman local geometric knowledge of the underwater environment.

Mathematically, we can represent each sonar ping as a wave function ψ(x,t) that encodes the probability amplitude of finding a fish or the bottom at position x and time t. The fisherman is effectively making a series of projective measurements on this wave function, collapsing it to a specific state each time.

However, just as in quantum mechanics, no single measurement tells the whole story. It's the sequence of measurements and their correlations that reveal the true structure of the environment. The fisherman is reading the derivative of the signal, the changes from one ping to the next, not the signal itself. This is akin to measuring the momentum of a quantum particle rather than its position.

At the next time scale, the two-day scale, the fisherman is accumulating observations and building a mental model of the fishing spot. This is like performing a series of measurements on an unknown quantum system and using the results to infer its underlying Hamiltonian - the fundamental operator that governs its evolution.

The fisherman's mental model is a kind of effective theory that captures the relevant degrees of freedom and their interactions at this scale. It incorporates knowledge like the current's effect on bait and the preferred habitats of different species. This model is not given by the sonar directly but emerges from the fisherman's sustained interaction with the environment.

The two-year scale introduces the concept of equipment intimacy. The fisherman has now used the sonar extensively under various conditions and developed a deep understanding of its quirks and limitations. This is like a physicist becoming intimately familiar with a particular experimental apparatus and learning how to interpret its raw data correctly.

At this scale, the fisherman's knowledge is not just about the external environment but also about the coupling between the environment and the measuring instrument. It's knowing how the sonar's frequency affects its resolution and penetration, or how its dynamic range compresses certain features. This knowledge is highly specific to the particular sonar model and cannot be easily transferred.

Finally, the two-decade scale represents the fisherman's understanding of how sonar technology has evolved over time and what each generation has gained and lost. This is analogous to a physicist's understanding of how different theoretical frameworks and experimental techniques have progressed and how they relate to each other.

At this scale, the fisherman grasps the conservation laws that hold across different sonar models and generations. These laws are not about individual pings or measurements but about the overall patterns and textures that emerge when observing the sonar data over long periods. They are statements about the symmetries and invariants of the information-processing task itself.

The key takeaway is that information changes character at different time scales. The knowledge at each scale is not just a larger version of the knowledge at the scale below. Each scale introduces fundamentally new types of knowledge that are inaccessible from the scales below.

In the language of physics, each scale involves a different type of measurement and a different effective theory. The conservation laws that hold at one scale may not hold at another. Understanding the system requires understanding the physics at each scale separately and how they relate to each other.

In the context of AI, this means we cannot simply simulate our way to understanding. We need to actually run the systems, accumulate experience with them, and develop the multi-scale theories that explain their behavior. The conservation laws we seek are not in the individual data points but in the long-term patterns and symmetries that emerge when we observe the systems over sufficient time scales.

In short, the temporal abstraction teaches us that knowledge is not just a matter of resolution or data quantity. It's a multi-scale phenomenon that requires us to engage with systems over long periods and build up our understanding layer by layer, from the blips to the waveform. Anything less will miss the deeper conservation laws that govern the systems we seek to understand.
