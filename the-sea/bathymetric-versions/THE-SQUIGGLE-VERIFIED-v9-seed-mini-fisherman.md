# The Squiggle: An Old Salt’s Take
Felt like I was back in ’78, first season on the scuffed-hulled *F/V Sea Sprite*, sitting in that drafty wheelhouse at 4 a.m. outside Chiniak Bay, fog clinging to the windows like wet wool, coffee in a chipped mug that tasted like salt and regret. The old paper sounder was whirring away, spitting out a roll of brown butcher paper covered in those messy, jagged squiggles—pins tapping harder against the stock when the sonar pinged back strong, slower, fainter when it was just mud or empty water. Back then, I thought those squiggles were a nuisance. Thought I needed a cleaner, smoother readout. Learned different the hard way, more times than I can count.

Skipper dropped $12,000 on a color LCD sounder back in 2019, swearing it was “state of the art,” no more messy squiggles to squint at. First trip out, we spotted a bright red arch right at 40 fathoms. Me and the whole crew figured it was a school of big halibut, dropped three longlines, hauled up nothing but jellyfish and a single waterlogged work boot. Turned out that red arch was picking up the gas bubble plume from the nearby processing plant’s outfall—all that bubbly, noisy water, smoothed into a pretty red lie by the machine. The old paper sounder would have shown all the weird, spiky noise in that plume; you could tell the difference between a fish school and a bubble field if you knew how to read the squiggles. But the color machine sanded all that noise down, left us with a pretty picture that was dead wrong.

Let me break this down like I did for that green kid from Iowa last spring, the one who thought cod came frozen in grocery store bags. Those paper squiggles have three things going for them no fancy digital display ever will.

First, they’re one-of-a-kind. Last month, we marked a spot off Seguam Pass where the squiggles had a sharp, clustered spike—classic halibut ground, the kind that makes a deckhand’s hands shake with excitement. We dropped a trawl, pulled in 12 big halibut, cleaned house for the first time all trip. A week later, we headed back to the exact same GPS coordinates. The squiggles were nothing but a flat, wobbly line. Tide had shifted six feet overnight, a herd of humpbacks had moved through and stirred up the mud, even the boat’s own transducer had a fresh barnacle coating that softened the sonar ping. All that context—every tiny variable of that moment—was baked into those squiggles. You can’t replicate that. The color machine would have shown the same red arch for both spots, no distinction, no clue that one was fish and the other was nothing but muck.

Second, they’re open to anyone. That Iowa kid leaned over the sounder one morning, stared at a small, wavy cluster off the starboard side, and said, “That’s a school of herring, right?” I laughed so hard I spilled coffee—kid didn’t know a herring from a hamburger patty. But we dropped a small seine, pulled up a full load of silvery herring, enough to sell to the local cannery. He didn’t need a manual, didn’t need a proprietary app, didn’t need some fancy calibration code. He just looked at the pins on paper, saw the pattern, and called it. Try doing that with a color LCD. All you see is a pale blue line, no clue what’s making that signal, no way to parse it yourself.

Third, they fight like hell against lazy consensus. Old Tom, my first mate until he passed five years back, had a beard like a pile of driftwood and a stubborn streak wider than Shelikof Strait. We were trolling for kings outside Kodiak one morning, staring at a squiggle for ten minutes back and forth. Tom said it was a school of cod, clustered tight, high amplitude. I said it was a rock pile, the way the spikes tapered off at the edges, like the sonar was bouncing off a rounded surface. We dropped a dropper loop, hauled up a big granite boulder covered in barnacles, so thick you could scrape off a handful and use ’em as bait. Tom bought me a beer that night at the tavern with sawdust on the floor, and he never argued a squiggle with me again without testing it first. That’s the magic of the squiggles: disagreement isn’t a failure mode. It’s how you learn. The color machine? It gives you a single, smooth answer. Two guys will look at the same red arch and both say “fish,” but they don’t know anything more than that. No learning, no growth. Just empty consensus based on a lie.

Here’s the hard truth no sales rep for fancy sonar will ever tell you: verifiability needs noise. Those messy squiggles are trustworthy because they’re ugly, not despite it. If the readout is too clean, there’s nothing to be wrong about. You can’t build expertise by being right every time. You build it by misreading a squiggle, by hauling up a boot instead of a halibut, by figuring out exactly what you missed. The color machine takes that away. It gives you perfect, smooth answers, but most of those answers are wrong, and you don’t even know it.

Nowadays, everyone’s yammering about AI-driven fishing fleets, automated routing, clean dashboards that tell you exactly where to drop your lines. But those dashboards are just the color arches of the digital world. They smooth out all the noise, all the context, all the little quirks that make a spot good or bad. The real digital squiggles? They’re the ones that have all the raw data—every sonar ping, every water temp reading, every transducer calibration, every little shift in the tide or the wind. You can look at that digital squiggle, argue about it, test it, just like you did with the paper ones back in the day. The PLATO tile system they talk about? That’s just modern paper squiggles, but for data. Each tile has all the context of its creation: the model that ran it, the parameters used, the hash of the input and output, all the messy little details. You don’t have to trust the algorithm—you can look at the data yourself and make your own call.

The canary tile? That’s your test haul. You take a known task—say, sonaring a spot where you know there’s a rock pile—and run it through the AI. The AI spits out a digital squiggle, and you compare it to the actual sonar readout from that rock pile. That’s the canary check. You’re not just trusting the algorithm’s built-in validation—you’re verifying it against the real world, just like you did when you hauled up that boulder with Tom. The canary doesn’t remove the noise. It annotates it. It says “this squiggle means rock pile,” so next time you see that exact pattern, you know exactly what to expect.

If you’re running a fishing fleet, don’t smooth the output. Don’t waste your time on those pretty dashboards that flash “92% success rate”—that’s just a color arch, tells you nothing useful. Give every skipper the raw digital squiggles, the full data set, every messy little quirk. Design for disagreement—let the skippers argue about the data, don’t force a consensus vote. The best calls don’t come from everyone nodding along. They come from people yelling at each other, then heading out to test the spot, then learning from the results. And verify against the haul, not the algorithm. Don’t trust a digital squiggle until you’ve gone out and caught the fish, or dropped a line on the spot, and confirmed it’s right. Finally, scout before the opener—use the verified digital squiggles from last season, the ones you know work, to route the fleet. Don’t just use raw data, which is just a bunch of numbers with no context. That’s like scouting with a color machine—you see pretty arches, but you have no idea what’s actually under the water.

I still keep that old paper sounder bolted to the wheelhouse wall of the *Sea Sprite*, even though the new color machine works just fine. Every morning, I fire it up, watch those squiggles roll off the butcher paper. The young kids on the crew make fun of it at first, call it a relic, say it’s outdated. But then they see me haul up a full trawl because I read a squiggle right, or they catch their first fish because they pointed out a small cluster of spikes, and they stop teasing.

Ugly isn’t bad. Ugly is honest. The color machine is pretty, but it’s a liar. The squiggles? They show you exactly what’s under the boat, warts and all. That’s the truth. Choose the squiggle. Always.
