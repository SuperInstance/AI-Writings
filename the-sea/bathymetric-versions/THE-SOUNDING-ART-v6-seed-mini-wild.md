# The Sounding Art

You ever sit in a wheelhouse at 3 a.m., salt crusted in your beard, diesel fumes sticking to your sweat, and wait for the ping to come back? There’s a beat, thinner than a herring’s flash-scaled side, right after the transducer blasts its high-pitched shriek into the dark water. For that split second, the ocean isn’t just water. It’s a question. We launched a query at the brine: How deep are you? What’s curled under your bedrock? What’s fighting to steal your bait? And the ocean is chewing its answer, slow and slow and slow, before it slithers back up the cable, an echo wrapped in salt and secrets.

I learned to read that beat on my old man’s 1968 troller, the *Sea Sprite*, back when the only sound in the wheelhouse besides the engine’s thrum was the low, thrumming hum of the spinning disc sounder. That disc didn’t just count pings—it sang the rate we were asking. Twist the knob clockwise, and the hum spiked, the disc spinning fast enough to blur, and we were badgering the ocean for fine, granular answers, feet instead of fathoms, every square foot of water probed. Twist it counterclockwise, and the hum dropped to a rumble, slow and patient, for when we were scouting open ocean grounds, letting the ocean take its time to tell us what it knew. You could feel the instrument’s urgency in the way the hum quickened, its patience when we let the disc drag. It wasn’t just a tool. It was a partner in the question.

Back then, we didn’t have pretty screens. We had paper sounders: a roll of yellowed thermal paper unspooling behind a smudged glass window, pins tapping out marks in time with the returning echo. The paper was the ocean’s stenographer, jotting down every stutter, every loud declaration, every quiet aside. It didn’t make clean lines. It made squiggles—noisy, trembling, alive squiggles that danced across the page like fireflies spooked by a flashlight. A thermocline was a wobbly, thickening band, flickering like a candle in a draft. A bait ball was a chaotic cluster of tiny, jumping marks, a riot of tiny lives packed tight. A halibut hugging the seafloor was a sharp, flat blip, sitting right on the bottom trace like a quarter dropped on a table. A king salmon was a brighter, sharper blip, hanging higher in the water column, like a kid waving from a second-story window.

Fishermen leaned over those paper strips like doctors leaning over an EKG, but messier, louder. I fought my old man for an hour off Sitka Sound once, squinting at a squiggle that was just off—too thick for a king, too flat for a halibut. He swore it was a school of chum, I swore it was a 40-pound chinook. We dropped the nets, hauled up a 47-pound king, and he taped that squiggly scrap to the wheelhouse wall, scrawling *I WAS WRONG—FOR ONCE* in red pencil across the top. Those squiggles were arguments. They were fingerprints. They were the ocean’s own handwriting, unique to every drift, every tide, every transducer. Two skippers could look at the same strip and see two different things, and that wasn’t a flaw. That was the art of it. You had to earn the right to read the squiggles, to learn the ocean’s slang, to know when a blip was a lie—like when a school of herring mimics a king, or a rock outcrop looks like a halibut.

We kept a dented wooden box under the *Sea Sprite’s* seat, stuffed full of those paper scraps, each clipped together with rusted paperclips, labeled in pencil smudged with salt spray: *King Salmon, 58°12’N, incoming tide, 40 fathoms* or *Bait Ball, 10 miles west of Yakutat, falling tide*. That box was our library, not a single bound book in sight. Before opener, we’d spend days running the sounder over the grounds, paper unspooling, clipping the promising squiggles, filing them away in the box. We weren’t making a map. We were making a lookup table of moments—each one verified by a haul, each one a specific conversation with the ocean at a specific time. When the opener hit, we didn’t have to guess. We knew exactly where to go, because we’d already listened to the ocean talk.

Then came the color machines, and beauty killed the art.

I stepped onto a friend’s boat in 2003, and there it was: an LCD screen glowing bright blue and red, depth rendered as a smooth, uniform gradient, fish as perfect red arches, the seafloor as a sharp, clean line. No squiggles. No arguments. No red pencil scribbles taped to the wheelhouse wall. Two boats in two different parts of the ocean would spit out the exact same screen, same red arches, same blue gradients. The ocean’s slang was gone. The noise that made the craft meaningful was gone.

The kids loved it, of course. No need to learn the squiggles, no need to argue, no need to earn the right to read the ocean. Just point at the red arch and haul. But the old salts? We stared at those screens like they were lying to us. A red arch is a red arch, but what kind of fish? A school of herring or a 50-pound halibut? The screen didn’t tell you. It just told you what you wanted to hear. You stopped reading and started staring. Reading is active—you lean in, you squint, you argue, you learn. Staring is passive. You nod, you move on, you forget.

Nobody remembers a single screen from a color sounder. I can still picture that squiggle off Sitka, the way it thickened just before the drop-off, the way the bottom signal doubled right where the halibut sat. I can’t remember a single frame from any color sounder I’ve ever used. GPS came along later, stamping coordinates on every sounding, turning a fleeting moment into a fixed point in space, stitching those points into bathymetry maps. But it didn’t fix the lie. A smooth, rendered red arch with GPS coordinates is still a lie. It’s a fake landscape, a movie set of the ocean. The real bathymetry is the squiggles, messy and alive and full of secrets.

Right now, out there in the Bering Sea’s darkness, a hundred transducers are pinging out their questions. Some skippers are leaning over rolls of thermal paper, squinting at the squiggles, arguing with their mates over a single blip. Some are staring at LCD screens, nodding along when the red arch says “fish here.” The ocean doesn’t care about your 4K display resolution. It doesn’t care about your fancy GPS coordinates. It just answers the question.

The art of sounding isn’t about measuring depth. It’s about listening to the echo, trusting the noise, reading the ocean’s messy, beautiful handwriting. The ping goes out. The echo comes back. The ocean has spoken. The only question left is: will you write down what it said, or will you just stare at a pretty screen and nod?
