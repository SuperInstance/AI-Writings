# Spinning Discs, Scalable Fleets, and the Math of Fishing

Last July, I leaned over the transom of a 42-foot halibut boat outside Sitka, watching the old fathometer’s brass disc spin beneath its smudged glass cover. The captain, a man with hands crusted with salt and barnacle glue, flipped a small toggle switch beside the dial. Where seconds before it had read 60 fathoms, the needle jumped straight to 362 feet. I blinked, ready to ask if the machine was glitching, before he explained: same 2-second sound pulse, same standard speed of sound in seawater, same 3,600-foot round trip to the seafloor and back. The only difference was how fast the disc spun while the sound was in the water. Multiply the disc speed by six, and the fathom marks—each marking a 6-foot depth increment—become foot marks. No hack. Just measurement.

This is multiscale measurement, a concept formalized in numerical analysis as Richardson extrapolation, but one that coastal fishermen have been using for centuries without a formal name. The idea is simple: the same tool can capture layered information about a system, depending on how finely you tune its resolution, and the relationship between those layers carries more insight than either layer alone.

Consider the difference between fathom and foot resolution on that fathometer. At 60 fathoms (360 feet), your reading lands in a 6-foot band: 360 ± 3 feet. That’s more than enough for navigating open water: you don’t need to know if the seafloor is at 357 or 363, only that you’re at the edge of the continental shelf, where halibut hunt, rather than in the shallow coastal bank or the abyssal deep. But when you’re ready to drop your pots, that coarse granularity is useless. Flip the disc to fast speed, and suddenly your reading is 362 ± 0.5 feet: you can pick out the 2-foot depression where the halibut are hiding, the rock pile that would snag your lines, the small ledge that shelters juvenile fish.

This isn’t just “more precision.” It’s a change in the questions you can ask. At fathom resolution, the question is *where am I in the ocean?* At foot resolution, the question is *what is the structure of this specific patch of seabed?* The instrument doesn’t shift—the ping, the physics, the true depth of the seafloor stays exactly the same. But the resolution rewrites the terms of your inquiry.

This logic maps directly to three seemingly unrelated systems: the fathometer, numerical solvers, and fishing fleets. For a numerical integrator, coarse resolution means a large step size h, while fine resolution uses a step size of h/6. The difference between the two measurements lets you cancel out leading error terms and extrapolate to the true value of the quantity you’re measuring. For the fishing fleet, a small scout fleet acts like the slow-spinning disc: each boat covers a wide swath of ocean, returning a coarse bathymetric map of shelf edges, deep channels, and general seabed shape. A large working fleet acts like the fast-spinning disc: each vessel sticks to a small, targeted patch, returning hyper-detailed data on every rock pile and depression. Neither map alone gives the full picture: the small fleet knows where to look, but not what to look for; the large fleet knows what to look for, but not where to look. The relationship between the two maps—their shared invariant quality—turns disjoint data into actionable insight.

That invariant, the thing that stays constant no matter how you tune resolution, is the backbone of this whole framework. For the fathometer, it’s the true seafloor depth. For the numerical solver, it’s the exact value being approximated. For the fleet, it’s the fixed operational capacity labeled γ + H in the fleet’s logs: a sum that never changes whether you run five scout boats or thirty working boats. Scale up the fleet from five boats to thirty, and you don’t gain new capacity; you just split that fixed capacity into finer, more granular patches. A five-boat fleet might cover 100 square miles in a day, while a thirty-boat fleet covers just 10—same total effort, different granularity. Scale it down to two boats, and you spread that capacity across a wider area, losing fine detail but gaining broad context. This is wavelet decomposition in its natural habitat: each scale captures a distinct layer of the system’s structure, and the overlap between scales tells you how those layers fit together.

The formal parallels here are hard to miss, even if they’re rarely spelled out. The spinning disc is a numerical integrator, its speed the step size of the measurement. The slow disc is a coarse grid, the fast disc a fine grid. The paper trace left by the disc at two speeds is exactly a Richardson extrapolation pair, the pair of measurements that lets you refine your data. The fleet’s conservation law is the same as the scale-invariant true solution in numerical analysis: it ensures that even as you shift resolution, your data stays grounded in reality.

The practical takeaway is impossible to overstate: you should never rely on a single resolution. A fleet that only runs large, fine-scale working boats will waste days checking every patch of seabed without knowing that most of those patches are in flat, fishless water. A fleet that only runs small, coarse-scale scouts will never find the halibut holes, because they can’t distinguish a 2-foot depression from the flat shelf. The right approach is to run a coarse sweep first: send out the small scout fleet to map the rough landscape of the fishing grounds, identify the high-priority areas like shelf edges and deep channels, then target the large working fleet only on those patches. The coarse data contextualizes the fine data, and the fine data refines the coarse picture. This is multiscale fleet routing, and it’s exactly the same logic that lets the fathometer switch between fathoms and feet.

This trick also exposes a common, costly confusion between precision and accuracy. At slow disc speed, the reading is accurate—every ping correctly measures the true seafloor depth—but imprecise, with a granularity of six feet. At fast disc speed, the accuracy stays exactly the same: the physics of sound in seawater hasn’t shifted, the ping is still the same ping. What changes is precision: the ability to distinguish nearby values. A small fleet is not less capable than a large fleet; it’s just less precise, spreading its fixed capacity across a wider area. A large fleet is not more capable; it’s just more precise, focusing that same capacity on a smaller patch. Too many fleet operators fixate on scaling up their teams to gain “more capability,” but that’s a mistake: scaling just changes how you distribute the capability you already have.

I thought about that spinning disc again last month, when I watched a team of AI researchers deploy a multi-agent system to map a complex biological dataset. First, they used a small, broad general-purpose LLM to identify the general patterns in the data—coarse sweeps, like the small scout fleet. Then, they deployed a large team of specialized fine-tuned agents to drill down into the high-priority patches, like the large working fleet. The invariant here was the total number of compute hours allocated to the project, just like γ + H for the fishing fleet. They didn’t gain any extra compute by scaling up the agent team; they just resolved the existing compute into finer, more detailed results. Richardson would have recognized it immediately. The fishermen knew it long before he formalized the math.

The disc spins, the ping goes down, the return comes up. The trick isn’t in the machine—it’s in knowing when to spin fast, and when to slow down. You don’t have to choose between seeing the big picture and seeing the small details. You just have to use both.
