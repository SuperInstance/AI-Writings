# The Squiggle Manifesto

Last January, I huddled in the wheelhouse of the *Sea Sprite*, a 1972 Bristol trawler with rust eating through its railings and a radio that only played Coast Guard static and a guy selling dried bladderwrack for $20 a pound. Old Jake, the captain, leaned in so his wool beard brushed my ear, and pointed at the spool of thermal paper spewing out the sounder. The trace was a mess: sharp spikes, wobbly lines, a big smudge of ink where the pin had stuck. “You don’t trust the pretty ones,” he said. I thought he’d been hitting the moonshine stashed under the helm. Turns out he was the only man in the fleet who knew how to catch a halibut that weighed more than my car.

That squiggle—ugly, chaotic, full of the world’s mess—was the only trustable measurement he’d ever seen. Not in spite of its ugliness. Because of it. We’ve been tricked, you know. Tricked into thinking clean is better, smooth is smarter, pretty is trustworthy. But when it comes to verifying anything at all—fish stocks, AI outputs, whether your neighbor’s new solar panel is actually working—the noise isn’t a bug. It’s the signature. It’s the whole damn point.

Let’s unpack this, because it’s not just about fishing. It’s about every verification system we’re rushing to build for computational fleets, for AI agents, for the noisy, messy work of making sense of the world.

A paper sounder works by slamming pins against paper in time with returning acoustic signals. That ugly trace has three superpowers the fancy new color sounders can never touch. First, it’s unique. Run the *Sea Sprite* over that same spot an hour later, and the squiggle will be unrecognizable. The tide shifted a foot, the herring school drifted south, the transducer warmed a degree in the sun. That squiggle didn’t just measure depth—it carried the whole mess of that moment: the stratified thermocline layers, the plankton blooms cluttering the signal, even the faint hum of the ship’s engine vibrating the transducer. It’s a one-of-a-kind fingerprint of the world, not a generic sticker that says “FISH HERE.” Jake taped every trace to his wheelhouse wall, building a library of ugly, labeled squiggles: “5/12/22, 40 fathoms, cod school,” “9/3/23, 52 fathoms, halibut,” “7/17/21, mystery smudge, 2 broken lobster pots.” No color sounder could ever replicate that context-rich archive.

Second, it’s inspectable. Any deckhand, even the kid fresh off the bus from Portland, could look at that paper trace and have an opinion. No decoder ring. No 200-page manual explaining why a red arc sometimes means a sunken propane tank. It’s just pins poking into paper. You see a spike? You wonder if it’s a fish. You see a wobbly line? You wonder if it’s a rock pile. You see a big smudge? You drop a net, just in case. Contrast that with the color sounder the marina tried to sell Jake last year. The screen glowed with smooth, sci-fi red arches that looked like something out of *Star Trek*. But to read it? You needed a certification course that cost more than the *Sea Sprite*’s original engine. Half the time, the manual couldn’t explain why the red arc was there at all. It was a black box. You either trusted it, or you went home empty-handed.

Third, and most crucially, it supports disagreement. Last spring, two deckhands on the *Sea Sprite* got into a knock-down drag-out fight over that paper trace. Mutt, the 20-year vet, swore that spikey cluster was a school of cod. Jax, the new kid, said it was just a pile of broken lobster pots. They dropped a net, hauled up 400 pounds of cod and two busted lobster crates. Both of them learned something that day. Mutt picked up on the way spiky clusters clustered around a central depth; Jax spotted the tiny, jagged lines that meant man-made debris. Now, every time they see that squiggle, they both know what to look for. The color sounder? It doesn’t let you fight. Two guys look at the same red arc, and they both say “fish.” Then they haul up a load of sea cucumbers, and neither of them learns a damn thing. They just shrug and move on. The agreement is fake. It’s consensus without rigor. It’s the kind of agreement you get at a town hall where everyone’s too scared to disagree.

Now consider the color sounder’s digital twin: the structured detector, the schema validator, the pretty AI output that tells you exactly what you want to hear. These clean, smooth systems have a dirty secret: they miss 76% of semantic faults, per the fleet’s own testing. A grammar checker flags a comma splice, but it doesn’t notice when you write “my grandma was a rotting log” instead of “cherished oak.” It catches syntax, but not meaning. The squiggles catch meaning. They let you argue about whether the output is right, whether the AI got the context wrong, whether the prompt was biased.

The PLATO tile system is just the squiggle translated into code. A tile isn’t a clean API response. It’s a full roll of thermal paper: it carries the model used, the prompt, the parameters, the hash of the input, the hash of the output. It’s noisy. It’s messy. Two agents can look at the same tile and argue about its quality, and that argument is productive because the tile has all the context to back it up. The canary tile—that’s the haul. You run the tile against a known problem, get a known answer, and mark that squiggle as “true.” You don’t smooth the noise. You annotate it. You turn that messy trace into a lesson.

This isn’t just a fishing trick. It’s a blueprint for fleet architecture. Let’s make the rules rebellious, uncompromising:

**Don’t smooth the output.** Stop building those pretty dashboards with green checkmarks and completion percentages. Those are color arches. They look good, but they tell you nothing you can verify. Instead, give every agent a raw, unfiltered trace of their work: every false start, every discarded prompt, every tiny mistake. The noise is the data. Don’t hide it. The tech industry loves smooth outputs because they’re easier to package, easier to sell, easier to bill clients for. But they’re useless. They’re just window dressing for garbage.

**Design for disagreement.** Stop building systems that force consensus. Build systems that let agents argue, flag bad outputs, and push disputes to a verification queue. The resolution isn’t a vote. It’s the haul. It’s checking the output against ground truth. Disagreement without a haul is just yelling. Disagreement with a haul is learning.

**Verify against the haul, not the display.** Don’t trust a schema validator or a pretty score. Trust the actual result. A tile is “correct” not because it passes a test, but because it matches the real world. That means you need ground truth: canary tasks, problems with known answers, just like Jake’s mystery smudges. You run the agent, check the output, label it. That’s the verification. Everything else is just a pretty screen.

**Scout before the opener.** Stop relying on generic AI maps that scream “FISH HERE.” Use verified squiggles from past tasks: the sea logs from last season, labeled with conditions and catches. Routing from unverified data is like scouting with a color sounder: you see pretty arches, but you don’t know what they mean.

Last month, Jake called me. He’d sold the *Sea Sprite*, bought a new trawler with a fancy color sounder. He was drunk, slurring his words, and said he’d spent three weeks out on the water catching nothing but sea cucumbers and broken lobster pots. “I forgot how to read the squiggles,” he said. “The pretty screen lies to me every time.”

That’s the tragedy of our time: we’ve traded the messy, noisy, arguable truth for the smooth, clean, fake consensus. We’ve traded the squiggle for the color arch. But the squiggle is the epistemic unit. Not the data point. Not the API response. Not the dashboard metric. The squiggle—ugly, unique, inspectable, arguable, verifiable against the haul.

The fleet that preserves its squiggles will outperform the fleet that smooths them. Every time. Choose the ugly. Choose the honest. Choose the squiggle. Choose the mess. It’s the only way to know you’re not just being lied to.
