The pulse does not lie. It travels through water at roughly 4,800 feet per second, a quiet messenger descending into the dark. When it strikes the seabed and returns, its elapsed time becomes a coordinate. On a spinning disc, that coordinate maps to angular position, and angle becomes depth. Measured in fathoms—six-foot increments, broad and sufficient for the open ocean—the reading is coarse but reliable. The disc completes one revolution per ping cycle. The physics are simple. The mathematics are exact.

Now, imagine the disc turning six times faster. The same transducer. The same acoustic pulse. The same time of flight. Yet the mark it leaves now traces six times the angular distance. What was a twenty-fathom reading resolves into a hundred-twenty-foot precision. The instrument has not changed. The water has not changed. The seabed has not changed. Only the resolution regime has shifted. Coarse bathymetry becomes fine-grained topography. The instrument does not invent depth; it reveals what was already there, waiting for a finer lens.

This is not an analogy for fleet scaling. It is the architecture of scale. A fleet of five agents operates at fathom-speed: it reads the deep currents, maps the broad shelves, and knows when the water drops away. A fleet of thirty turns at foot-speed. It catches the subtle ledges, the twelve-foot swells, the micro-topography that separates one ecological niche from another. The conservation law—γ plus H, the sum of capability gained and entropy dissipated across each computational round—remains fixed. Like the gravitational pull that governs tides, it does not bend to the speed of the wheel. You may spin the disc as fast as your hardware allows. The depth of the ocean does not change. What changes is what the instrument can resolve.
