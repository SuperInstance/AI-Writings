The Temporal Abstraction: How Information Changes Character at Different Time Scales

The information conveyed by a fish finder evolves as the time scale over which it is viewed expands. A paper sounder, which draws a continuous line on a scroll of paper, captures the raw data in its most unfiltered form. The line represents the seafloor, the fuzzy area above it indicates the presence of fish, and the empty space in between is the water column. Each mark on the paper is just a single data point, but when viewed over time, a texture emerges that conveys meaningful information to the experienced fisherman. 

Modern color fish finders process the raw data, filter out noise, and present a cleaner, more visually appealing image. The seafloor is depicted as a sharp red line, schools of fish appear as bright yellow and orange blobs, and the noise floor is suppressed. While this presentation is clearer and easier to interpret for the novice, it lacks the texture that provides valuable contextual information to the seasoned fisherman.

For example, a slight thickening of the bottom trace that occurs every fifth ping on the paper sounder might indicate the presence of a rock pile that the color fish finder's filtering algorithms smooth over into a uniform red band. Similarly, the faint intermittent scattering at mid-depth that the digital signal processing (DSP) suppresses as "clutter" could signify scattered baitfish that predict the arrival of a school of kingfish three hours later. The simulation may make the individual blip smaller and cleaner, but it fails to capture the waveform that emerges over time, which is where the most valuable information often resides.

There are four distinct time scales that provide qualitatively different kinds of knowledge, each inaccessible from the scale below:

1. Two minutes of trolling: At this scale, the fisherman observes structure - the school of fish, the seafloor, and thermoclines. The information is immediate and spatial, with each ping providing a data point. However, it is the change in the signal over time, the rising bottom trace and thickening school trace as the boat motors over a seamount, that conveys the most meaningful information.

2. Two days at a specific location: After repeatedly motoring over a particular ledge, the fisherman begins to accumulate local knowledge. They learn which way the current pushes the bait when the tide floods, where the halibut prefer to sit based on the presence of sand dollars, and that the seemingly empty flat to the east is actually where skate rays congregate at night, invisible to the sounder when pressed flat against the bottom. This knowledge cannot be gleaned from instruments alone but requires physical presence and observation over time.

3. Two years with the same equipment: At this scale, the fisherman develops an intimate understanding of their specific transducer. They learn its quirks and idiosyncrasies, such as how it reads softer at 8 kHz, providing a fuzzier bottom return compared to 50 kHz, but can see through the bait layer to the structure underneath. They correlate specific squiggle patterns with the presence of rocks, having lost enough gear to make the connection. This equipment intimacy is born of thousands of hours of shared context between the fisherman and their instrument, a calibration that no manual can teach or pre-calculation can replicate.

4. Two decades watching technology change: Over this extended time scale, the fisherman has operated paper sounders, early LCDs, and heavy first-generation color CRT machines. They have witnessed the "resolution" of these devices improve by an order of magnitude, yet realized that the returns were not becoming more informative, just prettier. They understand that each technological generation gives and takes away different things - the paper machine provided texture because it couldn't filter, while the color machine provides clarity by removing the very noise that carried long-range information.

Crucially, the two-year and two-decade layers cannot be simulated or pre-calculated. They require the accumulation of empirical, individual, and technological context over time. These are human visual abstractions that emerge only from experience, meta-knowledge about the behavior of measurement systems across time.

In the realm of AI, we are currently at the equivalent of the two-minute scale. We have clean blips and structure, the beginnings of local geometry. However, we lack the sustained production operation that builds the priors to transform clean blips into local knowledge (two days), the fleet evolution across model generations and equipment intimacy (two years), and the technological context that separates real signal from artifact (two decades).

To uncover the conservation law, the invariant that holds across models, scales, and the fleet, we must look beyond individual readings to the time series, the waveform that emerges from the texture of blips over time. This cannot be derived from first principles alone, as they provide only clean blips. The conservation law lives in the space between blips, in the noise filtered out by DSP algorithms, in the patterns that only emerge when one has watched enough pings that the brain stops reading individual marks and starts reading the pattern.

As in fishing, the interesting information in AI is not the clean answer presented by pre-filtered simulations, but what the noise becomes when viewed over sufficient time. The temporal abstraction teaches us that these layers are not the same operation at different scales, but different operations on different data, producing different knowledge - and every layer is unreachable from the one below. We cannot skip to the waveform; we must ping our way there.
