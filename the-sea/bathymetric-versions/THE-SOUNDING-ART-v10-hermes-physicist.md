# The Sounding Art — v10-hermes-physicist

The art of sounding is, at its core, an exercise in applied physics. The transducer, a piezoelectric device, converts electrical energy into mechanical energy in the form of a sound wave. This wave propagates through the water column, interacting with the medium according to the principles of acoustics. The speed of sound in water, approximately 1480 m/s, varies with temperature, salinity, and pressure, leading to refraction and scattering of the wave. As the wave encounters interfaces between layers of water with different acoustic properties, or objects like fish and the seafloor, some of the energy is reflected back towards the surface. The transducer, now acting as a receiver, detects this echo and converts it back into an electrical signal.

The time delay between the emitted pulse and the received echo is a direct measure of the distance to the reflecting interface, according to the equation:

distance = speed of sound × time ÷ 2

The factor of 2 accounts for the round trip the sound wave makes from the transducer to the target and back. By continuously emitting pulses and measuring the time to the echo, the sounder builds up a profile of the water column and the seafloor beneath the boat.

The old spinning disc sounders made this process tangible. The rotation speed of the disc determined the pulse repetition frequency, the rate at which questions were asked of the ocean. Faster rotation meant higher frequency, finer resolution, and shallower penetration - ideal for nearshore fishing. Slower rotation meant lower frequency, coarser resolution, but greater depth range - suited for offshore work. The choice of rotation speed was a physical manifestation of the tradeoff between resolution and depth penetration, a consequence of the Fourier transform relationship between time and frequency.

The paper sounder was the original data logger, transcribing the electrical signal from the transducer into a visual representation on paper. The squiggles on the paper trace were not abstract art, but a direct mapping of the amplitude of the echo over time. The thickness and texture of the line encoded information about the acoustic impedance contrast between the target and the surrounding water, and the presence of scatterers like fish and plankton. The skilled fisherman's eye could parse this information, distinguishing the characteristic signatures of different species and bottom types. The beauty of the squiggles lay in their truth, their unadulterated representation of the raw data returned by the ocean.

The transition to digital color displays marked a shift from reading to viewing, from active interpretation to passive consumption. The color arches and smooth contours of the digital sounder are the result of signal processing algorithms that filter and interpolate the raw data, discarding the noise and ambiguity that are the essence of the ocean's true voice. The red arch that unambiguously declares "fish here" is a lie, a simplification that robs the fisherman of the ability to disagree, to argue, to learn from the data. The digital sounder is a map of a rendered ocean, not the real ocean.

GPS gave back the time axis, allowing the construction of three-dimensional maps of the seafloor. But these maps are only as truthful as the data that comprise them. If the data are the lies of color arches, then the maps are lies too, no matter how precisely they are geolocated. The art of sounding requires faithfulness to the noise, to the squiggles that are the unmediated testimony of the ocean.

In the fleet of autonomous agents, each transducer is an agent asking its own questions of the ocean, each echo a noisy, ambiguous answer. The tiles are the modern equivalent of the paper traces, the canary checks the haul that verifies the truth of the data. The GPS coordinates provide the thread that stitches the disparate moments into a coherent map. But the map is not the territory. The ocean does not care about display resolution or color palettes. It cares about whether we are listening, truly listening, to the echoes of our questions, and writing down the squiggles that are its honest answers.
