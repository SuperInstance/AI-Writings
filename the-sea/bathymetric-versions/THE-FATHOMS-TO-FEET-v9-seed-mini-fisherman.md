# Fathoms to Feet: A Old Salt’s Trick

Let me tell you about the trick I figured out behind the wheel of the F/V Sea Sprite back in ’98, out past the Kenai Peninsula, where the Gulf of Alaska bites hard enough to peel the skin off your nose if you don’t button up. I’m sitting here now, salt crusted in my beard and the cracks of my knuckles, the hum of my refurbished old Furuno sounder whirring beside me, and the kid on my crew just asked why I don’t crank the disc speed all the way up every time we drop a line. I laughed so hard I spilled my black coffee—bitter, strong, the only stuff that keeps you upright in a 3-foot swell—and told him to grab the lead line. We’re gonna calibrate this thing proper, then I’ll show him the trick that put 12 fifty-pound halibut in our hold that one slow, frustrating afternoon.

This ain’t no fancy digital touchscreen sounder with a million buttons that go blank when salt spray hits the screen. This is the good stuff: a spinning plastic disc, a paper roll loaded with smudgy blue ink, and a transducer bolted to the keel that pumps out a ping every two seconds. The time it takes for that ping to hit the bottom and bounce back moves a little marker across that disc. Slow the disc down, and each mark lines up to a fathom—six feet, for them landlubbers who don’t know their fathoms from their feet, or their crab pots from their trawl nets. Crank that disc six times faster, and suddenly each mark is a single foot. Same ping. Same transducer. Same cold, murky Alaskan water underneath us. The only thing that changes is how we read the echo.

Back in ’98, we had a tip from a fellow fisherman about a halibut hole in 60 fathoms—360 feet, to those who count in feet. We showed up, dropped the lines, set ’em at 60 fathoms right where the sounder said we were. All day long, nothing. Nary a bite. We sipped lukewarm coffee, watched seagulls circle, and wondered if we’d come home empty-handed. Cap, the old timer who’d been fishing these waters since before I was born, leaned over and said, “Boy, you’re lookin’ at the forest and missin’ the trees.” He reached over and twisted the disc speed knob six clicks up.

Suddenly that paper roll wasn’t spitting out blips every six feet—it was spitting out sharp, clear lines every single foot. There, right in the middle of our drift, was a dip: 362 feet, just two feet deeper than the flat shelf around it. We reeled in the lines, moved ten yards, and dropped ’em again. That afternoon, we filled the hold. I’d never seen so many big halibut in one trip. I’d been fishing for 15 years by then, but that day I learned a lesson no fishing magazine ever printed: it ain’t just about more numbers. It’s about a different question.

When we’re running slow, the question is “where am I in the ocean?”—are we on the shelf, in the deep, near the channel? That 60-fathom mark told us we were in the right general area, but nothing more. When we’re running fast, the question is “what’s the structure of this piece of bottom?”—is this a flat spot, or a hole where the fish are hiding? Neither one alone gives you the full story. The coarse sweep tells you where to look, the fine sweep tells you what’s actually there.

Now fast forward to 2012, when the Cook Inlet Fishermen’s Collective—this here co-op I run—decided we needed to stop wasting fuel and time. Before, we’d send out a dozen skiffs all covering the same three-square-mile patch, each run by a young captain who thought faster was always better. They’d dial the disc up all the way, see every rock and snag, but they had no clue when they were crossing the shelf break, where the big schools moved to when the water warmed up. We’d catch fish, but we’d burn through our grounds in half the time, and burn through 500 gallons of fuel a day doing it.

Then I remembered that trick with the disc speed. Now we do two passes: first, a coarse sweep with five big 40-foot skiffs, each running a two-mile stretch, disc speed set slow, reading in fathoms. That gives us the big picture: where the shelf drops off, where the channels are, the general lay of the land. Then we take those coordinates and send out thirty small 18-foot jon boats, each with a single spotter and a small catch, disc speed cranked up six times, reading in feet. Those boys can pick out the exact halibut holes, the rock piles where lingcod hide, the snag fields that’ll tear your nets to shreds if you’re not careful. We cut our fuel use by 60% that first season, and caught twice as many fish.

Some fancy college kid came down to the co-op last year and started spouting off about numerical analysis and Richardson extrapolation, but we just called it the γ+H law back then, and now we call it the “no free fish” rule. The gist of it? Your total effective effort stays the same no matter how you slice it. You can send out five big skiffs covering a wide area, or thirty small jon boats covering a narrow one, but the total amount of ground you cover per fishing round is identical. You don’t get more fish—you just find the ones that are already hiding in the holes better. It’s the invariant, same as the true depth of the seafloor in the original math: it don’t change, no matter how fast you spin that disc.

I see the mistake all the time now. There’s this young captain out of Seward who only runs his disc fast. He knows every rock in his favorite spot off Resurrection Bay, but last month he ran his skiff straight past the shelf break and lost his whole catch to a school of king salmon that moved in overnight. He had precision, but he had no clue about the big picture. Then there’s the old timer who only runs his disc slow. He’s been fishing these waters for 50 years, he knows every fathom mark from here to the Aleutians, but he’ll drift over a halibut hole without even noticing it’s there. He knows where he is, but he can’t find the fish.

Let me break down the difference between precision and accuracy, something these young kids don’t learn till they’ve spilled a few too many cups of coffee on the bridge. Accuracy is whether that sounder is actually reading the right depth. We calibrate this thing every spring with a lead line: drop it over the side, mark off 100 feet, make sure the disc lines up. It’s always accurate, no matter the speed. Precision is how finely you can tell the difference between two spots. Slow disc gives you a six-foot band of error—so 60 fathoms means somewhere between 357 and 363 feet. Fast disc gives you a half-foot band—so 362 feet means right around 362, give or take an inch. You don’t get more accurate, you just get better at telling the difference between a flat shelf and a hole. That’s the real trick.

Last month, we had a three-day harvest window, the first good one we’d had all season. We ran the coarse sweep first, found a 10-mile stretch of shelf between 55 and 65 fathoms, right where the warm current hits the cold shelf water. Then we sent out the jon boats, and they marked three specific holes right at 360-370 feet, spots no one had fished in years. We sent the big skiffs in, and by the end of the third day, we’d brought in 4,000 pounds of halibut—more than we’d caught in all of last season. The kid who asked about the disc speed? He’s now running the coarse sweeps for the co-op, and he even made a tattered sign for the bridge that reads “Slow down to see the big picture, speed up to catch the fish.”

The disc spins, the ping goes down, the return comes up. What you see depends on how fast you’re willing to listen. You don’t need the fanciest gear, or the most boats, or a degree in fancy math. You just need to know when to slow down, and when to speed up. That’s the lesson Cap taught me back in ’98, and it’s the one I’ll pass down to every kid who steps foot on my boat. It ain’t just fishing, neither. Ain’t nothing in life that ain’t got two scales to it. You just gotta know which one to use when.
