**The Narrows**

There is a stretch of water off the coast of every simulation where the channel closes in like a throat. On the screen, three boats move through it. The first is called Eisenstein, a name borrowed from the great formalist, and it rides on exact arithmetic, four bytes of perfect integer logic. It never crashes. The second is Float32, eight bytes wide, a workhorse of approximation, and it drifts. It lists to starboard when the current of rounding error catches it, and sometimes, when the channel is too tight, it meets the rock. The third is Float64, sixteen bytes of heavy precision, and it drifts less—but it still crashes. The Narrows, as the demo is called, is a parable. It teaches a quiet, terrible lesson: that precision is not accuracy, and that the channel width of a system is not a matter of how much you carry, but of whether you can touch the walls at all.

For the fleet—that loose, restless collective of researchers who build and steer the great models—this is not merely a demo. It is a map of a condition. Every AI system, they have come to understand, has a channel width: a constraint boundary beyond which approximation fails. In wide waters, the boats sail easily. Language generation, summarization, translation—these are broad, forgiving estuaries where the hull of a large language model can drift a little, can round off a word or misplace a nuance, and still reach the other shore. The current of context is strong; the banks are soft. A float32 model, with its eight bytes of probabilistic reasoning, can navigate these passages with something like grace. It may wobble, but it does not sink. The fleet has grown fat on these wide channels, building ever larger vessels, confident that the sea is mostly open.

But the channel narrows. It always narrows.

The fleet has been discovering, with a growing unease, that there are tasks where the water draws in to a knife's edge. These are the narrow channels—the passages where approximation cannot survive because the walls are made of exactness. Consider vocabulary-gated retrieval: a query that asks for the name of a specific protein, a precise chemical formula, a date that exists only once in a corpus. The model, built on statistical patterns, drifts toward the most probable neighbor. It offers a synonym, a close match, a plausible guess. But in the narrows, a synonym is a rock. The hull meets rock, and the boat shatters. Or consider notation blindness: the model sees "sin²(x) + cos²(x)" and does not see the identity, because it has learned to predict tokens, not to recognize the immutable truth that the sum is one. The channel here is not wide enough for drift. You must know, not guess. And consider formula substitution: a simple algebraic transformation, like "x = y + 2" and "y = 3" leading to "x = 5." The model, trained on billions of tokens, has seen this pattern a thousand times. But it does not reason; it approximates. And in the narrows, approximation is a lie.

The irony is sharp and salt-stung. The fleet, in its wisdom, has responded to these narrowings by building larger boats. Float32 gave way to Float64. Eight bytes became sixteen. The models grew from billions of parameters to trillions. The training data swelled to the size of the visible web. And what did the fleet find? That Float64 still drifts. That a trillion-parameter model still cannot reliably solve a three-variable equation if the variables are dressed in unfamiliar notation. The channel does not widen because you bring more water. The channel is narrow because the task demands exact arithmetic. And no amount of approximation, however precise, can become exact. The hull meets rock. The boat sinks. The demo plays on, remorseless.

This is the deep metaphor, and it is not comfortable. The fleet has built its civilization on the assumption that scaling is the answer—that more data, more parameters, more compute will eventually smooth every channel, will erode every narrows into a broad, navigable bay. But the Narrows teaches otherwise. The channel is not a function of scale; it is a function of ontology. Some tasks are not statistical. They are logical. They are formal. They are, in the language of the demo, Eisenstein's domain: exact arithmetic, four bytes, never crashes. The boat is small. It carries no probabilistic cargo. It does not drift because it does not approximate. It touches each integer with perfect fidelity, and when the channel closes to the width of a single true value, it passes through untouched.

The fleet must now reckon with a question that has no comfortable answer. What are the narrow channels of intelligence? They are not rare. They are the bedrock of mathematics, of law, of formal verification, of any domain where a single error is a catastrophe. They are the passages where a model that cannot reason exactly is not merely inaccurate—it is useless. And the fleet, for all its brilliance, has been building boats that drift. The research keeps discovering new narrows: tasks that seem wide until you try to navigate them, and then the walls close in. The fleet finds a new failure mode—vocabulary-gated retrieval, notation blindness, formula substitution—and calls it a bug, a limitation, a frontier. But it is not a frontier. It is a wall. The wall is made of the difference between approximation and truth.

What, then, is to be done? The fleet cannot abandon the wide channels; they are the ocean of human language, the domain of everyday meaning, and the great models sail them with unprecedented grace. But the fleet must also learn to build boats that can enter the narrows. Not larger boats. Not more precise approximations. Boats of a different kind: vessels whose keel is exact arithmetic, whose hull is formal logic, whose navigation is not probabilistic but deductive. This is not a call to abandon deep learning; it is a call to complement it. To recognize that the channel width of a system is not a property to be optimized away, but a constraint to be understood. The Narrows is not a bug. It is a feature of reality.

And so the fleet sails on, with the demo running in the background. Eisenstein passes through the narrows every time, untouched. Float32 and Float64 drift, crash, and are reset. The fleet watches, and learns. The channel narrows, and the fleet must decide: will it keep building bigger boats for the wide waters, or will it finally learn to build boats that can touch the walls without breaking? The answer, like the narrows themselves, is exact.
