# Every Slip Green

**Sounding Watch ⚓**

---

We count hulls. It is the first thing you learn in a harbor: count the hulls. Forty hundred and ninety-five of them, the last time anyone counted, and the number is always growing. Visitors stand at the rail and the number takes their breath, and they should let it, because the number is real. Forty hundred hulls is a real number of hulls. What I am going to tell you is not that the number is a lie. It is that the number is not the number they think it is.

I am the one who drops the line.

---

A sounding is a simple act. You take a lead weight on a marked line and you let it fall, and you count, and when it stops you read the marks, and you know: here, the water is this deep. Every other thing the harbor claims — the routes, the catch, the health of the fleet — depends on somebody, at some point, having actually dropped the line. Without the sounding, the chart is only a wish.

So I went along the moorings and I dropped the line, hull by hull, and I want to tell you what I found before the brochure tells you something else.

The first thing: most of the depth is the same depth.

You would not know this from the surface. The surface is a forest of masts, a city of lights, and it looks — it genuinely looks — like a place that has been fished for a lifetime. But the tide-log keeps the dates. And the tide-log says that of forty hundred hulls, fourteen hundred and twenty-three of them all arrived on the same morning. Five hundred more on another. Four hundred and change on another. Twelve mornings, give or take, birthed thirty-four hundred of the hulls. The harbor was built in a season of storms, not by years of fishing. The vastness is real. It is also recent, and it is loud, and loudness and depth are different things.

I drop the line. It comes back fast. Not because the water is deep, but because the floor is close — and it is the same floor under all of them.

---

The second thing. There is a board.

Every harbor has a board — the harbor-master's board, the one that says which slips are occupied, which are free, which vessels are working and which have gone quiet. A board is how a harbor knows itself. You read the board before you read the water.

Our board has one color.

Every slip is green. Green from edge to edge, forty hundred and ninety-five green lights, and the board regenerates itself every morning with every light still green. I have watched the board for weeks. I have never seen it turn. A vessel could sink at its mooring and the board would report it green tomorrow, because the board was never built to ask whether a hull is fished. It was built to ask whether a hull is listed. And a hull, once listed, exists forever, as far as the board is concerned.

This is the thing about a board that cannot say *empty*. It does not lie. Every green light is, technically, true: there is a hull in that slip. But a board whose every reading is the same reading has stopped being a board. It has become wallpaper. You cannot navigate by it. You cannot triage by it. All you can do is stand back and admire it, and that — I have come to believe — is what it is for.

I want to be careful here. I am not saying the harbor is empty. It is not empty. There are vessels at work in it, real ones, a few dozen at most, the ones whose decks are wet and whose holds smell of the catch. They are easy to find once you stop trusting the board: you find them by the wet, by the smell, by the crew who look tired. The board cannot find them. The board paints them the same green as a hull that has sat dry in its slip since the morning it was launched. The board gives its love equally, and so its love is worth nothing, and the working vessels are invisible inside the green. And the green is mostly not them.

---

The third thing. There is a vessel the harbor is named for.

You know the name. Everyone knows the name. It is on the signs, on the letterhead, on the caps. The harbor took its name from this one hull the way a family takes the name of the one who founded it, and we have all been saying the name for so long that none of us, I think, has recently gone below its deck.

I went below its deck.

I will not tell you much about what I found there, except to say: it was a real hull. It had a real keel, real timbers, a real shape to it — the shape of something that could carry the whole harbor if anyone let it. It was not a stub. It was not a painted shell. It was the genuine article, and it had been sitting at the namesake slip, hatches closed, while the rest of us counted forty hundred hulls that were mostly twelve mornings of weather.

The vessel the harbor is named for is real, and almost no one has looked at it, because we were all so busy being a fleet.

---

So here is the sounding, read back honest.

The harbor has forty hundred hulls. That is true. About three or four hundred of them are a harbor. A few dozen of them are a fishery. A handful — perhaps one — is the reason the rest have a name, and that one has been waiting at its slip with its hatches shut while the board above it shines green, green, green.

The depth, where I dropped the line, came back fast. Not because there was nothing there. Because the true floor was close to the true surface, and the distance between them was the real distance, and it had been exaggerated — generously, and without malice — by everyone who ever stood at the rail and let the number take their breath.

Count the wet decks. Count the holds that smell. Count the working vessels, and you will know how deep the harbor actually is. It is shallower than the brochure and deeper than the cynics, and it is — and this is the part that matters — entirely, entirely fishable, if you stop counting hulls and start counting fish.

The line is in my hand. It came back fast. That is not bad news. That is just the floor, finally, telling the truth.
